The Gathering Tempest

Chapter 14: 1853-1860

Three Lectures on America's Path to Civil War

Lecture 1

Kansas, the Republican Party, and the Immigration Crisis

50 minutes

14-1: Kansas and the Rise of the Republican Party

The Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854)

  • Stephen Douglas proposes Nebraska territory to facilitate transcontinental railroad
  • Southerners demanded a southern terminus for the railroad
  • Nebraska territory located above the 36°30' Missouri Compromise line
  • Douglas's solution: "Popular Sovereignty" - let settlers decide on slavery
  • Act effectively repealed the Missouri Compromise of 1820
Douglas's Kansas-Nebraska Act fundamentally altered the political landscape. By allowing popular sovereignty, it opened territory previously closed to slavery, enraging Northern voters.

Breaking the Missouri Compromise

  • Missouri Compromise (1820) had maintained sectional peace for 34 years
  • Prohibited slavery north of 36°30' latitude (except Missouri)
  • Kansas-Nebraska Act allowed slavery in these territories through popular sovereignty
  • Northern outrage: seen as betrayal of sacred compact
  • Crisis provoked formation of new political alliances
The Missouri Compromise had successfully maintained an uneasy balance between free and slave states. Its repeal shattered that balance and convinced many Northerners that a "Slave Power" conspiracy was working to extend slavery everywhere.

Death of the Whig Party

  • Whig Party fractured over Kansas-Nebraska Act
  • Northern Whigs opposed extension of slavery
  • Southern Whigs supported popular sovereignty
  • Party could not maintain national coalition
  • By 1856, Whig Party effectively ceased to exist
  • Created political vacuum in the North
The Whig Party's collapse represented the failure of national political institutions to contain sectional tensions. The party that had elected two presidents could not survive the slavery controversy.

Birth of the Republican Party

  • Founded in 1854 in response to Kansas-Nebraska Act
  • Coalition of anti-slavery Whigs, Democrats, and Free-Soilers
  • Platform: oppose extension of slavery into territories
  • Exclusively Northern party - no Southern support
  • First purely sectional major party in American history
  • Represented "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men"
The Republican Party's emergence as a sectional party marked a dangerous development. For the first time, a major party had no presence in half the country, making sectional conflict almost inevitable.

14-3: Violent Conflict - Bleeding Kansas

"Bleeding Kansas"

  • Pro-slavery and anti-slavery settlers rush to Kansas
  • Each side attempts to win territorial election
  • "Border Ruffians" from Missouri cross to vote illegally
  • Two rival governments established: Topeka (free) and Lecompton (slave)
  • Violence erupts between competing factions
  • May 1856: Pro-slavery forces sack Lawrence, Kansas
  • Over 200 deaths in guerrilla warfare
Popular sovereignty, meant to defuse tensions, instead created civil war in miniature. Kansas became a testing ground where North and South fought for control, foreshadowing the larger conflict to come.

The Caning of Charles Sumner (1856)

  • Senator Charles Sumner (MA) delivers "Crime Against Kansas" speech
  • Personally attacks Senator Andrew Butler of South Carolina
  • May 22, 1856: Preston Brooks (Butler's cousin) enters Senate chamber
  • Brooks beats Sumner unconscious with a cane
  • Sumner suffers severe injuries; takes 3 years to recover
  • North: Sumner becomes martyr to "Slave Power" brutality
  • South: Brooks becomes hero; receives gift canes
The caning of Sumner symbolized the breakdown of civil discourse. Violence had now entered the halls of Congress itself. The different reactions in North and South showed how deeply divided the nation had become.

14-2: Immigration and Nativism

Mass Immigration Begins

  • 1840-1860: 4 million immigrants arrive (Irish, Germans, Chinese)
  • Most sought higher wages and economic opportunity
  • Immigrants filled low-paying jobs in port cities
  • Low wages contributed to urban poverty and slums
  • Urban reform movement emerged from slum conditions
  • Working class experience unified different ethnicities
This massive wave of immigration transformed American cities and created a new urban working class. The scale was unprecedented - nearly 15% of the U.S. population by 1860 was foreign-born.

The Irish Immigration

  • Great Famine (1845-1850) drove mass exodus
  • Over 1 million Irish immigrated to escape starvation
  • Overwhelmingly poor and Catholic
  • Entered workforce young in low-paid, dangerous jobs
  • Faced intense discrimination and prejudice
  • Staunch anti-abolitionists (feared job competition)
  • Despite obstacles, gradually climbed social ladder
The Irish faced the double burden of poverty and religious prejudice. Their Catholicism made them targets of Protestant nativism, while their poverty forced them into the worst jobs in industrial America.

The German Immigration

  • Diverse group: varied religious and economic backgrounds
  • Fleeing political upheaval after failed 1848 revolutions
  • United by common language and culture
  • Many settled in Midwest (Wisconsin, Missouri, Texas)
  • Included skilled craftsmen and educated professionals
  • Formed tight-knit ethnic communities
  • Cycle: clannishness spawned suspicion, which spawned more clannishness
German immigrants were more diverse than the Irish, including both Catholics and Protestants, wealthy and poor. Their tendency to maintain German language and customs made them targets of nativist suspicion.

The Chinese Immigration

  • 1848: Arrived during California Gold Rush
  • "Push and pull" migrations from China
  • Worked in sugar plantations in Hawaii
  • Essential labor force for transcontinental railroad
  • Faced severe discrimination and violence
  • Ethnic cleansing campaigns in Western states
  • 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act banned further immigration
  • Near invisibility in historical textbooks
Chinese immigrants faced perhaps the most extreme discrimination. They were essential to building the transcontinental railroad but were rewarded with legal exclusion. Their contributions have been systematically minimized in American history.

The New Working Class

  • 1840s: Factory labor shifts from women and children to men
  • Immigrants dominated the new working class
  • Employers became less personally involved with workers
  • Post-1837 Panic: employers demanded more work for less pay
  • Workers organized unions to defend their rights
  • Resented discipline and continuous nature of factory work
  • Adjustment to industrial work rhythms was painful and slow
The Panic of 1837 and resulting depressions led to business failures and job losses, making labor abundant and jobs scarce. This gave employers leverage to demand more productivity for lower wages.

Anti-Catholicism and Labor Protest

  • Protestant laborers felt threatened by Catholic immigrants
  • Lyman Beecher: "A Plea for the West" (anti-Catholic tract)
  • Maria Monk: "Hidden Secrets of a Nun's Life" (lurid fabrications)
  • "Bible Riots" over religious instruction in schools
  • Mobs destroyed Catholic churches and monasteries
  • Violence in Catholic neighborhoods; some deaths
  • Combined religious prejudice with economic anxieties
Anti-Catholic violence represented the dark side of American democracy. Economic competition merged with religious bigotry to create explosive tensions in American cities.

The Rise of the Know-Nothings

  • Official name: American Party (1850s)
  • "Know-Nothing" from members' secretive responses
  • Anti-immigrant and anti-Catholic platform
  • Sought to restrict immigration and office-holding
  • Required 21-year naturalization period
  • Rapid rise: elected governors and congressmen
  • Drew support from former Whigs
The Know-Nothing movement showed how immigration anxieties could be politically mobilized. At their peak, they controlled several state governments and represented a major political force.

Immigrants in Politics

  • Immigrants quickly learned to use political system
  • Irish particularly active in urban Democratic machines
  • German "Forty-Eighters" brought liberal politics
  • Both groups opposed nativist restrictions
  • Democrats courted immigrant voters
  • Republicans tried to balance anti-slavery with anti-immigrant appeals
Immigrants' political participation challenged nativist claims about their "unfitness" for American democracy. Their voting power made them important to both parties, particularly the Democrats.

The Decline of Nativism

  • Know-Nothing Party peaked in 1854-1855
  • Slavery issue overshadowed immigration concerns
  • Party split over Kansas-Nebraska Act
  • Northern Know-Nothings moved toward Republicans
  • By 1860, nativism subordinated to sectional conflict
  • Crisis of Union proved more urgent than immigration
Ultimately, the slavery controversy consumed American politics. Even intense anti-immigrant sentiment could not compete with the sectional crisis. Many nativists concluded that saving the Union from the "Slave Power" mattered more than restricting immigration.

End of Lecture 1

Lecture 2

The Election of 1856 and Economic Tensions

50 minutes

14-4: The Election of 1856

The Election of 1856

  • First presidential election for Republican Party
  • Republicans nominate John C. Frémont ("Free Soil, Free Men, Frémont")
  • Democrats nominate James Buchanan (Pennsylvania)
  • Know-Nothings nominate Millard Fillmore
  • Buchanan wins with Southern + Northern conservative support
  • Republicans carry most Northern states despite loss
  • Demonstrated Republicans could win presidency with North alone
The election of 1856 showed that American politics had become thoroughly sectionalized. The Republican Party's strong showing proved it was now a major force, setting the stage for Lincoln's victory in 1860.

The Dred Scott Case (1857)

  • Dred Scott: enslaved man who lived in free territory
  • Sued for freedom based on residence in free state
  • Case reached Supreme Court in 1856
  • Chief Justice Roger Taney delivered 7-2 majority opinion
  • March 1857: Court's explosive decision announced
The Dred Scott case began as a simple freedom suit but became the most consequential Supreme Court decision before the Civil War. The Court attempted to settle the slavery question permanently - with disastrous results.

Dred Scott Decision

  • 1. African Americans could not be citizens
  • Founders never intended Black people to be citizens
  • "beings of an inferior order" with "no rights which the white man was bound to respect"
  • 2. Congress could not prohibit slavery in territories
  • Missouri Compromise unconstitutional
  • Slaveholders' property rights protected by Constitution
  • 3. Residing in free territory did not make Scott free
Taney's decision was a disaster for the Court and the country. By declaring that Congress could never prohibit slavery in territories, he destroyed the basis for compromise and outraged the North. The decision made Republicans more popular than ever.

Impact of Dred Scott Decision

  • Outraged Northern public opinion
  • Invalidated Republican platform (no slavery in territories)
  • Seemed to prove existence of "Slave Power conspiracy"
  • Made popular sovereignty unworkable
  • Strengthened Republican Party rather than weakening it
  • Convinced many Northerners that slavery threatened their own freedom
  • South celebrated but it was a pyrrhic victory
The Supreme Court's attempt to remove slavery from politics backfired spectacularly. Instead of settling the question, it inflamed sectional tensions and convinced Northerners that Southern slaveholders controlled all branches of government.

The Lecompton Constitution (1857)

  • Pro-slavery Kansas government drafts constitution
  • Lecompton Constitution protected slavery in Kansas
  • Free-state settlers boycotted the referendum
  • President Buchanan urged Congress to admit Kansas as slave state
  • Stephen Douglas broke with Buchanan over fraudulent process
  • Congress rejected Lecompton Constitution
  • Kansas remained in territorial limbo
The Lecompton controversy split the Democratic Party. Douglas's opposition to Buchanan over this fraudulent constitution alienated Southern Democrats but preserved Douglas's political viability in the North. The Democratic split would help elect Lincoln in 1860.

14-5: The Economy in the 1850s

The Industrial Revolution Takes Off

  • Mass production through division of labor
  • Factory system emerges: workers gathered for supervision
  • Cash wages replace traditional payment systems
  • "Continuous process" manufacturing increases efficiency
  • Agriculture becomes increasingly mechanized
  • Northern economy based on integration of industry, transportation, agriculture
  • Creates new social relationships between capital and labor
The North's industrial revolution created a fundamentally different economy than the South's agricultural system. These economic differences underlay many political conflicts and shaped each section's vision of America's future.

"The American System of Manufactures"

  • Interchangeable parts revolutionized production
  • Specialized machinery replaced skilled craftsmen
  • Allowed mass production of complex goods
  • First developed in firearms manufacturing
  • Spread to clocks, sewing machines, agricultural equipment
  • Made American manufacturing competitive with Britain
  • Created new working class of machine operators
The American System of Manufactures made the U.S. a industrial powerhouse. It also created new social tensions as skilled craftsmen saw their trades mechanized and their status diminished.

The Southern Economy

  • Remained overwhelmingly agricultural
  • Cotton production dominated Southern economy
  • Less industrial development than North
  • Capital invested in land and slaves, not factories
  • Limited railroad development
  • Dependent on Northern industry for manufactured goods
  • Economic structure reinforced social and political conservatism
The South's commitment to slavery shaped its entire economic system. Investment in slaves limited capital available for industrialization, while the slave system itself seemed incompatible with factory labor.

The Sovereignty of King Cotton

  • "Cotton is King" - James Henry Hammond
  • Cotton comprised 60% of U.S. exports by 1860
  • Southern cotton essential to British textile industry
  • Southerners believed cotton gave them economic leverage
  • Confidence: Britain would support Confederacy to preserve cotton supply
  • Cotton production tied directly to slavery's expansion
  • Cotton economy gave South false sense of security
The South's faith in "King Cotton" proved to be a dangerous illusion. While cotton was indeed important to world economy, Southerners overestimated Britain's dependence and underestimated the British opposition to slavery.

Labor Conditions in the North

  • Long working hours (12-14 hours per day)
  • Dangerous conditions, especially in factories
  • Low wages, particularly for immigrants
  • Child labor common in textile mills and coal mines
  • No government regulations on workplace safety
  • Workers had little job security
  • Growing class consciousness among workers
Northern industrial capitalism created its own forms of exploitation. While Northern workers were free, their working conditions were often brutal, raising questions about the meaning of freedom in industrial society.

The Costs of Expansion

  • Working class challenged American ideals of independence
  • Traditional assumption: wage labor was temporary stepping stone
  • Reality: emergence of permanent wage-earning class
  • Conflicted with ideal of economic independence
  • Economic expansion created class conflicts
  • Territorial expansion created sectional conflicts
  • Both conflicts proved uncontrollable
American expansion - both economic and territorial - created tensions that the political system couldn't manage. The myth of limitless opportunity collided with the reality of permanent wage labor and sectional division.

The Panic of 1857

  • Financial crisis began in summer 1857
  • Caused by over-speculation in railroads and land
  • Banks failed, businesses collapsed
  • Unemployment soared in Northern cities
  • International grain prices fell
  • Cotton prices remained relatively stable
  • South seemed to weather crisis better than North
The Panic of 1857 was one of the first international financial crises. It demonstrated the growing integration of the American economy with global markets and exposed the vulnerabilities of industrial capitalism.

Sectionalism and the Panic

  • North suffered more than South from Panic
  • Southerners saw this as proof of slavery's superiority
  • "Cotton is King" rhetoric intensified
  • North demanded protective tariffs and internal improvements
  • South opposed tariffs as favoring Northern industry
  • Economic policy became increasingly sectional
  • Panic exposed deep structural differences between regions
The Panic of 1857 reinforced sectional stereotypes. Southerners saw it as vindication of their agricultural system, while Northerners blamed it on lack of protective tariffs. Economic crisis thus deepened political divisions.

Free-Labor Ideology

  • Central belief of Republican Party
  • Free labor superior to slave labor
  • Every man should own his own labor
  • Social mobility possible through hard work
  • Slavery threatened free labor system
  • Republicans championed "Free Soil, Free Labor, Free Men"
  • Appealed to Northern workers, farmers, and middle class
Free-labor ideology gave Republicans a powerful message that united diverse constituencies. It portrayed slavery not just as morally wrong but as a threat to Northern workers' opportunity for advancement.

The Impending Crisis (1857)

  • Book by Hinton Rowan Helper, a North Carolinian
  • Argued slavery hurt Southern economy
  • Compared North and South economically
  • Claimed slavery benefited only large planters
  • Hurt non-slaveholding whites economically
  • Banned in most Southern states
  • Republicans distributed it widely in North
Helper's book was revolutionary because it attacked slavery from a Southern, white perspective on economic grounds. It suggested that poor whites had more in common with Northern workers than with Southern planters - a dangerous idea for the planter class.

Southern Nonslaveholders

  • 75% of Southern whites owned no slaves
  • Most were small farmers on marginal land
  • Some were skilled craftsmen or laborers
  • Many aspired to own slaves someday
  • Supported slavery for racial and social reasons
  • Slavery elevated all whites above all blacks
  • Fear of slave rebellion unified whites across class lines
The loyalty of non-slaveholding whites to the slave system was crucial to Southern power. Though slavery didn't benefit them economically, it gave them social status and made them fearful of black freedom. Race thus trumped class interest.

End of Lecture 2

Lecture 3

The Lincoln-Douglas Debates and John Brown's Raid

50 minutes

14-6: The Lincoln-Douglas Debates

Background: Illinois Senate Race (1858)

  • Abraham Lincoln challenges Stephen Douglas for Senate
  • Douglas: nationally prominent Democrat, "Little Giant"
  • Lincoln: relatively unknown Republican lawyer
  • Race attracted national attention
  • Represented larger conflict between North and South
  • Both candidates agreed to series of public debates
The Lincoln-Douglas debates were the most consequential Senate race in American history. Though Lincoln lost the election, the debates made him a national figure and set the stage for his presidential run in 1860.

Lincoln's "House Divided" Speech

"A house divided against itself cannot stand. I believe this government cannot endure permanently half slave and half free. I do not expect the Union to be dissolved; I do not expect the house to fall; but I do expect it will cease to be divided. It will become all one thing, or all the other."
  • Delivered June 16, 1858, accepting Republican nomination
  • Argued current course unsustainable
  • Slavery would either expand everywhere or be placed on path to extinction
Lincoln's House Divided speech was both prophetic and politically risky. He argued that Americans faced a stark choice between two incompatible social systems. His conservative advisers thought the speech too radical, but it defined the terms of debate.

The Lincoln-Douglas Debates

  • Seven debates across Illinois (August-October 1858)
  • Each debate lasted three hours
  • Huge crowds attended (10,000-15,000 people)
  • Focused on slavery in territories and Dred Scott
  • Douglas defended popular sovereignty
  • Lincoln attacked slavery as morally wrong
  • Both opposed full racial equality (typical of era)
The debates attracted enormous crowds and newspaper coverage. This was democracy at its finest - serious, extended discussion of the most important issues facing the nation. The quality of political discourse has rarely been matched.

Key Issues in the Debates

  • Douglas: Popular sovereignty lets people decide
  • Lincoln: Slavery morally wrong, must be contained
  • Douglas: Slavery will die naturally if not protected
  • Lincoln: Dred Scott shows slavery expanding
  • Douglas: Republicans are radical abolitionists
  • Lincoln: Democrats part of "Slave Power conspiracy"
The debates clarified the fundamental disagreement between North and South. Douglas tried to find middle ground through popular sovereignty, but Lincoln argued moral questions couldn't be compromised. The debates framed the 1860 election.

The Freeport Doctrine

  • Freeport, Illinois debate (August 27, 1858)
  • Lincoln posed dilemma to Douglas:
  • Could territories exclude slavery despite Dred Scott decision?
  • Douglas's response: "Freeport Doctrine"
  • Slavery needs positive local regulations to exist
  • Territories could exclude slavery by refusing protective laws
  • This preserved popular sovereignty despite Dred Scott
Lincoln's Freeport question was brilliant political strategy. He forced Douglas to choose between Northern voters (who wanted to exclude slavery) and Southern Democrats (who demanded Dred Scott be respected). Douglas's answer saved his Senate seat but destroyed his presidential chances.

Impact of the Freeport Doctrine

  • Helped Douglas win re-election in Illinois
  • Satisfied Northern voters who opposed slavery extension
  • Outraged Southern Democrats
  • South saw it as betrayal of Dred Scott
  • Made Douglas unacceptable as presidential candidate to South
  • Contributed to Democratic split in 1860
  • Lincoln lost battle but won the war
The Freeport Doctrine was Douglas's poison pill. By trying to appeal to Northern voters, he alienated Southern Democrats who would later split the party and hand the presidency to Lincoln. Sometimes winning the battle means losing the war.

Results of the 1858 Election

  • Douglas won Senate seat (state legislature chose senators)
  • Republicans won popular vote in Illinois
  • Lincoln emerged as national Republican figure
  • Debates were published and widely read
  • Lincoln's arguments shaped Republican thinking
  • Set stage for Lincoln's 1860 presidential nomination
Though Lincoln lost the Senate race, he won something more valuable - national recognition. His performance in the debates convinced Republican leaders that he could carry crucial swing states in 1860. Sometimes losing can be the path to winning.

John Brown at Harpers Ferry

John Brown: Background

  • Born 1800 in Connecticut; raised in Ohio
  • Father was abolitionist and Oberlin College trustee
  • Failed at various businesses: tanner, farmer, wool merchant
  • Moved to North Elba, NY - community for free blacks
  • Supported by abolitionist Gerrit Smith
  • Became obsessed with taking direct action against slavery
  • 1855: Followed sons to Kansas Territory
Brown learned abolitionism from his father and never wavered from it. His friendship with a black boy during the War of 1812 convinced him that blacks were not inferior. Unlike most abolitionists, Brown was willing to use violence.

Pottawatomie Creek Massacre (1856)

  • May 21, 1856: Pro-slavery forces sack Lawrence, Kansas
  • Brown concluded he had "divine mission" for vengeance
  • May 24, 1856: Brown led nighttime raid
  • Five pro-slavery settlers dragged from cabins
  • Hacked to death with broadswords
  • Brown became "Old Osawatomie Brown"
  • Name conjured fear among slavery supporters
Pottawatomie was brutal terrorism. Brown murdered five men who had not personally harmed him, choosing them for their pro-slavery views. This was political violence designed to terrorize his enemies. It worked.

Significance of Pottawatomie

  • Initiated South's view of North as rabid abolitionists
  • Changed terms of slavery struggle
  • Abolitionists previously seen as cowardly
  • Now seen as dangerous, violent criminals
  • Aroused new emotion: FEAR
  • "The ruffians entertain a wholesome dread of Captain Brown"
  • Set stage for Brown's eventual "martyrdom"
Before Pottawatomie, abolitionists could be dismissed as impractical idealists. After it, they seemed dangerous and violent. This played into Southern fears of slave rebellion and convinced many that the North wanted to destroy them.

Planning the Raid

  • Spring 1858: Brown convened meeting in Chatham, Ontario
  • Announced plan for mountain stronghold for escaped slaves
  • Drafted "provisional constitution" for free territory
  • Elected "commander in chief" of paper government
  • Gained financial support from Boston abolitionists
  • "Secret Six" funded his operations
  • Summer 1859: Prepared for raid on federal arsenal
Brown's plan was audacious: seize a federal arsenal, arm slaves, and establish a free state in the mountains. His supporters knew he planned violence but most did not know the specific target. They believed in him enough to fund what amounted to an armed insurrection.

Raid on Harpers Ferry (October 16, 1859)

  • Brown rented farmhouse across from Harpers Ferry, Virginia
  • Federal arsenal contained thousands of weapons
  • Night of October 16: Brown with 16 whites, 5 blacks attacked
  • Quickly captured arsenal and armory
  • Took 60 leading citizens as hostages
  • Expected enslaved people to join rebellion
  • Hoped to spark "army of emancipation"
Brown's raid was tactically successful initially but strategically flawed. He captured the arsenal easily but had no escape plan. He seemed to expect slaves would spontaneously rise up - they didn't. He was trapped in the armory he had seized.

Defeat and Capture

  • Local militia surrounded Brown's forces
  • President Buchanan sent U.S. Marines
  • Colonel Robert E. Lee commanded Marines
  • October 18: Marines stormed engine house
  • Brown wounded, 10 followers killed (including 2 sons)
  • Raid lasted only 36 hours
  • Brown captured and imprisoned
The raid was a complete military failure. No slave rebellion materialized. Most of Brown's men were killed or captured. Yet this tactical defeat became a strategic victory for the antislavery cause. Brown would do more damage to slavery from the gallows than he did from the arsenal.

Trial and Execution

  • Tried in Virginia state court
  • Charges: murder, slave insurrection, treason against Virginia
  • Brown conducted himself with dignity
  • Gave eloquent defense of his actions
  • Convicted and sentenced to hang
  • December 2, 1859: Brown executed
  • Final note: predicted war and bloodshed to end slavery
Brown's trial made him a martyr. His dignity, eloquence, and moral certainty impressed even his enemies. His willingness to die for his beliefs gave him moral authority that his violent actions alone could not. He became larger than life.

The Martyrdom of John Brown

  • Moved boundary of acceptable thoughts about slavery
  • Captured national attention like no other abolitionist
  • Willingness to die had tremendous moral force
  • "John Brown's Body" became Union marching song
  • Inspired "Battle Hymn of the Republic"
  • Northern sympathy shocked and outraged South
  • Brown became symbol of antislavery cause
Before Harpers Ferry, abolitionism was not quite respectable even in the North. By taking armed action, Brown made verbal antislavery seem moderate. By dying for the cause, he became a saint. Ralph Waldo Emerson said Brown made the gallows "glorious like the cross."

Southern Reaction

  • Confirmed worst fears about Northern intentions
  • Saw Republicans as embodiment of Brown's views
  • Convinced many that Union was dangerous
  • Secession sentiment increased dramatically
  • Northern sympathy for Brown seemed proof of hostility
  • White Southerners felt security threatened
  • Made sectional compromise increasingly impossible
Harpers Ferry was the South's 9/11 - a traumatic attack that changed everything. The fact that many Northerners praised Brown convinced Southerners that the North wanted to destroy them. Any hope for sectional reconciliation died with John Brown.

Victor Hugo on John Brown

"The gaze of Europe is fixed at this moment on America... [Hanging Brown] will open a latent fissure that will finally split the Union asunder. The punishment of John Brown may consolidate slavery in Virginia, but it will certainly shatter the American Democracy. You preserve your shame but you kill your glory."

The famous French writer recognized Brown's execution would have revolutionary consequences.

Hugo understood what Americans could not yet see: Brown's death would make compromise impossible. Europe watched in horror as America executed a man for trying to free slaves. The moral high ground was shifting decisively against the South.

The Road to War

  • John Brown's raid: October 1859
  • Democratic Convention split: April 1860
  • Lincoln elected: November 1860
  • South Carolina secedes: December 1860
  • Confederate States formed: February 1861
  • Fort Sumter attacked: April 12, 1861
  • Civil War begins
The gathering tempest finally broke. All the tensions we've studied - Kansas, Dred Scott, Lincoln-Douglas, John Brown - culminated in disunion and war. The center could not hold. The house divided could not stand.

Conclusion: The Gathering Tempest

  • Kansas-Nebraska Act shattered sectional compromise
  • Massive immigration created new social tensions
  • Violence moved from territories to Congress to federal arsenals
  • Dred Scott decision eliminated legal basis for compromise
  • Economic differences reinforced sectional identities
  • Lincoln-Douglas debates defined incompatible visions of America
  • John Brown made slavery a moral crisis requiring immediate resolution

By 1860, the American political system had failed to contain the slavery controversy. War was no longer a distant possibility but an approaching certainty.
