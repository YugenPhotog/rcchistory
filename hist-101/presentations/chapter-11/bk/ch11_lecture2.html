Native Americans After the War of 1812

The Situation:

  • ~125,000 Native peoples live east of Mississippi River
  • "Five Civilized Tribes" in Southeast: ~60,000 people
  • Cherokee, Creek, Choctaw, Chickasaw, Seminole
  • Increasingly surrounded by white settlement

Federal Policy Claims:

  • "Civilize" and "Christianize" Native peoples
  • Then they can remain on their lands
  • Assimilation is the official goal

The Reality We'll See:

  • Many tribes DID adopt American practices
  • They were removed anyway
  • Key question: Was removal about "civilization" or about land and race?

Why Removal? Three Intertwined Reasons

1. Economic Greed: Land Hunger

  • Southeast has most fertile land in U.S.
  • Perfect for cotton cultivation (slavery expanding)
  • Gold discovered in Georgia (1828)
  • White settlers want this land NOW

2. Racial Ideology: "Civilization" Rhetoric as Cover

  • Official claim: "Indians can't civilize, so they must move"
  • Reality: Even when tribes adopted American culture, whites wanted them gone
  • Racial hierarchy: Indians seen as inferior regardless of behavior

3. Political Pressure

  • Georgia's 1802 Compact: U.S. promised to extinguish Indian title
  • Georgia threatens to defy federal government
  • Federal government caves to state pressure
The Pattern: Assimilation was never the real goal—land acquisition was

Competing Sovereignties: The Legal Battle

Tribes Assert Nationhood:

  • Cherokee Nation writes constitution (1827)
  • Declares Cherokee sovereignty and independence
  • "We are a nation, not subjects of Georgia"

U.S. Government's Position:

  • Tribes are "domestic dependent nations"
  • Subject to federal (not state) authority
  • But NOT fully sovereign

Georgia's Position:

  • Indians have NO sovereignty
  • State law applies to all people in Georgia
The Clash: Three competing claims to authority will be decided by Supreme Court (and then ignored)

Intra-Tribal Divisions: The McIntosh Case

Economic transformation creates winners and losers WITHIN tribes

Major shift in Creek society:

  • Away from communal sharing toward private property
  • Rise of planter-elite of mixed heritage
  • Christian missionaries catalyze changes

Chief William McIntosh (Creek):

  • Mixed Creek-Scottish heritage, wealthy planter
  • Believed removal was inevitable
  • Quote: Would be "left to wander the earth without homes and be beaten like dogs"
  • Better to negotiate best deal and move west

Creek National Council (1821):

  • Decreed that any Creek who sold tribal land would be put to death
  • Majority opposed removal

Treaty of Indian Springs (1825)

February 1825: McIntosh signs treaty at his inn

The Deal:

  • Ceded ALL Creek land in Georgia to United States
  • McIntosh and supporters receive $40,000 (bribe)
  • Opposed by majority of Creek people

The Response:

  • McIntosh branded a traitor
  • Executed by Creek warriors enforcing tribal law
  • From their perspective, justice was served
What This Reveals: Removal created INTERNAL violence, not just external • Many tribes split into factions • Pattern will repeat with Cherokee "Treaty Party"

Cherokee "Civilization" Strategy

Economic Transformation:

  • Most Cherokee abandon hunting, adopt settled agriculture
  • Own farms, plantations, enslaved people
  • Operate mills, stores, ferries, inns

Cultural Adaptation:

  • Sequoyah creates Cherokee syllabary (1821)
  • Cherokee literacy rate equals or exceeds white South
  • Cherokee Phoenix (1828): First Native American newspaper
  • Many convert to Christianity

Political Innovation:

  • Cherokee National Council passes written law codes
  • Establish police force and court system
  • Cherokee Constitution (1827): Mirrors U.S. Constitution

The Numbers (by 1835):

18 schools • 2 grist mills • 62 blacksmith shops • 8 cotton machines • 1,300 spinning wheels

By any measure, this was successful "civilization"

The Assimilation Paradox

What Cherokee "Civilization" Proved:

  • Tribes COULD adapt to American culture
  • They COULD become "civilized" by white standards
  • They COULD succeed economically

Why It Didn't Matter:

The More Successful the Cherokee Became, the More Whites Wanted Their Land

  • Successful farms = valuable land
  • Cherokee Nation's property value: $15-20 million (1835)
  • This made land MORE desirable to whites, not less

The Lesson:

  • Removal was NEVER about "civilization"
  • It was ALWAYS about land and racial hierarchy
  • No amount of adaptation would save them

By almost any standard, Cherokees were "civilized." They were hardly "uncivilized savages."

Yet they were removed anyway.

This is why we call it ethnic cleansing, not "civilization policy"

Georgia Forces the Crisis

Background: Georgia's 1802 Compact

  • U.S. promised to extinguish Indian title "as soon as it could be done peaceably"
  • Georgia has been waiting 27 years

The Catalyst: Gold Discovered (1828)

  • Gold found at Dahlonega, Georgia (Cherokee territory)
  • Gold rush begins
  • Whites flood onto Cherokee land illegally

Georgia's "Extension Laws" (1829-1831):

  1. Cherokee laws declared null and void in Georgia
  2. Cherokees cannot testify in court against whites
  3. Cherokees cannot mine gold on their own land
  4. White missionaries need state license to work in Cherokee territory
Goal: Make Cherokee life impossible, force them to leave

Cherokee Legal Resistance: Two Supreme Court Cases

Cherokee Nation v. Georgia (1831):

  • Question: Is Cherokee Nation a "foreign nation" that can sue Georgia?
  • Marshall's decision: NO—Cherokees are a "domestic dependent nation"
  • Not a foreign nation, but not Georgia citizens either
  • Case dismissed on technicality

Worcester v. Georgia (1832):

  • Question: Can Georgia enforce laws in Cherokee territory?
  • Marshall's decision: NO—Cherokee Nation is sovereign
  • Georgia laws in Cherokee territory are unconstitutional
  • Only federal government has authority over tribes

This Should Have Stopped Removal... But:

  • Georgia refuses to comply with ruling
  • Jackson refuses to enforce the decision
  • Apocryphally: "John Marshall has made his decision; now let him enforce it"

Legal victory becomes meaningless without enforcement

Jackson's Indian Policy

Jackson's Motivations:

  • Indians are barrier to American settlement of valuable farmland
  • Doubted Indians would ever join American society
  • Paternalistic racism: Thought Indians degraded by contact with whites
  • Believed removal would "save" Indians from extinction
  • Opening Indian land would strengthen Democratic Party
The Paternalism Makes It Worse:
Jackson genuinely believed removal was humanitarian—"saving" them from destruction.
This made policy more insidious—justified by claimed benevolence

Indian Removal Act (1830)

The Law:

  • Stipulated exchange of Indian territory in East for lands west of Mississippi
  • United States would "forever secure and guaranty" new lands in West
  • Government would pay for lost homes and removal
  • Government would protect Indians from all threats

The Reality:

  • "Forever" lasted less than 30 years
  • By 1850s-1880s, pressure to open "Indian Territory"
  • Promises were lies

The Vote:

  • Passed by only 5 votes in House
  • Strong opposition, especially from northern representatives
  • Shows removal was controversial even at the time

The Fraudulent Treaty of New Echota (1835)

Who Signed:

  • "Treaty Party" led by Major Ridge, John Ridge, Elias Boudinot
  • Represents perhaps 500 of 17,000 Cherokees (3%)
  • Principal Chief John Ross and elected National Council adamantly opposed

The Deal:

  • Cherokee cede all land east of Mississippi
  • Receive $5 million and land in "Indian Territory" (Oklahoma)
  • Government promises land will be theirs "forever"

Cherokee Opposition:

  • 15,000 Cherokees (88%) sign petition to Senate
  • Denounce treaty as fraudulent
  • Argue Treaty Party had no authority to sell tribal land

Senate Ratification:

  • May 1836: Senate ratifies by a single vote
  • 1839: Major Ridge, John Ridge, and Elias Boudinot assassinated after removal

Defining Ethnic Cleansing

What Is Ethnic Cleansing?

Definition: The systematic forced removal of an ethnic or religious group from a territory to create an ethnically homogeneous region

Key Elements:

  1. Forced displacement of entire population (not voluntary)
  2. Goal: Create ethnically "pure" territory
  3. Systematic policy (not random or spontaneous)
  4. Violence or coercion to compel removal
  5. Destruction of communities and way of life

Historical Examples:

  • Balkan Wars (1990s)—where term was coined
  • Armenian forced marches (1915)
  • Numerous 19th-century campaigns globally
Key Distinction:
Genocide: Intent to destroy a group entirely (kill them all)
Ethnic cleansing: Intent to remove a group from territory (force them out)

Building the Case: Systematic Policy and Forced Displacement

Let's Apply the Framework Systematically:

1. Was it Forced Displacement?

✓ YES

  • 60,000-100,000 people forcibly removed from Southeast
  • Military force used when tribes resisted
  • Those who tried to stay were hunted down and imprisoned
  • Not voluntary "migration"—coerced removal

2. Was the Goal Ethnic Homogeneity?

✓ YES

  • Explicit goal: Clear ALL Indians from East of Mississippi
  • Create "white" states east, "Indian Territory" west
  • Georgia's Extension Laws: No Indians can remain

3. Was it Systematic Policy?

✓ YES

  • Indian Removal Act (1830) = federal legislative authorization
  • 70+ removal treaties signed 1830-1842
  • Coordinated between federal government, states, military
  • General Winfield Scott commanded 7,000 troops for Cherokee removal

Building the Case: Violence and Community Destruction

4. Was Violence/Coercion Used?

✓ YES

  • Georgia militia harassment and intimidation
  • Stockade camps: Families imprisoned before removal
  • Forced marches with inadequate food, shelter, medicine
  • Those who resisted were hunted, killed, or imprisoned
  • Winter marches deliberately scheduled (Choctaw)

5. Was There Community Destruction?

✓ YES

  • Homes burned, farms confiscated
  • Cherokee capital (New Echota) destroyed
  • Burial grounds desecrated, sacred sites lost
  • Churches, schools, businesses abandoned
  • Property worth $15-20 million confiscated (Cherokee alone)

All Five Elements Present

→ This Meets the Definition of Ethnic Cleansing

The Human Cost: Mortality Statistics

The Numbers Tell the Story:

Tribe Number Removed Deaths Mortality Rate
Cherokee 17,000 4,000-8,000 23-47%
Creek 15,000 3,500 23%
Choctaw 15,000-20,000 2,500-6,000 17-30%
Chickasaw 6,000 ~600 ~10%
Seminole 3,000 Unknown Many died resisting

Total: ~60,000 removed; ~15,000-25,000 died (20-25% overall mortality)

For Comparison:

  • Transatlantic Middle Passage mortality: ~15%
  • Cherokee removal mortality was HIGHER than the slave trade

Causes of Death:

  • Exposure, disease, starvation during forced marches
  • Inadequate provisions provided by government
  • Deliberate scheduling in winter months
  • Overcrowded, unsanitary stockade camps

Why "Ethnic Cleansing" Rather Than "Removal"?

The Problem with "Removal":

  • Sounds voluntary or bureaucratic
  • Sanitizes the violence
  • Hides the intent
  • Makes it seem like policy, not atrocity

Why "Ethnic Cleansing" Is Accurate:

  • ✓ Describes racial ideology
  • ✓ Describes territorial goal
  • ✓ Describes systematic violence
  • ✓ Places U.S. in global context

1. Describes the Racial Ideology:

  • Indians seen as racially incompatible with white civilization
  • No amount of "civilization" would change white attitudes
  • Goal was racial purity of eastern territory

2. Describes the Territorial Goal:

  • Create homogeneous white settler state east of Mississippi
  • Indians must be completely removed from region
  • Not about behavior or culture—about race and land

3. Describes the Systematic Violence:

  • Not random, accidental, or unintended
  • Coordinated state policy with military enforcement
  • Resulted in mass death (20-25% mortality)

Historical Memory and Terminology

Why Does Terminology Matter?

Traditional Language (Sanitizing):

  • "Indian Removal"
  • "Relocation"
  • "Migration"
  • "Resettlement"

These Terms:

  • Normalize state violence
  • Obscure human suffering
  • Protect nationalist mythologies

Scholar's Language (Accurate):

  • "Ethnic cleansing"
  • "Forced displacement"
  • "Systematic violence"
  • "Mass death march"

These Terms:

  • Acknowledge violence accurately
  • Honor Indigenous memory
  • Place U.S. in global context
  • Recognize it as atrocity
