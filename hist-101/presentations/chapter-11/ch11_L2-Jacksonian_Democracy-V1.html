What Was Jacksonian Democracy?

The Era: ~1828‚Äì1850

Key Political Changes:

  • Elimination of property requirements for voting (white men only)
  • Rise of mass political parties (Democrats vs. Whigs)
  • Popular political participation: parades, rallies, campaigns

The Jacksonian Ideal:

  • "Common man" can succeed through hard work
  • Hostility to privilege and "aristocracy"
  • Suspicion of elites, banks, and federal power

The Central Contradiction:

This "democracy" was built on:

  • Exclusion of women (no voting rights)
  • Exclusion of free Blacks (even in North)
  • Violent removal of Native Americans
  • Expansion of slavery in South

Democracy for some required domination of others

Main idea: Mass parties, expanded suffrage for white men, and anti-elitist rhetoric define the political style‚Äîwhile skepticism of banks and federal power shapes policy fights.

‚ö° Content Breakdown

  • Suffrage shift: Property bars drop for white men; participation rises.
  • Party politics: Democrats vs. Whigs organize voters and spectacle.
  • Jacksonian ideal: Suspicion of privilege; celebration of the ‚Äúcommon man.‚Äù

üèõÔ∏è Big Picture Synthesis

Political inclusion for one group becomes a cultural identity. This means policy debates (banks, internal improvements) double as contests over who counts as the people.

The Market Revolution Transforms Daily Life

New Transportation & Communication:

  • Erie Canal (1825), railroads, telegraph connect distant markets
  • Result: Local economies become part of national system

From Self-Sufficiency to Wage Labor:

  • Before: Families produce what they consume (farm, artisan shop)
  • After: Work for wages, buy goods in market
  • Effect: More consumer goods BUT less economic independence

"Time Discipline" Replaces Natural Rhythms:

  • Factory system: Work by the clock, not by the task
  • Before: Farmer works by daylight and weather
  • After: Factory worker punches clock, fixed hours
  • New anxiety: "Time is money"
The Paradox: More opportunity AND more insecurity

üìñ Understanding This Slide

Main idea: Canals, rail, and telegraph knit markets together; families pivot from self-provisioning to wage labor; the clock, not the sun, sets the pace.

‚ö° Content Breakdown

  • Networks: Erie Canal, early railroads, telegraph integrate regions.
  • Labor shift: From household production to wages and purchases.
  • Time discipline: Factory schedules replace seasonal rhythms.

üèõÔ∏è Big Picture Synthesis

Opportunity grows‚Äîso does precarity. This means the same forces that widen choice also tighten dependence on volatile markets.

The "Self-Made Man" Ideal

New Definition of Success:

  • Old idea (aristocratic): Success = inherited wealth, family name
  • New idea (democratic): Success = hard work, ambition, talent
  • The ideal: Poor boy becomes rich through effort

Why This Matters:

  • Opens doors for white men without family connections
  • Celebrates mobility and opportunity
  • Creates intense competition

The Dark Side:

  • If success = personal effort, then failure = personal failing
  • Poverty seen as individual moral failure, not structural problem
  • Blames the victim for economic hardship

Reality Check: Most people did NOT become rich, but the myth was powerful

üìñ Understanding This Slide

Main idea: The self‚Äëmade man celebrates mobility by merit‚Äîwhile turning poverty into an individual failing rather than a structural risk.

‚ö° Content Breakdown

  • Upside: Doors open for white men without pedigree.
  • Downside: Hardship coded as personal fault; myth outruns reality.

üèõÔ∏è Big Picture Synthesis
