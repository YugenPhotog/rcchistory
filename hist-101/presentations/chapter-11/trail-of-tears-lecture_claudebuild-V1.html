What is Ethnic Cleansing?

Term emerged in 1990s during Yugoslav wars

UN Definition:

"Rendering an area ethnically homogeneous by using force or intimidation to remove persons from the area"

Define carefully: "Ethnic cleansing is a relatively new term, but the practice is ancient. The term itself emerged in the 1990s during the wars in the former Yugoslavia, particularly Bosnia."

Read the UN definition slowly. Let it sink in.

Emphasize: "Notice the key elements: ethnic/racial targeting, use of force, goal is removal from territory. Keep these in mind as we go through today's lecture."

Key Characteristics of Ethnic Cleansing

  • Forced displacement of population
  • State or military power enforcing removal
  • Targeting based on identity (ethnic, racial, religious)
  • Goal: demographic transformation of territory
  • Often wrapped in legal frameworks

Go through each characteristic:

  • Forced displacement - people don't choose to leave, they are made to leave
  • State/military power - individuals can't do ethnic cleansing; requires organized force
  • Identity-based - targeting isn't random, it's because of who people are
  • Demographic goal - the point is to change who lives in a territory
  • Legal frameworks - often disguised as legal policy, not criminal violence

Ethnic Cleansing: A Global Pattern

Before we examine the Trail of Tears,
let's see this pattern in global context

Segment 1: Global Context (6 minutes)

Purpose: Establish that ethnic cleansing is a recurring historical pattern across time and geography, not unique to any one place. This helps students see the Trail of Tears as part of a larger phenomenon.

Approach: Move quickly through examples - 1-2 minutes each. The goal is breadth, not depth. Students should see the pattern repeated.

Highland Clearances, Scotland (1750s-1860s)

  • Landlords forcibly removed tenant farmers
  • Goal: make way for sheep grazing (more profitable)
  • Thousands displaced → emigration or industrial cities
  • Economic justifications masking ethnic/cultural dimensions
[Image: "Highland Clearances Scotland eviction"]

Highland Clearances: "In Scotland, from the 1750s through the 1860s, wealthy landowners systematically cleared tenant farmers from their lands. Families that had lived on the same land for generations were forcibly evicted, their homes often burned. The landlords wanted to use the land for sheep farming, which was more profitable than collecting rent from tenants."

Key point: "Notice how economic motivations are used to justify displacement. The landlords had legal property rights, but they used those rights to ethnically cleanse Highland Scots from their ancestral lands. This shows how ethnic cleansing can be wrapped in legal and economic language."

Partition of India/Pakistan (1947)

  • 10-20 million people displaced
  • Religious identity used to determine who "belonged" where
  • Muslims to Pakistan, Hindus/Sikhs to India
  • Massive violence accompanied displacement
  • Up to 2 million killed in communal violence
[Image: "1947 India Pakistan partition refugees"]

Partition: "When British India gained independence in 1947, it was divided into two nations: India and Pakistan. The partition was supposed to separate Muslim-majority areas (Pakistan) from Hindu-majority areas (India). But millions of people suddenly found themselves on the 'wrong' side of new borders."

The result: "10-20 million people were displaced in one of the largest forced migrations in history. Hindu and Sikh families fled from what became Pakistan; Muslim families fled from India. Massive communal violence erupted - perhaps 2 million killed. Trains arrived at stations filled with corpses."

Key point: "Notice how identity - in this case religion - was used to determine who could stay and who had to leave. And notice the scale: this happened in the 20th century, under international observation."

Bosnia (1992-1995)

  • Term "ethnic cleansing" emerged here
  • Serb forces expelled Bosniak Muslims and Croats
  • Included massacres, rape camps, forced deportation
  • Srebrenica massacre (1995): 8,000+ killed
  • 2+ million displaced from homes
[Image: "Bosnia 1990s ethnic cleansing refugees"]

Bosnia: "In the 1990s, as Yugoslavia broke apart, Bosnia-Herzegovina became a site of horrific ethnic cleansing. Serbian forces, along with paramilitary groups, systematically expelled Bosniak Muslims and Croats from territories they wanted for a 'Greater Serbia.'"

Methods: "Villages were surrounded, populations expelled at gunpoint. Those who resisted were killed. Systematic rape was used as a weapon. At Srebrenica in 1995, Serbian forces massacred over 8,000 Bosniak men and boys in a single week - the worst atrocity in Europe since World War II."

Key point: "It was during these wars that journalists started using the term 'ethnic cleansing' because 'war' didn't capture what was happening - this was deliberate attempt to remove ethnic groups from territory. The term stuck, and now we use it to understand similar historical patterns."

Note: "Bosnia shows how ethnic cleansing can escalate to genocide. Srebrenica was genocide - the intent was to destroy the group. But the broader campaign was ethnic cleansing - the intent was to remove populations from territory."

The Pattern

Ethnic cleansing happens when:

  • Powerful groups want land or resources
  • Use identity (ethnicity, race, religion) to justify displacement
  • Have state/military power to enforce it

Ethnic Cleansing vs. Genocide

Understanding the Distinction

Segment 2: Ethnic Cleansing vs. Genocide (10 minutes)

Purpose: Students need to understand the relationship between these concepts. They are related but distinct, and the distinction matters both analytically and legally. This section also introduces the Armenian Genocide - important history most students have never heard.

Approach: Define genocide clearly, present Armenian case as example, then create comparison framework, finally apply to Trail of Tears through discussion.

Defining Genocide

Term created by Raphael Lemkin (1944)

  • Polish-Jewish lawyer
  • Lost 49 family members in Holocaust
  • Combined Greek genos (race/tribe) + Latin -cide (killing)
  • Coined to describe what Nazis were doing

Raphael Lemkin: "The term 'genocide' was created by a Polish-Jewish lawyer named Raphael Lemkin in 1944. Lemkin had fled Poland when the Nazis invaded. He lost 49 family members in the Holocaust - parents, siblings, extended family. He watched from abroad as the Nazis systematically murdered Europe's Jews."

Why new term needed: "Lemkin realized there was no word in international law for what the Nazis were doing. Murder was a crime, but this was something different - an organized attempt to destroy an entire people. He combined Greek and Latin roots to create 'genocide' - the killing of a race or tribe."

Impact: "His work led directly to the UN Genocide Convention in 1948. Genocide became recognized as the 'crime of crimes' under international law."

Historians Weigh In: Lemkin's definition actually included the destruction of Native American peoples, though this is often overlooked. In his unpublished writings, he explicitly analyzed U.S. Indian policies as genocide.

UN Genocide Convention (1948)

Acts committed with intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group

Including:

  • Killing members of the group
  • Causing serious bodily or mental harm
  • Deliberately inflicting conditions to destroy the group
  • Preventing births
  • Forcibly transferring children

Key word: DESTROY. Read the definition slowly. Emphasize "intent to destroy."

Important point: "Notice that genocide isn't just killing. It includes other acts IF the intent is to destroy the group. This will become important when we think about the Trail of Tears."

Note the third point: "'Deliberately inflicting conditions of life calculated to bring about physical destruction' - keep this in mind. Does creating conditions that will kill people count as genocide if destruction is the intent?"

The Relationship

Ethnic Cleansing

Goal is territorial

"Get the group OUT"

Genocide

Goal is exterminationist

"Eliminate the group ENTIRELY"

They can overlap: ethnic cleansing can escalate to genocide
Intent is the crucial distinction

Explain the relationship: "Think of these as overlapping circles. They're related but not identical."

Ethnic cleansing: "The goal is to get a group off particular territory. 'We want you gone from here.'"

Genocide: "The goal is to destroy a group entirely. 'We want you to cease to exist.'"

The overlap: "They can overlap. Ethnic cleansing can escalate to genocide if perpetrators decide killing is more efficient than displacement. And genocide often involves displacement as one method. In practice, they can be hard to distinguish."

Intent matters: "Legally and analytically, intent is crucial. What was the goal? Removal or destruction? This isn't always clear from actions alone."

Case Study: The Armenian Genocide

1915-1917

Why this case: "Let's look at a clear example of genocide to understand the distinction. Most of you have probably never heard of the Armenian Genocide. It's the first major genocide of the 20th century - it happened before the word 'genocide' even existed."

Context: "This is important history in its own right, but it also helps us think analytically about ethnic cleansing vs. genocide."

Background

  • Ottoman Empire (modern-day Turkey)
  • During World War I
  • Armenians: Christian minority, ~2 million in empire
  • Long history of discrimination and periodic massacres
  • Ottoman leaders feared Armenians would side with Russia (Christian enemy)
[Image: "Ottoman Empire map Armenian population 1915"]

Context: "The Ottoman Empire was the Turkish empire that had existed for centuries. By World War I, it was collapsing - losing territories, losing power. During the war, Ottoman leaders grew paranoid about internal enemies."

Armenians: "Armenians were a Christian minority in a Muslim empire. They had faced discrimination and periodic violence for decades. There had been massacres of Armenians in the 1890s. But what happened starting in 1915 was different in scale and intent."

Accusation: "Ottoman leaders accused Armenians of being potential traitors - they feared Armenians would ally with Christian Russia against the Muslim Ottoman Empire in WWI. This accusation became justification for genocide."

What Happened (1915-1917)

Systematic campaign ordered from top of government:
Killing squads murdered Armenian men and boys
Forced marches of women, children, elderly into Syrian desert
Deliberately given no food, water, shelter
Guards killed stragglers
Rape, torture, death from exposure
Concentration camps in the desert

The systematic nature: "This was organized from the top. The Ottoman government issued orders. Local officials carried them out. This wasn't random violence - it was policy."

The methods: "First, Armenian men and boys were rounded up and killed outright - shot, bayoneted, thrown off cliffs. Then women, children, and elderly were forced on death marches into the Syrian desert. These marches were designed to kill - no food, no water, guards killed anyone who fell behind."

Sexual violence: "Rape was systematic. Young women and girls were taken as slaves. This was part of destroying the Armenian community."

The marches: "These weren't marches to relocate people - they were marches to kill them through exposure, starvation, and exhaustion. Photographs from the time show skeletal children, corpses lining roads."

The Numbers

1-1.5 million Armenians killed

Out of approximately 2 million

Majority of the population destroyed

[Image: "Armenian Genocide memorial Yerevan"]

The scale: "1 to 1.5 million Armenians killed. That's the majority of the Armenian population in the Ottoman Empire. Nearly every Armenian family lost members. Entire villages were wiped out."

Historical memory: "Turkey still officially denies this was genocide. Recognition remains politically contentious. But the historical evidence is overwhelming. This was genocide - systematic attempt to destroy the Armenian people."

Why it matters today: "The Armenian Genocide is the first major genocide of the 20th century. Hitler reportedly said, when planning the Holocaust, 'Who, after all, speaks today of the annihilation of the Armenians?' Failure to acknowledge genocide enables future genocides."

Historians Weigh In: Samantha Power in "A Problem from Hell": America and the Age of Genocide documents how the U.S. government knew about the Armenian Genocide as it happened but did nothing, establishing a pattern of non-intervention that would repeat with future genocides.

Why This Is Genocide, Not Ethnic Cleansing

  • Intent to destroy: Documents show Ottoman leaders planning Armenian extermination
  • Systematic killing: Not just displacement - organized mass murder
  • Scale of death: Majority of population killed, not relocated
  • Methods: Deportations were method of killing (death marches), not the goal itself

The distinction: "Why do we call this genocide rather than ethnic cleansing? All four factors:"

Intent: "We have documents - telegrams, orders - showing Ottoman leaders intended to destroy the Armenian population. One leader telegraphed: 'The goal of these actions is the annihilation of Armenians.'"

Systematic killing: "This wasn't displacement with some deaths. It was organized killing. Killing squads, death marches designed to kill, concentration camps where people were starved to death."

Scale: "The majority of Armenians in the Ottoman Empire died. If the goal was just to move them elsewhere, this wouldn't have happened."

Methods: "The deportations themselves were a killing method. When you march people into a desert without food or water in the summer heat, and guards kill anyone who tries to find water, that's not relocation - that's extermination."

Critical Comparison

Ethnic Cleansing Genocide
Primary goal: REMOVE group from territory Primary goal: DESTROY group entirely
Deaths may occur, but not the objective Deaths are the objective
"We want you GONE" "We want you DEAD"
Example: Bosnia (mostly), Partition of India Example: Armenian Genocide, Holocaust, Rwanda

Review the comparison: Go through each row. Make sure students understand the distinction.

Emphasize: "Both are horrific crimes. We're not saying one is worse than the other. But understanding the difference helps us analyze what happened, understand perpetrators' goals, and recognize patterns."

Transition: "Now here's the big question: Where does the Trail of Tears fit? Let's discuss."

The Scholarly Debate

Ethnic Cleansing

  • Primary goal was territorial
  • U.S. offered "voluntary" relocation (coerced)
  • Cherokee not systematically hunted and killed
  • Allowed to rebuild in Oklahoma
  • Pattern repeated - removal was consistent policy

Genocide (or genocidal)

  • 25% death rate shows reckless disregard
  • Conditions deliberately inadequate
  • Destruction of Cherokee nation/culture
  • Continued assimilation policies (boarding schools)
  • UN definition: "conditions to destroy"

Present both sides fairly: This is a genuine scholarly debate. Historians disagree.

Ethnic cleansing argument: "Those who call it ethnic cleansing emphasize that the U.S. government's primary goal was getting Cherokee off land white settlers wanted. If the goal had been to destroy Cherokee as a people, they would have been hunted down and killed, not marched to new territory. The government officially offered relocation, even if coerced. And this pattern repeated with other tribes - removal was the policy, not extermination."

Genocide argument: "Those who call it genocide (or genocidal) point to the death rate - 25% or higher. The government knew conditions were deadly and proceeded anyway. They argue this fits the UN definition of 'deliberately inflicting conditions calculated to bring about physical destruction.' They also point to continued policies aimed at destroying Cherokee culture - boarding schools that forbade Cherokee language, forced assimilation."

Historians Weigh In:

  • Claudio Saunt (Unworthy Republic): Uses "ethnic cleansing" framework, arguing it's the most precise term for removal policy focused on territorial control
  • Jeffrey Ostler (Surviving Genocide): Argues for genocide framework, showing U.S. policies toward Native peoples fit UN definition, particularly "deliberately inflicting conditions"
  • Benjamin Madley (An American Genocide): While focused on California, provides comparative context showing some U.S. actions clearly genocidal; Trail of Tears sits in ambiguous space

Most Historians Today

Ethnic cleansing is the more precise term for the removal itself

But it occurred within broader context of settler colonialism that had genocidal effects

The line between the two can be blurry

Some Cherokee and Native scholars argue it should be called genocide

The consensus view: "Most historians today use the term 'ethnic cleansing' for the Trail of Tears specifically, while acknowledging it's part of a broader pattern that includes genocidal elements."

Context matters: "It's worth noting that U.S. policies toward Native peoples varied. California Gold Rush involved systematic hunting and killing of Native people - that looks more clearly genocidal. The Trail of Tears was ethnic cleansing with devastating death rates."

Native perspectives: "Many Cherokee and other Native scholars argue we should call it genocide. They emphasize that the UN definition includes 'deliberately inflicting conditions' and point to the continued attempts to destroy Native cultures even after removal."

Why precision matters: "We're not splitting hairs here. Whether we call it genocide or ethnic cleansing matters legally (genocide is the 'crime of crimes'), politically (recognition and reparations), and analytically (understanding what happened)."

The key point: "But here's what's NOT debatable: The Trail of Tears was a catastrophic crime that killed thousands and destroyed a nation. It was a deliberate policy choice. Whether we call it genocide or ethnic cleansing, we're talking about one of the darkest chapters in American history."

Why the Distinction Matters

  • Legally: Genocide is "crime of crimes" under international law
  • Analytically: Helps us understand perpetrators' goals and methods
  • Morally: Both are horrific, but we should be precise
  • Politically: Recognition and accountability differ
Our job as historians: be precise about what happened and why

Wrap up this section: "So why does any of this matter? Why spend time on these definitions?"

Legally: "Genocide is recognized as the worst crime under international law. Countries can be held accountable. It carries different weight."

Analytically: "Understanding whether the goal was removal or destruction helps us understand what happened and why. It helps us see patterns and prevent future atrocities."

Morally: "Both are horrific. We're not saying one is worse. But precision matters in understanding what happened."

Transition: "For today's lecture, we're going to use the term 'ethnic cleansing' for the Trail of Tears. But keep in mind the debate we've just had. Now let's turn to the Cherokee Nation before removal."

Historians Weigh In: Patrick Wolfe's concept of "settler colonialism" as an ongoing structure (not just an event) helps us understand that the debate over genocide vs. ethnic cleansing may miss the larger point - settler colonial societies are fundamentally organized around "elimination of the native," whether through killing, removal, or assimilation.

Three Key Points from the Documentary

1. Cherokee "civilization" didn't matter

Syllabary, constitution, Cherokee Phoenix, farms, schools - none of it protected them

2. Cherokee used every legal avenue

Worcester v. Georgia: Supreme Court ruled in their favor. Jackson refused to enforce it.

Quick recap: "You've watched 'We Shall Remain' and seen the Cherokee Nation before removal. Let me highlight three crucial points:"

Point 1: "Cherokee did everything white Americans said they should do - created a written language, adopted a constitution modeled on the U.S. Constitution, published a bilingual newspaper, built farms and schools. Their literacy rate was higher than surrounding white communities. But it didn't matter. White Georgians wanted the land, especially after gold was discovered in 1829."

Point 2: "Cherokee hired lawyers. They petitioned Congress. They won at the Supreme Court - Worcester v. Georgia ruled that Georgia's laws were unconstitutional, that the Cherokee Nation was sovereign. But President Jackson refused to enforce the decision. Law meant nothing when power wanted something."

Point 3: "The Cherokee were not united. The majority, led by Principal Chief John Ross, resisted removal. But the Treaty Party - the Ridge family and Elias Boudineau - believed removal was inevitable. They thought negotiating the best terms possible would save lives. In 1835, about 100 Cherokee (out of 16,000) signed the Treaty of New Echota. The Senate ratified it by one vote. The vast majority of Cherokee rejected it, but it didn't matter."

Historians Weigh In: Theda Perdue's work emphasizes the sophistication of Cherokee political strategy and the tragedy that no strategy - neither Ross's resistance nor the Treaty Party's pragmatic negotiation - could overcome the combination of white land hunger, racist ideology, and federal power.

Where We Are in 1838

Cherokee have done everything "right"

Used law, diplomacy, adaptation, resistance

None of it worked

Gold discovered. Cotton economy expanding.
White settlers wanted the land.
Federal government and Georgia had the power to take it.

Set up the transition: "So here's where we are in 1838. The Cherokee have done everything 'right' according to white American standards. They've used law - won at Supreme Court. They've tried diplomacy - sent delegations to Washington. They've adapted culturally. They've resisted through legal channels. None of it worked."

Why it failed: "Why? Because gold was discovered on Cherokee land. Because the cotton economy was expanding and planters wanted more land. Because white Georgians wanted the territory. And because the federal government and the state of Georgia had the power to take it, and they were willing to use that power."

Part 1: The Roundup

May-June 1838

Segment 4: The Removal - Deep Dive (20 minutes)

Part A: The Roundup (7 minutes)

Purpose: Students need to understand the violence of the initial removal - soldiers at doors, families torn from homes, looting, the stockades as concentration camps.

The Orders

President Van Buren authorized military removal.
General Winfield Scott commanded 7,000 U.S. Army troops

[Image: "General Winfield Scott portrait 1838"]

Context: "In 1838, President Martin Van Buren - Jackson's successor - authorized the military removal of the Cherokee. He assigned the task to General Winfield Scott, one of the most senior officers in the U.S. Army."

Scott's background: "Scott was a career military officer. He'd fought in the War of 1812. He would later become commanding general of the entire U.S. Army. He was not a brutal man by the standards of his time - in fact, he tried to conduct the removal with some humanity. But he followed orders."

"The Cherokee are to be removed... The full moon of May is already on the wane; and before another shall have passed away, every Cherokee man, woman and child... must be in motion to join their brethren in the far West... Will you, then, by resistance, compel us to resort to arms?... I am an old warrior, and have been present at many a scene of slaughter; but spare me, I beseech you, the horror of witnessing the destruction of the Cherokees."
— General Winfield Scott, May 1838
[Image: "1838 proclamation document General Scott"]

Read the order slowly. Let students hear Scott's words.

Ask students: "What's Scott's tone here? Is this a threat or a warning? What does it tell us about how the military viewed this operation?"

Expected responses: It's both a threat and warning. He's saying comply or face violence. But he also seems to dread what's about to happen.

Your analysis: "Notice Scott's language. He's telling Cherokee they have no choice - resistance is 'hopeless.' But he also asks them to 'spare' him from witnessing their destruction. He knows what's coming. He knows this will be catastrophic. And he's going to do it anyway. That's how ethnic cleansing works - even people who have qualms carry out orders."

May 26, 1838: The Roundup Begins

The process - this is what ethnic cleansing looks like:

  • Soldiers surrounded Cherokee homes, farms, communities
  • No advance warning to most families
  • Bayonets drawn, rifles ready
  • Happened in spring - planting season, families at work
  • Families given minutes to gather belongings

Paint the picture: "May 26, 1838. Imagine you're a Cherokee family. It's spring - planting season. You're working in your fields, or in your house. Your children are playing. And suddenly you see soldiers coming."

No warning: "Most families had no warning. They'd heard rumors of removal, yes. But they didn't know it would be today, this moment. Soldiers simply appeared - hundreds of them, spread across Cherokee territory, conducting a systematic sweep."

Armed force: "The soldiers had bayonets fixed, rifles ready. This was military operation. Any resistance would be met with violence. Cherokee knew this."

"When the soldiers came to our house my father wanted to fight, but my mother told him that the soldiers would kill him if he did and we surrendered without a fight."
— Rebecca Neugin, Cherokee survivor
Testimony given in 1932
[Image: "Cherokee family home 1830s Georgia"]

Read the quote slowly. Let it land.

Pause, then explain: "Think about that moment. Rebecca was three years old. She watched her father - probably a proud man, a farmer, head of household, someone who had always protected his family - have to submit to armed soldiers in his own home. Her mother had to convince him not to fight because any resistance meant death."

The choice: "What choice did he have? Fight and be killed in front of his children? Or submit and hope to keep his family alive? That's not a choice - that's coercion at gunpoint."

Multiply this: "This scene happened thousands of times. In every Cherokee home. Soldiers with guns, families forced to leave. That's what ethnic cleansing looks like on the ground."

"I saw the helpless Cherokees arrested and dragged from their homes, and driven at the bayonet point into the stockades... On the morning of November the 17th we encountered a terrific sleet and snow storm with freezing temperatures and from that day until we reached the end of the fateful journey on March the 26th, 1839, the sufferings of the Cherokees were awful. The trail of the exiles was a trail of death."
— Private John G. Burnett, U.S. Army soldier
Witness to the removal
[Image: "U.S. Army soldiers 1838 uniform"]

This is crucial testimony: "This is Private John Burnett. He was a U.S. Army soldier - one of the men who carried out the removal. He wrote this decades later, as an old man, describing what he witnessed."

Immediate Aftermath:

The survivors: About 13,000 Cherokee survivors arrived in Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma) between late 1838 and 1839. They were exhausted, traumatized, and had lost approximately one quarter of their population.

Old Settlers: They joined about 2,000 "Old Settlers" - Cherokee who had voluntarily moved west before removal became mandatory. This created some tension - the Old Settlers had established themselves, and now thousands of traumatized refugees were arriving.

North Carolina Cherokee: About 1,000 Cherokee managed to hide in the mountains of North Carolina and avoid removal entirely. Their descendants became the Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians, still there today.

Historians Weigh In: The immediate aftermath period is well documented in tribal records and missionary accounts. The work of reconstructing Cherokee society while processing collective trauma was enormous.

Immediate Challenges

  • No homes, no resources - had to build everything from nothing
  • Epidemics continued - whooping cough, measles still spreading
  • More deaths in first year after arrival
  • Bitter internal divisions over Treaty Party

Survival required immediate action despite overwhelming grief and trauma

Starting over: Imagine arriving after months of death and trauma. You have no house. No farm. Few possessions. Winter is coming. You have to find food, build shelter, and try to survive - all while grieving the loss of family members.

Continued deaths: The epidemics didn't stop at the Oklahoma border. Disease continued to kill people in the first year after arrival. Some estimates suggest another 1,000-3,000 died in that first year.

Internal conflict: And on top of everything else, Cherokee were bitterly divided. Many blamed the Treaty Party for making removal possible. This would lead to violence.

Internal Conflict and Revenge

The Treaty Party Assassinations

June 22, 1839

[Image: "Major Ridge John Ridge Elias Boudinot portraits"]

The Assassinations:

Context: The Treaty Party - Major Ridge, his son John Ridge, and Elias Boudineau - had signed the Treaty of New Echota against the will of the vast majority of Cherokee. Traditional Cherokee law prescribed death for anyone who sold tribal land without authorization.

The morning of June 22, 1839: In coordinated attacks, Cherokee who blamed the Treaty Party carried out executions.

What Happened on June 22, 1839

Major Ridge

Ambushed on the road, shot multiple times. Died instantly.

John Ridge

Dragged from bed in front of his family. Stabbed repeatedly. Died in his wife's arms.

Elias Boudineau

Tomahawked while helping build a house for missionaries. Killed immediately.

Stand Watie (Boudineau's brother)

Warned by a friend. Escaped. Would seek revenge for decades.

The violence: These were brutal, public executions. John Ridge was killed in front of his wife and children. The assassins wanted to send a message.

Stand Watie: Stand Watie, Boudineau's brother and another Treaty Party member, was warned and escaped. He would become a Confederate general during the Civil War and lead Cherokee who remained pro-Confederate. The divisions created by removal lasted for generations.

Historians Weigh In: Theda Perdue argues that the assassinations, while shocking to white observers, followed traditional Cherokee law regarding the sale of communal land. This wasn't random violence - it was execution of a death sentence that had been threatened for years.

Understanding the Assassinations

Why It Happened

  • Traditional Cherokee law: death for selling tribal land
  • Many blamed Treaty Party for enabling removal
  • Deep rage over 4,000+ deaths
  • Question of justice vs. revenge

The Tragedy

  • Ridge family believed they were saving the nation
  • Both resistance and negotiation failed
  • Violence solved nothing
  • Civil conflict continued for years

Reflection Question: Were the assassinations justified by Cherokee law? Was this justice or murder? How do societies deal with internal divisions after collective trauma?

The complexity: This is genuinely complex. From one perspective, the assassins were carrying out traditional law against those who violated it. From another, the Ridge family was trying to negotiate the best possible terms for an inevitable removal. Both sides believed they were doing what was best for the Cherokee people.

The tragedy: The real tragedy is that both strategies - John Ross's resistance and the Treaty Party's pragmatic negotiation - failed. The U.S. government took Cherokee land regardless. And then Cherokee turned on each other in the aftermath.

Long-term impact: These divisions persisted. During the Civil War, Stand Watie led pro-Confederate Cherokee while others supported the Union. The trauma of removal created fractures that lasted generations.

Rebuilding the Cherokee Nation

Recovery and Reconstitution (1840s-1860s)

Despite trauma and conflict, Cherokee rebuilt their nation

[Image: "Cherokee National Capitol Tahlequah Oklahoma"]

Rebuilding:

Remarkable resilience: What's remarkable is that despite losing one quarter of their population, despite internal divisions, despite starting from nothing, Cherokee rebuilt a functioning nation within a decade.

This section shows: Cherokee weren't just victims. They survived, rebuilt, adapted, and maintained their identity and sovereignty (to the extent possible). This is important for students to see - agency alongside suffering.

What Cherokee Rebuilt

Political

  • New constitution (1839)
  • United Old Settlers and new arrivals
  • Democratic government
  • Capital at Tahlequah, OK
  • Functioning legal system

Economic

  • Farms, ranches, businesses
  • Some became wealthy again
  • Trade networks
  • Newspapers restarted

Educational

  • Male & Female Seminaries (1850s)
  • Better educated than surrounding whites
  • Bilingual education maintained

Cultural

  • Cherokee language continued
  • Traditional practices preserved
  • Strong national identity

Political reconstruction: By 1839, Cherokee had created a new constitution that united the Old Settlers with the new arrivals. They reestablished democratic government with elected leaders, a functional legal system, and a capital at Tahlequah.

Economic recovery: Cherokee rebuilt farms and businesses. Some became quite wealthy. They restarted newspapers (Cherokee Advocate in 1844). Within a generation, they had recreated much of what they'd lost.

Education: By the 1850s, Cherokee had established Male and Female Seminaries - essentially high schools. Cherokee literacy rates were higher than surrounding white populations. They maintained bilingual education.

Cultural preservation: Throughout all this, Cherokee maintained their language, traditional practices, and strong sense of national identity. They adapted and survived.

Historians Weigh In: Devon Mihesuah's work on the Cherokee Female Seminary shows how Cherokee adapted Western education while maintaining Cherokee identity and values. This wasn't simple assimilation - it was strategic adaptation.

Important Context:

This rebuilding would be disrupted again by the Civil War,
and later by American allotment policiesThe Dawes Act (1887) forced division of communal tribal lands into individual plots, with "surplus" land opened to white settlement. This led to massive additional land loss.

It didn't end: Students should know that this rebuilding wasn't permanent peace. The Civil War would tear Cherokee Nation apart again. And later, American allotment policies would steal more land and attempt to destroy tribal governance entirely.

Pattern continues: The pattern of Cherokee building and America disrupting/stealing would continue for decades. But Cherokee kept rebuilding. That persistence is the story.

Civil War Impact

Cherokee Nation Divided Again (1861-1865)

[Image: "Stand Watie Confederate Cherokee general"]

Civil War:

Context: When the American Civil War began in 1861, Cherokee Nation faced an impossible choice. They were geographically surrounded by Confederate states. The U.S. federal government had betrayed them before. What should they do?

The Impossible Choice

  • Confederacy promised respect for Cherokee sovereignty
  • Union seemed unreliable - they'd broken treaties before
  • Internal divisions: Some Cherokee owned enslaved people, others opposed slavery
  • Geographic position: Surrounded by Confederate states

Cherokee Nation officially allied with Confederacy (1861)
But many Cherokee, including eventually John Ross, supported Union

The choice: Confederate states promised to respect Cherokee sovereignty and protect their land. The Union had just forced them through the Trail of Tears 20 years earlier. What would you do?

Divisions: Cherokee weren't united. Some owned enslaved people and supported Confederacy. Others opposed slavery. Some trusted Confederate promises. Others remained loyal to the Union. These divisions mapped partly onto old Treaty Party vs. Ross party divisions.

What happened: Cherokee Nation officially allied with the Confederacy in 1861. But many Cherokee, including John Ross eventually, supported the Union. Stand Watie became a Confederate general. Cherokee fought Cherokee - a civil war within the Civil War.

The Devastation

  • Cherokee fought Cherokee - families divided
  • Territory devastated: farms burned, civilians displaced
  • ~7,000 Cherokee died (out of ~20,000)
  • Stand Watie became last Confederate general to surrender (June 1865)
[Image: "Civil War devastation Oklahoma territory"]

Internal war: Cherokee fought Cherokee. Families were divided. Pro-Confederate Cherokee militia fought pro-Union Cherokee militia. The territory became a battlefield.

Deaths and destruction: About 7,000 Cherokee died during the Civil War - another devastating blow. Farms were burned, civilians displaced. Everything they'd rebuilt was destroyed again.

Stand Watie: Stand Watie, the Treaty Party member who had escaped assassination, became a Confederate general and was the last Confederate general to surrender in June 1865, two months after Lee surrendered at Appomattox.

Aftermath of Civil War

  • Federal government punished Cherokee for Confederate alliance
  • Forced to cede western half of territory (even though many Cherokee fought for Union)
  • Cherokee-owned enslaved people freed → became Cherokee citizens (Cherokee FreedmenAfrican Americans freed from slavery in Cherokee Nation became citizens. Their descendants' citizenship rights remain contested today.)
  • More land loss, more trauma

Punishment: After the Civil War, the federal government punished Cherokee Nation for allying with the Confederacy - even though many Cherokee had fought for the Union. They forced Cherokee to give up the western half of their territory.

Freedmen: Cherokee were also required to free enslaved people and make them citizens of Cherokee Nation. These Cherokee Freedmen and their descendants have faced ongoing debates about their citizenship status - a controversy that continues today.

Pattern continues: Once again, Cherokee had to rebuild from devastation. The pattern of building, destruction, rebuilding continues.

Long-Term Consequences

The Ongoing Impact

Long-Term Impact:

Purpose: Students need to see that the Trail of Tears wasn't just a historical event that ended in 1839. Its effects rippled through generations and continue today.

19th Century Continued

Dawes Act (1887)

Forced division of communal land into individual allotments. "Excess" land opened to white settlement. Cherokee lost millions more acres.

Oklahoma Statehood (1907)

Cherokee Nation dissolved as political entity by U.S. government

Cultural suppression

Boarding schools forced assimilation, forbade Cherokee language

Dawes Act: In 1887, the Dawes Act forced tribes to divide communal lands into individual plots. Any "excess" land was opened to white settlement. This was another massive land theft disguised as policy to help Native people become farmers.

Statehood: When Oklahoma became a state in 1907, the U.S. government dissolved Cherokee tribal government entirely. For decades, Cherokee had no recognized government.

Boarding schools: Cherokee children were forced into boarding schools designed to "kill the Indian, save the man." They were forbidden to speak Cherokee, practice traditional religion, or maintain their culture.

Cherokee Nation government reinstated (1970s)

Reinstatement: In the 1970s, Cherokee were finally able to reinstate their national government. They elected a Principal Chief and reestablished self-governance.

Legal battles: Cherokee Nation has fought ongoing legal battles for sovereignty, land rights, and resources. In 2020, the Supreme Court ruled that much of eastern Oklahoma remains Indian Country - a major legal victory.

Cultural revival: Cherokee have worked hard to revitalize their language and culture. Language immersion schools, cultural programs, and renewed traditional practices help keep Cherokee identity alive.

Cherokee Nations Today

  • Cherokee Nation of Oklahoma: ~380,000+ citizens (largest tribe)
  • Eastern Band of Cherokee: ~14,000 members in North Carolina (descendants of those who hid)
  • United Keetoowah Band: ~14,000 members
  • Self-governing nations with own laws, courts, services
  • Economic development: casinos, businesses, healthcare systems
  • Active cultural preservation

Three Cherokee Nations: Today there are three federally recognized Cherokee nations. The largest is Cherokee Nation of Oklahoma with over 380,000 citizens. The Eastern Band in North Carolina descends from those who hid in the mountains. The United Keetoowah Band is another Oklahoma-based Cherokee nation.

Self-governance: These are self-governing nations with their own laws, court systems, police, health services, and schools. They're not just cultural organizations - they're governments.

Economic power: Cherokee Nation has used gaming and other businesses to build economic power. They provide healthcare, education, and services to their citizens.

Survival: The key point: Cherokee survived. Despite everything - removal, civil war, allotment, cultural suppression - Cherokee Nation still exists, still governs itself, still maintains its culture.

Intergenerational Trauma

The Trail of Tears didn't just affect those who lived through it

Psychological effects passed through generations

Historical trauma recognized in Cherokee communities

Ongoing work on healing and remembering

Intergenerational trauma: Research shows that trauma can be passed through generations. Children and grandchildren of trauma survivors carry psychological effects even if they didn't directly experience the trauma.

In Cherokee communities: Historical trauma is recognized as a real phenomenon affecting Cherokee people today. The Trail of Tears happened nearly 200 years ago, but its effects continue.

Healing work: Cherokee communities engage in healing work - cultural renewal, talking about the trauma, teaching history, maintaining connections to each other and to the land.

Historians Weigh In: Scholars like Julie Reed in Serving the Nation: Cherokee Sovereignty and Social Welfare show how Cherokee communities have developed approaches to healing historical trauma while maintaining sovereignty.

Who Benefited?

Following the Money and Power

Who Benefited:

Critical analysis: This section is crucial. Students need to understand that ethnic cleansing isn't just about those who suffered - it's also about those who gained. Following the money and power reveals the motivations.

Direct Beneficiaries of Cherokee Removal

Land lottery winners

Georgia held lottery to distribute Cherokee land - before Cherokee even left. 40-acre plots for white citizens. Larger plots for gold areas.

Gold miners

Dahlonega gold rush enriched prospectors. Georgia got mining taxes.

Cotton planters

Prime agricultural land for plantation expansion. Slavery expanded into former Cherokee territory.

Land speculators

Bought and sold Cherokee lands for profit

Federal government

Consolidated territorial control, opened West for white settlement

Land lottery: Georgia literally held a lottery - white citizens could win Cherokee land. This happened BEFORE Cherokee were even removed. Families were still living on land that had been "won" by white Georgians.

Gold: The Dahlonega gold rush brought wealth to white miners and tax revenue to Georgia. That gold was on Cherokee land.

Cotton: Cotton planters got prime agricultural land. Slavery expanded westward onto former Cherokee territory. The cotton economy boomed.

Speculators: Land speculators made fortunes buying and selling Cherokee land.

Federal power: The U.S. government consolidated control over the Southeast, opened territory for white settlement westward, enhanced national power.

Who Paid

  • Cherokee Nation: 4,000+ dead, homeland lost, society shattered
  • Other removed tribes: Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, Seminole also forcibly removed
  • Thousands more Native deaths from other removal campaigns

The math of ethnic cleansing:

One group's catastrophic loss = Another group's windfall profit

Who paid: Cherokee paid with 4,000+ lives, their homeland, their society. But Cherokee weren't alone - the other "Five Civilized Tribes" were also removed, with similar death tolls.

The equation: This is the fundamental equation of ethnic cleansing: one group's catastrophic loss equals another group's gain. Cherokee lost everything; white settlers got land, gold, and cotton profits.

Why this matters: Understanding who benefited helps us understand why ethnic cleansing happens. It's not irrational hatred - it's often economically motivated violence backed by state power.

Historical Memory

How We Remember - Changing Narratives Over Time

Historical Memory:

Purpose: Students need to understand that how we remember history changes over time. The Trail of Tears has been remembered differently in different eras, and those changes reveal shifting values and power dynamics.

19th - Early 20th Century

  • Called "Indian Removal" in textbooks
  • Portrayed as inevitable, even benevolent
  • "Indians were in the way of progress"
  • Andrew Jackson seen as hero
  • Cherokee resistance mostly erased from history

Early memory: For most of American history, removal was presented as inevitable progress. Textbooks called it "Indian Removal" - neutral, bureaucratic language. Jackson was a hero - "Old Hickory," man of the people.

The narrative: The standard story was: Indians were "uncivilized" and "in the way of progress." Removal was presented as helping them - moving them to land where they could live peacefully. The suffering and deaths were minimized or ignored.

Erasure: Cherokee resistance, legal victories, and agency were erased. They were presented as passive victims of inevitable historical forces, not as political actors fighting for their rights.

Mid-20th Century

  • Some recognition of injustice emerged
  • Still often called "relocation" or "resettlement"
  • Focus on "tragic" but necessary policy
  • Passive voice: "Indians were moved"

Mid-century shift: By the mid-20th century, some historians began acknowledging that removal was unjust. But the language remained euphemistic - "relocation," "resettlement."

Passive voice: Notice the passive voice: "Indians were moved." Not "The U.S. government forcibly removed Indians." The passive voice hides agency and responsibility.

Still "necessary": Even when acknowledged as tragic, removal was still often portrayed as necessary or inevitable. This narrative protects American identity - "we did bad things, but we had to."

Late 20th-21st Century

  • "Trail of Tears" became standard term (Cherokee name: Nunna daul Tsuny - "The Trail Where They Cried")
  • Recognized as ethnic cleansing
  • Focus on Cherokee agency and resistance
  • Growing acknowledgment of American responsibility

Recent changes: More recently, historians use "Trail of Tears" - adopting the Cherokee name. This centers Cherokee experience and acknowledges the suffering.

Ethnic cleansing: Scholars now recognize removal as ethnic cleansing - precise language that places it in global context.

Cherokee agency: Modern historians emphasize Cherokee resistance, legal strategies, and agency. Cherokee weren't passive victims - they fought back using every tool available.

Responsibility: There's growing acknowledgment that this was American policy, American responsibility. Not an unfortunate accident of history.

Ongoing Debates

  • Should it be called genocide?
  • How should we memorialize it?
  • What reparations or recognition are owed?
  • Why is Andrew Jackson still honored? ($20 bill, monuments, etc.)

Memorialization Today

How We Remember the Trail of Tears

Memorialization:

Purpose: How societies memorialize atrocities matters. It shapes how future generations understand history and their responsibilities.

Physical Memorials

  • Trail of Tears National Historic Trail (established 1987): marks multiple routes
  • Monuments in Cherokee Nation, Oklahoma
  • Historical markers at key sites
  • Museum of the Cherokee Indian (North Carolina)
  • Cherokee Heritage Center (Oklahoma)
[Image: "Trail of Tears memorial monument"]

National Historic Trail: In 1987, Congress established the Trail of Tears National Historic Trail. It marks multiple routes (water and land) that removal detachments took. You can visit sites along the trail.

Museums: The Museum of the Cherokee Indian in Cherokee, NC (Eastern Band territory) and Cherokee Heritage Center in Tahlequah, OK both have extensive exhibits on the Trail of Tears.

Physical memorials matter: They keep memory alive, educate the public, and honor those who died.

In Cherokee Communities

  • Annual commemorative walks - Cherokee walk portions of the trail
  • Oral histories preserved and shared
  • Taught in Cherokee schools
  • Part of living cultural memory, not just history

For Cherokee people, the Trail of Tears isn't distant history - it's family history, passed down through generations

Living memory: For Cherokee people, this isn't abstract history. This is what happened to their great-great-grandparents. It's family history.

Commemorative walks: Cherokee communities organize annual walks along portions of the trail - physically retracing the steps of their ancestors, remembering, honoring those who died.

Oral histories: Stories passed down through families keep the memory alive in intimate, personal ways.

Education: Cherokee schools teach this history to Cherokee children, ensuring each generation knows what happened to their people.

In Mainstream American Memory

  • Required in some state history standards
  • National Parks Service interpretive programs
  • Growing public awareness

What's Missing?

  • Many Americans still don't know about Trail of Tears
  • Textbooks often give it minimal coverage
  • No national memorial comparable to Holocaust Museum
  • Andrew Jackson still on currency

Some progress: Some states require teaching about the Trail of Tears. The National Parks Service has interpretive programs. Public awareness is growing.

But gaps remain: Many Americans have never heard of the Trail of Tears. When it's taught, it often gets minimal coverage - a paragraph in a textbook. There's no national memorial comparable to the Holocaust Museum in Washington, DC.

Jackson on currency: And Andrew Jackson - the man who ordered this - is still on the $20 bill. Imagine Germany putting Hitler on their currency. That's not happening. Why is the U.S. different?

Historians Weigh In: David Blight's work on Civil War memory shows how nations selectively remember their past. The Trail of Tears has been systematically minimized in American memory because acknowledging it fully would require reckoning with foundational violence in American history.

Rebecca Neugin:

Rebecca Neugin (1835-1932)

After the Trail:

  • Survived to Oklahoma with her family
  • Married, had children
  • Lived through Cherokee rebuilding
  • Witnessed Cherokee Civil War divisions

Rebecca's life: Rebecca Neugin survived the Trail of Tears at age three. She lived in Oklahoma, married, had children. She witnessed Cherokee rebuilding their nation. She lived through the Civil War, watching Cherokee fight Cherokee. She saw allotment steal land again. She witnessed Oklahoma statehood and the dissolution of Cherokee government.

Nearly a century: She witnessed nearly 100 years of Cherokee history - from the Trail of Tears to the 1930s.

Her Testimony (1932)

"When the soldiers came to our house my father wanted to fight, but my mother told him that the soldiers would kill him if he did... I can remember when we started, it was early in the morning... I remember the looks on the faces of the people, we all looked the same way. They looked like they did not have any idea what was to become of them."
— Rebecca Neugin, age 97

Her testimony: Near the end of her life in 1932, Rebecca told her story to an interviewer. She was 97 years old. She was one of the last survivors.

What she remembered: She remembered the soldiers coming. Her father wanting to fight. The journey beginning. The looks on people's faces - uncertainty, fear. She remembered the ice on wagon wheels. Her baby sibling dying and being buried. Not knowing where they were going.

Why this matters: Rebecca's testimony preserves what she witnessed as a child. Her memories keep that history alive in human terms, not just as statistics or policy.

Why Her Story Matters

  • Puts a human face on statistics
  • Preserves child's perspective on trauma
  • Shows survival and resilience
  • Lived testimony into 20th century

This isn't ancient history - Rebecca died in 1932, within living memory of people still alive today

Not ancient history: Rebecca Neugin died in 1932. That means there are people alive today who could have met her. Your grandparents might have been alive when she died. This isn't ancient history.

Human face: Rebecca's story gives us a human face to remember. Not just "4,000 died" but "Rebecca's baby sibling died." That makes the history real.
