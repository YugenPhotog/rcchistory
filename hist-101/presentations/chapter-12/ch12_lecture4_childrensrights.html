Children's Rights Movement: A History of Advocacy and Protection

From the Moral Family to Civil Society

Welcome to today's lecture on the Children's Rights Movement. We'll trace how American society moved from viewing children primarily through the lens of family discipline to recognizing children as having rights deserving of public protection. This transformation reflects broader changes in how Americans understood virtue, citizenship, and the role of civil society.

Introduction: Why Children's Reform Matters

  • Antebellum moral reform expanded civic virtue beyond politics to moral life.
  • Child reform extended this moral project to the next generation.
  • Voluntary associations bridged private virtue and public welfare.
  • Scope: From Puritan discipline to Progressive child advocacy.
If virtue sustains democracy, childhood formation becomes a civic project.
Start by connecting children's reform to the broader theme of moral reform we've been studying. Emphasize that reformers saw children as the future of the republic—if democracy depends on virtuous citizens, then childhood becomes a matter of public concern, not just private family business. This lecture spans from colonial Puritan discipline to Progressive Era reforms.

Children in the Colonial World: Family as Moral Commonwealth

  • Puritan Ideal: Family as "Little Commonwealth," linking household order to civic virtue.
  • Body of Liberties (1641): Early legal protection for children.
  • Stubborn Child Law (1646): Salvation through discipline.
Household order was imagined as the seedbed of civic order.
[Insert image: Colonial family / New England schoolhouse]
Puritans saw the family as a "little commonwealth"—a miniature version of the larger political community. The father's authority over children mirrored the magistrate's authority over citizens. Note the Body of Liberties gave children some legal protections, but the Stubborn Child Law shows discipline was primarily about salvation and social order, not individual rights.

The Colonial & Early Republic Era: Orphans and Dependency

  • New England "placing out" children to other families.
  • Chesapeake courts appoint guardians—early state oversight.
  • Vendue and early orphanages: from auctions to faith-based care.
Care shifted from kinship to organized benevolence.
Birth of child welfare as a civic obligation.
[Insert image: Ursuline orphanage / charitable notice]
When families failed, communities had to step in. In New England, orphans were "placed out" with other families. In the Chesapeake, courts appointed guardians. The shift from vendue (essentially auctioning children to the lowest bidder for their care) to organized orphanages marks the beginning of child welfare as a community responsibility, often led by religious organizations.

Enlightenment, Romanticism, and the Moral Psychology of Childhood

  • Locke's tabula rasa: environment shapes virtue.
  • Rousseau's romantic innocence: childhood as sacred and pure.
  • Moral psychology: discipline and affection form character.
  • Institution building: moral engineering through education.
Ideas reframed childhood as improvable through environment and care.
[Insert image: 18th-c. primer / school scene]
Two key intellectual shifts: Locke's idea that children are blank slates shaped by experience, and Rousseau's romantic view of childhood innocence. Together, these ideas suggested that proper environment and education could produce virtuous citizens. This optimism about human improvement drove the creation of schools, reformatories, and other institutions designed to shape character.

Industrialization, Urbanization, and the Crisis of Childhood

  • Industrial labor and child poverty (mills, mines, glassworks).
  • Immigration and urban overcrowding concentrate poverty.
  • "Street Arabs" / "Street Rats"; ≈30,000 homeless children in NYC by 1850s.
  • Reformers warned of a "terrible social crisis."
  • Civil society awakens: churches & charities mobilize.
Rapid urbanization created visible crises that demanded organized response.
The "discovery" of childhood poverty sparked a national reform movement.
[Insert image: Street children in Five Points / child mill workers / Newsboy sleeping in alley]
Industrialization and urbanization created a visible crisis. Tens of thousands of children lived on the streets, worked in dangerous factories, or languished in overcrowded slums. Reformers called them "street Arabs" and warned of social catastrophe. This visible crisis galvanized middle-class reformers to create new institutions and push for new laws.

Public Schools: From Moral Laboratory to Children's Right

  • We already met the Common School crusade in Lecture 2 (Slides 21–32): Horace Mann's tax-funded classrooms, the hidden curriculum of punctuality & obedience, and the dream of education as "the great equalizer."
  • New lens today: By the 1850s reformers re-branded schools as a children's right — a legal duty of the state to rescue poor, immigrant, and working kids from factories and streets.
If you missed it: Review Lecture 2 "Common Schools and the Shaping of Citizens"
Quick callback to Lecture 2 where we covered Horace Mann and the Common School movement in detail. Today's new angle: reformers increasingly framed education not just as civic duty but as a children's right—something the state owed to children, especially poor and immigrant children. This reframing made compulsory education laws possible.

Houses of Refuge & Reform Schools (1825–1900)

Origins & Philosophy

  • First House of Refuge: New York, 1825.
  • Alternative to adult prisons for children.
  • Goal: moral reform through discipline, labor, education.
  • Based on belief that environment shapes character.

Reality vs. Ideal

  • Brutal discipline, harsh labor, overcrowding.
  • Children imprisoned for minor offenses: begging, vagrancy, "stubbornness."
  • Ex parte Crouse (1838): Parents' rights could be overridden for child's "welfare."
Reform schools claimed benevolent purpose but operated like prisons.
[Insert image: House of Refuge exterior / children in work detail / dining hall]
"The object of the charity is reformation, by training its inmates to industry; by imbuing their minds with principles of morality and religion."
—Pennsylvania Supreme Court, Ex parte Crouse (1838)
Houses of Refuge were supposed to save children from adult prisons, but they often became brutal institutions themselves. The Ex parte Crouse decision justified state intervention in families by claiming it served the child's welfare—a principle that could be both protective and coercive. These institutions show the dark side of reform: good intentions implemented through harsh, dehumanizing methods.

Girls in Moral Reform: Child Prostitution & “Fallen Women”

  • Due to Urban poverty and exploitation, many destitute girls turn to or forced in prostitution.
  • Magdalene & Female Moral Reform Societies: rescue, repentance, rehabilitation.
  • Institutions:
    • First Stand Alone Female Reformatory – Boston Female Moral Reform Society's Home (1833)
    • New York House of Mercy (1855)
Women-led organizations pioneered rescue but enforced Victorian purity.
[Insert image: FMRS pamphlet / House of Mercy]

Moral discipline over legal protection: girls treated as sinners, not victims.

Gendered reform: female delinquency as moral failure, not social injustice.

Age of Consent Reform: Protecting Girls from Sexual Exploitation

The Problem

  • Mid-19th century: age of consent as low as 7–10 years in most states.
  • Girls could be legally married or sexually exploited with minimal protection.
  • Double standard: girls blamed for "seduction," men rarely prosecuted.

Reform Campaigns (1880s–1910s)

  • Women's Christian Temperance Union (WCTU) leads national campaign.
  • Frances Willard: "Organize the motherhood of the world."
  • 1885–1905: Most states raise age of consent to 16–18.
  • Reformers framed this as protecting female virtue and childhood innocence.
Age of consent reform was a major victory for children's rights advocacy.
[Insert image: WCTU poster / Frances Willard portrait / age of consent petition]
Age of consent laws codified the idea that children—especially girls—needed legal protection from adult exploitation.
Age of consent reform shows women's organizations exercising real political power before they even had the vote. The WCTU mobilized thousands of women across the country to pressure state legislatures. Raising the age of consent from 7 or 10 to 16 or 18 represented a major shift in how society viewed children—as vulnerable beings deserving protection, not as miniature adults.

Charles Loring Brace & The Children's Aid Society (1853)

The "Dangerous Classes" Theory

  • Brace believed street children threatened social order.
  • Saw Catholic immigrants as unable to raise proper citizens.
  • Solution: remove children from cities to Protestant farm families.

The Orphan Trains (1854–1929)

  • ≈200,000 children sent West between 1854–1929.
  • Children displayed at train stops; farmers selected workers.
  • Some found loving homes; others faced exploitation and abuse.
Brace combined genuine concern with nativist prejudice and class anxiety.
[Insert image: Orphan train group photo / Children lined up at train station / CAS poster]
"The class of a large city most dangerous to its property, its morals, and its political life, are the ignorant, destitute, untrained, and abandoned youth."
—Charles Loring Brace, The Dangerous Classes of New York (1872)
Brace's work is complicated. He genuinely cared about children's welfare, but his solution reflected deep prejudices—he assumed Protestant farm life was superior to Catholic urban culture. The orphan trains show both the possibilities and limitations of 19th-century reform: good intentions mixed with class bias and cultural imperialism. Some children thrived, but many were essentially used as farm labor.

Orphan Trains: The Shadow Side

  • 1865–1870 Southern “apprentice” laws bind 40,000+ Black orphans to former slave-owners — legal re-enslavement.
  • 1879–1934 Federal Indian Boarding Schools: 100,000 Native children forcibly removed; motto “Kill the Indian, Save the Man.”
  • 1854–1929 Orphan Trains refuse Catholic & Jewish children in Protestant towns; Irish kids re-labeled “incorrigible.”
  • Result: the same reformers who “saved” white street arabs built pipelines to plantation, reservation, and reformatory for children of color.
Shadow Truth
Child “protection” was never color-blind.
The train west for white orphans was the chain south or the wagon north for Black and Native children.
[Insert image: Carlisle Indian School “before/after” 1890s / Black child apprentice contract, Mississippi 1866]
“We were taken from our mothers and put on the train with numbers pinned to our coats.”
—Mary Englund, Orphan Train rider, 1980 oral history

The Mary Ellen Moment, 1874–1875

  • 9 April 1874 — Etta Wheeler carries a scarred, chained 10-year-old out of a Hell's Kitchen tenement.
  • 21 April 1874 — Judge rules: a living child can be removed by habeas corpus.
  • 15 Dec 1874 — New York Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children chartered ("The Cruelty").
  • First agency on earth with police powers to enter homes and seize abused children.
  • By 1910 → 250 sister societies nationwide.
One whip-scarred girl + one courtroom photograph = the legal invention of child protective services.
Private charity became public authority in eight months.
Moral outrage → statute → badge → CPS.
[Insert image: Mary Ellen Wilson 1874 intake photo + NYSPCC silver shield badge]
"Mama has been in the habit of whipping me with a rawhide and scissors."
—Mary Ellen Wilson, testifying in her own tiny voice, 10 April 1874
Learn More Here
This is one of the most important moments in child welfare history. Mary Ellen Wilson was a 10-year-old girl being beaten and chained by her foster mother. When social worker Etta Wheeler found her, there was no legal mechanism to remove an abused child from a home—so they used an animal cruelty law to argue she deserved at least as much protection as a dog. The case sparked outrage and led to the creation of the first child protective services agency with actual police powers. Within eight months, moral outrage became law enforcement authority. Ask students: What does it say about society that animals had legal protection before children did?

The First Juvenile Court: Cook County, Illinois (1899)

Revolutionary Principles

  • Children not tried as criminals—treated as "wards" of the state.
  • Judge acts in loco parentis (in place of parents).
  • Focus on rehabilitation, not punishment.
  • Informal proceedings; no jury trials.
  • Probation instead of incarceration when possible.

The Chicago Model Spreads

  • By 1925: juvenile courts in every state.
  • Progressive reformers see this as triumph of scientific child-saving.
  • Critics note: children lose due process rights in the name of protection.
Juvenile courts embodied Progressive faith that expertise could reform society.
The juvenile court system represented both genuine progress and new forms of state control over children and families.
[Insert image: First juvenile courtroom, 219 S. Clark St. / Judge Richard Tuthill on the bench / 1899 probation badge]
"The child is not prosecuted for a crime; the child is protected from a condition."
—Judge Richard Tuthill, opening day, July 3, 1899
The juvenile court was revolutionary: children would no longer be tried as criminals but treated as victims of circumstance needing guidance. But this benevolent system also meant children lost rights to jury trials and formal procedures. Ask students: Can a system be both protective and paternalistic? The juvenile court shows how Progressive reform often gave experts enormous power over people's lives.

Child-Labor Reform Milestones, 1904–1938

  • 1904 National Child Labor Committee (NCLC) founded in NYC; Lewis Hine hired to photograph "the shame of the states."
  • 1910 Hine's photos of 8-year-olds in coal breakers and cotton mills printed in every major magazine.
  • 1916 Keating-Owen Act bans interstate sale of goods made by children under 14 (or 16 in mines).
  • June 3, 1918 Supreme Court strikes it down in Hammer v. Dagenhart ("Congress cannot regulate local labor").
  • 1938 Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) survives court challenge; sets first federal floor: 16 for regular work, 18 for hazardous.
1904 → 1916 → 1918 → 1938
One Lewis Hine photograph → one federal law → one permanent childhood.
Child labor did NOT vanish in 1850. It took 34 years of lobbying, photography, and two Supreme Court defeats to outlaw it nationwide.
[Insert image: Lewis Hine "Addie Card, 12 years old, spinner in North Pownal Cotton Mill, Vermont, 1910"]

"I'm 12. I been workin' here two years. I like it."
—Addie Card to Lewis Hine, 1910

Child labor reform took decades. Lewis Hine's photographs shocked middle-class Americans by making child labor visible. But even after Congress passed laws, the Supreme Court struck them down as unconstitutional. Not until 1938—after the New Deal Court revolution—did federal child labor laws finally stick. This shows how slowly reform could move, even when reformers had public opinion on their side.

Gilded-Age Nationalization: The WCTU "Do Everything" Revolution

  • Frances Willard (WCTU president 1879–1898) turned temperance into a total social gospel.
  • "Do Everything" platform: child labor laws, kindergartens, age-of-consent reform, juvenile courts, suffrage.
  • 1886–1905: WCTU raises statutory rape age from 10 to 16 in forty states.
  • 1899 Illinois Juvenile Court Act—first children's court—drafted with WCTU lobbyists.
  • 1904 National Child Labor Committee founded in WCTU offices.
Willard's motto: "Organize the motherhood of the world."
Women's moral networks became the scaffolding of the 20th-century welfare state.

WCTU & Frances Willard

  • Founded 1874, Cleveland. World's largest women's organization by 1890.
  • Willard's alliances: Prohibition Party, Knights of Labor, NAWSA.
  • Laid groundwork for 18th Amendment (1919) and 19th Amendment (1920).
The WCTU shows how women used moral authority to gain political power before they could even vote. Frances Willard's "Do Everything" strategy meant the WCTU didn't just fight alcohol—they fought for child labor laws, juvenile courts, kindergartens, and women's suffrage. Their organizational networks became the foundation for the modern welfare state. This is a crucial example of how excluded groups can still shape policy.

Conclusion: The Legacy of Children's Rights Reform

Achievements

  • Childhood recognized as a distinct life stage requiring protection.
  • Legal reforms: juvenile courts, child labor laws, compulsory education.
  • New institutions: orphanages, foster care, social work professions.
  • Shift from family sovereignty to state responsibility for child welfare.

Ongoing Questions

  • How much state power over families is justified?
  • Who decides what's in a child's "best interest"?
  • Do children have rights independent of their parents?
  • How do class and race shape who gets "protected" vs. punished?
The children's rights movement transformed American society by making childhood a matter of public concern, not just private family business.
But reform was never neutral—it always reflected the values, prejudices, and power dynamics of the reformers themselves.
Wrap up by emphasizing both the genuine progress and the persistent problems. Childhood became legally protected, but that protection often came with surveillance and control. Reformers saved many children, but they also imposed middle-class values on working families and immigrant communities. These tensions continue today in debates over child protective services, juvenile justice, and parents' rights. The history of children's reform reminds us that even well-intentioned reforms can have complicated, sometimes troubling, consequences.
