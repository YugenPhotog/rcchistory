📚 What You Need to Know

This lecture explores the American Civil War (1861-1865), the deadliest conflict in American history with approximately 750,000 deaths. We'll examine why the Union won despite early Confederate advantages and how the war fundamentally transformed the United States.

📖 Key Questions

  • Why did the South lose a war it seemed positioned to win?
  • How did the war evolve from preserving the Union to ending slavery?
  • What were the turning points that shifted momentum?
  • How did the war change American society forever?

🎓 Senior Historians' Corner

James McPherson emphasizes contingency—the war's outcome was not predetermined. Drew Gilpin Faust examines how the scale of death transformed American culture. Gary Gallagher argues we must understand why the Confederate war effort lasted so long, not just why it failed.

The Civil War's Central Question

  • Why did the Union win a war it was losing early?
  • How did the war transform the nation?
  • Political, military, social, and moral dimensions intertwine
  • War as both national rupture and national rebirth

📚 Framing the Narrative

We often think of Northern victory as inevitable given its advantages in population, industry, and resources. But the Confederacy nearly won on multiple occasions through 1863. The Union had to develop the will to fight AND figure out how to use its advantages effectively.

📖 Key Concepts

  • National rupture: Complete breakdown of the political system
  • National rebirth: Lincoln's Gettysburg phrase—fulfilling the Declaration's promises
  • Contingency: Different decisions could have changed the outcome

SECTION I

The Road to War

Long-Term Causes

  • Expansion of slavery divides every national debate
  • Failure of compromises (Missouri, Compromise of 1850)
  • Growth of sectional party identities
  • Polarization accelerates in the 1850s
×

The Slavery Question

By 1860, slavery defined American politics. The question wasn't just whether slavery should exist, but whether it would expand into new territories.

  • Economic: Enslaved people were America's largest financial asset
  • Political: Balance of free/slave states determined Senate and presidency
  • Social: Shaped race relations, class, regional identities
  • Moral: Increasingly seen as sinful (North) or divinely ordained (South)

📚 Why Compromises Failed

The Missouri Compromise (1820) and Compromise of 1850 tried to balance free and slave state power. But each compromise became harder because moral arguments against slavery grew stronger in the North while Southern whites became more defensive.

🎓 Historical Context

David Potter examined how political institutions failed to contain the crisis. Eric Foner shows how western expansion radicalized Northern politics.

Political Collapse of the 1850s

  • Kansas-Nebraska Act shatters the old order
  • Republican Party rises in the North
  • Dred Scott intensifies sectional crisis
  • Violence in "Bleeding Kansas" and Congress
×

Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854)

Senator Douglas proposed "popular sovereignty" for Kansas and Nebraska territories, repealing the Missouri Compromise line.

Consequences: Destroyed the Whig Party, created the Republican Party, led to violent conflict in Kansas, convinced Northerners of "Slave Power conspiracy."

📖 The Cascade of Crises

  • 1854: Kansas-Nebraska Act
  • 1855-56: "Bleeding Kansas" violence
  • 1856: Preston Brooks beats Charles Sumner on Senate floor
  • 1857: Dred Scott decision
  • 1859: John Brown's raid
  • 1860: Democratic Party splits; Lincoln wins

Election of 1860: The Breaking Point

  • Four-way race fractures national politics
  • Lincoln wins without Southern electoral votes
  • Southern states see permanent minority status
  • Secession begins within weeks

📖 The Candidates

  • Lincoln (Republican): 40% popular vote, electoral victory in North
  • Breckinridge (Southern Democrat): Pro-slavery
  • Douglas (Northern Democrat): Popular sovereignty
  • Bell (Constitutional Union): Avoided slavery issue

Southerners saw Lincoln's victory as their permanent exclusion from power.

Secession Winter 1860–61

December 20, 1860 South Carolina secedes first
Jan–Feb 1861 Six more states follow (MS, FL, AL, GA, LA, TX)
February 1861 Confederate States of America formed
April 12, 1861 Fort Sumter fired upon; war begins
April–May 1861 Upper South joins (VA, AR, TN, NC)

📖 Two Waves of Secession

Lower South: Left quickly and decisively. Upper South: Only joined after Fort Sumter and Lincoln's call for troops. Border States: Maryland, Delaware, Kentucky, Missouri stayed in Union—critical to Union victory.

SECTION II

Early War: Overconfidence & Reality

First Bull Run (Manassas)

July 21, 1861

  • Americans expect a short war
  • Confederate victory shocks the North
  • Demonstrates inexperience on both sides
  • War becomes a long struggle
"On to Richmond!" Reality: Union army fled in panic back to Washington

📚 What Happened

First major battle fought near Manassas, Virginia. Civilians came with picnic baskets. Union initially pushed back Confederates, but Confederate railroad reinforcements turned the tide. Union retreat became a rout.

Consequences: North realized this would be a long war. South became overconfident.

Early Union Struggles

  • McClellan's caution slows momentum
  • Poor coordination between armies
  • Limited goals: restore Union, not end slavery
  • Confederate morale and leadership advantage

📖 Union Problems 1861-1862

McClellan organized well but wouldn't fight. Political generals appointed for connections. No unified command. Limited war aims confused goals. Early defeats questioned whether war was worth the cost.

Early Confederate Advantages

  • Strong officer corps and early unity
  • Defensive strategy fits Southern terrain
  • High early enlistment and confidence
  • Cotton diplomacy hopes for European aid

📖 Confederate Strengths

Best U.S. Army officers chose Confederacy. Defensive advantage—didn't need to conquer, just outlast. Interior lines, defending home territory. Early motivation high. But these couldn't overcome long-term Union strengths.

SECTION III

Turning the Tide (1862–63)

Antietam: Bloodiest Day

September 17, 1862

  • Lee invades the North
  • Tactical stalemate but strategic Union victory
  • Lincoln gains opportunity for Emancipation
  • Ends Confederate hopes for foreign recognition
23,000 casualties in a single day—the bloodiest day in American military history

📚 Why It Mattered

Lee invaded Maryland hoping to win on Northern soil. McClellan had Lee's battle plans but barely won. Savage combat at Cornfield, Bloody Lane, Burnside Bridge. Lee retreated. Gave Lincoln victory needed for Emancipation Proclamation. Once war became about slavery, European intervention became impossible.

Emancipation Proclamation

January 1, 1863

  • Reframes the war around slavery
  • Undermines Confederate labor system
  • Prevents European intervention
  • Opens enlistment to Black soldiers

📖 What It Did

Only applied to areas in rebellion, but it transformed the war. Made emancipation a Union war aim. Authorized 180,000 Black soldiers. Made European intervention impossible. Encouraged enslaved people to flee to Union lines.

Eric Foner: "Lincoln's most revolutionary act."

Black Military Service

  • 180,000 Black soldiers enlist by war's end
  • Provides crucial manpower
  • Symbolic demonstration of citizenship
  • Strengthens moral purpose of the war

📚 Impact

180,000 Black soldiers (10% of Union army), 19,000 in Navy (25%). Fought in 449 battles. 40,000 died. 54th Massachusetts proved Black soldiers' courage at Fort Wagner. Frederick Douglass: "Once let the black man get...an eagle on his button, and a musket on his shoulder...there is no power on earth that can deny that he has earned the right to citizenship."

Gettysburg & Vicksburg (1863)

Twin Turning Points - July 1863

Gettysburg (July 1-3)

  • Lee's second invasion halted
  • 51,000 casualties
  • Pickett's Charge fails
  • Lee never recovers strength

Vicksburg (July 4)

  • Union controls Mississippi River
  • Splits Confederacy in two
  • Grant emerges as top general
  • Opens Deep South to invasion

📚 The Turning Point

Gettysburg: Three days of savage fighting. Pickett's Charge catastrophic. 51,000 casualties total. Lee's offensive capacity ended. Vicksburg: Grant's brilliant campaign gave Union control of Mississippi River. Confederacy cut in two. After July 1863, question changed from "Can Union win?" to "Will Union keep fighting?"

SECTION IV

Total War & The Home Front

War Becomes a National Effort

  • Drafts and mass mobilization
  • New taxes and national banking system
  • Railroads and industry power the Union
  • Enormous logistical demands

📖 The Scope

2.1 million Union soldiers, 750,000-1 million Confederate. Cost $6.7 billion. Required industrial-scale production. Union created income tax, national banks, transcontinental railroad, Homestead Act, land-grant colleges. Confederacy faced contradiction: seceded for states' rights but needed strong central government.

Northern Advantages Solidify

  • Superior industrial output
  • Larger population and immigrant manpower
  • Greater financial capacity
  • Effective naval blockade
By 1864, North producing war materials at levels South couldn't match. Union armies had food, ammunition, replacements. Confederate armies went hungry.

📖 The Numbers

Population: 22M North vs 9M South (including 3.5M enslaved). North manufactured 97% of firearms, 96% of railroad equipment. Union 22,000 miles of rail vs. Confederate 9,000. Naval blockade dropped Southern exports 95% by 1864.

Social Upheaval

  • Women enter nursing and wartime industries
  • Food shortages and inflation in the South
  • Enslaved people flee plantations
  • War reshapes daily life on both fronts

📖 Transformation

Women: Thousands served as nurses, worked in factories, managed farms/plantations. Richmond Bread Riot (1863). Enslaved people: Fled to Union lines, provided intelligence, worked as laborers. South: Inflation rose 9,000%. Soldiers deserted to help starving families.

War as Social Revolution

  • Collapse of slavery becomes inevitable
  • Union armies act as agents of emancipation
  • Southern social order destabilizes
  • New definitions of freedom emerge
The Civil War was the Second American Revolution—it destroyed an entire social system and forced the nation to rebuild on new foundations.

💭 Multiple Meanings of Freedom

For freedpeople: personal autonomy, family reunification, economic independence, political rights, education, dignity. What they didn't get: land redistribution, sustained federal protection, economic resources, long-term political power.

Eric Foner: "Reconstruction was an 'unfinished revolution.'"

SECTION V

Endgame

Union Strategy Under Grant

  • Coordinated offensives across all theaters
  • War of attrition strains Confederate resources
  • Relentless pressure on Lee's army
  • Goal: destroy capacity to resist

📖 Grant's Grand Strategy (1864)

Attack everywhere at once. Grant fought Lee through Wilderness, Spotsylvania, Cold Harbor—horrific casualties but never retreated. Petersburg siege (June 1864-April 1865). Sherman destroyed armies in Georgia. Sheridan destroyed Shenandoah Valley. "I propose to fight it out on this line."

Sherman's March

  • March to the Sea targets infrastructure
  • Breaks Confederate morale
  • Psychological warfare as strategy
  • Shows the shift to total war

📖 Total War

After capturing Atlanta (September 1864—secured Lincoln's re-election), Sherman marched 60,000 men to Savannah, cutting 60-mile path of destruction. Destroyed railroads, factories, crops, plantations. Then turned north through Carolinas. Sherman: "War is cruelty. The crueler it is, the sooner it will be over."

Collapse of the Confederacy

Winter 1864-65 Desertion rises as shortages worsen
April 2, 1865 Richmond falls; Davis flees
April 9, 1865 Lee surrenders at Appomattox
April-May 1865 Other Confederate armies surrender

📖 The End

By 1865, perhaps half of Confederate soldiers had deserted. Money worthless, food scarce. Grant broke Petersburg lines April 2. Lee fled west but was surrounded. April 9: Appomattox. Grant's generous terms: Soldiers paroled, officers kept sidearms, soldiers with horses kept them for planting. Why? Lincoln wanted reconciliation.

SECTION VI

Legacy

The Human Cost

  • ~750,000 deaths reshape the nation
  • Wounded and amputee population enormous
  • National cemeteries created
  • Trauma becomes part of public memory
More Americans died in the Civil War than in all other American wars combined until Vietnam. Nearly every family was touched by death.

📚 The Scale of Death

750,000 deaths = 2.5% of population (equivalent to 7-8 million today). Union: ~360,000. Confederate: ~290,000. Disease killed twice as many as battle. Drew Gilpin Faust examines how Americans struggled with mass death. Gettysburg Address tried to give meaning: "these honored dead shall not have died in vain."

A New Birth of Freedom

  • Slavery abolished; freedom's future contested
  • Federal power dramatically expanded
  • Black citizenship struggles begin immediately
  • Reconstruction emerges from wartime debates

📖 Reconstruction Amendments

13th (1865): Abolished slavery. 14th (1868): Defined citizenship, equal protection, due process—most important amendment. 15th (1870): Voting rights (but didn't prohibit literacy tests/poll taxes). War permanently changed federal-state relationship and expanded federal power over civil rights.

Eric Foner: "Reconstruction was an 'unfinished revolution.'"

Competing Memories

  • Union narrative vs. Lost Cause mythology
  • Battle over meaning begins immediately
  • Memory wars shape textbooks and monuments
  • Legacy still contested today
×

Lost Cause Mythology

Post-war interpretation created by white Southerners to justify Confederacy and maintain white supremacy.

Claims: War was about states' rights, not slavery. Slavery was benevolent. Confederate soldiers were heroes. South lost only due to numbers. Reconstruction was corrupt disaster.

Reality: Confederate leaders explicitly said they seceded to preserve slavery.

💭 Why Memory Matters

How we remember shapes how we think about race today. Lost Cause mythology (peak 1890s-1950s) became mainstream. Most Confederate monuments erected during Jim Crow (1890s-1920s) and Civil Rights era (1950s-60s) as statements against Black equality. David Blight: White reconciliation came at cost of Black equality.

Closing Question

Did the Union win the war but lose the peace?

The Confederacy was defeated militarily, but many of the ideas it fought for—white supremacy, racial hierarchy—survived and shaped the post-war South.

📚 The Central Paradox

Union won decisively—Confederacy destroyed, slavery abolished, federal authority established. But in many ways, white Southerners won the peace. By 1877: federal troops withdrawn. 1890s-1900s: Black Southerners disenfranchised. Jim Crow lasted until 1960s.

What Was Won: Slavery destroyed—revolutionary and permanent. Constitutional amendments created legal foundation for later civil rights.

The Long Struggle: Civil War was first phase. Reconstruction briefly gave Black political power, then Jim Crow, then Civil Rights Movement—ongoing struggle.
