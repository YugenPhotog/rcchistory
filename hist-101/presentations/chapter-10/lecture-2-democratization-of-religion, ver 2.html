The Second Great Awakening

From the 1790s, a series of intense religious revivals swept the western frontier, spreading nationwide by the 1850s, known as the Second Great Awakening.

Main Features:

Mass Revivals

Large, emotional gatherings where people experienced conversion

Democratization of Religion

Ordinary people claimed spiritual authority, not just ministers or elites

Rise of Evangelical Denominations

Methodists and Baptists surged ahead of older churches

Religious Innovation

Birth of new sects like the Mormons, Adventists, and Shakers

Providential Interpretation of History

Events understood as signs of God's plan

The Second Great Awakening (1790s–1850s) was a democratic, emotional movement. Methodists and Baptists led, new sects emerged, and a providential worldview shaped American identity. Engage students: 'Can you think of modern examples of mass gatherings that inspire personal change, like music festivals or protests?'

Charles G. Finney

The Great Revivalist

Most responsible for bringing the awakening to the northern middle class

Theological Perspective

Arminian – Emphasized human free will in salvation, appealing to middle-class values of personal agency

Main Contributions:

  • Personal conversion and revival meetings
  • Active role of the Holy Spirit
  • Christian perfectionism
  • Deep involvement in abolitionism
  • Vocal critic of Calvinist doctrines

"A revival is nothing else than a new beginning of obedience to God."

— Charles G. Finney
Charles Finney, a former lawyer, brought the Second Great Awakening to the northern middle class. His Arminian theology emphasized free will, resonating with middle-class values of self-improvement. He promoted perfectionism and linked revivals to reform, especially abolitionism. Engage: 'How does Finney’s idea of personal choice in faith mirror today’s self-help culture?'

Mass Revivals and Camp Meetings

Cane Ridge, Kentucky (1801)

Often cited as the most dramatic early revival

  • Reports of 20,000 people attending
  • Lasted for several days and nights
  • People traveled hundreds of miles to attend

Key Characteristics:

  • Itinerant preachers—especially Methodists and Baptists—took center stage
  • Emotional displays: people "jerking," rolling on the ground, ecstatic singing, shouting
  • Outdoor venues: Revivals were often outdoors because churches couldn't contain the crowds
  • Multi-denominational cooperation in evangelistic efforts

Itinerant Preachers and "Circuit Riders"

Methodist ministers, usually young men, traveled vast circuits on horseback, bringing religion to frontier communities.

Like modern influencers, they spread ideas widely with passion.

Their Appeal:

Simple messages: repentance, personal faith, conversion experience

Plain language: spoke with passion rather than polished theology

Accessible: reached ordinary people regardless of class or education

Cane Ridge (1801) was a landmark revival, with emotional displays and massive attendance. Circuit riders, young Methodist preachers, used simple, passionate language to reach frontier settlers, making faith accessible. Engage: 'Imagine traveling hundreds of miles for a revival—how does that compare to attending a concert today?'

Democratization of Faith

Breaking Down Authority

  • Decline of Clerical Authority – No need for formal education to preach or lead
  • Emphasized accessibility: anyone could testify, anyone could be converted
  • Preaching became emotional, direct, and plainspoken
  • Rejection of Calvinist Predestination – Emphasis on individual choice

Expanding Participation

Women

Emerged as exhorters, prayer leaders, and spiritual guides, gaining religious authority

African Americans

Established vibrant churches, like the AME (20,000 members by 1850), fostering community and moral reform

Lay Participation

Ordinary believers led worship, testimony, and church governance

Christianity became more participatory, uniting diverse groups in a shared moral mission that strengthened civic cohesion.

The SGA democratized religion, emphasizing emotion and free will. Women and African Americans gained religious authority, with AME churches fostering community. This paralleled Jacksonian democracy, uniting Americans in a moral mission. Engage: 'Discuss in pairs: How did AME churches build community, similar to modern social networks?'

Come-Outer Sects

Independent groups rejected established churches for a direct, personal relationship with God.

Key Characteristics

Religious Stance

  • Rejection of established churches
  • Personal conversion focus
  • Emotional worship

Social Impact

  • Social reform involvement
  • New denominations formed
  • Grassroots organizing

Implications:

  • Religion became grassroots and populist
  • Spread west and south, engaging diverse communities
  • Fueled social activism through faith
Come-outers formed independent groups for personal faith, often tied to reform movements like abolitionism. They made religion populist and accessible. Engage: 'What modern groups, like online communities, act like come-outers today?'

New Movements

The SGA sparked uniquely American religious movements:

Mormonism (1820s)

Founded by Joseph Smith in New York's "Burned-Over District." Emphasized new revelations and millennial hopes.

Shakers (1800s)

Founded by Mother Ann Lee. Practiced celibacy, equality, and ecstatic worship.

Adventists (1840s)

Emerged from William Miller’s prophecy of Christ’s return in 1844, leading to Seventh-day Adventists.

Mormonism, Shakers, and Adventists reflected the SGA’s innovation. Each created unique communities with lasting cultural impact. Engage: 'How do these sects’ utopian ideas compare to today’s intentional communities?'

The Providential Worldview

Providential Worldview Defined:

Americans saw history as God’s cosmic plan, with events reflecting divine purpose.

Examples in American Life:

Moral Challenges

Epidemics seen as calls for reform

National Progress

Economic growth viewed as divine blessing

Social Reform

Temperance, abolition seen as fulfilling God’s will

Americans felt they were actors in a divine story.

The providential worldview, embraced by diverse groups including African Americans, inspired reforms as acts of moral duty.

Americans viewed events as part of God’s plan, giving urgency to reforms like temperance and abolition. This unified diverse groups in a shared mission. Engage: 'How does this worldview compare to modern ideas of American exceptionalism?'

Respectability and Cultural Policing

Moral Gatekeeping

What Evangelicals Policed:

  • Dance halls and social dancing
  • Theater performances
  • Taverns and alcohol
  • Sunday activities
  • Immoral reading material

Methods of Control:

  • Sabbatarian laws
  • Anti-theater campaigns
  • Moral reform societies
  • Temperance movements
  • Community oversight

Evangelicals promoted temperance and Sabbath observance to foster self-discipline and community cohesion, values shared by African American congregations.

Evangelicals enforced moral standards to promote discipline and unity, adopted by diverse groups. Engage: 'Debate: Was this moral policing helpful, like modern wellness trends?'

  • Empowered Ordinary People

  • Birthed New Movements

  • Shaped Worldview
    Inspired Americans to see history as divine mission

The SGA transformed American culture, politics, and reform, uniting diverse groups in a shared moral vision.

The Northern Middle Class

Economic Transformation

  • Market Revolution created new towns and cities
  • Turned rural North into commercial farms
  • Enabled social mobility through entrepreneurship

Who Comprised the New Middle Class?

Merchants

Shopkeepers and traders linking rural and urban economies

Manufacturers

Artisans who scaled workshops into enterprises

Farmers

Grew crops for commercial sale

Defined by self-discipline, moral improvement, economic ambition

The Market Revolution created a middle class of merchants, manufacturers, and farmers, defined by evangelical values. Engage: 'How do these values echo in today’s gig economy?'

A New Middle Class

New England exported Yankee values of work ethic, reform, and self-improvement across the North.

Liberty and Equality Redefined

Liberty

Self-ownership and freedom to pursue opportunity

  • Economic opportunity
  • Independence from patrons
  • Self-made success

Equality

Opportunity, not outcome

  • Fair chance for all
  • Success by merit
  • Hierarchy by achievement

From Republican Motherhood to Cult of Domesticity

Republican Motherhood (1790s–1820s)

Raising virtuous sons for the republic

  • Moral influence via motherhood
  • Required women’s education
  • Tied to Revolutionary ideals

Separate Spheres

  • Men: market, politics
  • Women: home, religion
  • Family as moral refuge

Cult of Domesticity (1830s–1850s)

Four virtues:

  • Piety
  • Purity
  • Domesticity
  • Submission

Women as moral anchors amid market chaos.

Elevated women’s moral influence, fostering stable families and evangelical reforms.

Industrialization created separate spheres. The Cult of Domesticity made women moral anchors, supporting reforms. Engage: 'How do these ideals influence modern gender roles in media?'

Beyond the Cult of Domesticity

Class, Race, and Regional Realities

A Middle-Class Ideal

Reflected white, middle-class, northern women. Free Black and working-class women upheld moral leadership in their communities.

Working-Class Women

  • Worked in factories, mills, service
  • Faced long hours, low pay
  • Example: Lowell Mill Girls’ protests (1830s–1840s)

Maria W. Stewart

First woman to lecture publicly on politics (1832). Advocated Black education.

Sarah Mapps Douglass

Founded schools for Black children, active in abolition.

The Cult of Domesticity was a middle-class ideal. Working-class and free Black women, like Stewart and Douglass, adapted it for moral leadership. Engage: 'Role-play: How might a working-class or free Black woman adapt these ideals?'

Who It Included

  • Northern, educated women
  • Those with economic security

Who Adapted It

  • Working-class women
  • Free Black women
  • Immigrant women

Domesticity defined middle-class identity but was adapted by diverse groups.

Women across class and race used moral influence to strengthen communities and reforms.

Home as sacred space for worship and moral instruction

Women’s Authority

Bible Reading

Daily family devotions, often led by mothers

Cultural Influence

Domestic fiction like Godey’s Lady’s Book taught proper domesticity

Class Distinctions:

Middle-class ideals distinguished respectable women from those working outside the home.

The "Cult of True Womanhood"

Four Cardinal Virtues

Piety

Women as spiritual guardians of family faith and morality.

Purity

Sexual virtue as the foundation of respectability.

Submissiveness

Strength through deference to family authority.

Domesticity

Home as women’s domain for child-rearing and moral refuge.
