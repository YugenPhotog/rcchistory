Democratization of Religion

Toward an American Culture

HIST 101: American History to 1865

Welcome to Lecture 2. Today we examine the Second Great Awakening and its profound impact on American culture, including the emergence of the Northern middle class and the Cult of Domesticity. The lecture is divided into three parts: religious revival, social reform, and domestic ideals.

The Second Great Awakening

From the 1790s, a series of intense religious revivals took place on the western frontier, spreading throughout the country by the 1850s. This became known as the Second Great Awakening.

Main Features:

Mass Revivals

Large, emotional gatherings where people experienced conversion

Democratization of Religion

Ordinary people claimed spiritual authority, not just ministers or elites

Rise of Evangelical Denominations

Methodists and Baptists surged ahead of older churches

Religious Innovation

Birth of new sects like the Mormons, Adventists, and Shakers

Providential Interpretation of History

Events understood as signs of God's plan

The Second Great Awakening began in the 1790s and lasted until the 1850s. Unlike the First Great Awakening, this movement was more democratic and emotional. Key denominations were Methodists and Baptists. It produced entirely new religious movements and established a providential worldview that shaped American culture.

Charles G. Finney

The Great Revivalist

Most responsible for bringing the awakening to the northern middle class

Theological Perspective

Arminian – Emphasized human free will in salvation, rejecting strict Calvinist predestination

Main Contributions:

  • Personal conversion and revival meetings
  • Active role of the Holy Spirit
  • Christian perfectionism
  • Deep involvement in abolitionism
  • Vocal critic of Calvinist doctrines

"A revival is nothing else than a new beginning of obedience to God."

— Charles G. Finney

Charles Grandison Finney was the most influential revivalist, especially among the northern middle class. A former lawyer, he emphasized free will—anyone could choose salvation. He promoted perfectionism—living without sin. His revivals led directly to reform movements, especially abolitionism. His emphasis on human agency fit perfectly with northern middle-class values.

Arminianism

Jacobus Arminius began as a devoted Calvinist theologian, trained under Theodore Beza—the successor to John Calvin in Geneva. Yet over time, Arminius grew uneasy with the strict determinism he saw in Calvinist teaching, especially the idea of double predestination, which held that God had eternally chosen some people for salvation and others for damnation. Arminius believed this interpretation of divine sovereignty conflicted with God’s justice and love, and undermined genuine human responsibility. His goal was not to overthrow Calvinism but to reform it from within, preserving its respect for divine grace while restoring room for human choice and moral accountability.

After Arminius’s death in 1609, his followers formalized his ideas in a statement known as the Remonstrance of 1610, outlining five key points that challenged Calvinist orthodoxy. They argued for conditional election, resistible grace, and universal atonement—beliefs emphasizing that salvation was available to all who freely accepted it. This document directly responded to Calvinist theology, seeking to correct what the Arminians saw as an imbalance that made God appear arbitrary and humanity powerless. In short, Arminianism was a direct theological reaction to Calvinism’s emphasis on divine predestination.

The controversy came to a head at the Synod of Dort (1618–1619), where church leaders rejected Arminianism and codified what became known as the Five Points of Calvinism—summarized by the acronym TULIP. Ironically, these Calvinist points only took their definitive form in response to the Arminian challenge. Though condemned at Dort, Arminian thought survived and flourished, especially through John Wesley and the Methodist movement, shaping much of modern evangelical Christianity. In this way, Arminianism stands as both a protest and a legacy—an enduring effort to reconcile God’s sovereignty with human freedom.

Mass Revivals and Camp Meetings

Cane Ridge, Kentucky (1801)

Often cited as the most dramatic early revival

  • Reports of 20,000 people attending
  • Lasted for several days and nights
  • People traveled hundreds of miles to attend

Key Characteristics:

  • Itinerant preachers—especially Methodists and Baptists—took center stage
  • Emotional displays: people "jerking," rolling on the ground, ecstatic singing, shouting
  • Outdoor venues: Revivals were often outdoors because churches couldn't contain the crowds
  • Multi-denominational cooperation in evangelistic efforts

Itinerant Preachers and "Circuit Riders"

Methodist ministers, usually young men, traveled vast circuits on horseback, bringing religion to frontier communities.

Their Appeal:

Simple messages: repentance, personal faith, conversion experience

Plain language: spoke with passion rather than polished theology

Accessible: reached ordinary people regardless of class or education

Cane Ridge Revival of 1801 in Kentucky symbolizes frontier revivals' explosive nature. Methodist circuit riders were key—young, energetic preachers on horseback across vast territories. They used simple, emotional language rather than complex theology, making Christianity accessible to ordinary frontier settlers.

Democratization of Faith

Breaking Down Authority

  • Decline of Clerical Authority – No need for formal education or established institutional authority to preach or lead
  • Emphasized accessibility: anyone could testify, anyone could be converted
  • Preaching became emotional, direct, and plainspoken rather than intellectual
  • Rejection of Calvinist Predestination – Emphasis on individual choice and free will

Expanding Participation

Women

Emerged as exhorters, prayer leaders, and spiritual guides—gaining religious authority despite limited political rights

African Americans

Formed independent churches and religious communities, creating spaces for Black spiritual and social leadership

Expansion of Lay Participation

Ordinary believers took active roles in worship, testimony, and church governance

Christianity became more egalitarian, emotional, and participatory—shifting power from established clergy to ordinary believers

The awakening democratized religion by breaking down clerical authority, emphasizing emotion over intellect, and rejecting predestination for free will. This opened doors for women and African Americans to gain religious authority even when they lacked political rights. Religious democratization paralleled and reinforced America's political democratization in the Jacksonian era.

Come-Outer Sects

People dissatisfied with established churches left to form independent groups, rejecting traditional religious institutions for a more personal, direct relationship with God.

Key Characteristics

Religious Stance

  • Rejection of established churches
  • Emphasis on personal conversion
  • Emotional religious experience

Social Impact

  • Deep involvement in social reform
  • Formation of new denominations
  • Grassroots organizing

Implications:

  • Religion was no longer dominated by elites in New England
  • It spread west and south, transforming faith into a more grassroots, populist experience
  • Created pathways for social activism through religious conviction
"Come-outers" left established churches to form their own communities. They wanted more direct, personal relationships with God without institutional mediation. They often experimented with alternative social structures and had utopian visions. Many connected to broader reform movements like abolitionism and women's rights.

New Movements

The religious ferment produced entirely new movements, many uniquely American:

Mormonism (1820s)

Founded by Joseph Smith in upstate New York's "Burned-Over District." Claimed new revelations—Book of Mormon, visions of angel Moroni. Emphasized restoration of true Christianity and millennial expectation. Faced persecution and moved westward.

Shakers (Peak Growth 1800s)

Founded by Mother Ann Lee. Practiced celibacy and communal living. Emphasized equality of sexes and ecstatic worship with dancing. Known for exceptional craftsmanship, simple design, and utopian communities.

Adventists (1840s)

Emerged from William Miller's preaching about Christ's imminent return. Predicted Christ would return in 1844—"Great Disappointment" when He did not appear. Some followers reorganized as Seventh-day Adventists, emphasizing prophecy and health reform.

Three major new movements emerged: Mormonism from Joseph Smith in New York, claiming new revelations. Shakers led by Mother Ann Lee, practicing celibacy and gender equality. Adventists from William Miller's prophecies about Christ's return in 1844. Each reflected the era's religious innovation and experimentation.

The Providential Worldview

Providential Worldview Defined:

Americans interpreted daily events and history itself as part of God's cosmic drama. Suffering, disasters, political changes—all read as divine signs and messages.

Examples in American Life:

Disease and Disaster

Cholera epidemics seen as divine punishment or wake-up calls for moral reform

Territorial Expansion

Westward expansion interpreted as God's plan—seeds of "Manifest Destiny"

Social Reform

Reform movements (temperance, abolition, women's rights) seen as fulfilling divine purpose

Consequences:

Ordinary Americans believed they were actors in a cosmic story.

This gave urgency to social reform—if history is God's story, then changing society is a moral duty.

Americans saw all events as part of God's plan. Epidemics were divine punishment. Westward expansion was God's will—early seeds of Manifest Destiny. Reform movements were seen as fulfilling divine purpose. This worldview gave urgency to social reform and made Americans feel they were actors in a cosmic drama.

Respectability and Cultural Policing

Moral Gatekeeping

What Evangelicals Policed:

  • Dance halls and social dancing
  • Theater and dramatic performances
  • Taverns and alcohol consumption
  • Sunday activities (Sabbatarian laws)
  • Reading material deemed immoral

Methods of Control:

  • Sabbatarian laws restricting Sunday commerce
  • Anti-theater campaigns and boycotts
  • Moral reform societies
  • Temperance movements
  • Social pressure and community surveillance

Moral discipline became a marker of status and self-control—distinguishing the "respectable" middle class from the working class and immigrants.

Critical Question: Did moral reform liberate people or regulate them?

Evangelicals policed public morality—banning dancing, theater, taverns, Sunday activities. They used Sabbatarian laws, boycotts, reform societies, and social pressure. Moral discipline became a marker of middle-class status, distinguishing "respectable" people from workers and immigrants. This raises questions about whether reform liberated or regulated people.

Significance of the Second Great Awakening

Shattered Religious Complacency

Challenged established religious hierarchies and reinvigorated American Christianity with emotional vitality

Democratized Faith and Gave Authority to Ordinary People

Shifted spiritual power from educated clergy to common believers, making religion accessible to all classes

Birthed New Religious Movements

Created uniquely American denominations and sects that would shape national identity

Instilled Providential Worldview

Framed how Americans would interpret everything—from personal struggles to national destiny—as part of God's plan

The Second Great Awakening transformed not just American religion, but American culture, politics, and social reform movements for generations to come.

Summary of the awakening's significance: shattered religious complacency, democratized faith, birthed new movements, and instilled a providential worldview. This transformed American religion, culture, politics, and social reform for generations.

From Religious Revival to Social Reform

The Second Great Awakening didn't just change churches—it transformed American society

Religious Fervor Fueled Reform Movements

Temperance, abolitionism, women's rights, prison reform, education reform—all energized by evangelical Christianity

Revival Culture's Values

Key Connection:

The same democratizing impulse that empowered ordinary believers also empowered the new middle class to reshape American society. Religious authority became social authority.

The awakening sparked major reform movements: temperance, abolitionism, women's rights, prison reform, education. Revival culture emphasized self-improvement, moral discipline, and individual agency. These values became markers of the emerging Northern middle class. The democratizing impulse in religion carried over into social reform.

The Northern Middle Class,
Reform, and Domestic Ideals

Section transition. We now examine the Northern middle class, its connection to reform, and the emergence of domestic ideals.

The Northern Middle Class

Economic Transformation

  • Market Revolution created new towns and cities and transformed old ones
  • Turned the rural North into a landscape of family-owned commercial farms
  • Created opportunities for social mobility through entrepreneurship and trade

Who Comprised the New Middle Class?

City and Country Merchants

Shopkeepers, traders, and commercial entrepreneurs connecting rural and urban economies

Master Craftsmen Turned Manufacturers

Artisans who transformed small workshops into larger manufacturing enterprises

Market-Oriented Farmers

Agricultural producers growing crops for commercial sale rather than subsistence

A new class defined not just by wealth, but by values of self-discipline, moral improvement, and economic ambition

The Market Revolution created the Northern middle class. It included merchants, craftsmen-turned-manufacturers, and market-oriented farmers. This class was defined not just by wealth but by values: self-discipline, moral improvement, economic ambition. These values came directly from evangelical religion.

A New Middle Class

New England played a disproportionate role in the making of middle-class culture—exporting Yankee values of work ethic, moral reform, and self-improvement throughout the North.

Liberty and Equality Redefined

Liberty

For the Yankee middle class, liberty meant self-ownership and the freedom of action and ambition.

  • Freedom to pursue economic opportunity
  • Independence from masters or patrons
  • Self-made success through hard work

Equality

Equality meant equality of opportunity, not outcome.

  • Everyone should have a fair chance
  • Success depends on individual merit
  • Hierarchy based on achievement, not birth

This redefinition would become fundamental to American capitalism and individualism—but also created tensions with ideas of communal obligation and structural inequality.

New England exported Yankee values throughout the North. The middle class redefined liberty and equality: liberty meant economic opportunity, equality meant equal chance to succeed. Success became a moral question—wealth demonstrated virtue, poverty suggested moral failure. This was a major shift from Revolutionary-era definitions.

From Republican Motherhood to Cult of Domesticity

Republican Motherhood (1790s–1820s)

Women's civic duty centered on raising virtuous, educated sons to sustain the republic

  • Political role expressed through motherhood
  • Emphasized women's moral influence
  • Required women's education
  • Tied to Revolutionary ideals

The Rise of Separate Spheres

Industrialization and the Market Revolution divided public (male) and private (female) realms

  • Men: market, politics, wage labor in public sphere
  • Women: home, religion, moral nurture in private sphere
  • Family became a moral refuge in an industrializing world

Cult of Domesticity (1830s–1850s)

Ideal of womanhood defined by four cardinal virtues:

  • Piety
  • Purity
  • Domesticity
  • Submission

Women positioned as moral anchors amid market chaos—guardians of virtue, not participants in politics or commerce.

Contradiction:

Domestic ideology limited women's public roles yet created space for activism in education, abolition, and reform movements—women claimed moral authority extended beyond the home.

Industrialization created separate spheres: men in public (market, politics), women in private (home, religion). The Cult of Domesticity defined ideal womanhood: piety, purity, domesticity, submission. Women were moral anchors but excluded from politics and commerce. Paradoxically, this ideology both limited and empowered women—it created space for activism in reform movements.

Beyond the Cult of Domesticity

Class, Race, and Regional Realities

A Middle-Class Ideal

The Cult of Domesticity reflected the experiences of white, middle-class, northern women. It required leisure, literacy, and financial stability—privileges of class. It reinforced racial and economic boundaries under the guise of morality.

Working-Class Women

  • Entered factories, mills, and domestic service as wage earners
  • Faced long hours (12-14 hour days), low pay, and little autonomy
  • Could not afford to stay home as "moral guardians"
  • Example: Lowell Mill Girls organized early labor protests (1830s–1840s), demanding better conditions
The Cult of Domesticity was a middle-class ideal requiring leisure and financial stability. Working-class women worked in factories, mills, and domestic service with long hours and low pay. They couldn't afford to stay home. The Lowell Mill Girls organized early labor protests, showing resistance to exploitation.

Free Black Women

Forging Identity Outside the Cult of Domesticity

Free Black women lived outside the "separate spheres" ideal yet forged their own definitions of respectability and leadership, combining economic necessity with moral authority.

Their Reality:

  • In northern cities, combined wage work and home-based labor (laundry, sewing, boarding houses) to sustain families
  • Could not afford the luxury of domesticity—economic survival required public work
  • Excluded from white women's reform societies due to racism

Their Leadership:

Institution Building

Founded schools, mutual aid societies, and churches such as the AME (African Methodist Episcopal) movement

Activism and Advocacy

Advocated for education, racial uplift, and abolition. Created intellectual and moral communities that paralleled white middle-class activism while confronting racism and exclusion

Maria W. Stewart

First American woman to lecture publicly on politics and women's rights (1832). Called for Black education and self-determination.

Sarah Mapps Douglass

Founded schools for Black children in Philadelphia. Active in abolitionist and women's rights movements.

Free Black women lived outside separate spheres, combining wage work with home-based labor. They founded schools, mutual aid societies, and churches like the AME. They advocated for education, racial uplift, and abolition. Maria W. Stewart was the first American woman to lecture publicly on politics (1832). Sarah Mapps Douglass founded schools for Black children.

Enslaved Women

Total Exclusion from Domestic Ideals

Enslaved women lived in complete opposition to the cult of domesticity—their labor, bodies, and families were controlled by others. No access to piety, purity, domesticity, or submission as sources of power.

Their Reality:

Labor Exploitation

Performed brutal field labor alongside men—picking cotton, cultivating tobacco, harvesting rice. Also worked as domestic servants in slaveholders' homes.

Denial of Motherhood

Children could be sold away at any time. No legal rights over their own children or families. Motherhood was not protected or sentimentalized.

Sexual Exploitation

Faced sexual violence from white enslavers with no legal recourse. The ideal of "purity" was weaponized against them—denied protection while blamed for their own exploitation.

No Domestic Sphere

Could not create a "haven" or "moral refuge"—their labor belonged to their enslavers, not their own families.

The cult of domesticity assumed freedom, autonomy, and legal personhood—none of which enslaved women possessed.

Enslaved women lived in complete opposition to domesticity. They performed brutal field labor and domestic service. Their children could be sold away. They faced sexual violence with no protection. They could not create their own domestic sphere. The cult of domesticity assumed freedom and legal personhood that enslaved women did not possess.

The Cult of Domesticity: Conclusion

Who It Included

  • White middle-class women
  • Northern, educated women
  • Those with economic security
  • Women with leisure time

Who It Excluded

  • Working-class women
  • Immigrant women
  • Free Black women
  • Enslaved women

The language of virtue and woman's sphere defined class identity but excluded most women from its privileges.

What appeared as universal ideals of womanhood were actually markers of race, class, and regional privilege—used to distinguish "respectable" women from those deemed outside respectability's boundaries.

Summary: domesticity included white middle-class northern women with economic security. It excluded working-class, immigrant, free Black, and enslaved women. The language of virtue was actually about class identity and racial privilege, not universal womanhood.

Domesticity and the Moral Household

The Home as "Moral Refuge"

The Home as Miniature Church

Domestic space became sacred space—site of daily worship, moral instruction, and spiritual formation

Women's Spiritual Authority

Led prayer circles, organized Sunday schools, taught children scripture and hymns

Parlor Bible Reading

Family devotions became daily rituals, with mother often leading

Cultural Influence

Domestic fiction and publications like Godey's Lady's Book extended moral influence, teaching proper domesticity

Gender and Class Distinctions:

Middle-class domestic ideals distinguished "respectable" women from working-class and immigrant women, who were often portrayed as morally suspect for working outside the home.

The home became a moral refuge and miniature church. Women gained spiritual authority by leading prayers, organizing Sunday schools, and conducting family devotions. Domestic fiction like Godey's Lady's Book taught proper domesticity. These ideals distinguished "respectable" middle-class women from working-class and immigrant women.

The "Cult of True Womanhood"

Four Cardinal Virtues

Piety

Women viewed as inherently more religious and moral than men. Spiritual guardians of the family who maintained household faith and morality.

Purity

Sexual virtue essential for women's moral authority. Purity was a reflection of character and the foundation of respectability. Loss of purity meant social ruin.

Submissiveness

A "true woman" was gentle, submissive, and acquiescent. Expected to defer to father and husband in all matters—strength shown through yielding.

Domesticity

Women's rightful domain was the home. Excellence in household management, child-rearing, and creating a moral refuge from the corrupt public world.

Note: Primarily a white middle-class ideal that did not reflect the lived reality of most American women.

Critical Question: How did this ideal both empower and restrict women?

It gave women moral authority and a sphere of influence, but it also confined them to the home and justified their exclusion from politics, higher education, and economic independence.

The four cardinal virtues: Piety (spiritual guardians), Purity (sexual virtue), Submissiveness (deference), Domesticity (home as domain). This was a white middle-class ideal that didn't reflect most women's reality. Critical question: it both empowered (moral authority) and restricted (confined to home, excluded from politics and economic independence).

Summary

The Second Great Awakening democratized American religion, empowering ordinary people with spiritual authority and fueling social reform movements. This religious transformation reshaped American culture, giving rise to the Northern middle class with its values of self-discipline, moral improvement, and economic ambition. Yet these ideals—including the Cult of Domesticity—primarily served white, middle-class interests, excluding working-class, immigrant, and Black women from respectability's privileges while using moral language to justify social hierarchies.

Conclusion: The Second Great Awakening democratized religion and fueled reform. It gave rise to the Northern middle class with values of self-discipline and moral improvement. But these ideals, including domesticity, were exclusionary—serving white middle-class interests while excluding working-class, immigrant, and Black women. Moral language justified social hierarchies.
