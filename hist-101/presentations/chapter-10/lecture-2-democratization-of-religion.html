HIST 101: American History to 1865

Welcome to Lecture 2. Today we examine the Second Great Awakening and its profound impact on American culture, including the emergence of the Northern middle class and the Cult of Domesticity. The lecture is divided into three parts: religious revival, social reform, and domestic ideals.

The Second Great Awakening

From the 1790s, a series of intense religious revivals took place on the western frontier, spreading throughout the country by the 1850s. This became known as the Second Great Awakening.

Main Features:

Mass Revivals and Camp Meetings

Large, emotional gatherings where people experienced conversion

Democratization of Religion

Ordinary people claimed spiritual authority, not just ministers or elites

Rise of Evangelical Denominations

Methodists and Baptists surged ahead of older churches

Religious Innovation

Birth of new sects like the Mormons, Adventists, and Shakers

Providential Interpretation of History

Events understood as signs of God's plan

The Second Great Awakening represents a pivotal transformation in American religious culture, moving from elite-controlled, rationalist religion toward democratic, emotionally accessible faith. Background Context (Religion in the Early Republic, 1790s): - Church membership was declining post-Revolution - Deism and rationalism were popular among elites - Traditional clergy were losing influence - Westward expansion was creating new, unchurched communities - Fear of moral decay and irreligion - Concern about social disorder on the frontier - Desire to preserve Christian identity in new nation - Growing tension between elite theology and popular piety This anxiety about religious decline and social disorder created fertile ground for revival. Wood emphasizes republican virtue concerns, while Johnson highlights market anxieties and the need for moral order. Camp meetings democratized religion by bringing it out of elite-controlled churches into open fields where ordinary people could participate. Itinerant preachers bypassed traditional authority structures. Emotional worship validated popular religious experience. Arminian theology empowered individuals. Charles Grandison Finney: The Master Revivalist

Background

  • Lawyer turned revivalist preacher
  • Active 1820s–1830s, especially in upstate New York
  • Most influential revivalist of the era

Finney's Innovations

  • "New Measures": Emotionally manipulative techniques to induce conversion
  • Anxious bench: Public seating for those seeking salvation
  • Protracted meetings: Multi-day revival campaigns in cities
  • Women's prayer groups: Encouraged female participation
"You are not helpless sinners waiting for God's inscrutable decree. You can choose salvation today!"
Finney professionalized revival techniques and brought them to urban areas. He emphasized that conversion could be engineered through proper methods—very American, very practical. Wood sees Finney as creating republican moral citizens. Johnson views him as disciplining labor for capitalism. Theological Context - Arminianism and Free Will: Traditional Calvinism taught predestination: God predestines who will be saved (the "elect"), humans cannot earn or choose salvation, grace is irresistible and limited, and religious authority rests with trained clergy who interpret God's will. Arminianism (Free Will) offered a democratic alternative: Universal grace—salvation available to all who choose it; Human agency—individuals can accept or reject grace; Optimistic anthropology—humans capable of moral improvement; Democratic implications—everyone has equal access to God's grace. Revival preachers commonly said: "You are not helpless sinners waiting for God's inscrutable decree. You can choose salvation today!" Arminianism democratized salvation by making it a choice rather than predetermined fate. This empowered ordinary people and validated individual religious experience. Wood sees this as republican theology. Johnson views it as creating responsible moral actors for the market.

Mass Revivals and Camp Meetings

Cane Ridge, Kentucky (1801)

Often cited as the most dramatic early revival

  • Reports of 20,000 people attending
  • Lasted for several days and nights
  • People traveled hundreds of miles to attend

Key Characteristics:

  • Itinerant preachers—especially Methodists and Baptists—took center stage
  • Emotional displays: people "jerking," rolling on the ground, ecstatic singing, shouting
  • Outdoor venues: Revivals were often outdoors because churches couldn't contain the crowds
  • Multi-denominational cooperation in evangelistic efforts

Itinerant Preachers and "Circuit Riders"

Methodist ministers, usually young men, traveled vast circuits on horseback, bringing religion to frontier communities.

Their Appeal:

Simple messages: repentance, personal faith, conversion experience

Plain language: spoke with passion rather than polished theology

Accessible: reached ordinary people regardless of class or education

Cane Ridge Revival of 1801 in Kentucky symbolizes frontier revivals' explosive nature. Methodist circuit riders were key—young, energetic preachers on horseback across vast territories. They used simple, emotional language rather than complex theology, making Christianity accessible to ordinary frontier settlers.

Part II: Democratization of Religion

Democratization of Religion

Definition: The process by which religious authority, practice, and interpretation shifted from elites to ordinary people

Breaking Down Authority

  • Decline of Educated Clergy: No need for formal education or institutional authority to preach
  • Emotional Over Intellectual: Preaching became direct, plainspoken, and passionate rather than theological
  • Rejection of Predestination: Free will and individual choice replaced Calvinist determinism
  • Voluntary Association: People "shopped" for churches; congregations voted on leadership
  • Rise of Lay Preachers: Circuit riders were often poor, itinerant, self-taught—authenticity over credentials
  • Personal Experience Valued: "Heart religion" trumped "head religion"—ordinary people could judge their own salvation
  • Accessible to All: Anyone could testify, anyone could be converted

Expanding Participation

Women

Emerged as exhorters, prayer leaders, and spiritual guides—gaining religious authority despite limited political rights

African Americans

Established vibrant churches, such as the AME, which grew to 20,000 members by 1850, expanding the SGA's influence and supporting education and moral reform

Lay Participation

Ordinary believers took active roles in worship, testimony, and church governance—religion became truly participatory

Result: Christianity became more participatory and emotional—broadening religious engagement to foster civic and moral cohesion among diverse groups, including women and African Americans

How the Second Great Awakening Democratized Religion

Hey there! The Second Great Awakening (1790–1840) was a game-changer that made religion open to everyone, not just the elite. It shifted power away from stuffy, educated clergy and put it in the hands of regular folks. Let’s dig into how it did this and what historians Gordon Wood and Paul Johnson say about it, keeping it clear for a first-year college student.

  • Breaking Down Clerical Power: Before, ministers like Congregationalists needed fancy degrees, but the Awakening said passion mattered more. Methodists and Baptists let people with little training lead if they had a strong faith story. This meant anyone could preach, making religion less exclusive.
  • Emotion Over Intellect: At big, lively camp meetings, preachers got crowds fired up, focusing on heartfelt belief over book smarts. This made faith feel personal and reachable for everyone, not just scholars.
  • Free Will for All: The movement rejected the old idea that God pre-picked who’s saved (predestination). Instead, it said you could choose salvation, empowering regular people to take charge of their faith.
  • Including Everyone: Women and African Americans, often shut out of politics, could lead in religion. Women ran prayer groups, and African Americans started churches like the African Methodist Episcopal (AME), giving them a voice.

What Historians Say

  • Gordon Wood’s View: Wood sees this as religion becoming more equal, like America’s growing democratic spirit. By letting ordinary people, including women and African Americans, lead, it created a sense of “republican virtue”—everyone working together as equals in faith, building a united national community.
  • Paul Johnson’s View: Johnson says the Awakening taught people moral discipline through their faith choices. This helped them fit into America’s new market economy, where self-control was key. He sees women and African Americans as part of this “moral economy,” actively shaping society, not just sitting on the sidelines.

In short, the Second Great Awakening made religion a space where anyone could lead and belong, boosting equality and discipline in ways that shaped America. Cool, right? If you want more, just ask!

Women and Religious Democratization

Women's Majority in Churches

  • By 1830s, women were 60–70% of church members
  • More likely to convert and attend regularly than men
  • Dominated prayer meetings and benevolent societies
  • Black women: Central to independent Black churches (AME, AME Zion, Baptist)

Why Women?

  • Excluded from formal political participation
  • Religion offered authority and voice
  • Churches provided community and social networks
  • Moral reform aligned with women's domestic roles
  • For Black women: Churches served as centers for mutual aid, education, and resistance

Women's Religious Authority

  • Exhorters: Women who spoke at revival meetings
  • Prayer leaders: Led prayer circles and maternal associations
  • Reformers: Founded and led benevolent societies
  • Missionaries: Single women as foreign missionaries
  • Writers: Published religious literature and hymns
  • Black women leaders: Teachers, mutual aid organizers, church founders (e.g., Jarena Lee, preacher in AME Church)

Limitations: While women gained religious authority, they were still barred from formal ordination (with rare exceptions like Phoebe Palmer). Their authority was real but constrained by gender norms. Black women faced additional barriers of racism but created powerful networks through churches and reform societies.

Women's religious participation was a form of democratization, giving them voice and authority denied in politics. Yet it also reinforced separate spheres ideology. Wood emphasizes women's republican virtue. Johnson shows how moral reform disciplined families for market economy. Black women were particularly active in independent Black churches, where they formed the majority of congregations. They organized mutual aid societies, taught in church schools, and led prayer groups. While facing both racism and sexism, Black women like Jarena Lee became powerful preachers and exhorters. Their religious participation was crucial to building and sustaining Black community institutions.   • Black preachers as community leaders
  • Churches as political, social, and economic hubs
  • Religion tied to resistance and liberation

The "Burned-Over District"

Location & Term

  • Region: Upstate New York (especially along Erie Canal)
  • Term origin: "Burned over" by so many revivals that spiritual fuel was exhausted
  • Period: 1820s–1840s

Why This Region?

  • Rapid settlement and economic growth along Erie Canal
  • Social disruption from market revolution
  • Mix of New England migrants and diverse settlers
  • Anxiety about social change and moral order

Religious Movements and Reforms Born Here

  • Mormonism (Joseph Smith, 1830)
  • Seventh-Day Adventism (William Miller's millennialism)
  • Spiritualism (Fox sisters)
  • Oneida Community (John Humphrey Noyes)
  • Temperance movement (strongest here)
  • Women's rights activism (Seneca Falls, 1848)

Significance: The Burned-Over District shows how religious enthusiasm could produce both mainstream reform and radical experimentation. It was a laboratory for democratic religion and social change.

🚀 American Originals: New Religions of the SGA

What’s Happening on this Slide?

The **Second Great Awakening** didn't just boost old churches like the Methodists and Baptists; it was a fertile ground for entirely new faiths, often called **"sects."** This slide highlights three of the most unique and enduring new American-born religions, all showing the era's spirit of **innovation and democratization**:

  • Mormonism: A major new faith founded by Joseph Smith in the "Burned-Over District," based on new scriptural revelation (**The Book of Mormon**) and a belief in the restoration of the "true" Christian Church.
  • Shakers: A radical utopian group founded by Mother Ann Lee. They practiced communal living, absolute **celibacy**, and an ecstatic, active style of worship (hence the name). They are also famous for their dedication to **gender equality** within the community.
  • Adventists: These groups emerged from the teachings of William Miller, who became very popular for predicting the literal **Second Coming of Christ** in 1844. When it didn't happen, the event (known as the **Great Disappointment**) led followers to reorganize into enduring denominations like the Seventh-day Adventists.

🧐 Historians Weigh In (Wood vs. Johnson)

How do these completely new, American-made faiths fit into the big historical picture?

  • Gordon Wood (Republican Experimentation): Wood views these new movements as evidence of **extreme democracy** at work. The American Revolution unleashed a spirit of **individual liberty** so powerful that people felt free to reject *all* established authorities—religious, social, and political. Sects like Mormonism and the Shakers were **republican experiments** where people tried to build a **perfect, virtuous community** from scratch, a reflection of the revolutionary promise.
  • Paul Johnson (Market Anxieties and Moral Order): Johnson focuses on the **social instability** caused by the new **Market Revolution**. These new sects often arose in response to the chaos and competition of the emerging capitalist economy. Groups like the Shakers and early Mormon communities offered a **strict, communal, and highly disciplined alternative** to the individualistic, greedy world outside. They were attempts to create a moral order that the marketplace seemed to destroy.

Self-Study Prompt: Why would **celibacy** (Shakers) or the expectation of the **End of the World** (Adventists) be popular during a time of rapid economic growth and high optimism? Think about the anxiety that change brings!

🚪 "Come-Outers": Leaving the Old Church Behind

What’s Happening on this Slide?

The **Second Great Awakening** gave ordinary people the confidence to believe they didn't need educated clergy or fancy church buildings to find God. The "Come-Outers" were people who acted on this feeling—they **literally left** the established Congregational and Presbyterian churches to form their own, smaller, more intense groups. They were seeking a **direct, personal, and emotional** relationship with the divine, free from the mediation of traditional institutions.

Key Takeaways:

  • **They democratized authority:** If everyone has access to God's grace (Arminianism), then anyone can lead and interpret scripture, not just the college-educated elite.
  • **They led to reform:** Since they saw themselves as creating a purer, more perfect Christianity, they often experimented with radical social ideas, connecting their faith directly to movements like abolition and women's rights.
  • **The Result:** Religion became a **grassroots, populist experience**, shifting power away from elites in New England toward common believers across the West and South.

🧐 Historians Weigh In (Wood vs. Johnson)

This willingness to reject authority and form new communities is key to understanding the era:

  • Gordon Wood (Republican Independence): Wood sees the "Come-Outers" as the ultimate expression of the **democratic and republican spirit** of the age. They represent individuals claiming their **independence** from institutional power—whether political or religious. They were building a new, virtuous society one small, self-governing religious community at a time, fulfilling the revolutionary promise of self-determination.
  • Paul Johnson (Moral Economy of Discipline): Johnson notes that while they rejected *old* authority, these sects often established a very **strict *new* moral authority** among their members. By forming disciplined, close-knit communities, they created a **moral and social stability** that helped their members survive the chaos of the **Market Revolution**. Their utopian visions were often about controlling the temptations and disruptions of the new capitalist world.

New Movements

The religious ferment produced entirely new movements, many uniquely American:

Mormonism (1820s)

Founded by Joseph Smith in upstate New York's "Burned-Over District." Claimed new revelations—Book of Mormon, visions of angel Moroni. Emphasized restoration of true Christianity and millennial expectation. Faced persecution and moved westward.

Shakers (Peak Growth 1800s)

Founded by Mother Ann Lee. Practiced celibacy and communal living. Emphasized equality of sexes and ecstatic worship with dancing. Known for exceptional craftsmanship, simple design, and utopian communities.

Adventists (1840s)

Emerged from William Miller's preaching about Christ's imminent return. Predicted Christ would return in 1844—"Great Disappointment" when He did not appear. Some followers reorganized as Seventh-day Adventists, emphasizing prophecy and health reform.

Three major new movements emerged: Mormonism from Joseph Smith in New York, claiming new revelations. Shakers led by Mother Ann Lee, practicing celibacy and gender equality. Adventists from William Miller's prophecies about Christ's return in 1844. Each reflected the era's religious innovation and experimentation.

The Providential Worldview

Providential Worldview Defined:

Americans interpreted daily events and history itself as part of God's cosmic drama. Suffering, disasters, political changes—all read as divine signs and messages.

Examples in American Life:

Disease and Disaster

Cholera epidemics seen as divine punishment or wake-up calls for moral reform

Territorial Expansion

Westward expansion interpreted as God's plan—seeds of "Manifest Destiny"

Social Reform

Reform movements (temperance, abolition, women's rights) seen as fulfilling divine purpose

Consequences:

Ordinary Americans believed they were actors in a cosmic story.

This gave urgency to social reform—if history is God's story, then changing society is a moral duty.

📜 The Providential Worldview: God's Plan for America

What’s Happening on this Slide?

The Providential Worldview is the belief that God intervenes directly in human affairs and history. After the Second Great Awakening energized American faith, this worldview became extremely common. For 19th-century Americans, nothing was an accident; everything—from a cholera epidemic to a major political victory—was a sign from God.

Key Examples:

  • Disasters as Messages: If a town was hit by a disease like cholera, it wasn't just bad luck; it was divine punishment for a specific moral failing (like drunkenness or Sabbath-breaking). This belief drove the Temperance Movement.
  • National Destiny: Westward expansion was quickly interpreted as God's will for the nation, laying the spiritual groundwork for the concept of Manifest Destiny (the belief that the U.S. was destined to expand across the continent).
  • Urgency of Reform: If history is a "cosmic drama," then Americans were actors in it, compelled to clean up society (abolish slavery, end drinking) to prepare the world for Christ's return.

🧐 Historians Weigh In (Wood vs. Johnson)

How does this religious lens affect how Americans behave?

  • Gordon Wood (Republican Morality): Wood sees the Providential Worldview as empowering the new, virtuous republican citizen. Since God was watching and judging the nation, individual citizens had a sacred duty to uphold moral standards. This worldview fueled the Benevolent Empire (the network of reform societies) as a way for citizens to prove their virtue and keep the Republic on God's good side.
  • Paul Johnson (Moral Discipline and the Market): Johnson argues this belief system was crucial for instilling the self-control needed for a modern capitalist society. If every personal action (like drinking or debt) could bring divine judgment (like disease or financial ruin), it was a powerful religious incentive to live a disciplined life, making individuals more reliable as workers, consumers, and debtors in the chaotic new Market Economy.

🤝 From Personal Salvation to Public Action

What’s Happening on this Slide?

This slide is about the vital link between the religious passion of the **Second Great Awakening** and the explosion of reform in the North. It wasn't enough for individuals to be saved; the goal became saving **society** itself!

The Chain Reaction:

  • Theological Drive: The belief in **Free Will** meant that individuals could choose righteousness. Reformers extended this idea: if individuals could choose good, society could eliminate sin (like slavery or drunkenness). This concept is known as **Perfectionism**.
  • Moral Values: The revival culture emphasized **self-improvement**, **moral discipline**, and the importance of **individual agency**. These values—sobriety, hard work, personal responsibility—became the cultural bedrock of the emerging **Northern middle class**.
  • Reform Movements: The SGA provided the spiritual energy, the moral justification, and the organizational networks (like women's prayer groups) that fueled all the major reform movements of the antebellum period, including **temperance** and **abolition**.

🧐 Historians Weigh In (Wood vs. Johnson)

Both historians agree that religion fueled reform, but they emphasize different outcomes:

  • Gordon Wood (Republican Virtue): Wood sees these reforms (especially abolition and temperance) as attempts to secure a truly **virtuous republic**. The goal was to eliminate moral flaws that threatened the nation, uniting diverse citizens—including African American leaders like Maria W. Stewart—around shared, non-partisan, **republican morality** and civic responsibility.
  • Paul Johnson (Moral Economy of Discipline): Johnson connects these reforms directly to the rise of the **Market Revolution**. Reforms like temperance enforced the **moral discipline**—sobriety, punctuality, and self-control—that were required for a stable workforce and a successful capitalist economy. The goal was to create disciplined, reliable actors who could thrive in the new, competitive marketplace.

📚 Understanding the Big Picture

Think of this slide as the "why should I care?" moment of the lecture. The Second Great Awakening wasn't just another religious event that happened and then disappeared—it fundamentally rewired how Americans thought about themselves, their country, and their place in the world. Let's break down each of these four major impacts.

🔨 Shattered Religious Complacency

Before the SGA, religion in America was getting a bit stale. The old Congregationalist and Episcopalian churches that dominated the colonial period were formal, controlled by educated ministers, and honestly, not super exciting to most people. The SGA came along like a spiritual earthquake—it challenged these established churches and brought raw emotion, personal connection, and enthusiasm back into American Christianity. Picture the difference between a quiet, formal church service where everyone sits still versus a camp meeting where people are crying, singing, and experiencing powerful emotional conversions. That's the shift.

🗳️ Democratized Faith and Gave Authority to Ordinary People

This is arguably the most important point. Before the SGA, you needed formal theological training to be a minister. You needed to know Latin, Greek, maybe Hebrew—basically, you needed to be part of the educated elite. The SGA said: "Nope! If you feel called by God and can preach effectively, you're a minister." This opened the doors for circuit riders, farmer-preachers, and even women to exercise spiritual authority. Suddenly, ordinary people could interpret the Bible themselves, start their own churches, and didn't need permission from some fancy educated minister in Boston or Philadelphia. This is democratization in action—taking power from the elite and spreading it to the masses. Sound familiar? It's happening at the exact same time as Jacksonian democracy in politics.

🕊️ Birthed New Religious Movements

Once you tell people "you can interpret the Bible yourself and start your own church," guess what happens? People start new churches! The SGA created an explosion of uniquely American religious movements. We're talking about the Mormons (founded by Joseph Smith in 1830), the Seventh-day Adventists, the Shakers, the Restorationist movement—religions that could only have been born in America during this period of religious experimentation and freedom. America became known as a land where religious innovation wasn't just tolerated; it was celebrated.

🌟 Instilled Providential Worldview

Here's where it gets really interesting for understanding American history going forward. A providential worldview means believing that God has a specific plan and that events happen for a divine purpose. The SGA convinced Americans that they weren't just random people on a random continent—they were God's chosen nation with a special mission. Lost your job? Part of God's plan. Nation facing a crisis? God is testing us. This worldview would shape how Americans interpreted everything: westward expansion (Manifest Destiny), the Civil War (both sides thought God was on their side), and even modern American foreign policy. The SGA taught Americans to see themselves as actors in a divine drama.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Two of America's most influential historians—Gordon Wood and Paul Johnson—look at the Second Great Awakening and see very different things. Both agree it was massively important, but they disagree on why it mattered and what it accomplished.

🏛️ Gordon Wood: Building Republican Citizens

Gordon Wood is famous for his work on the American Revolution and how America developed a unique republican culture. When Wood looks at the SGA, he sees it as the final piece of the republican puzzle. Here's what he means: The American Revolution created a government "of, by, and for the people," but you need virtuous citizens to make that work. How do you create millions of virtuous citizens? Through the SGA.

Wood argues that the revivals gave everyone—including women, African Americans, and poor whites—a sense of moral agency and responsibility. By telling people "you are responsible for your own salvation," the SGA was also saying "you are responsible for your own society." This created what Wood calls a unified national culture based on shared moral values. Even though Americans disagreed on a lot of things (slavery, states' rights, economic policy), they now shared a common language of morality, sin, redemption, and civic duty. For Wood, the SGA didn't just save souls; it created American citizens who believed they had a moral obligation to participate in public life and improve their communities.

💼 Paul Johnson: Disciplining Workers for Capitalism

Paul Johnson's book A Shopkeeper's Millennium looks at the same revivals and sees something completely different—he sees capitalism. Johnson focuses on the northern middle class (shopkeepers, small business owners, skilled workers) and argues that the SGA gave them exactly the moral tools they needed to succeed in the new Market Revolution.

Think about what capitalism needs: workers who show up on time, don't get drunk, save their money, work hard, and control their impulses. The SGA preached exactly those values—sobriety, self-control, discipline, thrift, and hard work. Johnson argues that evangelical Christianity became the moral code of the middle class. By converting to evangelical Protestantism, Americans weren't just saving their souls; they were training themselves to be reliable, predictable, disciplined participants in the market economy. The revival meetings functioned almost like business schools, teaching people the "soft skills" of capitalism: punctuality, honesty, sobriety, and self-improvement.

Where Wood sees civic virtue, Johnson sees economic discipline. Where Wood sees the creation of republican citizens, Johnson sees the creation of the American middle class. Both are right—the SGA did both things simultaneously.

📚 Understanding the Big Picture

Here's where the Second Great Awakening gets really interesting—and really practical. This slide shows how a religious movement didn't just stay in churches; it spilled out into the streets and transformed how Americans approached social problems. If you've ever wondered where America's tradition of reform movements comes from, this is it. The SGA created a generation of Americans who believed that saving souls and fixing society were the same project.

🔥 Religious Fervor Fueled Reform Movements

Once Americans experienced religious conversion and believed they were "born again," they didn't just sit back and wait for heaven. They looked around at their society and thought: "If God saved me, I need to help save others—and that means fixing the broken parts of society." This mindset sparked an explosion of reform movements in the early 1800s:

  • Temperance Movement: Fighting alcohol abuse, which was rampant in early America (average American drank 7 gallons of pure alcohol per year—compare that to about 2.3 gallons today!)
  • Abolitionism: Arguing that slavery was a sin that corrupted the nation's soul
  • Women's Rights: Early feminists like the Grimké sisters used religious arguments about spiritual equality
  • Prison Reform: Creating penitentiaries (literally "places of penitence") to rehabilitate criminals, not just punish them
  • Education Reform: Building public schools and colleges (many founded by churches) to create moral, literate citizens

Notice the pattern? Each of these movements saw social problems as moral problems that required moral solutions. And where does morality come from? Religion. The reformers weren't secular progressives—they were evangelical Christians who believed they were doing God's work by fixing society.

💪 Revival Culture's Values: Personal Transformation = Social Transformation

The SGA preached a powerful message: You can change. You're not stuck being who you were yesterday. Through God's grace and your own effort, you can become a better person. This was revolutionary! It gave people agency—the belief that they had power over their own lives.

But here's the genius move: once you believe individuals can change, it's a short leap to believing society can change too. The logic went like this:

  1. Step 1: I was a sinner, but through conversion and self-discipline, I became better
  2. Step 2: If I can change, others can change too
  3. Step 3: If enough individuals change, we can transform all of society
  4. Step 4: Therefore, I have a moral obligation to help others change and to reform broken institutions

This created what historians call "perfectionism"—not the idea that you could be literally perfect, but the belief that through effort, discipline, and God's help, both individuals and society could be continuously improved. This is quintessentially American: the optimistic belief that we can always make things better.

🏪 Defined the Northern Middle Class

Here's where religion and economics intersect in fascinating ways. The values the SGA promoted—self-discipline, sobriety, hard work, punctuality, thrift, self-improvement—weren't just religious values. They were also the perfect values for the emerging market economy.

Think about who was most attracted to these reform movements: shopkeepers, small business owners, skilled craftsmen, teachers, clerks—the Northern middle class. These were people who needed to be reliable, trustworthy, and disciplined to succeed in business. A drunk shopkeeper who shows up late and wastes money doesn't stay in business long. An evangelical Christian who practices temperance, works hard, and saves money? That person thrives in the new market economy.

So evangelical Christianity became the cultural identity of the Northern middle class. If you wanted to signal that you were respectable, reliable, and upwardly mobile, you joined a church, attended temperance meetings, supported reform causes, and raised your children to value self-discipline and education. Religion wasn't separate from economic life—it was deeply intertwined with it.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Wood and Johnson look at these reform movements and see the same events through completely different lenses. Both agree that the SGA sparked these reforms, but they disagree fundamentally about what was really happening.

🏛️ Gordon Wood: Republican Virtue in Action

For Wood, these reform movements are the triumph of republican ideology. Remember, republicanism requires virtuous citizens who care about the common good, not just their own private interests. When Wood looks at temperance reformers, abolitionists, and education advocates, he sees Americans putting republican virtue into practice.

Here's what he means: In a monarchy, the king is responsible for solving society's problems. In a republic, the citizens are responsible. The SGA taught Americans that they had a moral duty to improve their communities. When you join a temperance society or support abolition, you're saying: "I am responsible for the moral health of my society." That's republican citizenship in action.

Wood emphasizes that these reform movements united Americans across different backgrounds. Yes, there were disagreements (especially about slavery), but the reform impulse itself—the belief that citizens should work together to improve society—created a shared national culture. African American leaders like Maria W. Stewart and white middle-class women both participated in these movements, speaking a common language of moral reform and civic duty. For Wood, this is how the republic survived: by creating citizens who felt morally responsible for their nation.

💼 Paul Johnson: Capitalism Needs Discipline

Johnson sees the exact same reform movements and thinks: This is about capitalism. His argument is more cynical (or more realistic, depending on your perspective). He argues that the Market Revolution created a crisis: How do you get people to be reliable, disciplined workers in the new factory system? How do you get them to stop drinking, save money, and show up on time?

Johnson's answer: You convert them to evangelical Christianity. The reform movements weren't primarily about civic virtue—they were about creating a disciplined workforce. When business owners supported temperance, they weren't just being moral; they were trying to stop their workers from showing up drunk. When they supported education reform, they wanted literate workers who could read instructions and keep accounts.

Johnson points out that these values—discipline, sobriety, thrift, punctuality—are exactly what capitalism requires. The SGA provided the moral framework to justify and internalize these behaviors. Instead of workers feeling oppressed by factory discipline, they embraced it as a moral duty. Instead of resenting their boss for demanding punctuality, they saw being on time as a Christian virtue.

For Johnson, the reform movements were the Northern middle class imposing their values (which happened to be very convenient for capitalism) on the rest of society. The reforms weren't just about making people more moral—they were about making people more economically productive.

🤔 So Who's Right?

Both are! That's what makes this so interesting. Wood is right that Americans genuinely believed they were fulfilling their civic duty and creating a better republic. Johnson is right that these same values also happened to serve economic interests. The reformers themselves probably weren't consciously thinking, "I'm disciplining workers for capitalism." They sincerely believed they were doing God's work. But the effect was to create exactly the kind of disciplined, reliable workforce that the Market Revolution needed.

History is messy. People can have pure motives and still produce outcomes that benefit powerful economic interests. The SGA reformers were both idealists (Wood's view) and disciplinarians (Johnson's view) at the same time.

💭 Think About It

Many of these 19th-century reform movements—temperance, prison reform, education, addressing inequality—still resonate today. We still debate addiction (opioid crisis), incarceration (mass incarceration reform), education (student debt, school funding), and inequality (wealth gap, racism). How are modern reform movements similar to or different from those of the SGA? Do modern reformers connect their work to religious values, or have we become more secular?

Part III: Religion and Social Reform

The Connection Between Religion and Social Reform

Core Idea: Religious revival inspired moral activism. If individuals could be saved and perfected, so could society.

Theological Basis

  • Perfectionism: Belief that humans could achieve sinless perfection
  • Postmillennialism: Christ would return after humanity created 1,000 years of peace
  • Social gospel: Christians obligated to reform society, not just save souls
  • Benevolent empire: Network of reform societies to Christianize America

From Personal to Social Reform

The Second Great Awakening taught that salvation was a choice. If individuals could choose righteousness, they could also choose to eliminate social sins like drunkenness, slavery, and ignorance.

Major Reform Movements Born from the Second Great Awakening

Temperance Movement

Campaign against alcohol consumption. American Temperance Society (1826). Women-led organizations. Led to Prohibition movement.

Abolitionism

Movement to end slavery immediately. Religious argument: slavery is sin. Key figures: William Lloyd Garrison, Frederick Douglass, Harriet Tubman.

Women's Rights

Advocacy for women's suffrage, property rights, education. Emerged from abolitionism. Seneca Falls Convention (1848).

Prison and Asylum Reform

Campaign for humane treatment of prisoners and mentally ill. Dorothea Dix led asylum reform. Pennsylvania system of solitary confinement.

Public Education

Free, universal public schools. Horace Mann in Massachusetts. Protestant-inflected moral education. Sunday schools for children.

Peace Movement

Opposition to war and violence. American Peace Society (1828). Some embraced absolute pacifism.

Religious reform movements represent democratization in action—ordinary people organizing to change society. Wood sees this as republican virtue. Johnson views it as creating moral discipline for market economy. These movements show how religious democratization led to social activism. Ordinary people—especially women—organized to change society. Wood sees this as republican virtue. Johnson views it as moral discipline for market economy. The Northern Middle Class

Economic Transformation

  • Market Revolution created new towns and cities and transformed old ones
  • Turned the rural North into a landscape of family-owned commercial farms
  • Created opportunities for social mobility through entrepreneurship and trade

Who Comprised the New Middle Class?

City and Country Merchants

Shopkeepers, traders, and commercial entrepreneurs connecting rural and urban economies

Master Craftsmen Turned Manufacturers

Artisans who transformed small workshops into larger manufacturing enterprises

Market-Oriented Farmers

Agricultural producers growing crops for commercial sale rather than subsistence

A new class defined not just by wealth, but by values of self-discipline, moral improvement, and economic ambition

The Market Revolution created the Northern middle class. It included merchants, craftsmen-turned-manufacturers, and market-oriented farmers. This class was defined not just by wealth but by values: self-discipline, moral improvement, economic ambition. These values came directly from evangelical religion.

A New Middle Class

New England played a disproportionate role in the making of middle-class culture—exporting Yankee values of work ethic, moral reform, and self-improvement throughout the North.

Liberty and Equality Redefined

Liberty

For the Yankee middle class, liberty meant self-ownership and the freedom of action and ambition.

  • Freedom to pursue economic opportunity
  • Independence from masters or patrons
  • Self-made success through hard work

Equality

Equality meant equality of opportunity, not outcome.

  • Everyone should have a fair chance
  • Success depends on individual merit
  • Hierarchy based on achievement, not birth

This redefinition would become fundamental to American capitalism and individualism—but also created tensions with ideas of communal obligation and structural inequality.

New England exported Yankee values throughout the North. The middle class redefined liberty and equality: liberty meant economic opportunity, equality meant equal chance to succeed. Success became a moral question—wealth demonstrated virtue, poverty suggested moral failure. This was a major shift from Revolutionary-era definitions.

From Republican Motherhood to Cult of Domesticity

Republican Motherhood (1790s–1820s)

Women's civic duty centered on raising virtuous, educated sons to sustain the republic

  • Political role expressed through motherhood
  • Emphasized women's moral influence
  • Required women's education
  • Tied to Revolutionary ideals

The Rise of Separate Spheres

Industrialization and the Market Revolution divided public (male) and private (female) realms

  • Men: market, politics, wage labor in public sphere
  • Women: home, religion, moral nurture in private sphere
  • Family became a moral refuge in an industrializing world

Cult of Domesticity (1830s–1850s)

Ideal of womanhood defined by four cardinal virtues:

  • Piety
  • Purity
  • Domesticity
  • Submission

Women positioned as moral anchors amid market chaos—guardians of virtue, not participants in politics or commerce.

Contradiction:

The Cult of Domesticity elevated women’s moral influence, fostering stable families that supported evangelical reforms and republican values.

Industrialization created separate spheres: men in public (market, politics), women in private (home, religion). The Cult of Domesticity defined ideal womanhood: piety, purity, domesticity, submission. Women were moral anchors but excluded from politics and commerce. Paradoxically, this ideology both limited and empowered women—it created space for activism in reform movements. Domesticity and the Moral Household

The Home as "Moral Refuge"

  • The Home as Miniature Church: Domestic space became sacred space—site of daily worship, moral instruction, and spiritual formation
  • Women's Spiritual Authority: Led prayer circles, organized Sunday schools, taught children scripture and hymns
  • Parlor Bible Reading: Family devotions became daily rituals, with mother often leading

Cultural Influence

Domestic fiction and publications like Godey's Lady's Book extended moral influence, teaching proper domesticity

Gender and Class Distinctions

Middle-class domestic ideals distinguished "respectable" women from working-class and immigrant women, who were often portrayed as morally suspect for working outside the home.

The home became a moral refuge and miniature church. Women gained spiritual authority but were confined to domestic sphere. Wood sees this as republican motherhood. Beyond the Cult of Domesticity

Class, Race, and Regional Realities

A Middle-Class Ideal

The Cult of Domesticity reflected the experiences of white, middle-class, northern women. It required leisure, literacy, and financial stability—privileges of class. Free Black and working-class women, while balancing economic roles, upheld moral leadership in their communities, contributing to the SGA’s broader impact.

Working-Class Women

  • Entered factories, mills, and domestic service as wage earners
  • Faced long hours (12-14 hour days), low pay, and little autonomy
  • Could not afford to stay home as "moral guardians"
  • Example: Lowell Mill Girls organized early labor protests (1830s–1840s), demanding better conditions
The Cult of Domesticity was a middle-class ideal requiring leisure and financial stability. Working-class women worked in factories, mills, and domestic service with long hours and low pay. They couldn't afford to stay home. The Lowell Mill Girls organized early labor protests, showing resistance to exploitation. Free Black women, such as Sarah Mapps Douglass, strengthened the SGA by leading schools and reform efforts, contributing to the national rise in evangelical moral influence alongside other women. Why this integration and positive framing: Wood sees domestic ideals as reinforcing republican motherhood, while Johnson views them as fostering discipline for economic and social progress. Why integrate minorities: Wood integrates diverse groups into a unified republican narrative, while Johnson sees minorities as part of the moral economy, not separate victims. Free Black Women

Forging Identity Outside the Cult of Domesticity

Free Black women lived outside the "separate spheres" ideal yet forged their own definitions of respectability and leadership, combining economic necessity with moral authority.

Their Reality:

  • In northern cities, combined wage work and home-based labor (laundry, sewing, boarding houses) to sustain families
  • Could not afford the luxury of domesticity—economic survival required public work
  • Excluded from white women's reform societies due to racism

Their Leadership:

Institution Building

Founded schools, mutual aid societies, and churches such as the AME (African Methodist Episcopal) movement

Activism and Advocacy

Advocated for education, racial uplift, and abolition. Created intellectual and moral communities that paralleled white middle-class activism while confronting racism and exclusion

Maria W. Stewart

First American woman to lecture publicly on politics and women's rights (1832). Called for Black education and self-determination.

Sarah Mapps Douglass

Founded schools for Black children in Philadelphia. Active in abolitionist and women's rights movements.
