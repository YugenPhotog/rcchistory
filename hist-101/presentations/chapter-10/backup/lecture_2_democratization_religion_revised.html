Lecture 2:
Democratization of Religion

Toward an American Culture

HIST 101: American History to 1865

Welcome to Lecture 2 on the Democratization of Religion in early America. Today we'll explore how the Second Great Awakening transformed American religious life, making faith more accessible and participatory for ordinary people. This democratization paralleled the political democratization happening in the same era.

The Second Great Awakening

From the 1790s, a series of intense religious revivals took place on the western frontier, spreading throughout the country by the 1850s. This became known as the Second Great Awakening.

Main Features:

Mass Revivals

Large, emotional gatherings where people experienced conversion

Democratization of Religion

Ordinary people claimed spiritual authority, not just ministers or elites

Rise of Evangelical Denominations

Methodists and Baptists surged ahead of older churches

Religious Innovation

Birth of new sects like the Mormons, Adventists, and Shakers

Providential Interpretation of History

Events understood as signs of God's plan

The Second Great Awakening began in the 1790s on the frontier and spread nationwide by the 1850s. Unlike the First Great Awakening of the 1730s-40s, this movement was more democratic and emotional. Key features included mass outdoor revivals, a rejection of formal clerical authority, and the explosive growth of Methodist and Baptist churches. This movement also gave birth to entirely new religious movements like Mormonism and Adventism. The Awakening coincided with American democratization and shared its populist spirit.

Charles G. Finney

The Great Revivalist

Most responsible for bringing the awakening to the northern middle class

Theological Perspective

Arminian – Emphasized human free will in salvation, rejecting strict Calvinist predestination

Main Contributions:

  • Advocacy for personal conversion and revival meetings
  • Emphasis on the active role of the Holy Spirit in conviction and sanctification
  • Belief in Christian perfectionism and the potential to live a sinless life
  • Deep involvement in social reform movements, especially abolitionism
  • Opposition: Vocal critic of traditional Calvinist doctrines

"A revival is nothing else than a new beginning of obedience to God."

— Charles G. Finney
Charles Grandison Finney was the most influential revivalist of the Second Great Awakening, particularly among the northern middle class. A former lawyer, Finney brought the awakening to cities like Rochester, NY, in the 1830s. His Arminian theology emphasized free will—anyone could choose salvation. He promoted "perfectionism," the idea that Christians could live without sin. Finney's revivals led directly to reform movements, especially abolitionism. His emphasis on human agency and moral improvement fit perfectly with northern middle-class values.

Mass Revivals and Camp Meetings

Cane Ridge, Kentucky (1801)

Often cited as the most dramatic early revival

  • Reports of 20,000 people attending
  • Lasted for several days and nights
  • People traveled hundreds of miles to attend

Key Characteristics:

  • Itinerant preachers—especially Methodists and Baptists—took center stage
  • Emotional displays: people "jerking," rolling on the ground, ecstatic singing, shouting
  • Outdoor venues: Revivals were often outdoors because churches couldn't contain the crowds
  • Multi-denominational cooperation in evangelistic efforts

Itinerant Preachers and "Circuit Riders"

Methodist ministers, usually young men, traveled vast circuits on horseback, bringing religion to frontier communities.

Their Appeal:

Simple messages: repentance, personal faith, conversion experience

Plain language: spoke with passion rather than polished theology

Accessible: reached ordinary people regardless of class or education

The Cane Ridge Revival of 1801 in Kentucky symbolizes the explosive nature of frontier revivals. An estimated 20,000 people gathered for days of preaching, singing, and emotional religious experiences including "the jerks" and other physical manifestations. Methodist circuit riders were key to spreading the awakening—young, energetic preachers who rode horseback across vast territories, preaching in homes, barns, and outdoor settings. They used simple, emotional language rather than complex theology, making Christianity accessible to ordinary frontier settlers.

Democratization of Faith

Breaking Down Authority

  • Decline of Clerical Authority – No need for formal education or established institutional authority to preach or lead
  • Emphasized accessibility: anyone could testify, anyone could be converted
  • Preaching became emotional, direct, and plainspoken rather than intellectual
  • Rejection of Calvinist Predestination – Emphasis on individual choice and free will

Expanding Participation

Women

Emerged as exhorters, prayer leaders, and spiritual guides—gaining religious authority despite limited political rights

African Americans

Formed independent churches and religious communities, creating spaces for Black spiritual and social leadership

Expansion of Lay Participation

Ordinary believers took active roles in worship, testimony, and church governance

Christianity became more egalitarian, emotional, and participatory—shifting power from established clergy to ordinary believers

The Second Great Awakening democratized American religion in several ways. First, it broke down clerical authority—you didn't need seminary training to preach or lead. This opened doors for women and African Americans to gain religious authority even when they lacked political rights. Second, it emphasized emotion over intellect, making faith accessible to all classes. Third, it rejected Calvinist predestination in favor of free will—everyone could choose salvation. This religious democratization paralleled and reinforced America's political democratization in the Jacksonian era.

Come-Outer Sects

People dissatisfied with established churches left to form independent groups, rejecting traditional religious institutions for a more personal, direct relationship with God.

Key Characteristics

Religious Stance

  • Rejection of established churches
  • Emphasis on personal conversion
  • Emotional religious experience

Social Vision

  • Alternative communities
  • Experimentation with social structures
  • Often utopian aspirations

Cultural Impact

  • Challenged social norms
  • Promoted individual autonomy
  • Influenced reform movements
"Come-outers" were people who left established churches to form their own communities and sects. They wanted more direct, personal relationships with God without institutional mediation. These groups often experimented with alternative social structures and had utopian visions. They challenged conventional society and promoted individual spiritual autonomy. Many come-outer communities connected to broader reform movements like abolitionism and women's rights.

The Shakers

Shaker worship included ecstatic dancing and shaking

Origins and Leadership

  • Founded by Ann Lee (Mother Ann) in England, brought to America in 1774
  • Lee claimed to embody the second coming of Christ in female form
  • Grew significantly during Second Great Awakening

Core Beliefs and Practices:

Communal Living

  • Members lived in closed communities
  • Shared all property
  • Practiced celibacy

Gender Equality

  • Radical for the time
  • Women held leadership positions
  • Dual-gendered God concept

Worship Style

  • Ecstatic dancing and shaking
  • Simple living and craftsmanship
  • Spiritual perfectionism

Legacy: Famous for their fine furniture and craftsmanship. Declined after mid-19th century due to celibacy requirement, but left lasting influence on American design and communal living movements.

The Shakers were one of the most successful utopian communities of the era. Founded by Ann Lee, a working-class English woman who claimed to be the female embodiment of Christ. Shakers practiced celibacy, communal property ownership, and gender equality—all radical for the time. Their worship included ecstatic dancing (hence "Shakers"). They were famous for their simple, elegant furniture and architecture. At their peak in the mid-1800s, they had about 6,000 members in 19 communities. Their celibacy requirement meant they could only grow through conversions and adoptions, leading to their eventual decline.

The Mormons (Latter-day Saints)

Joseph Smith
Founder and Prophet

Origins

  • Founded by Joseph Smith in upstate New York, 1830
  • Claimed to receive golden plates containing the Book of Mormon
  • Combined Christian theology with new revelations
  • Believed America was the site of ancient Israelite migrations

Key Beliefs and Practices:

Theology

  • Restoration of "true" Christianity
  • Continuing revelation through prophets
  • Sacred history rooted in Americas

Social Organization

  • Tight-knit communities
  • Initially practiced polygamy
  • Strong central leadership

Economic System

  • "United Order" - communal economics
  • Cooperative enterprises
  • Self-sufficient settlements

Persecution and Migration:

  • Faced intense persecution in New York, Ohio, Missouri, and Illinois
  • Joseph Smith murdered by a mob in 1844
  • Brigham Young led the faithful to Utah Territory (1846-1847)
  • Established successful settlements in the Great Basin
Mormonism emerged from the religious ferment of the Second Great Awakening in upstate New York's "Burned-Over District." Joseph Smith claimed to receive golden plates from an angel, which he translated as the Book of Mormon. The faith combined Christian theology with new scripture claiming ancient Israelites migrated to America. Mormons faced violent persecution wherever they settled—partly due to their distinct beliefs, but also due to their practice of polygamy, their political cohesion, and their economic success. After Smith's murder in 1844, Brigham Young led most followers to Utah, where they established a thriving religious commonwealth.

The Millerites (Adventists)

William Miller
Baptist preacher and prophet

The Prophet

William Miller (1782-1849) – Baptist lay preacher who calculated Christ's return through intensive Bible study

The Prophecy

Predicted Christ would return between March 21, 1843 and March 21, 1844

Later revised to October 22, 1844

The Movement:

  • Gained tens of thousands of followers (estimates: 50,000-500,000)
  • Believers sold possessions, quit jobs, left crops unharvested
  • Dressed in white "ascension robes" and gathered to await Christ's return
  • Created intense emotional and spiritual preparation

The Great Disappointment (October 22, 1844):

Christ did not return as predicted

Followers experienced devastating disillusionment – many lost everything

The Legacy:

Seventh-day Adventists

Reinterpreted the prophecy: Christ entered heavenly sanctuary to begin "investigative judgment"

Other Groups

Jehovah's Witnesses and other Adventist denominations emerged from Millerite remnants

William Miller, a Baptist farmer-preacher, studied the Bible intensively and calculated that Christ would return between 1843-44. His movement attracted tens of thousands who sold their property and prepared for the Second Coming. When October 22, 1844 passed without Christ's return, it became known as the "Great Disappointment." Rather than disappearing, Millerism spawned several new denominations. Seventh-day Adventists reinterpreted Miller's prophecy, claiming Christ did enter a heavenly sanctuary on that date. This shows how religious movements can survive and adapt even after dramatic prophetic failures.

Oneida Community

Oneida Mansion House, central building of the community

Founded by John Humphrey Noyes

  • Established in Oneida, New York, 1848
  • Noyes was a Yale-educated minister
  • Believed in achieving Christian perfectionism on Earth
  • Community lasted until 1881

Radical Social Experiments:

Complex Marriage

  • Every man married to every woman
  • No exclusive pair bonds
  • Eugenic "stirpiculture" program

Communal Property

  • No private possessions
  • Shared living quarters
  • Communal child-rearing

Mutual Criticism

  • Public self-examination sessions
  • Community feedback on behavior
  • Tool for social control and improvement

Economic Success:

  • Manufactured steel animal traps (highly profitable)
  • Later produced silverware (Oneida Community silverware still exists today)
  • Community was financially successful throughout its existence

Decline and Transformation:

External pressure and internal dissent led to abandonment of "complex marriage" in 1879. Community reorganized as a joint-stock company (Oneida Community Limited) in 1881. The Oneida silverware company continued as a successful business into the 21st century.

The Oneida Community was one of the most radical utopian experiments of the era. John Humphrey Noyes believed in perfectionism—that humans could live without sin. His community practiced "complex marriage" where every adult was married to every other adult, with no exclusive relationships. They also practiced eugenic breeding ("stirpiculture") where the community decided who could have children together. Despite these radical practices, Oneida was economically successful, manufacturing animal traps and silverware. External pressure eventually forced them to abandon complex marriage, and they reorganized as a conventional business. The silverware company survives today as a reminder of this utopian experiment.

African American Religion

Development of Independent Black Churches

Free Black Churches in the North:

  • African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church – Founded by Richard Allen in Philadelphia, 1816
  • AME Zion Church – Founded in New York, 1821
  • These churches became centers of Black community life, education, and political organizing

Richard Allen
Founder of AME Church

Religion in Enslaved Communities:

Visible Religion

  • Attendance at white churches (often segregated)
  • White-supervised services
  • Emphasis on obedience and submission

Invisible Religion

  • Secret "hush harbor" meetings
  • Spirituals with coded messages
  • African spiritual traditions blended with Christianity

Key Themes in Black Christianity:

  • Liberation theology: Emphasis on Moses leading people from bondage
  • Exodus narrative: God delivers the oppressed from slavery
  • Promised Land: Hope for freedom and justice
  • Spiritual resistance: Faith as a form of defiance against dehumanization

Black Christianity became both a source of comfort and a language of resistance—providing community, hope, and moral authority to challenge slavery's legitimacy.

African American Christianity developed along two tracks. In the North, free Blacks established independent churches like the AME Church, which became centers of community life and abolitionist activity. In the South, enslaved people practiced both "visible" religion (attending white churches) and "invisible" religion (secret gatherings in "hush harbors"). Enslaved Christians emphasized Old Testament themes of liberation—the Exodus, Moses, the Promised Land. Spirituals contained coded messages about freedom. Black Christianity became both a source of hope and a language of resistance, providing moral authority to challenge slavery's legitimacy.

Women in the Second Great Awakening

Expanding Religious Authority

Women were the majority of converts and became key leaders in spreading evangelical Christianity, despite being denied formal ordination in most denominations.

Roles Women Played:

Exhorters and Prayer Leaders

Led prayer meetings, testified publicly, and encouraged conversions

Sunday School Teachers

Educated children in religious and moral instruction

Missionary Workers

Organized and funded missionary societies

Reform Movement Leaders

Led temperance, moral reform, and abolitionist movements

Notable Female Religious Leaders:

Phoebe Palmer (1807-1874)

Methodist evangelist and writer who promoted holiness theology. Led revival meetings and wrote influential theological works. Advocated for women's right to preach publicly.

Jarena Lee (1783-1864)

First woman authorized to preach in the AME Church. Traveled thousands of miles preaching. Wrote her autobiography, one of the first published by an African American woman.

Impact on Women's Rights:

  • Religious authority gave women a public voice in an era when they lacked political rights
  • Experience in religious organizing prepared women for reform activism
  • Evangelical emphasis on moral equality laid groundwork for women's rights arguments
  • Many early feminists (like the Grimké sisters) came from evangelical backgrounds

Women's religious activism in the Second Great Awakening was a crucial stepping stone toward organized women's rights movements in the mid-19th century.

Women were the majority of converts in the Second Great Awakening and became crucial to its spread. Though most denominations still banned female ordination, women served as exhorters, prayer leaders, Sunday school teachers, and missionary organizers. Figures like Phoebe Palmer and Jarena Lee achieved significant influence as preachers and writers. This religious authority gave women a public voice when they lacked political rights. Evangelical religion emphasized moral equality and gave women organizational experience. Many early feminists came from evangelical backgrounds and used religious arguments to support women's rights. The religious activism of the awakening was a crucial stepping stone to organized feminism.

Religious Impact on Reform Movements

Evangelical Perfectionism and Social Reform

The Second Great Awakening didn't just change individual souls—it sparked a wave of reform movements aimed at perfecting society.

Key Reform Movements Rooted in Evangelical Religion:

Temperance Movement

  • Campaigns against alcohol consumption
  • Led by evangelical women
  • American Temperance Society (1826)

Abolitionism

  • Moral crusade against slavery
  • Slavery seen as sin against God
  • Finney and other revivalists were abolitionists

Education Reform

  • Public education movement
  • Sunday schools and literacy campaigns
  • Colleges founded by religious groups

Prison and Asylum Reform

  • Humane treatment of prisoners
  • Mental health institutions
  • Dorothea Dix's advocacy

Women's Rights

  • Emerged from religious activism
  • Grimké sisters, Lucretia Mott
  • Seneca Falls Convention (1848)

Peace Movement

  • American Peace Society (1828)
  • Christian pacifism
  • Opposition to war

The Logic of Religious Reform:

If individuals can be perfected through conversion, why not perfect society as a whole?

  • Evangelical postmillennialism: belief that Christ would return after Christians created a perfect society
  • This gave religious urgency to social reform—reformers were preparing the way for Christ's return
  • Reform became a moral duty and religious calling
The Second Great Awakening sparked widespread reform movements. Evangelical perfectionism taught that individuals could be perfected through conversion—so why not perfect society too? Postmillennialists believed Christ would return after Christians created a perfect society, giving religious urgency to reform work. This logic drove temperance campaigns, abolitionism, education reform, prison reform, women's rights, and peace movements. Reformers saw their work as preparing for Christ's return. This is why so many reform movements of the antebellum era had religious roots and used religious language and moral arguments.

Transcendentalism

A Philosophical and Literary Movement

While not exactly a religious revival, Transcendentalism emerged from the same cultural ferment and shared the era's optimism about human potential.

Key Figures:

Ralph Waldo Emerson

Henry David Thoreau

Core Beliefs:

Divine Within

God exists within each person—no need for institutional church

Intuition Over Reason

Truth comes through intuition, not logic or scripture

Nature as Divine

Nature reveals spiritual truth and God's presence

Individual Self-Reliance

Trust yourself and your own conscience

Connections to Reform:

  • Many Transcendentalists were abolitionists
  • Thoreau's Civil Disobedience influenced nonviolent resistance movements
  • Brook Farm and other utopian experiments
  • Emphasis on individual conscience over social convention

"Trust thyself: every heart vibrates to that iron string."

— Ralph Waldo Emerson, "Self-Reliance"
Transcendentalism emerged in the 1830s-40s among New England intellectuals, particularly around Boston and Concord. Ralph Waldo Emerson and Henry David Thoreau were its most famous figures. Transcendentalists believed divinity existed within each person, not in institutions. They trusted intuition over reason, saw nature as divine, and emphasized individual self-reliance and conscience. While rejecting traditional Christianity, they shared the era's optimism about human potential and perfectibility. Many Transcendentalists were abolitionists and social reformers. Thoreau's "Civil Disobedience," written after refusing to pay taxes to support the Mexican-American War, became a foundational text for nonviolent resistance movements worldwide.

The Burned-Over District

Upstate New York's Religious Ferment

"Burned-Over District" – A term coined by Charles Finney referring to western and central New York, so named because the region had been "burned over" by so many religious revivals.

Why This Region?

Economic Change

  • Erie Canal opened 1825
  • Rapid commercialization
  • Social disruption and anxiety

Migration

  • New Englanders moving west
  • Diverse religious backgrounds
  • Weak established churches

Reform Culture

  • Strong reform networks
  • Educational institutions
  • Print culture and newspapers

Religious Movements Born in the Burned-Over District:

  • Mormonism – Joseph Smith's visions in Palmyra, NY
  • Millerism/Adventism – William Miller's prophecies
  • Spiritualism – Fox sisters in Hydesville, NY
  • Oneida Community – John Humphrey Noyes
  • Numerous smaller sects and utopian experiments

The Burned-Over District shows how social change, geographic mobility, and religious ferment combined to create an environment of extraordinary religious innovation and experimentation.

The Burned-Over District in upstate New York was America's most religiously innovative region in the antebellum period. The Erie Canal (1825) brought rapid economic change and social disruption. New Englanders migrated west, bringing diverse religious traditions but no strong established churches. This created fertile ground for religious experimentation. The region produced Mormonism, Millerism, Spiritualism, the Oneida Community, and countless other sects. Charles Finney held some of his most successful revivals there. The region's combination of economic transformation, geographic mobility, and weak religious establishment made it uniquely receptive to new religious ideas.

Nativism and Anti-Catholic Sentiment

The Dark Side of Protestant America

As Protestant revivals democratized American religion, they also strengthened anti-Catholic prejudice and nativist sentiment against immigrants.

Sources of Anti-Catholic Sentiment:

Religious

  • Protestant theology opposed "Popery"
  • Feared Catholic hierarchical authority
  • Believed Catholicism incompatible with democracy

Political

  • Catholics seen as loyal to Pope, not America
  • Worried about Catholic voting bloc
  • Fear of "papal conspiracy"

Economic

  • Irish and German Catholic immigration
  • Job competition
  • Class resentment

Anti-Catholic Literature and Propaganda:

The Awful Disclosures of Maria Monk (1836)

A fabricated "exposé" of convent life claiming nuns were sexually exploited and babies murdered. Complete fiction, but became a bestseller and fueled anti-Catholic hysteria.

Violence and Discrimination:

  • Ursuline Convent burning (1834): Mob burned a convent school near Boston
  • Philadelphia Bible Riots (1844): Anti-Catholic riots killed dozens
  • Know-Nothing Party (1850s): Nativist political party opposed Catholic immigration
  • Discrimination in employment: "No Irish Need Apply"

Anti-Catholic nativism shows how religious revivals could simultaneously democratize religion for some while fostering intolerance toward religious outsiders.

The Second Great Awakening's Protestant character contributed to intense anti-Catholic sentiment. Evangelicals saw Catholicism as hierarchical, authoritarian, and incompatible with American democracy. Irish and German Catholic immigration increased in the 1830s-50s, intensifying nativist fears. Anti-Catholic propaganda like "The Awful Disclosures of Maria Monk" spread lurid (false) stories about convents. Violence included the burning of an Ursuline convent in 1834 and deadly riots in Philadelphia in 1844. The Know-Nothing Party of the 1850s was explicitly anti-Catholic and anti-immigrant. This shows the dark side of religious democratization—while it opened Christianity to more Americans, it also fostered intolerance toward those outside the Protestant evangelical consensus.

Religion and the Sectional Crisis

How Churches Split Over Slavery

The same religious movements that promoted reform also contributed to sectional division, as Northern and Southern Christians interpreted the Bible differently on slavery.

Churches Split Along Sectional Lines:

Denomination Split Date Cause
Methodists 1844 Dispute over a slaveholding bishop
Baptists 1845 Conflict over missionary appointments of slaveholders
Presbyterians 1837-1861 Theological and slavery disputes led to multiple splits

Competing Biblical Interpretations:

Northern View

  • Slavery is a sin – violates human dignity and God's image
  • Emphasis on Exodus narrative – God liberates the oppressed
  • New Testament: "There is neither slave nor free" (Galatians 3:28)
  • Golden Rule: Do unto others as you would have done to you

Southern View

  • Slavery is biblically sanctioned – appears in Old and New Testaments
  • Biblical patriarchs owned slaves
  • Paul's instruction: "Slaves, obey your masters" (Ephesians 6:5)
  • Slavery as a civilizing and Christianizing institution

Impact on National Unity:

Church splits foreshadowed political division

When national denominations split North-South over slavery in the 1840s-50s, they removed important bonds of national unity. These splits prefigured and facilitated the political rupture that led to Civil War.

By the 1840s-50s, major Protestant denominations split along sectional lines over slavery. Methodists split in 1844, Baptists in 1845, Presbyterians in phases from 1837-1861. Both sides claimed biblical support. Northerners emphasized the Exodus narrative and New Testament's "neither slave nor free." Southerners pointed to biblical patriarchs owning slaves and Paul's instruction to "slaves, obey your masters." These church splits were significant because they removed important bonds of national unity before the political rupture. Historian C.C. Goen argued that church divisions made political compromise more difficult and the Civil War more likely. Religion both promoted reform and contributed to sectional crisis.

Religion and American Identity

Forging a Protestant Nation

The Second Great Awakening helped create a distinctively American Protestant identity that shaped national culture and politics.

Key Elements of Protestant American Identity:

Voluntarism

No state church—religion thrives through individual choice and voluntary association

Denominationalism

Multiple competing Protestant groups rather than one established church

Revivalism

Periodic spiritual renewal through mass conversion experiences

Moral Reform

Churches leading social improvement campaigns

Civil Religion

Blending of Protestant Christianity with American nationalism

America as a "Redeemer Nation":

Many Americans came to see their nation as chosen by God for a special mission

  • America as the "New Israel" or "City on a Hill"
  • Manifest Destiny justified through religious language
  • American democracy seen as God's plan for human government
  • Mission to spread Protestant Christianity and republican government worldwide

Exclusions from This Identity:

  • Catholics – Seen as un-American and authoritarian
  • Native Americans – Targeted for Christianization and "civilization"
  • Enslaved people – Christianity used to justify their bondage (in the South)
  • Non-believers – Atheists and freethinkers marginalized

The Protestant evangelical consensus of the antebellum era created a powerful American identity—but one that excluded or marginalized those outside its boundaries.

The Second Great Awakening helped forge a distinctively American Protestant identity. Key features included voluntarism (no state church), denominational diversity, periodic revivals, and moral reform activism. Many Americans came to see their nation as divinely chosen—a "redeemer nation" with a mission to spread Christianity and democracy. This "civil religion" blended Protestant Christianity with American nationalism. Manifest Destiny was justified in religious terms. However, this identity was exclusionary—it marginalized Catholics, Native Americans, enslaved people, and non-believers. The Protestant evangelical consensus was powerful but limited in its vision of who counted as truly "American."

Long-Term Impact of the Second Great Awakening

Lasting Changes in American Life

Religious Landscape:

  • Evangelical dominance: Methodists and Baptists became largest Protestant denominations
  • Religious pluralism: Proliferation of denominations and sects
  • Revival tradition: Periodic awakenings became pattern in American religion
  • Anti-intellectualism: Suspicion of formal theology and educated clergy

Social and Political Impact:

Reform Movements

  • Abolition, temperance, education reform
  • Women's rights movements
  • Social Gospel tradition

Democratic Culture

  • Populist religious leadership
  • Emphasis on individual choice
  • Distrust of elites and institutions

Political Activism

  • Religious voters as political force
  • Moral issues in politics
  • Religious justifications for policy

Cultural Legacy:

Optimism

Belief in human and social perfectibility

Activism

Expectation that religion should improve society

Individualism

Personal conversion and conscience over institutional authority

Emotionalism

Feeling and experience valued over formal theology

The Second Great Awakening fundamentally shaped American religious, social, and political culture in ways that persist to the present day.

The Second Great Awakening's impact lasted long beyond the antebellum period. It established evangelical Protestantism as America's dominant religious culture, created the pattern of periodic religious revivals, and spawned numerous denominations. It made reform activism central to American religion—from abolitionism to the Social Gospel movement to modern Christian activism. It reinforced democratic and populist culture, emphasizing individual choice and experience over institutional authority. It made moral issues and religious voters central to American politics. The awakening's emphasis on perfectibility, both individual and social, became a lasting American trait. Its optimistic, activist, emotional, individualistic spirit continues to shape American religion and culture today.

Historiographical Debates

How Historians Have Interpreted the Second Great Awakening

Major Interpretive Approaches:

Religious/Spiritual

Focus: Genuine religious experience and theological change

Argument: The awakening represented authentic spiritual renewal and theological innovation

Emphasis: Doctrinal shifts, religious sincerity, theological debates

Social Control

Focus: Elite attempts to manage social change

Argument: Revival leaders used religion to control workers and impose middle-class values

Emphasis: Class conflict, industrialization, social order

Democratic Movement

Focus: Popular empowerment and religious democratization

Argument: Common people seized religious authority from elites

Emphasis: Populism, lay leadership, rejection of hierarchy

Market Revolution

Focus: Connection to economic transformation

Argument: Revival religion helped people cope with market economy's disruptions

Emphasis: Commercialization, anxiety, adaptation to capitalism

Key Historians and Works:

  • Nathan O. Hatch – The Democratization of American Christianity (1989): Emphasizes populist rebellion against religious elites
  • Paul Johnson – A Shopkeeper's Millennium (1978): Argues Rochester revivals were about social control by business class
  • Christine Heyrman – Southern Cross (1997): Examines how evangelicalism adapted to Southern culture
  • Jon Butler – Awash in a Sea of Faith (1990): Critiques "awakening" framework, emphasizes diversity

These debates reflect broader questions about religious sincerity, social power, democratization, and economic change in early America. Most historians now recognize the awakening's complexity—it was simultaneously spiritual, social, economic, and political.

Historians have debated the meaning of the Second Great Awakening for decades. Some emphasize genuine spiritual experience and theological innovation. Others see it as social control—elites using religion to discipline workers during industrialization. Nathan Hatch stressed democratization—common people seizing religious authority. Paul Johnson connected it to the market revolution and class conflict in Rochester. Christine Heyrman examined how evangelicalism adapted to Southern slave culture. Jon Butler criticized the "awakening" framework as overgeneralizing. Most current historians recognize the awakening was multifaceted—simultaneously spiritual, social, economic, and political. It meant different things to different participants and had complex, sometimes contradictory effects.

Comparing the Awakenings

First (1730s-40s) vs. Second (1790s-1850s)

Aspect First Great Awakening Second Great Awakening
Timing 1730s-1740s 1790s-1850s
Geography Colonial seaboard, established communities Western frontier and growing cities
Key Figures Jonathan Edwards, George Whitefield Charles Finney, circuit riders
Theology Calvinist (predestination, election) Arminian (free will, choice)
Leadership Educated clergy leading Lay preachers, ordinary believers
Methods Powerful sermons in churches Camp meetings, outdoor revivals, "new measures"
Social Impact Challenged church authority, prepared for Revolution Reform movements, utopian experiments, sectional crisis
Winning Groups New Light Congregationalists, Baptists Methodists, Baptists, new sects
Cultural Tone Anxiety, fear of damnation Optimism, perfectibility, progress

Both awakenings democratized religion and challenged authority, but the Second was more optimistic, diverse, and connected to social reform.

While both awakenings democratized religion and challenged authority, they differed significantly. The First occurred in colonial seaboard communities with educated Calvinist preachers like Edwards and Whitefield emphasizing predestination and damnation. The Second happened on the expanding frontier with populist lay preachers emphasizing free will and perfectibility. The First prepared colonists psychologically for revolution by legitimating challenges to authority. The Second spawned reform movements, utopian experiments, and contributed to sectional crisis. The First's tone was anxiety and fear; the Second's was optimistic and forward-looking. The Second was more diverse in leadership, methods, and sects produced. Both made religion more emotional and participatory, but the Second went much further in democratizing religious authority.

Conclusion

The Democratization of American Religion

The Second Great Awakening fundamentally transformed American religious, social, and political life.

Key Takeaways:

Religious Democratization

Authority shifted from educated clergy to ordinary believers; emphasis on personal choice and experience

Evangelical Dominance

Methodists and Baptists became America's largest denominations; revivalism became central pattern

The Second Great Awakening's legacy continues to shape American religion and culture: evangelical Protestantism's dominance, revival tradition, religious activism in politics, optimism about perfectibility, and tension between religious individualism and social reform.

The Second Great Awakening was one of the most important movements in American history. It democratized religion by shifting authority to ordinary people and emphasizing personal choice and experience over institutional hierarchy. It made Methodists and Baptists America's dominant Protestant groups and established revivalism as a central pattern. It drove major reform movements including abolitionism, temperance, and women's rights. It produced new religious movements like Mormonism and Adventism. It expanded religious participation for women and African Americans. However, it also contributed to sectional crisis as churches split over slavery. The awakening's emphasis on optimism, perfectibility, individual choice, emotional experience, and social reform continues to shape American religion and culture today.
