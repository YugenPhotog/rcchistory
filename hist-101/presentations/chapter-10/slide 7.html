The Print Revolution: Scale & Impact

  • Steam presses, mass-produced paper, stereotyping
  • Advertising revenues increasingly eclipsed subscriptions for mass-circulation newspapers
  • Rising literacy and expanding distribution (post, canals, railroads) amplified circulation
  • Cheap dailies and reprint series broadened access across classes and regions

Stereotyping (printing)

Definition: Stereotyping creates a single, durable metal plate of a fully typeset page. Printers set the page in movable type, take a mold (flong), then cast a solid plate (lead alloy) from it. The plate prints quickly and can be stored for identical reprints.

  • Why it mattered: Lowered costs for long runs, freed expensive handset type, and ensured consistent reprints.
  • Timeline (very brief): Late 18th-century development; widely used in the 19th century—central to the antebellum print boom.
  • Context: Electrotyping (from the 1830s–40s) made high-fidelity copper shells; Linotype (1880s) mechanized composition (later era).
