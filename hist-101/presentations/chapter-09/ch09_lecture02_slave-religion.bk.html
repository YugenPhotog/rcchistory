White Religious Control

Christianity as Social Discipline

  • Selective Scripture: “Curse of Ham” (Gen 9) and “Slaves obey your masters” (Eph 6) naturalized hierarchy.
  • Missionary Catechisms: Charles Colcock Jones’s “proper” slave Christianity centered on obedience over deliverance.
  • Supervised Worship: Plantation services under white oversight; enslaved preachers vetted for loyalty.
  • Purpose: Legitimize slavery as divine plan; suppress solidarity and rebellion.

From Withholding the Gospel to Weaponizing It

  • Early Reluctance: Many planters feared baptism implied manumission, delaying evangelization.
  • Shift in Perspective: Great Awakening revivals reframed religion as moral discipline useful to the slave regime.
  • “Civilizing” Rationale: Christianity pitched as obedience training and social order.
  • Monitored Preachers: Enslaved preachers allowed only under surveillance for “safe” messages.
  • Outcome: A theology of control—faith became both a cage and the language of future rebellion.

Slave Theological Resistance

Reading Scripture from Below

  • Exodus Narrative: Moses as liberator; “Let my people go.”
  • Christ the Suffering Servant: Shared pain and divine justice.
  • Creation Theology: All made in God’s image → equality.
  • Prophetic Faith: God sides with the oppressed; slavery as sin.
  • Afterlife Hope: “Great gettin’ up mornin’” promised ultimate justice.
Emphasize: standpoint changes scriptural meaning.

The “Invisible Institution”

Hidden Religious Life

  • Hush harbors & brush arbors: worship beyond white oversight.
  • Overturned pots to muffle songs and prayers.
  • African Worship Elements: Rhythm, dance, call-and-response, spirit possession.
  • Severe punishment if discovered.
  • Foundation for independent Black churches post-emancipation.
Raboteau: “invisible institution” as underground church.

Spirituals as Resistance

  • “Go Down, Moses”: Exodus metaphor; coded defiance.
  • “Wade in the Water”: Practical escape instruction.
  • “Swing Low, Sweet Chariot”: Hope of deliverance.
  • Function: Theology + protest + communication network.

Slave Preachers & Leadership

  • Role within the Slave Community: Provided spiritual guidance and stability.
  • Self-Ordained Leadership: Emerged without formal training.
    • Blended African and Christian traditions.
    • Served as moral and ethical leaders; acted as mediators.
  • Dangers and Challenges:
    • Tension between slaveholder demands and community allegiance.
    • Risk of punishment for “wrong” or subversive messages.
    • Constantly balancing collaboration and resistance.
  • Outcome: Religious leadership became education in freedom and self-determination.
Dual nature: agent of control vs. vessel of resistance.

Nat Turner: Prophet & Revolutionary

  • Early Life & Spiritual Formation:
    • Literate; immersed in biblical study.
    • Fasting and prayer; nicknamed “The Prophet.”
    • Interpreted eclipses and natural signs as divine commands.
  • 1831 Rebellion: Southampton County, Virginia (~60 whites killed).
    • Feb 1831: Solar eclipse interpreted as divine signal.
    • Aug 21–23, 1831: Rebellion launched; freed enslaved people; ~50 whites killed.
    • Militia quickly suppressed the uprising.
    • Nov 1831: Turner captured, tried, executed.
    • Shockwaves across the South; harsh new restrictions on enslaved people and free Blacks.
  • Aftermath: Crackdown on literacy, Black preaching, and unsupervised gatherings.
  • Meaning: Faith and apocalyptic vision fused with revolutionary purpose.
Crescendo; narrate with gravity and precision.
