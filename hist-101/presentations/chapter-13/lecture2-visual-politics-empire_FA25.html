Native Visual Sovereignty

Two Ways of Seeing

[IMAGE: George Catlin Portrait, 1830s]

A Catlin painting of a Native leader in full ceremonial dress. Dignified, romanticized, carefully composed. The caption might read: "Pigeon's Egg Head Going to and Returning from Washington" or similar. Note the painter's admiring yet controlling gaze.

The Paradox of Catlin's Vision

George Catlin's portraits from the 1830s expressed admiration and ethnographic control simultaneously. They created what scholars call the "noble but vanishing" paradox—Indigenous peoples were portrayed as dignified and worthy of study precisely because they were assumed to be disappearing.

This gaze served empire even as it seemed to honor its subjects.

[IMAGE: Ledger Drawing or Hide Painting]

A Plains ledger drawing showing a battle scene, hunting scene, or ceremonial moment. Made by Indigenous artist on ledger book paper or buffalo hide. Bold lines, symbolic representation, internal perspective. Artist maintains control of narrative.

Acts of Self-Representation

Ledger drawings and hide paintings were acts of self-representation that preserved ceremony, warfare, and identity. These were not made for white audiences but for Indigenous communities themselves—to record history, to pass down knowledge, to assert memory.

They were visual sovereignty in practice.

Thomas Cole and the Moral Sublime

The Course of Empire (1833–36)

Cole's five-painting cycle traces civilization from savage state through pastoral beauty, imperial triumph, destruction, and desolation. It was a warning against hubris—but was it also a prediction, or an attempt at redemption?

[IMAGE: Thomas Cole, "The Savage State"]

First painting in the series: wilderness, hunters, storm clearing. Nature dominates, humans are small and primitive. Dawn light suggests potential.

[IMAGE: Thomas Cole, "The Consummation of Empire"]

Third painting: massive classical architecture, crowds, fountains, golden light. Empire at its height—beautiful, ordered, powerful. Peak of civilization before the fall.

[IMAGE: Thomas Cole, "Destruction"]

Fourth painting: the same city in flames, statues toppled, battle raging. Bridge collapsing, smoke and chaos. The price of imperial overreach.

[IMAGE: Thomas Cole, "Desolation"]

Final painting: ruins overtaken by nature, single column standing, moonlight over crumbling grandeur. Nature reclaims what empire built. The cycle complete.

Does Cole's cycle predict America's own empire, or try to redeem it?

Can a warning also be a justification?

Albert Bierstadt and the Western Spectacle

Monumental Visions of Possession

[IMAGE: Albert Bierstadt, "Among the Sierra Nevada, California"]

Massive canvas (6 x 10 feet typical). Dramatic mountain peaks, waterfall catching golden light, pristine lake in foreground. Deer drinking at water's edge. NO human presence. Scale, light, and emptiness aestheticize possession. This is nature as cathedral.

Exhibition as Imperial Theater

Bierstadt's paintings toured exhibition halls like trophies. Viewers paid admission to stand before these monumental canvases, experiencing the West as spectacle before they ever set foot there. The paintings were advertisements for empire, making conquest beautiful.

Light, scale, and emptiness worked together to transform stolen land into sublime property.

What emotions does this scale provoke?

Awe? Reverence? Entitlement?

How do the absent peoples define the meaning of "wilderness"?

Landscape as Theology

When Art Makes Power Look Sacred

The fusion of art and providence transformed Western landscapes into evidence of divine sanction. Beauty became proof that God intended this land for American possession. The paintings did not just represent the West—they sacralized conquest.

When art makes power look sacred,
can we still see the politics beneath the paint?

Photography and the
Technology of Possession

Daguerreotypes from the Mexican-American War

The Camera Follows the Army

Portable cameras followed troops into Mexico. Photographers captured portraits of officers, views of occupied cities, ruins, even executions. Photography's "truth effect" turned conquest into documentation.

[IMAGE: Daguerreotype from Mexican-American War]

A daguerreotype showing U.S. officers posed in occupied Mexican territory, or ruins of a Mexican fortification, or a street scene in occupied Veracruz. The image has the characteristic mirror-like surface, precise detail, and aura of authenticity that made daguerreotypes powerful. Note the stillness, the formality, the sense of "this happened."

The Truth Effect

Unlike paintings, which everyone knew were interpretations, photographs claimed to show things exactly as they were. The camera did not seem to have opinions or agendas. This supposed neutrality made photographs powerful tools of empire.

How does the camera's supposed neutrality shape our moral distance?

Does photographic "truth" make us more or less critical of what we see?

Carleton Watkins and the Survey Image

Yosemite as Data

[IMAGE: Carleton Watkins, Yosemite Valley View]

A Watkins mammoth-plate photograph (18 x 22 inches) of Yosemite. Crystal clear detail, perfect composition, sense of ordered grandeur. El Capitan or Half Dome in sharp focus. The photograph is both beautiful and informational—art and advertisement for investors and railroad planners.

Survey Aesthetics: Order, Clarity, Control

Watkins photographed Yosemite and Pacific railroad routes for investors. His images were designed to show order, clarity, and scale—proof that this landscape could be managed, developed, possessed. The photographs were not just beautiful; they were persuasive.

How does composition itself claim ownership?

In what sense is seeing already seizing?

From Observation to Occupation

Government Survey Photography

[IMAGE: Timothy O'Sullivan, Survey Photograph]

An O'Sullivan photograph from one of the Western surveys (Wheeler, King). Geological formations, vast distances, technical precision. Perhaps "Ancient Ruins in the Cañon de Chelle" or a Great Basin landscape. The image catalogs, measures, and renders the land legible to government planners.

[IMAGE: William Henry Jackson, Survey Photograph]

A Jackson photograph from the Hayden Survey. Perhaps "Mountain of the Holy Cross" or a Yellowstone thermal feature. Like O'Sullivan, Jackson converts landscape into information. The camera becomes an instrument of territorial policy.

Technology Transforms Wonder into Management

Government survey photographers like Timothy O'Sullivan and William Henry Jackson converted imagery into territorial policy. Their photographs appeared in official reports, lobbying materials, and congressional debates. What began as observation became the basis for occupation.
