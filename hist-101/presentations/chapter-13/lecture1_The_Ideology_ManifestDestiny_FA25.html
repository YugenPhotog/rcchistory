What is a National Myth?

Not a lie, but a story society tells itself about:

  • Identity
  • Origins
  • Destiny

Myths explain the present, justify the past, script the future

Examples You Recognize

  • Founding Myth: Freedom-loving patriots vs. tyranny
  • Frontier Myth: Rugged individuals taming wilderness
  • Melting Pot Myth: America welcomes all who work hard

Today's Focus

Manifest Destiny as THE defining American myth of the 19th century

We're putting on the "glasses" Americans wore in the 1840s

Goal: Learn to see the myth so you can decode the documentary

II. Anatomy of the Manifest Destiny Myth

Five Core Elements

  1. The Chosen People
  2. The Sacred Mission
  3. The Empty/Virgin Land
  4. Inevitable Progress
  5. The Virtuous Outcome

1. The Chosen People

  • Anglo-Saxon Protestants uniquely blessed by God
  • Special virtues: industry, self-discipline, love of freedom
  • Mythic function: Explains why Americans should expand, not others

2. The Sacred Mission

  • Not just seeking land—fulfilling divine purpose
  • Spreading democracy, Christianity, civilization
  • Mythic function: Transforms conquest into moral duty

3. The Empty/Virgin Land

  • West is "unsettled" wilderness waiting to be cultivated
  • Indigenous peoples and Mexicans rendered invisible or temporary obstacles
  • Mythic function: Erases violence required — if land is empty, taking it isn't theft

4. Inevitable Progress

  • History has a direction: westward, toward "civilization"
  • Resistance is futile — like resisting gravity
  • Mythic function: Makes expansion seem natural, not a choice

5. The Virtuous Outcome

  • Expansion benefits everyone (including the conquered)
  • Creates prosperity, spreads freedom, fulfills human potential
  • Mythic function: Retrospective justification

What Myths Do: Resolve Anxieties

Americans in 1845 worried about:

  • Political instability (would republic survive expansion?)
  • Regional tension (slave vs. free territories?)
  • Moral doubt (is taking land right?)
  • International criticism (Europeans called it barbaric)

The Myth's Answers

  • Expansion equals stability (fulfilling destiny)
  • It's God's plan (sidesteps slavery question temporarily)
  • It's not theft — it's civilization (moral doubt resolved)
  • Europeans are jealous of our divine favor (dismisses criticism)

John L. O'Sullivan: Myth-Maker

IMAGE: Portrait of John L. O'Sullivan

Journalist and editor, coined the term "Manifest Destiny" in 1845

He gave expansionist desire a sacred story

O'Sullivan's Language

"...our manifest destiny to overspread and to possess the whole of the continent which Providence has given us for the development of the great experiment of liberty and federated self-government entrusted to us."

— John L. O'Sullivan, "Annexation" (1845)

Dissecting Mythic Language

  • "Manifest" = obvious, undeniable
    Claims obviousness
  • "Overspread" = organic metaphor
    Naturalizes conquest (like water flowing)
  • "Providence has given us" = divine gift
    Establishes prior claim

III. The Racial Imagination of the 1840s

Racial Hierarchy Based On:

  • Civilization stages
  • Religion
  • Environmental theories of race
  • Political capacity

Critical Point

The myth requires racial inequality to function

Without racial hierarchy, expansion = invasion

The Racial Ordering

  1. Anglo-Saxons (top)
  2. Mexicans (degraded/mixed)
  3. Native Americans ("vanishing")
  4. African Americans (enslaved/excluded)

Tier 1: Anglo-Saxons

Attributes believed:

  • Protestant faith
  • Northern climate vigor
  • "Free institutions"
  • Providence

Evidence cited: Commercial success, stable republic, technological advancement

Destiny: Spread these virtues across continent

Tier 2: Mexicans (Degraded/Mixed)

Attributes ascribed:

  • Catholic (priest-ridden)
  • Racially mixed (Spanish + Indigenous = "mongrel")
  • Politically unstable

"Evidence" cited: Mexico's coups, land "underused," skin color variation

Key anxiety: If we take territory, do we have to make them citizens?

Tier 3: Native Americans ("Vanishing")

Attributes ascribed:

  • "Savage" stage (hunters, not farmers)
  • Warlike
  • Incapable of "improvement"

Dominant theory: "Vanishing race" doomed to extinction before white civilization

Key rhetoric: "Removal" assumed they couldn't coexist with white civilization

Tier 4: African Americans (Enslaved/Excluded)

Proslavery ideology: Slavery is their "natural condition" or "positive good"

Abolitionist challenge: Enslavement caused degradation, not nature

Key contradiction: How does Manifest Destiny spread "liberty" while extending slavery?

Voices of the Racial Imagination

Three key primary sources

Senator Thomas Hart Benton (Missouri, 1846)

"The White race alone received the divine command to subdue and replenish the earth...The Red race has disappeared from the Atlantic coast; the tribes that resisted civilization met extinction."

— Sen. Thomas Hart Benton, 1846

Note: Religious-providential racism, "disappearance" as natural law

Rep. Alexander Stephens (Georgia, 1848)

"I am opposed to the acquisition of any territory which will introduce a larger number of an inferior race to be governed by us."

— Rep. Alexander Stephens, 1848

Note: Worried about governance, not genetic contamination

Sen. John C. Calhoun (South Carolina, 1848)

"Ours, sir, is the Government of a white race...We have never dreamt of incorporating into our Union any but the Caucasian race—the free white race."

— Sen. John C. Calhoun, 1848

Note: Political incorporation is the anxiety

The Circular Logic of Myth

  1. We must expand because we're racially superior ↓
  2. We're racially superior because God chose us to expand ↓
  3. God chose us because look—we ARE expanding ↓
  4. Our expansion proves we're racially superior ↑

The myth uses its own existence as evidence

IV. Cultural Nationalism & "Young America"

Expansion as Cultural Project

"Young America" movement (1840s–50s):

  • Writers, artists, intellectuals
  • Vision: American culture that's democratic, energetic, masculine, continental

Political empire + cultural empire = two halves of same project

Art as empire

V. Race, Slavery, and the Boundaries of Destiny

The Central Contradiction

Ideology promised: "Liberty for all"

Reality delivered: "Liberty for whites"

Expansion as racial filter: Who can become citizens?

Incompatible Visions

Both North and South wanted expansion, but for incompatible reasons:

South: Spread slavery, maintain political power

North: Free soil for white farmers, without slavery OR Black people

"Racial Capacity for Self-Government"

The theoretical justification for empire without democracy

The logic:

  1. Democracy is hard (requires virtue, education, self-discipline)
  2. Only Anglo-Saxons have these traits
  3. Therefore: We can take territories, but can't grant citizenship to inhabitants

This Solved Several Problems

  • Justified taking Mexican land without making Mexicans citizens
  • Justified removing Native peoples (incapable of republican institutions)
  • Justified slavery (African Americans can't handle freedom)
  • Allowed expansion without demographic integration

Why Not Annex All of Mexico?

Senator Calhoun's anxiety (1848):

Wanted the land, NOT the people

If Mexicans become citizens → racial hierarchy collapses

If hierarchy collapses → how do you justify slavery?

Consequence: Empire without democratic consent

Performing the Ideology: Expansion as Spectacle

The ideology wasn't just written—it was performed

Public Rituals & Visual Culture

  • Public rituals: Parades celebrating annexation, volunteer soldier send-offs, patriotic rallies
  • Patriotic sermons: Ministers blessing troops, interpreting war as divine will
  • Popular prints & lithographs: Mass-produced images in parlors nationwide

Ideology became public theater

Photography as "Truthful Witness"

Mexican-American War = first war extensively photographed (daguerreotypes, 1846–48)

Heroic images: American officers posed, dignified, victorious

Devastating images: Battlefield dead, destroyed Mexican towns

The paradox: Photography claimed objectivity ("camera doesn't lie") but still framed through ideology

War Photography Examples

IMAGES: Mexican-American War daguerreotypes

Include both heroic officer portraits and battlefield scenes

Side-by-side comparison if possible

What gets photographed? Who holds the camera? These choices shape "truth"

Expansion as Visual Experience

Americans consumed the war visually:

  • Lithographs in magazines
  • Paintings in galleries
  • Photographs circulated as curiosities
  • Maps showing territorial gains

They saw themselves conquering the continent

Discussion Prompt

  • What emotions did these images invite?
  • When you see a heroic officer portrait vs. a battlefield carnage photo—what does each ask you to feel?
  • How can different images (heroic vs. devastating) serve the same ideology?

VII. Evolution of the Myth

Powerful Myths Don't Die — They Adapt

Manifest Destiny didn't disappear; it evolved

1850s: Myth Starts Cracking

  • Slavery extension divides North and South
  • Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854), Bleeding Kansas, Dred Scott (1857)
  • Contradiction becomes unsustainable

Post-Civil War: Myth Gets Recycled

  • Now about "reuniting" nation through Western expansion
  • Transcontinental railroads as binding force
  • "Civilizing" the Plains (completing conquest of Native nations)

1890s–1900s: Myth Goes Global

  • Same language applied beyond continent: Philippines, Puerto Rico, Hawaii, Panama
  • "Destiny," "civilization," "improvement"
  • Theodore Roosevelt's "civilizing mission"

20th Century: "American Exceptionalism"

  • Still the chosen people with sacred mission
  • Now spreading democracy worldwide (Cold War rhetoric)
  • "Leader of the free world"

Today: Echoes Persist

  • "City on a hill" (Reagan, Obama)
  • "Make America Great Again"
  • Debates about immigration, borders, who belongs

Teaching point: Powerful myths adapt to new contexts—they don't disappear

Because Myths Aren't About What Happened

Myths are about what we want to believe happened

They make:

  • The past usable
  • The present justified
  • The future imaginable

Your Job as Historians—and Citizens

  • See the myth for what it is
  • Recognize it as powerful story that shaped real events and real lives
  • Not true or false, but functional—and still functioning today

When You Watch the Documentary

  • Don't just learn what happened
  • Watch HOW the story gets told
  • Notice whose voices you hear, whose you don't
  • Ask: Is the documentary reinforcing the myth or interrogating it?

Because how we tell the past shapes what we think is possible in the future
