## üìö Speaker Notes ‚Äî What You Need to Know

This presentation examines one of the most consequential federal policies in U.S. history: the forced displacement of tens of thousands of Indigenous people from the southeastern United States to lands west of the Mississippi River during the 1830s. This was not a spontaneous event but a coordinated program involving federal law, state coercion, and military enforcement.

### üìñ Context & Scope
While the ‚ÄúTrail of Tears‚Äù commonly refers to the Cherokee removal of 1838‚Äì1839, this presentation covers the broader removal policy affecting multiple nations: Cherokee, Choctaw, Creek (Muscogee), Chickasaw, and Seminole.

### üí≠ Why This Matters
Indian removal demonstrates how state power can use law to legitimize dispossession, how political coalitions form around territorial expansion, and how administrative choices determine life-or-death outcomes.

## What Was Indian Removal?

- Federal program to relocate Indigenous nations from east of the Mississippi to ‚ÄúIndian Territory‚Äù (present-day Oklahoma)
- Operated through a combination of law, treaties, and coercion
- Decade-spanning policy (1830s)
- Resulted in mass displacement, deaths from disease and exposure, and territorial reorganization

---

## Antecedents: The Cotton Economy

### Post-War of 1812 Expansion
- Cotton production explodes with spread of short-staple cotton
- Entrenchment of enslaved labor system
- Land becomes the critical bottleneck

### Pressure on Native Lands
- Planters, speculators, small farmers push into Indigenous homelands
- Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Tennessee
- Native landholding treated as illegitimate

---

## The ‚ÄúCivilization‚Äù Paradox

- U.S. policy encouraged Native ‚Äúcivilization‚Äù
- Southeastern nations adopted written constitutions, schools, newspapers
- Assimilation intensified conflict instead of preventing removal

---

## Georgia and the Sovereignty Crisis

- Extension of state law over Cherokee territory
- Criminalization of Cherokee governance
- Seizure and redistribution of land
- Acceleration after the 1828 gold discovery

## Worcester v. Georgia (1832)

- Supreme Court affirms Cherokee sovereignty
- Georgia ignores ruling
- Federal government refuses enforcement

---

## Treaty of New Echota (1835)

- Signed by minority faction
- Opposed by majority of Cherokee
- Ratified by Senate by one vote
- Legal basis for forced removal

---

## Removal Across the Southeast

- Choctaw: early removal, high mortality
- Creek: military force and violence
- Chickasaw: negotiated terms, severe hardship
- Seminole: armed resistance and war

---

## The Trail of Tears (1838‚Äì1839)

- Military roundup into detention camps
- Winter marches over 1,000 miles
- Disease, exposure, starvation

---

## The Death Toll

**~4,000 Cherokee deaths**  
Approximately one-fifth of the population, based on demographic reconstruction by Russell Thornton.

---

## What Killed People?

### Disease
- Dysentery
- Respiratory infections
- Measles
- Cholera

### Exposure
- Winter cold
- Malnutrition
- Exhaustion

---

## Aftermath in Indian Territory

- Rebuilding under extreme hardship
- Political division and violence
- Long-term demographic and cultural trauma
