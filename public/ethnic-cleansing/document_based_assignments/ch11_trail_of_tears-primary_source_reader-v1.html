Trail of Tears — Primary Source Reader

Slow reading • Quiet analysis • Evidence-based thinking

The Cherokees are nearly all prisoners. They have been dragged from their homes and camped at the forts.... The most unfeeling and insulting treatment has been experienced by them.... Multitudes were not allowed to take anything with them but the clothes they had on. Well furnished houses were left a prey to plunderers.... It is a painful sight ... the soldiers standing with guns and bayonets...

— Evan Jones, June 16, 1838

Source: Missionary letter

URL: Missionary Correspondence Archive

Date: June 16, 1838

See also

URL: HMdb.org The Historical Marker Database

Evan Jones was a Baptist missionary working among the Cherokee. This letter reports directly from a military holding camp during the forced roundup. Key interpretive move: Jones emphasizes the word "prisoners." His choice carries a legal and moral claim — this was involuntary detention, not migration. Historical thinking prompt: - What does the language of imprisonment imply about state intent? - How does a religious witness function rhetorically in political history?
Cherokees! The President of the United States has sent me, with a powerful army, to cause you, in obedience to the Treaty of 1835, to join that part of your people who are already established in prosperity, on the other side of the Mississippi. . . . Every Cherokee man, woman and child . . . must be in motion to join their brethren in the far West.

— Gen. Winfield Scott, May 24, 1838

Source: Removal Orders

URL: NPS Archive

Date: May 24, 1838

See also: - NARA DocsTeach (Order No. 25, May 17, 1838) — useful corroboration: https://docsteach.org/document/scott-order-25/ - NPS overview page for context: https://www.nps.gov/articles/000/winfield-scott-orders-1838.htm This is the formal order initiating mass removal. Scott's directive applies to every Cherokee — totalizing and categorical. Notice: The verb "compelled." There is no ambiguity here. Removal was not optional, negotiated, or voluntary. Analytical lens: This is the administrative voice of the state — legal command as violence.
The people suffer extremely in the stockades. Many are sick, and some die daily.

— Daniel Butrick, June 1838

Source: ABCFM missionary diary

URL: National Museum of the American Indian

Date: June 1838

See also: - Scholarly article discussing/quoting the diary (PDF): https://perspectivia.net/servlets/MCRFileNodeServlet/pnet_derivate_00004216/garrison_trail.pdf - Cataloging the diary itself: https://www.familysearch.org/en/search/catalog/1162329 • https://search.library.wisc.edu/catalog/999931951102121 Butrick describes conditions *created* by removal policy: confinement, illness, death. Key shift: Violence here is logistical, not battlefield — lethal conditions as method. This distinguishes *physical eradication* from *administrative elimination*. Thinking move: Ask what intent looks like when death results from "conditions" rather than blades or bullets.
A nation of people, peaceful and unoffending, is to be driven from their homes and country.

— Cherokee Phoenix, March 1838

Source: Cherokee Nation newspaper

URL: Digital Archive

Date: March 1838

Indigenous voice. Not outsider pity — internal diagnosis. Rhetorical power: "Nation" — a political body, not a tribe or population. "Driven" — not leaving, not relocating, but forced out. Interpretive practice: Indigenous press asserts sovereignty and experience. Students should privilege primary Indigenous voices, not just federal or missionary ones.
I was sent as interpreter into the Smoky Mountain Country in May, 1838, and witnessed the execution of the most brutal order in the History of American Warfare. I saw the helpless Cherokees arrested and dragged from their homes, and driven at the bayonet point into the stockades. And in the chill of a drizzling rain on an October morning I saw them loaded like cattle or sheep into six hundred and forty-five wagons and started toward the west.

— John G. Burnett, 1890

Source: Veteran's reminiscence

URL: Anchor Digital Textbook

Date: 1890

Memory source — emotionally gripping, historically tricky. Classroom truth: Memory is evidence, but not the same as contemporaneous documentation. Scholarly move: Corroborate emotional testimony with administrative records and Indigenous press. Why include it? Because memory shows *how witnesses needed to remember* — conscience speaking.
The Cherokee Nation hereby cede to the United States all the land… east of the Mississippi River.

— Treaty of New Echota, 1835

Source: Treaty text

URL: Smithsonian

Date: December 29, 1835

Legal instrument. Signed by a minority — not the Cherokee government. Key concept: Law can be both a tool of power and a disguise for coercion. Ask students: Does legality justify action? What happens when sovereignty is violated through law?
