The judiciary acted as a brake on Progressive reform. Even when Congress passed laws reflecting public will, the courts could nullify them by declaring they exceeded constitutional authority. The 20-year gap between the NCLC's peak lobbying (1916-1924) and ultimate success (1938) reveals a fundamental truth about American reform: exposure and public pressure can shift culture, but changing law requires either judicial cooperation or constitutional transformation.

Hine's quote about showing "things that had to be corrected" and "things that had to be appreciated" captures his dual vision: documentary photography could critique exploitation while also celebrating human dignity. Both impulses drove his work throughout his career.
