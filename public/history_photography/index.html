Photography transformed how Americans understood their nation and themselves. From the daguerreotype studios of the 1840s to the documentary work of the Great Depression, cameras captured moments of triumph and tragedy, bearing witness to the American experience in ways words alone could not.

This gallery explores the dual nature of photography as both art and evidence‚Äîa medium that shaped public consciousness while documenting the realities of American life. Through the lens, we see battlefields and reform movements, portraits of power and images of poverty, the constructed and the captured.

Lewis Hine & Industrial America

Child Labor and Progressive Reform

Hine's photographs of child workers in mills, mines, and factories provided visual evidence that fueled Progressive-era labor reforms. His images documented exploitation while celebrating workers' dignity and strength.

A Note on Historical Sources: These presentations examine photographs as both primary sources and constructed artifacts. We analyze not just what cameras captured, but what photographers chose to frame, how they staged scenes, and how images were circulated and interpreted by different audiences. Photography is never neutral‚Äîit is always shaped by the photographer's perspective, available technology, and intended purpose.
