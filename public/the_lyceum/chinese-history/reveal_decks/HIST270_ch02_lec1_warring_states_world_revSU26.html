📖 Lecture Overview

Chapter 1 ended with a warning: the Zhou held their realm with kinship and ritual, but domains became hereditary and kinship fades across generations. This lecture is the consequence. When the Western Zhou collapsed in 771 BCE, central authority drained away — and what rushed in was five centuries of escalating competition among independent states. The argument of the lecture is that interstate competition was an engine of transformation: it remade government, warfare, the economy, and the social order itself, and in doing so created the conditions for the greatest age of philosophy in Chinese history. Four review questions track four faces of that engine.

Review Questions Covered

Q1 — How did the political structure of the Eastern Zhou differ from the Western Zhou, and what was the role of the "hegemon"?
Q2 — How did warfare transform from the Spring and Autumn to the Warring States period?
Q3 — What economic and demographic transformations drove the decline of serfdom?
Q4 — How did the shi class emerge, and how did the new landscape let them influence statecraft?

📖 Roadmap Notes

The unifying thread is competition under anarchy. With no central referee after 771 BCE, states that failed to adapt were conquered and erased. That brutal selection pressure drove every change in this lecture: rulers who could field bigger armies (Q2), grow more food and tax it directly (Q3), and recruit the best advisers regardless of birth (Q4) survived; those who couldn't, didn't. Q1 sets up the political arena; Q2–Q4 are the transformations that arena forced. Keep returning to the engine — each review question is competition expressing itself in a different domain.

Part I

From One Order to Many States

The Eastern Zhou and the hegemon — Q1

771 BCE: The Center Cannot Hold

  • Western Zhou: the king held real authority over subordinate lords
  • Eastern Zhou: the capital moves east; the king becomes a figurehead
  • Power flows to increasingly independent states — a true multistate system
  • Two phases: Spring and Autumn, then the Warring States

One Zhou realm fractures into rival states

📖 Understanding This Slide

Pick up exactly where Chapter 1 left off. In 771 BCE non-Chinese tribesmen and rebellious vassals killed the Zhou king, and the royal house fled east to Luoyang. The dynasty survived in name — historians call everything after this the Eastern Zhou — but the substance of royal power was gone.

Breaking It Down

The before and after: Under the Western Zhou, the king genuinely commanded his lords through the kinship-and-ritual system you studied last chapter. Under the Eastern Zhou, the relationship inverts: the king reigns but does not rule, and the once-subordinate states act as independent powers. This is the core of the Q1 contrast — real central authority versus a multistate system of competitors.

The two phases: The Eastern Zhou splits into the Spring and Autumn period (named for a chronicle of the era), when many states still observed Zhou forms, and the later Warring States period, when the gloves came off and states fought openly for supremacy and survival. The drift across these phases is from a system that still pretended to be the Zhou order toward one that was nakedly about power.

Why This Matters

This slide establishes the political arena. The hegemon on the next slide is the institution that tried — and ultimately failed — to paper over the gap left by the absent king.

The Hegemon: A King-Sized Hole

  • The strongest ruler was recognized as hegemon — leader of an alliance of states
  • He acted in the name of the powerless Zhou king, preserving the order's facade
  • A revealing compromise: real power was now earned by strength, not inherited by right
The hegemon proves the Zhou ideal still mattered — and that nobody believed the king could enforce it.

Covenant ritual still spoke the language of Zhou legitimacy

📖 Understanding This Slide

The hegemon (ba) is one of those institutions that tells you more by its contradictions than by its function. It was a workaround for a structural problem: the Zhou king was the only legitimate source of authority over the states, but he had no power — so who keeps order?

📌 Textbook Anchor

"During this period, the ruler of one state would sometimes be recognized as the hegemon, the leader of the alliance of Zhou states." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 21. This is the Q1 anchor.

Breaking It Down

How it worked: Periodically the ruler of the strongest state would be acknowledged by the others as hegemon — the recognized leader of an alliance of Zhou states. He convened the lords, organized common defense, and disciplined troublemakers, all formally on behalf of the Zhou king. The first and most famous was Duke Huan of Qi, guided by his brilliant minister Guan Zhong.

What it reveals: Read the institution closely. The hegemon still ruled "in the name of" the Zhou king, which means the Zhou ideal of a single legitimate order remained powerful — nobody yet claimed to replace the king. But the hegemon was always the strongest ruler, which means everyone understood that actual power now came from military and economic might, not from royal appointment. The hegemon is the hinge between an old world of inherited legitimacy and a new world of earned power.

Its failure: The system was inherently unstable — it depended on one state being clearly strongest and willing to bear the costs of leadership. As multiple states grew powerful, hegemony broke down into the open warfare of the Warring States period.

Why This Matters

The full causal Q1 answer: where the Western Zhou king exercised real authority, the Eastern Zhou became a multistate system of increasingly independent states — and the hegemon served as the strongest ruler's recognized leadership of an alliance, preserving the Zhou order's form while exposing that power now flowed from strength, not royal right.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Cho-yun Hsu – Ancient China in Transition

Hsu's classic study traces how the Spring and Autumn interstate system gradually dissolved the old aristocratic order. He shows the hegemony as a transitional institution — an attempt to maintain collective order among states that were simultaneously becoming sovereign competitors. The contradiction the slide highlights is Hsu's central theme.

Part II

The Transformation of War

From chariot honor to total war — Q2

War as an Aristocratic Sport

  • Spring and Autumn warfare: aristocrats fighting from chariots
  • Governed by codes of honor and ritual conventions
  • Small-scale, seasonal, limited — battles between nobles, not nations
War was a contest of aristocratic prestige — bounded by rules that everyone shared.

The chariot: expensive, aristocratic, and soon obsolete

📖 Understanding This Slide

To see how dramatically war changed, you need the starting point. Early Eastern Zhou warfare was an aristocratic affair, and it had more in common with a ritualized tournament than with modern war. The chariot — the prestige weapon the Shang imported across the steppe, as you saw in Chapter 1 — was at its center.

Breaking It Down

Who fought: Combat belonged to the aristocracy. A noble rode to battle in a chariot, accompanied by a modest body of foot soldiers. Owning and maintaining chariots, horses, and bronze weapons was enormously expensive, which kept warfare in elite hands.

The codes of honor: Spring and Autumn warfare was constrained by shared conventions about how nobles should behave — conventions that could look chivalric or even absurd to later eyes (waiting for an enemy to form ranks, not striking a wounded opponent twice). The point is that combatants shared an aristocratic culture and fought within agreed limits. War was bounded.

The scale: Battles were comparatively small, seasonal, and limited in their aims. They settled questions of prestige and dominance among nobles without mobilizing or devastating whole societies.

Why This Matters

This is the "before" half of Q2. Everything about it — who fights, by what rules, at what scale — is about to be overturned by the competitive pressure of the Warring States.

The Coming of Total War

  • Massed infantry of conscripted commoners — armies in the hundreds of thousands
  • New tools of war: cavalry, siege warfare, the crossbow
  • Honor codes vanish; the goal becomes annihilation and conquest
  • The state mobilizes its whole society for war
Infantry that could defeat chariots meant commoners now decided the fate of states.

The crossbow trigger: precision-cast, deadly, and democratizing

📖 Understanding This Slide

Now the transformation. As states competed for survival, the constraints on warfare fell away one by one, and war scaled up into something that could mobilize and devastate entire societies. The key that unlocks the whole change is on the anchor.

📌 Textbook Anchor

"Large, well-drilled infantry armies were becoming a potent military force, able to withstand and defeat chariot-led forces." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 23. This is the Q2 anchor — and it's the hinge of a social revolution, not just a tactical note.

Breaking It Down

Infantry over chariots: Once large, disciplined infantry armies could reliably defeat chariot-led aristocratic forces, the entire logic of war changed. You no longer won with a few hundred noble charioteers; you won with tens or hundreds of thousands of drilled foot soldiers — and those had to be commoners, conscripted from the farming population.

New technologies: Cavalry added mobility (a steppe borrowing). Siege warfare emerged as states attacked each other's walled cities. And the crossbow — with its precision-cast bronze trigger mechanism — was a mass-producible weapon a conscript could be trained to use quickly, lethal even against armored aristocrats. Technology and mass mobilization reinforced each other.

The end of limits: The honor codes disappeared. Warring States conflict aimed at conquest and annihilation; sources describe staggering casualties. War became total in the sense that it drew on the entire resources and population of the state.

The social consequence: This is why Q2 matters beyond military history. When commoners in the infantry decide the fate of states, the state has powerful reasons to count them, tax them, and manage them directly — which leads straight into Q3 and the decline of serfdom. War was an engine of social change.

Why This Matters

The causal Q2 answer: warfare evolved from small aristocratic chariot combat bound by honor codes into massive conflicts of conscripted infantry, cavalry, siege warfare, and the crossbow — far more destructive, mobilizing whole societies, and shifting the decisive role from nobles to commoners.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Mark Edward Lewis – Sanctioned Violence in Early China

Lewis argues that the transformation of warfare and the transformation of the state were the same process: mass infantry armies required registration, taxation, and direct administration of the population, building the machinery of the centralized state. War didn't just change society — for Lewis, organized violence built the early Chinese state.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

When a drilled peasant with a crossbow could defeat a noble in a chariot, the social order itself was on the battlefield.

Part III

A New Economy, a New Society

Iron, taxes, and the decline of serfdom — Q3

Iron, Irrigation, and Surplus

  • Rulers expand farmland through irrigation and land clearance
  • Iron tools raise agricultural output dramatically
  • More food → population growth → more soldiers and taxpayers
A bigger harvest wasn't just prosperity — it was military and fiscal power.

Cast iron in the fields — cheap tools, bigger harvests

📖 Understanding This Slide

Q3 is where competition reaches into the soil. A state at constant risk of conquest needs two things above all: food to feed armies, and revenue to pay for them. Both pushed rulers to revolutionize agriculture — and the technology was ready.

Breaking It Down

Expanding the land: Rulers sponsored large irrigation and water-control projects and opened new land to cultivation. This was a deliberate state policy, not just organic growth — more farmland meant more taxable production and more population.

Iron: China developed cast-iron technology centuries before Europe. Iron tools — plowshares, hoes, sickles — were cheaper and more effective than bronze or stone, so they spread to ordinary farmers and raised output significantly. Note the contrast with Chapter 1, where farmers worked with near-Neolithic stone tools while bronze stayed in elite ritual hands. Iron was a commoner's technology, and it changed the economic base.

The compounding effect: More land plus better tools meant surplus; surplus meant population growth; a larger population meant more infantry (Q2) and more taxpayers (next slide). Each gain fed the others — a feedback loop driven by interstate competition.

Why This Matters

This is the economic engine behind Q3. The next slide shows how rulers converted this agricultural surplus into a direct relationship with the peasantry — and dissolved serfdom in the process.

Cutting Out the Middleman

  • Rulers wanted direct access to peasants' labor — bypassing the old lords
  • They taxed farmers directly and undermined lords' power over serfs
  • Serfdom declines; even commoners begin acquiring family names
Destroying the lords' grip on serfs centralized the state and freed the peasant — two effects of one policy.

Spade and knife coins — a monetizing, taxable economy

📖 Understanding This Slide

Here's the political payoff of the economic story — and it's a beautiful example of how a ruler's self-interest produced a sweeping social change almost as a side effect. Rulers weren't trying to liberate anyone; they were trying to get their hands on resources the old aristocracy controlled.

📌 Textbook Anchor

"They wanted to undermine the power of lords over their serfs in order to get direct access to peasants' labor power." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 24. This is the Q3 anchor — notice the motive is fiscal, not humanitarian.

Breaking It Down

The old arrangement: Under the Zhou manorial system (Chapter 1), peasants were serfs bound to aristocratic lords, who controlled their labor and stood between them and the ruler. The ruler couldn't reach the peasant directly — the lord was the middleman.

The rulers' move: Competitive rulers wanted that labor and revenue for the state. So they deliberately undermined the lords' power over serfs — registering peasants, taxing them directly, and granting them land to farm on the state's behalf. The serf became, in effect, a taxpaying subject of the ruler rather than a dependent of a lord.

Two consequences at once: This single policy did two things. It centralized power by destroying the aristocracy's economic base and binding peasants directly to the state. And it dissolved serfdom — peasants gained a more direct, freer status, and the spread of family names to commoners (once an aristocratic privilege) signals their rising standing. Liberation and centralization were the same act seen from two angles.

Why This Matters

The causal Q3 answer: rulers expanded agriculture through irrigation and new land, iron tools raised output, and rulers taxed farmers directly to undermine the lords — producing the decline of serfdom and the spread of family names to commoners. The driver throughout is competitive rulers seeking resources, not social ideals.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Cho-yun Hsu – Ancient China in Transition

Hsu documented how the old hereditary aristocracy was hollowed out across the Eastern Zhou as rulers built direct ties to the population. The decline of serfdom and the rise of commoners are, in his account, the social underside of state centralization — the same transformation that produces the shi class in the next section.

Rulers freed peasants from their lords not out of mercy, but because they wanted the peasants' labor for themselves.

Part IV

The Rise of the Shi

Talent, opportunity, and the Hundred Schools — Q4

A New Class of Talent

  • The shi: lower-level aristocrats — and nobles of conquered states who lost their lands
  • Educated and ambitious, but without inherited position
  • Their only asset was skill: literacy, strategy, administration, persuasion
A class defined not by birth or land, but by what it could do.

Bamboo slips: the shi traded in words, knowledge, and counsel

📖 Understanding This Slide

The shi are the human product of everything in this lecture — and they're about to become the most consequential group in Chinese intellectual history. You met the term in Chapter 1 as the lowest rank of the Zhou aristocracy. In the Eastern Zhou, that modest rank explodes in importance.

Breaking It Down

Where they came from: Two streams fed the shi. First, the lower ranks of the existing aristocracy — men with education and noble status but little land or hereditary office. Second, and increasingly, the displaced elites of conquered states: as states swallowed each other (Q1–Q2), whole noble families lost their domains and titles but kept their education. The result was a growing pool of cultivated, ambitious men with no secure place in the old order.

What defined them: Not birth, not land — capability. The shi were literate in an age when literacy was power, and they offered rulers practical skills: administration, military strategy, diplomacy, law, persuasion, ritual expertise. In a world being remade by competition, those skills were exactly what states needed.

Why This Matters

This slide sets up the Q4 payoff. A class defined by talent rather than birth needs only one thing to transform a civilization: a market for its talent. The next slide shows competitive rulers creating exactly that market.

Competition Creates Opportunity

  • Rival rulers competed to recruit the most talented advisers
  • A skilled shi could rise high by serving a ruler well — advancement by merit
  • This open market for ideas fueled the Hundred Schools of Thought
States needed every advantage — so they bought ideas. The result was a golden age of philosophy.

The Hundred Schools: an arms race in ideas

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the climax of the lecture, where every thread ties together. The political fragmentation (Q1), the demands of total war (Q2), and the drive for resources (Q3) all created rulers desperate for any competitive edge — and that desperation became the shi's opportunity.

📌 Textbook Anchor

"For the shi (lower-level aristocrats), serving a ruler in this way offered new opportunities for advancement." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 26. This is the Q4 anchor.

Breaking It Down

The market: Because states were locked in survival competition, rulers actively sought out talented men to advise them on government, war, and economy — and they couldn't afford to care about pedigree. A shi who could strengthen a state might be raised from obscurity to high office. Advancement now depended on demonstrated ability, an extraordinary break from a world of inherited position.

The intellectual explosion: Here's the payoff that makes this chapter pivotal. With rulers hungry for ideas and shi competing to offer the most compelling vision of good government, an open marketplace of philosophy emerged — the Hundred Schools of Thought. Confucians, Mohists, Daoists, Legalists, and others all developed rival answers to the era's central question: how do you create order out of chaos? The disorder of the age generated its philosophy.

The bridge: This is why Chapter 2 pairs "Warring States" with "Philosophers" in its title. The next two lectures are the content of the Hundred Schools — and they exist because of the social transformation you've traced today. Confucius himself was a shi.

Why This Matters

The causal Q4 answer: the shi emerged from the lower aristocracy and from displaced nobles of conquered states; as competitive rulers sought talented advisers, the shi gained new opportunities for advancement by merit — fueling the intellectual flowering of the Hundred Schools of Thought.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Yuri Pines – Envisioning Eternal Empire

Pines argues that the shi thinkers of this era did more than advise rulers — they generated the political ideas that would define Chinese statecraft for two millennia, including the ideal of unified empire itself. The "marketplace of ideas" wasn't just career opportunity; for Pines it was the workshop where China's enduring political tradition was forged.

Spring and Autumn Period (770–476 BCE)

The first phase of the Eastern Zhou, named for the Spring and Autumn Annals, a chronicle of the state of Lu. Many states still observed Zhou ritual forms and the hegemon system maintained a fragile collective order, even as the states grew increasingly independent. Warfare remained relatively limited and aristocratic.

Warring States Period (475–221 BCE)

The second phase of the Eastern Zhou, when the pretense of Zhou order collapsed and a handful of large states fought openly for supremacy and survival. Warfare became massive and total; states centralized, mobilized their populations, and competed for talent. The period ends in 221 BCE when Qin conquers all rivals and founds the first unified empire.

The War Chariot

The prestige weapon of Spring and Autumn warfare: a horse-drawn platform from which an aristocrat fought, supported by a small body of foot soldiers. Owning chariots, horses, and bronze weapons was expensive, which kept war in elite hands. As large infantry armies learned to defeat chariot-led forces, the chariot — and the aristocratic warrior culture built around it — became obsolete.

The Crossbow

A mechanical bow with a precision-cast bronze trigger mechanism, developed in the Warring States period. Its great advantage was accessibility: a conscript could be trained to use it quickly, and its bolts could penetrate armor and kill an aristocrat at range. Mass-produced and deadly, the crossbow helped make infantry armies of commoners militarily decisive — undermining the battlefield dominance of the noble charioteer.

Hundred Schools of Thought (諸子百家)

The flowering of Chinese philosophy during the Eastern Zhou, when competing thinkers — Confucians, Mohists, Daoists, Legalists, and many others — developed rival answers to the era's central problem: how to create order out of chaos.
