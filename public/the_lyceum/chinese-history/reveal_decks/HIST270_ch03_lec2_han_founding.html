üìö Lecture Overview

This lecture covers the founding of the Han Dynasty (206 BCE‚Äì9 CE), examining how Liu Bang rose from commoner to emperor, the evolution of Han governance, Emperor Wu's Confucian turn, and the dynamics of court politics that led to Wang Mang's interregnum.

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Compare Han governance with Qin‚Äîwhat changed and what remained
  • Explain the adoption of Confucianism as state ideology under Emperor Wu
  • Analyze the role of empresses, eunuchs, and consort families in Han politics

The Five Major Dynasties

Chinese history is often organized around five "great" unified dynasties that each lasted more than two centuries:

  1. Han (206 BCE‚Äì220 CE) ‚Äî ~400 years
  2. Tang (618‚Äì907 CE) ‚Äî ~300 years
  3. Song (960‚Äì1279 CE) ‚Äî ~300 years
  4. Ming (1368‚Äì1644 CE) ‚Äî ~275 years
  5. Qing (1644‚Äì1912 CE) ‚Äî ~270 years

Each set lasting patterns for Chinese civilization that influenced all successors.

üìö Opening Hook

Start by reminding students of Qin's rapid collapse. The Han founders faced a genuine puzzle: how to maintain a unified empire without Qin's brutal methods? Their solution shaped Chinese governance for the next 2,000 years.

üéØ Key Point

From the textbook: "The Han Dynasty is the first of the five major dynasties that lasted more than two and a half centuries (Han, Tang, Song, Ming, and Qing), and scholars often look at the Han with these later dynasties in mind."

üí° Cultural Legacy

The ethnic identity "Han people" (Hanren) and "Han characters" (Hanzi) show how deeply the Han Dynasty shaped Chinese identity. No other dynasty gave its name to the Chinese people themselves.

The Civil War (206‚Äì202 BCE)

After Qin Fell

  • Xiang Yu dominated militarily
  • Divided empire among 18 kings
  • Liu Bang received remote western territory (King of Han)
  • This recreated the old fragmentation

Liu Bang's Advantages

  • Attracted talented advisors and generals
  • Built coalitions through rewards and promises
  • Xiang Yu made enemies through cruelty
  • Gradually consolidated power

Xiang Yu committed suicide rather than surrender

üìö The Turning Point

The Battle of Gaixia (202 BCE) ended the civil war. According to legend, Liu Bang's forces surrounded Xiang Yu and sang songs from Chu (Xiang Yu's home region) to demoralize his troops‚Äîshowing how isolated Xiang Yu had become.

üéØ Key Points

  • Xiang Yu's decision to divide the empire among 18 kings recreated the old Warring States chaos
  • Liu Bang understood that China needed unity‚Äîbut achieved through persuasion rather than force alone
  • The name "Han" came from Liu Bang's title as "King of Han" during the civil war

üìö The First Han Emperor

From the textbook: "Emperor Gao did not disband the centralized government created by Qin but he did remove its most unpopular features. He set up his capital at Chang'an, not far from the old Qin capital."

üéØ Key Decision

Liu Bang's choice to keep Qin's administrative structure while softening its methods was crucial. He understood that the centralized bureaucracy was an effective tool‚Äîthe problem was how it had been used, not the tool itself.

üí° Teaching Note

Explain the difference between personal names, temple names, and reign era names. Chinese history uses all three, which can confuse students initially.

Early Han Reforms

Lightening the Burden

  • Eliminated some laws‚Äîremoved Qin's most hated provisions
  • Cut taxes‚Äîreduced to one-fifteenth of harvest (later one-thirtieth!)
  • Lessened labor demands‚Äîfewer massive construction projects
  • Abolished mutilation punishments‚Äîstopped branding, cutting off noses, etc.
"After a century of almost constant war and huge labor mobilizations, China was given several decades to recover. Responding to the desire to restore the old order, Emperor Gao gave out large and nearly autonomous fiefs to his relatives and chief generals."
‚Äî Ebrey, Cambridge Illustrated History of China

Philosophy: "Resting with the people" (ËàáÊ∞ë‰ºëÊÅØ)‚Äîlet society recover from war

üìö Policy of Recovery

The early Han policy of minimal intervention drew from both Daoist and Confucian ideas. The Daoist text Laozi had advised rulers to "be tranquil and unassertive" and "keep taxes low." The early Han actually implemented this advice.

üéØ Key Points from Textbook

"Emperor Gao did not disband the centralized government created by Qin but he did remove its most unpopular features... He eliminated some laws, cut taxes, and lessened the burdens on the people."

üí° Comparison to Qin

Under Qin, taxes were high and labor demands constant. Under early Han, the state deliberately stepped back. This "rest" period allowed the population and economy to recover‚Äîlaying the foundation for later expansion under Emperor Wu.

A Mixed System: Kingdoms and Commanderies

Western Half

  • Commanderies and counties
  • Directly administered by central government
  • Like the Qin system
  • Officials appointed by emperor

Eastern Half

  • Kingdoms (ÁéãÂúã)
  • Given to relatives and allies
  • Nearly autonomous governance
  • Reward for helping win the war

The Problem: "Very soon he [Gaozu] recognized that giving followers independent resources was a mistake, and he spent much of his reign eliminating the fief holders who were not relatives."

üìö The Compromise

Liu Bang faced a practical problem: he couldn't simply impose Qin-style centralization. He owed his victory to generals and allies who expected rewards. The solution was a compromise‚Äîdirect control in the west, kingdoms in the east.

üéØ Key Insight

From the textbook: "Very soon he recognized that giving followers independent resources was a mistake, and he spent much of his reign eliminating the fief holders who were not relatives. After his death, the fiefs of imperial relatives were also gradually reduced in size."

üí° Foreshadowing

This compromise would cause problems. The kingdoms would eventually rebel (154 BCE), forcing the Han to recentralize. Note the pattern: practical short-term compromises creating long-term problems.

The Rebellion of the Seven Kings

  • Kings controlled territories and could become threats
  • Central government tried to reduce kingdom powers
  • 154 BCE: Seven kings rebelled against Emperor Jing
  • Rebellion crushed within three months
  • Demonstrated the danger of divided authority

Aftermath

  • Subsequent emperors gradually reduced kingdom sizes
  • Kings lost right to appoint their own officials
  • Kingdoms became largely ceremonial by Emperor Wu's reign

The Rebellion of the Seven Kings (154 BCE)

The most serious internal threat to the early Han:

  • Seven kingdoms (Wu, Chu, Zhao, Jiaoxi, Jiaodong, Zichuan, Jinan) rose in revolt
  • Led by the King of Wu, who had enormous wealth from salt and copper
  • Claimed to be removing "evil ministers" from the emperor's court
  • Imperial armies defeated them decisively within three months
  • All seven kings were killed or forced to commit suicide

The rebellion proved that the kingdom system was too dangerous to maintain. Centralization accelerated afterward.

üìö The Crisis

The Rebellion of the Seven Kings was a turning point. It demonstrated that the compromise with feudalism was unsustainable‚Äîautonomous kingdoms would inevitably challenge central authority.

üéØ Key Outcome

After 154 BCE, the Han moved steadily toward greater centralization. By Emperor Wu's reign, kingdoms were nominal titles without real power. The Qin model of direct bureaucratic control proved more durable than feudal arrangements.

üí° Pattern Recognition

This pattern‚Äîcompromising with powerful figures, then gradually reasserting central control‚Äîwould repeat throughout Chinese history. It's a useful framework for understanding dynastic politics.

üìö The Great Transformation

Emperor Wu marks a turning point. The early Han policy of "resting with the people" gave way to aggressive expansion and active governance. He built up armies, launched campaigns, and established Confucianism as the official ideology.

üéØ Key Point

From the textbook: "Emperor Wu was the first Han emperor to privilege Confucian scholars within the government." This decision would shape Chinese governance for the next 2,000 years.

üí° Discussion Setup

Emperor Wu's reign raises important questions: Was active expansion good for the empire? What were the costs of his policies? We'll return to these questions in Lecture 3 when we discuss the Xiongnu wars and Silk Road.

Note the parallel with Li Si's advice to the First Emperor to burn books. Dong Zhongshu essentially proposed privileging one school and suppressing others‚Äîusing state power for intellectual conformity. The method was similar to Legalism, but the content was Confucian.

üí° Teaching Note

Students often ask: Were these really Confucius's own teachings? The answer is complicated. These texts are ancient compilations that Confucius may have edited or commented on, but they weren't written by him. Han scholars studied them as the authoritative source of wisdom.

The Scholar-Official System

A distinctive feature of Chinese government for 2,000 years:

  • Officials trained in Confucian values and classics
  • Expected to admonish the ruler against misguided policies
  • Moral education emphasized over harsh punishments
  • Officials with Confucian values didn't need to be supervised as closely
"Because one of the highest duties of the Confucian scholar was to admonish the ruler against misguided policies, officials whose educations imbued them with Confucian values did not comply automatically with the emperor's wishes."
‚Äî Ebrey, Cambridge Illustrated History of China

üìö A Different Kind of Official

From the textbook: "Still, emperors found employing Confucian scholars as officials efficient; because of their ingrained sense of duty, they did not have to be supervised as closely as the Legalist model required."

üéØ Key Insight

This is the key to the Confucian-Legalist synthesis. Legalism required constant supervision and harsh punishments to keep officials in line. Confucianism created officials who internalized values of duty and service‚Äîthey supervised themselves.

üí° But Also...

The textbook adds a caveat: "That did not mean that emperors took all aspects of the Confucian model of governing to heart themselves or always treated their Confucian officials with respect. Emperor Wu was so averse to criticism that he once had an official executed on the charge that a wry twist of his lips showed that he disapproved in his heart."

The Confucian-Legalist Synthesis

Han government drew from both traditions:

From Confucianism

  • Moral education and cultivation
  • Scholar-official class
  • Rituals and ceremonies
  • Hierarchy based on virtue
  • Officials who could criticize

From Legalism

  • Clear laws and procedures
  • Bureaucratic organization
  • Rewards and punishments
  • Centralized control
  • Performance evaluation

This synthesis became the foundation of Chinese imperial governance.

üìö The Practical Synthesis

From the textbook: "The relative success of the Han form of government validated the imperial system, which drew from both Confucian rhetoric and Legalist bureaucratic methods."

üéØ Key Insight

The synthesis wasn't hypocritical‚Äîit was practical. Confucianism provided legitimacy and values; Legalism provided methods and structure. Together they created a system more durable than either could have achieved alone.

üí° Discussion Question

Is this combination hypocritical, or a practical way to govern a large empire? (There's no single right answer‚Äîencourage students to think about the tensions and trade-offs.)

The Vulnerability of the Imperial Institution

A Structural Problem

  • Hereditary succession meant young children could become emperor
  • First century BCE: several boys succeeded to the throne
  • Adult men of the imperial lineage kept out of the capital (seen as threats)
  • This left power vacuums filled by others

Who filled the vacuum?

  • Empress Dowagers ‚Äî mothers/grandmothers of young emperors
  • Consort families ‚Äî relatives of empresses
  • Eunuchs ‚Äî palace servants with constant access to the emperor

üìö The Problem with Child Emperors

From the textbook: "The Han practice of hereditary succession to the throne from father to son meant that the heir might be a young child. During the last decades of the first century B.C.E., several boys succeeded to the throne."

üéØ Key Insight

The Han created a dilemma: adult male relatives were threats to a young emperor, so they were kept away from court. But this left the child emperor surrounded by women (his mother's family) and eunuchs (palace servants). These groups gained enormous power.

üí° Pattern Recognition

This pattern‚Äîchild emperors, powerful empresses, court factions‚Äîwould repeat throughout Chinese history. It's not unique to the Han, but the Han established the template.

Palace Eunuchs

Who Were Eunuchs?

  • Castrated men who served in the palace
  • Originally managed the emperor's private quarters (especially women's quarters)
  • Had constant access to the emperor
  • Could be trusted not to start rival dynasties (no heirs)

Growing Power

  • Emperors often trusted eunuchs more than officials
  • By late Han: eunuchs became a powerful political faction
  • Competed with scholar-officials for influence
  • Court officials looked down on them as "southern barbarians"
×

Palace Eunuchs (ÂÆ¶ÂÆò hu√†nguƒÅn)

A distinctive feature of Chinese (and other East Asian) court politics:

  • Castration could be voluntary (for career advancement) or punishment
  • Some came from families in the castration profession; others were captured "southern barbarians"
  • Could rise to great wealth and power through palace service
  • Scholar-officials despised them but couldn't match their access to the emperor
  • By the Eastern Han, eunuchs formed a faction that could make or break emperors

From the textbook: "Court officials looked on palace eunuchs with contempt. Emperors who had grown up with them, however, often saw them as more reliable than officials because they had no outside base of power."

üìö The Eunuch Phenomenon

From the textbook: "During the Eastern Han period, eunuchs were able to build a base of power within the palace, with the result that weak emperors became their captives rather than their masters."

üéØ Key Points

  • Eunuchs had structural advantages: constant access, no competing loyalties (no families)
  • Their power grew when emperors were weak or isolated
  • The conflict between eunuchs and scholar-officials became a defining feature of later Han politics

üí° Modern Parallel

Students sometimes struggle with this concept. A modern parallel might be the power of presidential staffers vs. cabinet officials‚Äîthose with daily access often have more influence than those with formal authority.

üìö Wang Mang's Path to Power

From the textbook: "Wang Mang (wahng mahng) came to power as a relative of Empress Wang (d. 13 C.E.), who for forty years had been influential at court as the widow of one emperor, mother of a second, and grandmother of a third."

üéØ Key Points

  • Wang Mang represents the ultimate example of consort family power
  • He was genuinely learned‚Äîthis wasn't just a power grab
  • He believed he could create a better government based on the classics

üí° Irony

Wang Mang was condemned as a usurper, but he was actually a sincere Confucian who tried to implement policies described in ancient texts. His failure raises questions about the relationship between ideals and practical governance.

Wang Mang's Reforms

Trying to Implement Confucian Ideals

  • Nationalized land ‚Äî redistribute to peasants
  • Revived public granaries for famine relief
  • Limited private landholding
  • Outlawed slavery
  • Issued new coinage
  • Cut court expenses

The Problem: These reforms looked good on paper but disrupted the existing system without helping ordinary people.

üìö Idealistic but Impractical

From the textbook: "Although he was condemned as a usurper, Wang Mang was a learned Confucian scholar who wished to implement policies described in the classics. He asserted state ownership of forests and swamps, built ritual halls, revived public granaries, limited private landholdings, outlawed slavery, and cut court expenses."

üéØ Key Insight

Wang Mang's reforms were derived from idealized descriptions of ancient governments in the classics. But implementing ancient ideals in a real society proved disastrous‚Äîhe disrupted existing arrangements without creating workable alternatives.

üí° Discussion Point

Why did well-intentioned reforms fail? (Alienated powerful families, disrupted trade, currency changes caused economic chaos, no mechanism to actually redistribute land)

üìö A Perfect Storm

Wang Mang's reforms might have caused problems anyway, but the Yellow River flood made everything worse. Natural disaster plus economic disruption plus political instability created conditions for massive rebellion.

üéØ Key Points

  • Currency changes disrupted trade and made existing money worthless
  • Land reform was announced but never implemented‚Äîworst of both worlds
  • The Yellow River flood was catastrophic, displacing millions
  • Wang Mang was blamed for everything, including the flood

üí° Confucian Irony

In Dong Zhongshu's Confucian cosmology, natural disasters were Heaven's warning that the ruler had failed. The Yellow River flood could be interpreted as Heaven rejecting Wang Mang‚Äîundermining his Confucian legitimacy.

Conclusion: What Made Han Governance Work?

Success Factors

  • Balance: Qin efficiency + Confucian morality
  • Flexibility: Adjusted taxes and policies as needed
  • Legitimacy: Scholar-officials with shared values
  • Adaptation: Learned from mistakes

Persistent Problems

  • Child emperors
  • Consort family power
  • Eunuch influence
  • Weak succession mechanisms

The Han model worked‚Äîbut it had vulnerabilities that would resurface again and again.

üìö Summary

The Han created a durable system by combining Qin's administrative efficiency with Confucian legitimacy. But structural problems‚Äîespecially around succession and court factions‚Äîwould cause repeated crises.

üéØ Key Takeaways

  • The Confucian-Legalist synthesis worked better than either alone
  • Practical flexibility mattered more than ideological purity
  • Court politics were a recurring source of instability
  • The Han model became the template for later dynasties
