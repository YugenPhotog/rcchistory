Daoism, Legalism & the Intellectual Legacy

HIST 270 — Chapter 2, Lecture 3

Review Questions: Q8, Q9

📖 Lecture Overview

This lecture covers the two most radical alternatives to the Confucian tradition: Daoism (Q8) and Legalism (Q9). Both reject Confucian moral cultivation as the primary mechanism of order — but in completely different directions. Daoism says you're trying too hard; Legalism says you're not trying the right thing. The lecture closes with the synthesis: Legalism conquers China short-term, Confucianism wins the long game — and understanding why requires understanding what each school got right and wrong.

Review Questions Covered

Q8 — How did Daoist thinkers (Laozi, Zhuangzi) respond to Confucian striving? Describe their alternative vision.
Q9 — Analyze Legalism (Han Feizi / Qin). Why did Legalists argue Confucian virtue was insufficient for governance?

Two Radical Alternatives

Daoism (Q8)

  • Stop forcing things — go with the natural flow
  • Dao: the undivided, unnameable ground of all things
  • Wu-wei: non-action / effortless action
  • Laozi and Zhuangzi — different styles, same skepticism

Legalism (Q9)

  • Virtue is a fairy tale — use law, rewards, punishment
  • Han Feizi: govern according to human nature as it is
  • The Qin state adopted it — and conquered everyone
  • The state that won in the short term

Legalism wins 221 BCE. Confucianism wins 206 BCE onward. Why?

The answer requires understanding both what Legalism got right (it works for military expansion) and what it got wrong (it's unsustainable as a peacetime governance system).

Part I

Daoism — The Way Beyond Words

Laozi and Zhuangzi — Q8

What Is "Daoism"?

⚠️ Important caveat: Western scholars once separated "philosophical Daoism" (pure, rational) from "religious Daoism" (temples, rituals, popular). Current scholarship sees these as intertwined aspects of one tradition — not a pure philosophy that got corrupted.

What Daoism Includes

  • Philosophical texts (Daodejing, Zhuangzi)
  • Religious traditions — priests, temples, liturgy
  • Practices — meditation, alchemy, physical cultivation
  • Folk traditions — gods, spirits, feng shui

The Shared Core

  • The Dao is prior to all human categories and values
  • Human intervention and "improvement" typically make things worse
  • Wisdom means aligning with the Dao rather than forcing your will on things
  • Skepticism about all human institutions, including Confucian ritual

📖 The Philosophical/Religious Split

The old Western scholarly narrative was: Laozi and Zhuangzi wrote pure philosophical texts, and then popular religious traditions corrupted this pure philosophy with superstition and ritual. This narrative was wrong in two ways. First, it imposed a Western Protestant distinction between "pure" and "corrupted" religion onto a tradition that didn't use those categories. Second, the "philosophical" texts themselves contain plenty of material about longevity techniques, cosmology, and practices that blur the philosophy/religion boundary.

For teaching purposes: acknowledge the distinction but don't over-rely on it. The thinkers in Q8 (Laozi, Zhuangzi) are primarily philosophical — they're engaging with the same political and ethical questions as the Confucians. But situating them within a broader Daoist tradition (rather than treating them as isolated rationalists) is more historically accurate.

Laozi & the Daodejing 道德經

The Text

  • ~81 short chapters — about 5,000 characters total
  • Date of composition: 3rd century BCE (not ancient as legend claims)
  • Attributed to "Laozi" — probably a composite, not a single author
  • One of the most translated books in world history

The Core Argument

  • The Dao that can be named is not the eternal Dao
  • Language and concepts divide what is originally whole
  • Good rulers govern by doing less, not more
  • Wu-wei: effortless, non-coercive action aligned with natural flow
"The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao."
— Daodejing, Chapter 1 (paraphrase)

📖 Q8 Core Content — Laozi

The opening line is the key: "The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao." This is not mystical obscurantism — it's a specific philosophical point. The Dao is the undivided, unnameable ground of all existence. The moment you name it ("the Way," "Heaven's principle," "the Good"), you've already divided it and captured only a part. Confucians, with their elaborate categories of virtues and relationships, are doing exactly this — they're imposing human-made categories onto something that precedes and exceeds all categories.

Wu-wei in political terms: The Daodejing's political vision is of a ruler who governs so lightly that people barely notice they're being governed. "When the best ruler's work is done, the people say 'we did it ourselves.'" This is the antithesis of both Confucian ritual cultivation (active moral engineering of society) and Legalist law enforcement (explicit coercion).

Water imagery: The Daodejing constantly uses water as an analogy. Water is soft but wears away stone. It flows around obstacles rather than forcing through them. It seeks the lowest places. The sage ruler is like water — yielding, adaptive, seeking the lowest place, and ultimately irresistible.

Wu-Wei 無為 — Non-Action

Not "do nothing" — but act in harmony with the natural flow rather than forcing your will on things.

What Wu-Wei Rejects

  • Confucian wei: deliberate cultivation, moral engineering
  • Legalist wei: coercive law enforcement
  • Over-planning and over-controlling make things worse
  • The more laws, the more criminals

What Wu-Wei Recommends

  • Simplify — reduce the complexity that creates friction
  • Yield — water wears away stone by flowing around it
  • Respond rather than initiate
  • The best ruler: people say "we did it ourselves"

📖 Wu-Wei in Context

Wu-wei is Daoism's direct response to the Hundred Schools problem. Every other school (Confucians, Mohists, Legalists) assumes that the solution to political chaos is more and better human intervention — more virtue cultivation, more universal love, more laws and punishments. The Daodejing's radical move is to question that assumption: what if the intervention is the problem? What if the Warring States chaos is partly caused by rulers trying too hard to engineer their societies?

The political analogy is counterintuitive but not absurd. States that over-regulate often produce the behaviors they're trying to prevent. The Daodejing's observation that "the more laws, the more criminals" anticipates debates about over-criminalization that are still very much alive today.

Zhuangzi 莊子 ca. 369–286 BCE

His Approach

  • More playful and literary than the Daodejing
  • Uses stories, paradoxes, and humor to make philosophical points
  • Deeply skeptical of all human categories — including "life" and "death"
  • Questions whether book learning captures what matters

The Butterfly Dream

"Once I dreamed I was a butterfly. Now I am awake. But am I a man who dreamed of being a butterfly, or a butterfly now dreaming of being a man?"

The boundaries we draw between things — including "self" and "other" — may be less fixed than we assume.

📖 Zhuangzi's Philosophical Method

Where Laozi is aphoristic and compressed, Zhuangzi is expansive and literary. He uses extended parables, philosophical dialogues between unlikely characters (a giant fish, a mushroom, a cooking expert), and humor to destabilize readers' assumptions. The butterfly dream is his most famous example: it appears simple but points toward deep uncertainty about the categories of "self," "other," "real," and "dream."

What he challenges: Confucian certainty about the Five Relationships, about ritual, about what constitutes proper behavior. If the boundary between butterfly and man is uncertain, why are you so sure about the boundary between "proper ritual" and "improper ritual"? Zhuangzi's skepticism is not nihilism — he's not saying nothing matters — but it is a deep challenge to Confucian confidence about moral categories.

On death: Zhuangzi famously sang and beat a bowl when his wife died, rather than mourning. When challenged, he said: before birth she had no form; before that, no spirit; before that, no vital energy. She returned to what she had always been. Is that cause for grief or acceptance? His approach to death is one of the most striking examples of philosophy applied to life in the Chinese tradition.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

A.C. Graham's Chuang-tzu: The Inner Chapters argues that the Zhuangzi is one of the greatest works of world literature — philosophically sophisticated, literarily brilliant, and genuinely funny. Graham distinguishes the "inner chapters" (probably by Zhuangzi himself) from the outer and miscellaneous chapters (by later hands). The inner chapters are among the richest texts in the Chinese tradition.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Daoism says the more you try to fix things through deliberate intervention — ritual cultivation, law enforcement, moral engineering — the more you make them worse.

Can you think of a situation — in your own life, or in politics — where that argument seems right?

Instructor Notes

Good examples students might raise: over-parenting (helicopter parents producing anxious children), micromanagement in workplaces, over-criminalization producing more crime, strict dietary rules producing disordered eating. The Daoist argument is not unique to ancient China — it recurs in Western thought in the idea of "unintended consequences" and in libertarian skepticism of government intervention. The point is not that intervention is always wrong, but that there's a real phenomenon Daoism is pointing at.

Part II

Legalism — The Philosophy That Conquered China

Han Feizi and the Qin state — Q9

Legalism: A Different Starting Point

Confucians: If rulers cultivate virtue, good government follows.
Legalists: Rulers are not virtuous. People are not virtuous. Design the system around this fact.

Why Virtue Fails (Q9)

  • Virtue can't be reliably produced or verified
  • One virtuous ruler followed by an evil one collapses everything
  • Officials who claim virtue use it to disguise self-interest
  • A system dependent on good people is fragile

What Works Instead

  • Clear, public laws — predictable and impersonal
  • Automatic rewards for following law; severe punishments for violating it
  • Incentives that align self-interest with state interest
  • A system that works even with mediocre rulers and selfish officials

📖 Q9 Core Answer — Part 1

The Legalist critique of Confucianism is sophisticated and not easily dismissed. Their core argument: any system of governance that depends on moral virtue in its officials is fragile by design. Good rulers are rare; virtuous officials are rarer. Why design a system that breaks down whenever a bad person gets into power? Better to design a system of laws and incentives that produces good outcomes regardless of whether the people running it are virtuous.

This is a recognizably modern argument — it's the logic behind constitutional constraints, checks and balances, and rule-of-law systems. The Legalists weren't simply brutes; they had a genuine insight about institutional design. The problem (as the Qin's failure showed) is that their version was too focused on maximizing state power and too indifferent to the welfare of ordinary people.

Han Feizi 韓非子 d. 233 BCE

His Background

  • Student of Xunzi — took the pessimistic view of human nature to its logical extreme
  • Ironically, a prince of the state of Han — which Qin would later destroy
  • Died in Qin prison — possibly murdered by his classmate Li Si
  • His writings became the Qin state's governing handbook

His Core Principle

"When a sage rules, he governs according to the nature of men as they are, not according to what they should be."

— Han Feizi

📖 Han Feizi's Tragic Irony

The biographical irony: Han Feizi was a prince of Han, one of the states Qin would conquer. His political philosophy provided the intellectual justification for the power that destroyed his own state. He was summoned to Qin court (the king of Qin was an admirer of his writings), but his classmate Li Si — who had become a Qin minister — saw him as a threat and had him arrested. He died before he could return home.

The quote in the legalist box is a good one for discussing with students. "Men as they are, not as they should be" — this is a critique of Confucian idealism. Confucians design government for the best possible version of human beings; Legalists design it for actual human beings, with their self-interest, their capacity for deception, and their rational responses to incentives.

Legalist Principles (Q9)

Law (法)

  • Clear, public, written laws — no exceptions
  • Even nobles subject to law
  • Predictability creates compliance

Method (術)

  • Ruler controls officials through technique, not trust
  • Keep intentions secret — officials who can predict ruler's wishes will manipulate them
  • Bureaucratic accountability mechanisms

Power (勢)

  • The ruler's positional authority must be maintained
  • Centralized bureaucracy replaces hereditary lords
  • All power flows from the ruler downward — no rivals
The goal: A state so well-designed it produces good outcomes even with mediocre rulers.

📖 The Three Pillars

The three pillars of Legalism — fa (law), shu (method/technique), and shi (power/positional authority) — were systematized differently by different Legalist thinkers. Han Feizi synthesized all three. Fa is public and applies to everyone equally; shu is secret and applies to how the ruler manages officials; shi is the structural authority that makes both possible.

The ruler's secrecy: The requirement that rulers keep their intentions secret seems counterintuitive but has internal logic. If officials know what the ruler wants, they'll perform for him rather than doing their actual jobs. By maintaining uncertainty, the ruler forces officials to produce real results rather than managing impressions. This anticipates modern discussions of information asymmetry in organizational management.

Legalism in Action — The Qin State

What Qin Did

  • Adopted Legalist reforms under Lord Shang (d. 338 BCE)
  • Abolished hereditary aristocracy — officials appointed by merit
  • Strict laws with automatic punishments; collective family responsibility
  • Rewarded agricultural output and military service — nothing else
  • By 3rd century: conquering neighbors one by one

Why It Worked

  • Military effectiveness rewarded with land grants — strong incentives
  • Administrative efficiency outperformed rivals
  • Rivals were still using semi-feudal structures Qin had abolished
  • 221 BCE: Qin First Emperor unifies China

📖 Why Legalism Won Militarily

The Qin state's Legalist reforms gave it decisive advantages in the military competition. By abolishing hereditary aristocracy and replacing it with merit-based appointments, Qin could promote talented administrators and generals regardless of birth. By rewarding military service with land grants, Qin created powerful incentives for soldiers to fight effectively. By standardizing weights, measures, and laws, Qin reduced administrative friction across a large territory.

The rival states were still operating with semi-feudal structures that limited their administrative reach. They couldn't conscript and tax as efficiently; they couldn't promote talent as freely; they couldn't coordinate military action as effectively. The Qin's Legalist administrative state was simply better designed for total war. This is the empirical argument for Legalism — it worked, at least in the short term.

Part III

The Long Game

Why Legalism won in 221 BCE and lost in 206 BCE

What Actually Happened

Legalism's Short-Term Victory

  • 221 BCE: Qin uses Legalism to unify China for the first time
  • Qin First Emperor centralizes power, standardizes writing, weights, measures
  • Brutal suppression of dissent — burning books, executing scholars
  • Massive forced labor — Great Wall, imperial tomb, palaces

Legalism's Long-Term Failure

  • Qin Dynasty lasts only 15 years (221–206 BCE)
  • People resent brutality and forced labor — rebellions break out
  • No moral framework for loyalty — officials defect when things go wrong
  • Han Dynasty overthrows Qin; adopts Confucianism as state ideology
Legalism wins short-term. Confucianism wins long-term.

📖 The Synthesis Question

Why did Legalism fail? Several reasons. First, Legalism is designed for conquest and military expansion — it's optimized for the Warring States situation. Once China was unified, the incentives structure changed. There were no more rivals to conquer; the rewards for military service dried up. Second, Legalism has no mechanism for popular legitimacy. When things went wrong (and things always go wrong), there was no Mandate of Heaven framework, no moral appeal, no reason for ordinary people to remain loyal. Third, Legalism treats people as instruments of the state — there's no concept of the state's obligation to its subjects. This produced resentment that became rebellion as soon as the coercive apparatus showed weakness.

The Han synthesis (covered in Chapter 3) preserved Legalist administrative structures under a Confucian ideological surface. The empire was run with Legalist efficiency but justified with Confucian rhetoric. As the slide in the original deck put it: "Confucian rhetoric, Legalist methods." This is the template for Chinese imperial governance for the next 2,000 years.

Why Confucianism Won the Long Game

  • Offered moral legitimacy to rulers — the Mandate of Heaven framework
  • Gave officials a reason for loyalty beyond self-interest
  • Flexible enough to incorporate other traditions over time
  • Provided a vision of society that ordinary people could invest in
  • Daoism remained as a spiritual and personal counterpoint — not replaced
For 2,000+ years: Confucian rhetoric, Legalist methods. Both traditions shaped Chinese governance simultaneously.

📖 The Lasting Synthesis

The "Confucian rhetoric, Legalist methods" formula is one of the most important interpretive keys for Chinese history. Every subsequent dynasty maintained Confucian ceremonial, used Confucian texts for the civil service examination, and justified its rule in Confucian terms — while running its administration with bureaucratic techniques that would have pleased Han Feizi. The split was not usually acknowledged openly, which is part of why Confucianism appeared to be the governing ideology while Legalism was doing much of the actual governing work.

Daoism didn't disappear — it became the tradition of personal cultivation, artistic freedom, and spiritual practice that complemented Confucian public engagement. Many officials were publicly Confucian and privately Daoist. The two traditions were understood as addressing different dimensions of life rather than competing for the same space.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

You are a Warring States ruler. Your kingdom is under threat. Which philosophy do you adopt — and which do you tell your subjects you've adopted?

Is there a difference between the two answers?

Instructor Notes

The split between what you actually use (Legalism) and what you claim to use (Confucianism) is the real insight this question is after. Historical Chinese emperors regularly announced Confucian policies and implemented Legalist ones. Modern politicians do something similar — the gap between rhetoric and implementation is a feature of governance, not a bug. The question is whether that gap is always cynical or whether it sometimes reflects genuine tension between the demands of governing effectively and the demands of governing legitimately.

Chapter 2 — The Big Picture

Q1–Q4: The Warring States world — political fragmentation, military revolution, economic change, the shi class — created the demand for new ideas and the people capable of supplying them.

Q5–Q7: The Confucian tradition answered with virtue, ritual, and the redefined junzi. Mozi challenged with utility and universal love. Mencius and Xunzi argued over whether human nature needed nurturing or correcting.

Q8–Q9: Daoism said stop trying so hard. Legalism said stop moralizing and use law. Legalism won militarily. Confucianism won institutionally. The synthesis — Confucian rhetoric, Legalist methods — governed China for 2,000 years.

Dao 道 — The Way

In Daoist thought, the Dao is the undivided, unnameable ground of all existence — prior to all categories, all distinctions, all human values. It cannot be adequately described in language (hence the opening of the Daodejing: "The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao"). For Daoists, wisdom means aligning with the Dao rather than imposing human will on things. Note: Confucians also use "Dao" but to mean the moral Way of civilization — a specific, human-centered path, not the cosmic ground.

×
Wu-Wei 無為 — Non-Action

Literally "non-doing" or "non-action" — but not passivity. Wu-wei means acting in harmony with the natural flow of things rather than forcing your will on them. Water practices wu-wei: it flows around obstacles rather than pushing through them, seeks the lowest places, and ultimately wears away stone. The political implication: the best ruler governs so lightly that people feel they governed themselves. Over-regulation and over-planning violate wu-wei and make things worse.

Daoism (道家)

A broad Chinese religious and philosophical tradition centered on the concept of the Dao (the Way). Includes philosophical texts (the Daodejing attributed to Laozi, and the Zhuangzi), organized religious traditions with priests and liturgy, meditation and physical cultivation practices, and folk traditions. Western scholars once distinguished "philosophical" from "religious" Daoism — current scholarship views these as intertwined dimensions of one tradition that developed together from early on.

×
Laozi (老子) — The Old Master

The legendary figure to whom the Daodejing is attributed. Tradition describes him as an archivist who taught Confucius, then departed westward on a water buffalo, leaving behind the text at a border official's request. Historically, "Laozi" is likely a composite figure — the Daodejing was probably composed in the 3rd century BCE by multiple hands, not by a single ancient sage. The name itself means "Old Master" — a title of respect rather than a personal name.

×
Zhuangzi 莊子 (ca. 369–286 BCE)

A Daoist philosopher and one of the greatest literary stylists in the Chinese tradition. The book bearing his name combines philosophical argument, extended parables, humor, and poetry. The "inner chapters" (probably by Zhuangzi himself) are considered among the most sophisticated philosophical writing in Chinese history. His central themes: the relativity of all human categories, skepticism about conventional values including life and death, and the wisdom of spontaneous action aligned with the Dao rather than deliberate moral cultivation.

Han Feizi 韓非子 (d. 233 BCE)

The greatest systematizer of Legalist philosophy. A student of Xunzi who took his teacher's pessimistic view of human nature to its logical extreme — if people are naturally selfish, governance must work with that selfishness through incentives and laws rather than against it through moral cultivation. His writings synthesized the three pillars of Legalism: fa (law), shu (administrative technique), and shi (positional authority). Died in Qin prison, possibly murdered by his classmate Li Si, after his writings had already been adopted as the Qin state's governing handbook.
