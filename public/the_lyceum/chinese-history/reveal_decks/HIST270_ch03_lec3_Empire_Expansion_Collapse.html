Empire, Expansion, and Collapse

The Han at Its Height and Fall

HIST 270 ‚Äî Chapter 3, Lecture 3

üìö What You Need to Know

This lecture covers two connected stories: first, how the Han dynasty projected power outward ‚Äî confronting the Xiongnu, opening the Silk Road, and expanding into Central Asia, Korea, and Vietnam. Second, how that same empire collapsed from within through palace factionalism, social crisis, rebellion, and militarization. The key insight is that these aren't separate stories ‚Äî the costs and structures of expansion created vulnerabilities that the dynasty couldn't survive.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Last time we looked at the founding and consolidation of the Han ‚Äî the civil service, Confucian governance, and early imperial institutions. Today we see both the height of that system and its unraveling. This sets up our next unit on the Three Kingdoms and the Age of Division.

The Xiongnu Challenge

Who Were the Xiongnu?
  • Nomadic confederation north of China
  • Herding: horses, sheep, cattle, camels
  • Lived in felt tents (yurts) ‚Äî moved with the seasons
  • Tribal organization under a chanyu (supreme leader)
A fundamentally different way of life ‚Äî and a military problem China could never permanently solve

üìö What You Need to Know

The Xiongnu represented a fundamentally different civilization from agricultural China. They were pastoral nomads ‚Äî their wealth was in herds, not land. Their mobility was both a military advantage and made them impossible to conquer permanently. This conflict between settled farmers and nomadic herders is a recurring theme in world history and will shape Chinese foreign policy for centuries.

The Xiongnu Military Advantage

  • Every man a warrior from childhood
  • No cities to defend (or capture)
  • Could retreat endlessly into the steppe

Mounted archery was the revolutionary military technology of the steppe. Xiongnu children learned to ride and shoot as naturally as Chinese children learned to farm. This gave the nomads a decisive tactical edge. The fundamental strategic problem: Han armies could drive the Xiongnu out of a region, but the Xiongnu would simply retreat into the grasslands and return later. There was nothing permanent to occupy.

Early Han Policy: Heqin

The Disaster at Baideng (200 BCE)
  • Emperor Gaozu (Liu Bang) led army against Xiongnu
  • Surrounded by cavalry for seven days
  • Barely escaped ‚Äî humiliating defeat
The "Peace and Kinship" Solution
  • Chinese princesses sent to marry the chanyu
  • Annual "gifts" of silk, grain, and wine ‚Äî essentially tribute
  • Border markets established for trade
Practical and effective ‚Äî but deeply humiliating to Han pride

üìö What You Need to Know

After the Baideng disaster, the Han adopted the heqin policy ‚Äî essentially paying the Xiongnu to not attack. It worked, but the optics were terrible: China was paying tribute to "barbarians," reversing the expected relationship. Both sides could spin it differently ‚Äî the Chinese called them "gifts," the Xiongnu called them tribute ‚Äî but everyone knew what was really happening.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Emperor Wu would eventually reverse this policy and pursue military confrontation ‚Äî but at enormous cost. The tension between diplomacy-through-payment and military confrontation is a recurring theme in Chinese statecraft.

Emperor Wu's Aggressive Turn

The New Policy (141‚Äì87 BCE)
  • Reversed the heqin policy ‚Äî no more tribute
  • Launched major military campaigns
  • Key generals: Wei Qing and Huo Qubing
  • Cavalry armies sent deep into the steppes

üìö What You Need to Know

Emperor Wu abandoned the humiliating heqin policy and pursued military victory. His campaigns were initially successful ‚Äî pushing the Xiongnu far from the Chinese heartland. But victory was never final. The Xiongnu would return whenever Han military pressure relaxed. The cost of these campaigns drained the treasury built up by decades of early Han frugality.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Eventually (1st century BCE) the Xiongnu confederation split. Some groups surrendered and were settled inside China ‚Äî a pattern that would have major consequences in later centuries. The cost of empire would also contribute to the financial problems that weakened the later Han.

Zhang Qian and the Silk Road

The Turning Point
  • 139 BCE: Sent west to find allies against the Xiongnu
  • Captured for 10 years ‚Äî escaped and reached Central Asia
  • Discovered wealthy states, new trade networks, and superior Ferghana horses
  • Returned after 13 years with intelligence that changed Han policy
What Happened Next
  • Han armies expanded west (104‚Äì102 BCE)
  • Garrisons secured routes across Central Asia
  • A trade network linked China ‚Üí Central Asia ‚Üí Persia ‚Üí Rome
A failed diplomatic mission became the foundation of the Silk Road

Story to Tell

Zhang Qian was sent by Emperor Wu in 139 BCE to find the Yuezhi and form a military alliance against the Xiongnu. He was captured almost immediately and held for about ten years, during which he married and had a family among the Xiongnu. He eventually escaped and continued west anyway ‚Äî reaching Bactria and the Ferghana region.

The Yuezhi refused to fight. The diplomatic mission failed.

But the intelligence he brought back was revolutionary. He reported powerful kingdoms to the west (Parthia, regions connected to India), active trade networks, and the surprising fact that Chinese silk was already circulating in Central Asia through intermediaries. He also described the famous "heavenly horses" of Ferghana, which Emperor Wu considered essential for fighting steppe nomads.

Han policy shifted immediately. The empire expanded westward, fought a war to obtain the Ferghana horses (104‚Äì102 BCE), and established military colonies and protectorates to secure the routes. Trade was not a private merchant phenomenon alone ‚Äî it depended on Han military power.

Important clarification: the "Silk Road" was not a single road and the term itself is modern. It was a network of relay trade. Goods moved step by step through many hands ‚Äî often carried by Sogdian and other Central Asian merchants using Bactrian camels.

What Moved Along the Network

  • West: silk, lacquerware, luxury goods
  • East: horses, glass, metals, new foods (grapes, walnuts, pomegranates)
  • Most important long-term transfer: Buddhism, arriving in China in the 1st century CE

Conceptual takeaway for students: The Silk Road did not open because merchants wandered west. It opened because a state realized the wider world existed ‚Äî and then used military power to connect itself to it.

Borderlands and Expansion

Southern Expansion
  • Into what is now southern China and northern Vietnam
  • Indigenous peoples conquered and incorporated
  • Chinese settlers moved south along rivers
  • Garrisons and counties to assimilate frontiers
Korean Peninsula
  • 108 BCE: Established commanderies in northern Korea
  • Lelang Commandery near modern Pyongyang
  • Spread Chinese writing, culture, institutions
  • Lasted for over 400 years
Han expansion created the template for "China proper" ‚Äî but empire was expensive

üìö What You Need to Know

Han expansion wasn't just westward along the Silk Road ‚Äî it also went south and northeast. This is when the boundaries of what we think of as "China" really start to take shape. But expansion had costs: garrisons had to be supplied and defended, and government monopolies on salt and iron were increased to fund the wars. Some historians argue Emperor Wu's expansion overextended the empire ‚Äî sowing the seeds of later financial crisis.

üí≠ Context & Connections

The Lelang Commandery is particularly important for East Asian history ‚Äî it was the primary channel through which Chinese writing, bureaucratic practices, and material culture reached Korea. Korean kingdoms would later adopt Chinese-style government.

Part I

The Rise of the Eunuchs

How child emperors created a power vacuum at the heart of the empire

Child Emperors & the Power Vacuum

  • Most late Eastern Han emperors ascended as children
  • When emperors are minors, power doesn't disappear ‚Äî it relocates
  • Two factions competed to control the boy-emperor
The throne was occupied ‚Äî but real power was elsewhere

üìö What You Need to Know

This is a structural problem, not a moral one. The Han didn't have a consistent rule about who becomes regent when the emperor is a child. So every time a minor took the throne, it triggered a power struggle. Think about it like a company where the CEO is a toddler ‚Äî everyone around them scrambles to make decisions "on their behalf."

üìñ Key Definitions

  • Regent: Someone who governs on behalf of a child or incapacitated ruler
  • Consort clan: The family of the empress ‚Äî they gained power through the marriage connection

Two Factions, One Palace

Empress Families
  • Military connections
  • Aristocratic political networks
  • Power through marriage alliance
Eunuchs
  • Lived inside the palace
  • Managed the emperor's daily life
  • Power through physical proximity
One faction controlled the outside world ‚Äî the other controlled access to the emperor

üìö What You Need to Know

The empress families (consort clans) had real political and military power OUTSIDE the palace. But the eunuchs had something potentially more valuable: daily access to the emperor. They controlled who got in and who didn't, what documents reached his desk, what information shaped his decisions.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This isn't unique to China ‚Äî think of the role of advisors, chiefs of staff, or gatekeepers in any government. Whoever controls the leader's daily information flow has enormous indirect power. The modern analogy might be: whoever controls the president's daily briefing shapes policy.

Why Eunuchs Won

Structural Advantages
  • Constant proximity ‚Äî always with the emperor
  • Personal trust ‚Äî no heirs, no dynastic ambition
  • Information control ‚Äî managed documents and audiences
They controlled the flow of reality to the emperor.

üìö What You Need to Know

The key phrase here is "flow of reality." Eunuchs didn't have armies or land. Their power was informational. They decided what the emperor knew. That's a different KIND of power, but in a system where everything depends on imperial decision-making, it's potentially the most important kind.

  • Eunuch (ÂÆ¶ÂÆò, hu√†nguƒÅn): A castrated male serving in the imperial palace. Because they could not father children, emperors trusted them near the royal family. Over time, some accumulated enormous political power.
  • Ten Attendants (ÂçÅÂ∏∏‰æç): A notorious group of powerful eunuchs in the late Eastern Han who effectively ran the court.

Part II

The Partisan Prohibitions

When the reformers fought back ‚Äî and lost

The Confucian Counterattack

The Charges
  • Corruption and self-enrichment
  • Selling government offices
  • Manipulating imperial authority
The Principle
  • Government should be run by morally cultivated gentlemen ‚Äî not palace servants

üìö What You Need to Know

To Confucian officials, the eunuchs were everything wrong with the system. Government was supposed to be about moral cultivation and scholarly merit. The eunuchs represented the opposite: power through manipulation, proximity, and corruption. This wasn't just a political fight ‚Äî it was an ideological one about what government is FOR.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Remember from our Confucius lecture: the ideal of the junzi (gentleman-scholar) who governs through moral example. The eunuchs were the anti-junzi ‚Äî powerful through institutional manipulation rather than virtue.

The Partisan Prohibitions

166‚Äì169 CE: Eunuchs portray their critics as factional conspirators

The reformers are recast as the threat

The Results:

Hundreds arrested or executed

Thousands banned from office

Reform networks destroyed

The Han court had become a political battlefield.

üìö What You Need to Know

The Partisan Prohibitions (ÂÖöÈî¢‰πãÁ•∏) were a series of political purges in 166 and 169 CE. Confucian officials organized to oppose eunuch power, and the eunuchs convinced the emperor that THEY were the conspirators. The result was devastating: the most talented and reform-minded officials were purged from government. This gutted the Han bureaucracy of its best people at the worst possible time.

Part III

Meanwhile, Outside the Capital‚Ä¶

The empire was breaking while the court fought itself

An Empire in Crisis

Economic Collapse
  • Land concentrated into large estates
  • Small farmers fell into debt or tenancy
  • Tax burdens increased on those who could least afford it
Environmental & Administrative
  • Floods and famine displaced populations
  • Local administration weakened
  • Dynasty's legitimacy eroding far from the capital
The court was fighting over power ‚Äî the people were fighting to survive

üìö What You Need to Know

While the court tore itself apart in the Partisan Prohibitions, the empire's rural population was suffering. This is a pattern you'll see again and again in Chinese history: when the central government is consumed by internal politics, it stops addressing the real problems ‚Äî land distribution, taxes, famine relief. The result is always the same: people lose faith in the dynasty.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Remember the concept of the Mandate of Heaven from our earlier lectures. Natural disasters and social suffering aren't just practical problems ‚Äî they signal that the dynasty may have lost Heaven's favor. When floods and famine hit, people start asking: is this dynasty still legitimate?

Part IV

The Fatal Solution

How suppressing the rebellion destroyed the empire

The Fatal Solution

The Problem
  • Central army too weak to suppress the Yellow Turbans alone
The "Solution"
  • Authorized regional commanders to raise private armies
  • Local elites armed their own forces
  • Future warlords gained military authority
The rebellion was suppressed. But the Han lost its monopoly on force.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is the turning point. The Han government was too weak to crush the Yellow Turbans with its own military, so it gave local leaders permission to raise their own armies. Think about what that means: the central government just admitted it can't protect its own territory. Men like Cao Cao, Liu Bei, and Sun Jian ‚Äî who will become the Three Kingdoms warlords ‚Äî got their start right here, authorized by a desperate government to build private military forces.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This is the same pattern that destroyed the Roman Empire ‚Äî when central authority can't maintain its monopoly on military force, regional strongmen fill the vacuum. It will happen again in Chinese history during the Tang-Song transition.

The Crisis of 189 CE

  • Emperor Ling dies
  • General He Jin plans to eliminate the eunuchs
  • The eunuchs assassinate He Jin inside the palace
  • He Jin's allies call in regional armies
  • Outside troops storm Luoyang ‚Äî massacre thousands of eunuchs
The eunuch faction was destroyed ‚Äî but the military did not withdraw

üìö What You Need to Know

189 CE is the year everything collapses. The sequence matters: He Jin (a general and brother of the Empress Dowager) tried to use military force to solve the eunuch problem. The eunuchs killed him first. His supporters then called in the provincial armies ‚Äî the very forces created to fight the Yellow Turbans. Those armies destroyed the eunuchs, but they didn't go home. Why would they? They now held the capital.

  • Dong Zhuo seized control of the emperor
  • Ruled by raw military force
  • Burned Luoyang when forced to retreat
  • Regional commanders began acting independently

üìö What You Need to Know

Dong Zhuo is the first true warlord of the post-Han period. He didn't bother with Confucian legitimacy or court protocol ‚Äî he simply held the emperor hostage and ruled by force. When a coalition formed against him, he burned Luoyang (the capital!) and moved the court by force. This is the moment when military power definitively replaced civil authority as the basis of rule in China.

The Decline of the Han

Problems of the Eastern Han
  • Land concentration: wealthy families accumulated estates; peasants lost land
  • Court factions: eunuchs vs. scholar-officials in bitter struggles
  • Weak emperors: often young, manipulated by eunuchs or consort families
  • Eroded tax base: powerful families evaded taxes; burden fell on the poorest
"During the Eastern Han period, eunuchs were able to build a base of power within the palace, with the result that weak emperors became their captives rather than their masters."
‚Äî Ebrey, Cambridge Illustrated History of China
The cycle of land concentration ‚Üí peasant distress ‚Üí rebellion would become a persistent theme in Chinese history

üìö What You Need to Know

The problems that brought down the Han were structural ‚Äî built into the system itself. Land concentration created desperate peasants. Court factions undermined governance. Weak emperors couldn't control either. These weren't accidents or the result of bad individuals ‚Äî they were recurring vulnerabilities in the imperial system that would reappear in dynasty after dynasty.

Not one crisis ‚Äî a chain reaction.

Multiple power centers emerged ‚Äî palace, bureaucracy, provinces, and military

The imperial system could no longer balance them.

This is the thesis slide. The key analytical framework for today is chain reaction ‚Äî each crisis didn't just happen on its own. üìö What You Need to Know

These patterns will repeat throughout Chinese history. When we study the Tang, the Song, the Ming, and the Qing, you'll see variations on these same structural vulnerabilities. The specific actors change, but the dynamics are strikingly similar. This is what makes the late Han so important ‚Äî it establishes the template for dynastic collapse.

The Historical Irony

Confucian officials believed they were purifying the state by eliminating corrupt eunuchs.

They succeeded.

But the real danger was no longer inside the palace.

It was outside the capital ‚Äî in the hands of generals who no longer needed the court at all.

When the army becomes the most stable institution in a political system, the dynasty has already ended ‚Äî it just hasn't formally fallen yet.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is the big lesson. The Confucian officials "won" their fight against the eunuchs. But by the time they did, the real power had shifted to regional military commanders who didn't need court approval to act. The officials were solving yesterday's problem while tomorrow's problem ‚Äî warlordism ‚Äî had already taken root.
