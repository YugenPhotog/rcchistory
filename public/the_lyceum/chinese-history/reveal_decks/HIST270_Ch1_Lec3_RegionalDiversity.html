üìö Welcome to Lecture 3!

Today we challenge the sinocentric narrative that "Chinese civilization = Yellow River Shang/Zhou." We'll see that Bronze Age China was actually multiple sophisticated cultures developing in parallel across different regions.

üéØ Learning Goals for Today

  • Identify key regional cultures: Sanxingdui, Yangtze valley, northern steppe
  • Understand how "China" emerged from diverse populations
  • Challenge sinocentric narratives of Chinese civilization

üí° Big Picture

This lecture corresponds to the textbook section "Developments Outside the Shang Core." The key insight: Chinese unity is always CONSTRUCTED, not natural or inevitable. Regional diversity persists even after political unification.

‚ùì The Question

"Why haven't you heard of Sanxingdui culture?" This is a rhetorical question that sets up today's theme: our textbooks and narratives focus on the Yellow River "core" (Shang/Zhou) and marginalize or ignore other regions. This is sinocentrism in action.

The 1986 Discovery

The Sanxingdui site was known to archaeologists earlier, but the spectacular finds that made it famous came from two sacrificial pits discovered in 1986.

What was found:

  • Over 1,000 artifacts including bronze masks, heads, vessels, and a sacred tree
  • Gold masks and scepters
  • Jade and stone objects
  • Elephant tusks (evidence of long-distance trade or different climate?)

The mystery: All these precious objects were deliberately broken and buried. Why? Ritual termination? Invasion? We still don't know for certain.

üó∫Ô∏è Geographic Context

Sichuan is a basin surrounded by mountains, geographically isolated from the Yellow River valley. This isolation helps explain why Sanxingdui culture developed so independently.

üìÖ Timeline

Sanxingdui flourished roughly 1600-1200 BCE, overlapping with the Shang Dynasty. They were contemporaries, not sequential.

üé® Artistic Differences

Shang bronzes are elegant, symmetrical, with intricate geometric patterns and taotie (monster face) motifs. Sanxingdui bronzes are bizarre, exaggerated, naturalistic in some ways (human faces) but wildly unrealistic in proportions (those EYES!).

üîó Connection to "Outside the Core"

This exemplifies the textbook section perfectly‚Äîsophisticated Bronze Age culture developing completely independently of Shang.

Sanxingdui's Strange Treasures

What They Made

  • Large bronze masks (some 5+ feet wide!)
  • Bronze "sacred trees"
  • Gold masks and scepters
  • Elephant tusks
  • Jade objects

What's Missing

  • No writing system (or none discovered yet)
  • No oracle bones
  • Different ritual emphasis than Shang

üå≥ The Bronze Sacred Tree

One of the most spectacular finds is a bronze tree about 13 feet tall with birds perched on branches. Nothing like this exists in Shang culture. What did it represent? A world-tree? Connection between earth and heaven? We can only speculate.

üêò Elephant Tusks

The presence of elephant tusks is fascinating. Were elephants native to Sichuan in the Bronze Age? (Climate was warmer then, so possibly.) Or did these come via long-distance trade? Either way, it shows connections beyond the local region.

‚úçÔ∏è The Writing Question

Sanxingdui had sophisticated bronze casting‚Äîarguably MORE sophisticated than early Shang. But no writing system (that we've found). This challenges the idea that "civilization = writing." They clearly had complex social organization, specialized crafts, monumental art, and ritual systems without written records.

üí≠ What Does "Civilization" Mean?

This is a great moment to question Eurocentric definitions of civilization. Does a culture need writing to be "civilized"? Sanxingdui forces us to expand our categories.

The Mystery: What Happened?

Sanxingdui culture disappears around 1200 BCE

Theories (all speculative):

  • Migration ‚Äî did they move elsewhere?
  • Environmental crisis ‚Äî earthquake, flooding, climate change?
  • Conquest ‚Äî defeated by another group?
  • Ritual transformation ‚Äî shifted capital/practices?

The deliberate burial of treasures suggests intentional ritual closure, but we may never know for certain.

ü§î Historical Mystery

Unlike the Shang (which we know ended with Zhou conquest), Sanxingdui just... ends. The archaeological record goes silent. This is one of the great mysteries of Chinese archaeology.

üè∫ The Burial Pits

The fact that they carefully buried these precious objects (rather than having them looted or destroyed in warfare) suggests a planned ritual. Maybe they were closing a temple? Ending a religious practice? Preparing to move? We don't know.

üîÑ Possible Continuity

Some scholars think Sanxingdui culture might have transformed into later Shu cultures in the same region. Maybe they didn't disappear‚Äîjust changed. But without writing, we can't trace clear continuity.

üìö Teaching Point

This is a good reminder that history has GAPS. We don't know everything. Students need to be comfortable with uncertainty and mystery‚Äîit's part of doing history, not a failure.

Why Sanxingdui Matters

Sanxingdui exemplifies "Developments Outside the Shang Core":
  • Independent political form (not Shang-controlled)
  • Separate ritual system (different gods, different practices)
  • Different material culture (unique artistic style)
  • Sichuan basin developed sophisticated Bronze Age society on its own terms

Bronze Age "China" was NOT one unified civilization!

üéØ The Big Point

This is the thesis of today's lecture: "China" in the Bronze Age was actually multiple sophisticated cultures developing in parallel. The Shang/Zhou narrative is just ONE story among many.

üìñ Textbook Connection

This directly addresses the textbook section "Developments Outside the Shang Core." Make sure students understand this isn't just trivia about weird bronzes‚Äîit's challenging a fundamental narrative about Chinese civilization.

üåè Broader Implications

This pattern repeats throughout history: dominant cultures (Shang, Zhou, Han, etc.) write the histories, and peripheral cultures get marginalized or forgotten. Sanxingdui was "rediscovered" only in the 20th century! How many other cultures have we forgotten?

The Yangtze River Valley

While Shang dominated the Yellow River, different cultures flourished in the south:

üó∫Ô∏è North-South Divide

The Yellow River and Yangtze River are separate drainage basins with different climates, geographies, and agricultural possibilities. This creates distinct cultural zones.

üåæ Agricultural Difference

North = millet (dry farming, loess soil). South = rice (wet farming, paddy fields). This is HUGE‚Äîdifferent crops mean different labor patterns, different settlement types, different social organizations.

‚è∞ Timeline Note

Southern cultures like Liangzhu actually PREDATE the Shang! Liangzhu flourished 3300-2300 BCE, well before Shang dominance in the north. This challenges any narrative that "civilization spread from north to south."

Earlier Foundations: Liangzhu Culture

3300-2300 BCE ‚Äî Predates the Shang!

  • Jade working traditions ‚Äî exquisite jade cong (ritual tubes) and bi (discs)
  • Hydraulic engineering: Dams, irrigation systems
  • Social stratification WITHOUT bronze technology
  • Evidence of complex ritual system centered on jade, not bronze

üíé Jade vs. Bronze

This is crucial: Liangzhu had complex society, monumental architecture, sophisticated art, and clear social hierarchy‚Äîbut their ritual objects were JADE, not bronze. This shows there are multiple pathways to social complexity.

üèóÔ∏è Hydraulic Engineering

Liangzhu people built sophisticated water control systems including dams and canals. This required centralized planning, coordinated labor, and engineering knowledge‚Äîall signs of state-level organization.

üî∑ Cong and Bi

Cong (ÁêÆ) are jade tubes, square on the outside and round on the inside. Bi (Áíß) are flat jade discs with holes in the center. Both have cosmological significance (heaven = round, earth = square in Chinese thought). These objects show up in elite burials across the Yangtze region.

‚öñÔ∏è Different Priorities

Shang elites valued bronze ritual vessels for ancestor worship. Liangzhu elites valued jade for cosmological rituals. Different cultures, different ritual priorities‚Äîboth sophisticated!

Different Ritual Priorities

Liangzhu (South)

Jade Ritual Objects

  • Cong (tubes) and bi (discs)
  • Cosmological symbolism
  • Earth-heaven connection
  • Durability = eternal

Shang (North)

Bronze Ritual Vessels

  • Ding, jue, gu, zun
  • Ancestor worship focus
  • Food/wine offerings
  • Technological prowess

Connection to "Outside the Core": Different economic base, different material culture priorities

üé® Material Culture Reflects Values

The objects a culture values tell us about their beliefs and priorities. Liangzhu's jade focus suggests cosmological/spiritual concerns. Shang's bronze focus suggests ancestor worship and technological display.

üí∞ Value Systems

Why jade in the south? Jade is rare, beautiful, durable, and difficult to work (it's very hard). It became associated with purity, heaven, and immortality. Why bronze in the north? Bronze requires mining, smelting, alloying‚Äîit demonstrates technological mastery and control over resources.

üîó Later Synthesis

Eventually, Chinese elite culture will value BOTH jade and bronze. But in the Bronze Age, these are regionally distinct preferences.

Rice Agriculture: A Different Economy

Wet rice paddies vs. northern millet farming:

Rice agriculture requires intensive communal labor

  • Water management (irrigation, flood control)
  • Transplanting seedlings (labor-intensive)
  • Terracing hillsides
  • Creates denser settlements
  • Different social organization (more cooperation required)
×

Rice Agriculture: Labor Implications

Wet rice cultivation (paddy farming) is fundamentally different from dry grain farming:

Labor Intensity:

  • Requires building and maintaining paddy fields (leveled, flooded areas)
  • Seedlings must be started in nursery beds, then transplanted by hand
  • Constant water management throughout growing season
  • Much more labor per acre than millet farming

Social Implications:

  • Requires coordinated group labor (can't be done by individual families)
  • Creates tight-knit village communities
  • Supports higher population densities (rice yields more calories per acre)
  • Different land tenure patterns (water rights matter!)

üåæ Why Rice Matters

This isn't just about crops‚Äîit's about fundamentally different social and economic systems. Rice agriculture shapes settlement patterns, labor organization, social cooperation, and even political structures.

üë• Communal Labor

Unlike millet (which individual families can farm on their own plots), rice paddies require GROUP labor. You need to coordinate irrigation, transplanting, harvesting. This creates strong village communities and collective decision-making traditions.

üìä Population Density

Rice produces more calories per acre than millet or wheat. This allows the Yangtze region to support denser populations, which eventually (in later dynasties) makes southern China economically dominant.

üåç Wet-Rice Ecology

Paddy fields create unique ecosystems‚Äîstanding water supports fish, snails, certain diseases (like schistosomiasis), different insects. This affects diet, health, and daily life in ways that differ from northern dry farming.

Connection to "Outside the Core"

Yangtze valley cultures exemplify regional diversity:

  • Different economic base (rice vs. millet)
  • Different settlement patterns (denser, more communal)
  • Different material culture priorities (jade vs. bronze)
  • Gradual incorporation into "Chinese" cultural sphere over centuries

Major turning point: Han Dynasty (206 BCE-220 CE) conquest and colonization of the south

‚è≥ Long Process

The Yangtze valley doesn't get "incorporated" into China overnight. It's a gradual process over many centuries involving migration, conquest, cultural exchange, and intermarriage.

üèõÔ∏è Han Expansion

The Han Dynasty (starting 206 BCE) is when massive Chinese expansion into the south happens. The Han establish commanderies, move northern settlers south, build infrastructure, and begin the "sinicization" of southern peoples. But that's 1000+ years after the Shang!

üí° "Chinese" Is Constructed

This is a key point: "Chinese" identity isn't primordial or natural. It's created through political conquest, cultural assimilation, and historical narrative. People in the Yangtze valley during the Bronze Age didn't think of themselves as "Chinese"‚Äîthat category didn't exist yet!

üîÑ Persistence of Difference

Even after political unification, cultural differences persist. Southern Chinese dialects, cuisines, and customs remain distinct from the north. Regional identity matters throughout Chinese history.

Pastoral Nomadism

A completely different subsistence strategy from agriculture:

Key Features:

  • Herding animals (sheep, horses, cattle) rather than farming crops
  • Mobile lifestyle following seasonal pastures
  • No permanent cities or large monuments
  • Different political organization (tribal confederations, not bureaucratic states)
  • Military advantage: mobility + horsemanship

Why This Matters:

Pastoral nomads aren't "primitive" or "less developed"‚Äîthey're adapted to environments (grasslands, steppes) where agriculture doesn't work well. They represent a viable alternative to sedentary civilization.

üèá The Steppe World

The Eurasian steppe (grasslands stretching from Mongolia to Eastern Europe) supports pastoral nomadism but not intensive agriculture. People here developed a completely different way of life based on herding and mobility.

‚öîÔ∏è Military Threat

Pastoral nomads were DANGEROUS to sedentary states. Their mobility and horsemanship gave them military advantages. They could raid agricultural settlements, then disappear into the steppe. This threat shapes Chinese history for millennia (eventually leading to the Great Wall!).

üîÑ Exchange and Conflict

The relationship between China and the steppe is always both hostile AND productive. There's constant warfare, but also trade, cultural exchange, and intermarriage. It's complicated!

  • Horse gear (bits, bridles)
  • Possibly some burial practices (horse sacrifice)
  • Military tactics

üåç Eurasian Connections

The chariot appears across Eurasia around the same time (2000-1500 BCE): Near East, Central Asia, India, China. This suggests transmission, not independent invention. But the exact route is debated.

üî¨ Evidence vs. Speculation

We have evidence that chariots existed in Central Asia before appearing in China. We have evidence of cultural contacts between steppes and China. But we DON'T have a clear "chain of transmission" showing exactly how the technology spread. Hence: "plausible hypothesis, not proven fact."

üê¥ Horses Are Key

Chariot warfare requires war horses, which weren't native to China. The steppe peoples specialized in horse breeding and horsemanship. This created a dependence‚ÄîChinese states needed horses from the north, creating trade relationships (and tensions).

üí° Teaching Point

This is a good example of how to talk about scholarly debates honestly. "We think X based on evidence Y, but we're not 100% certain because Z is missing." This is how history actually works!

Zhou Texts: "Barbarians"

Zhou texts call non-Zhou peoples yi (Â§∑), di (ÁãÑ), rong (Êàé), man (Ë†ª)

CRITICAL: "Barbarians" is a Zhou textual category

A political/ritual boundary marker, NOT objective ethnographic label

These terms marked who was "inside" vs. "outside" the Zhou ritual/cultural order

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Source Analysis

This is CRUCIAL for students. When we read ancient Chinese texts calling people "barbarians," we need to understand this is the Zhou perspective, not objective reality.

üìñ What "Barbarian" Meant

  • Political: People who don't acknowledge Zhou authority
  • Ritual: People who don't participate in Zhou ancestor worship
  • Cultural: People who don't follow Zhou customs (language, clothing, food)

‚ùå What It Didn't Mean

"Barbarian" didn't necessarily mean primitive, uncivilized, or technologically inferior. Some "barbarian" groups had sophisticated cultures, advanced military tech, and complex societies. The Zhou called them barbarians because they were OUTSIDE, not because they were inferior.

üåç Universal Pattern

This happens everywhere: dominant cultures create categories ("civilized" vs. "barbarian," "us" vs. "them") that serve political purposes. Think: Greeks calling non-Greeks "barbaroi," Romans calling everyone else "barbarians," Europeans calling indigenous peoples "savages." Same pattern!

Zhou Anxieties About the North

How do Zhou texts describe northern peoples?

  • Often as threats (raids, invasions)
  • Occasionally as allies (when convenient)
  • Always as "outside" the ritual/cultural order

771 BCE proves these fears well-founded!

Rong and Di peoples invade, kill Zhou king, force capital relocation

üò∞ Existential Threat

The northern steppe peoples represented a constant existential threat to Chinese states. They could (and did!) invade, loot, kill, and disappear. This anxiety runs throughout Chinese history.

üìÖ 771 BCE Callback

Remember from Lecture 2: the 771 BCE invasion that ended Western Zhou was carried out by groups the Zhou called "barbarians." This wasn't just rhetoric‚Äîit was real military danger.

ü§ù Complicated Relationship

But it's not just hostility! There was also trade (Chinese wanted horses, steppe peoples wanted grain and manufactured goods), diplomatic alliances, intermarriage, and cultural exchange. The relationship is always ambivalent.

üîó Long-Term Pattern

This tension between sedentary China and mobile steppe continues for millennia: Xiongnu, Turks, Khitans, Jurchens, Mongols, Manchus. Understanding this Bronze Age foundation helps make sense of later history.

Connection to "Outside the Core"

Northern steppe peoples exemplify regional diversity:

  • Different subsistence (herding vs. farming)
  • Different political forms (tribal vs. bureaucratic)
  • Different material culture (mobile vs. monumental)
  • Constant tension shapes Chinese thinking about civilization vs. "outside"

Long-term pattern: Steppe peoples as recurring military threat AND cultural exchange partners

üîÑ The Pattern Continues

This Bronze Age pattern‚Äîsteppe military threat + cultural exchange‚Äîcontinues throughout Chinese history. Eventually it leads to:

  • The Great Wall (multiple dynasties trying to keep nomads out)
  • Tribute system (paying nomads NOT to raid)
  • Nomadic conquest dynasties (Yuan/Mongols, Qing/Manchus)

üí° Civilizational Dialogue

Chinese civilization is shaped BY its interactions with the steppe, not in isolation from it. The constant pressure from the north influences Chinese military technology, political philosophy, and cultural identity.

Looking Forward

Later dynasties will try to absorb/conquer these regions:

  • Qin Dynasty (221-206 BCE) ‚Äî First empire, pushes south and west
  • Han Dynasty (206 BCE-220 CE) ‚Äî Major expansion into Yangtze valley, Sichuan, northern frontiers

Remember: "Chinese unity" is always CONSTRUCTED, CONTESTED, NEGOTIATED

‚è≠Ô∏è Preview of Later History

The regional diversity we've discussed doesn't just disappear! It takes centuries of conquest, migration, and cultural assimilation to create a somewhat unified "China."

üèõÔ∏è Qin and Han

The Qin (first imperial dynasty) and Han (longest-lasting early dynasty) will expand Chinese political control dramatically. But even then, regional cultures persist beneath political unity.

üí° The Big Lesson

"Chinese unity" is an achievement, not a given. It's constantly being created, maintained, and negotiated through political power, cultural assimilation, and historical narrative.

Standardizing Diversity

"We'll watch later states try to standardize this diversity‚Äîsometimes successfully, sometimes not."

Examples of standardization attempts:

  • Qin Dynasty: Standardized writing, weights, measures, currency
  • Han Dynasty: Established Confucianism as state ideology
  • Later dynasties: Examination system, official histories, ritual codes

But regional languages, cuisines, customs, and identities persist throughout Chinese history!

üéØ The Persistence of Difference

Despite repeated attempts at standardization and unification, regional diversity never completely disappears from China. Even today, Chinese regions have distinct identities.

üìö Examples of Persistence

  • Languages: Mandarin, Cantonese, Shanghainese, Hokkien, etc. are mutually unintelligible
  • Cuisines: Sichuan, Cantonese, Jiangsu, Shandong cuisines remain distinct
  • Local customs: Marriage practices, festivals, architecture vary by region

üí≠ Why This Matters

Understanding this helps students see that "China" is not a monolithic, unchanging entity. It's diverse, dynamic, and constantly negotiating unity and difference.
