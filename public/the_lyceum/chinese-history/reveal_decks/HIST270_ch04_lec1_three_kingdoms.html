  • Identify Three Kingdoms and explain stalemate

The Big Question

What happens when a 400-year empire collapses?
Why couldn't anyone put it back together?

Today's Thesis:

The late Han did not simply "fall"‚Äîit devolved into militarized regional states competing over food, manpower, and legitimacy. Even reunification under the Western Jin proved fragile because the institutions and elite power arrangements that emerged during war were hard to reverse.

üéØ Distinguish from Qin Collapse

Qin fell quickly (15 years); Han lasted 400 years, creating deep networks and entrenched elites. When Han breaks, you can't just "restart"‚Äîit's fundamental restructuring.

üí° Why This Matters

Sets up conditions for: non-Han regimes (L2), Buddhism's expansion (L3), South China as cultural center.

The Han Collapse: A Perfect Storm

The Yellow Turban Rebellion (184 CE)

  • Not just "peasant revolt"‚Äîa religious uprising
  • Way of Great Peace (Taiping Dao): Daoist-inspired millenarian movement
  • Large-scale coordinated violence across regions
  • "The Blue Heaven is dead; the Yellow Heaven shall rise"
×

Yellow Turban Rebellion (184 CE)

Massive uprising organized by Zhang Jue through Way of Great Peace religious movement.

  • Religious foundation: Daoist beliefs, healing rituals, millenarian prophecy
  • Yellow symbolism: Earth element, coming age; followers wore yellow
  • Multi-regional: Simultaneously erupted in multiple provinces
  • Mass following: Hundreds of thousands joined
  • Impact: Though suppressed by 205, permanently fractured Han military control
×

Way of Great Peace (Taiping Dao)

Daoist-inspired movement founded by Zhang Jue combining:

  • Healing practices, confession, talismanic water
  • Millenarian prophecy: current age ending, new age beginning
  • Social critique: corrupt officials vs. righteous followers
  • 36 divisions with military-style hierarchy

Religious vision of cosmic transformation mobilized masses quickly.

üìö Yellow Turban as Catalyst

Time: ~3 min. Different from earlier revolts because: (1) Religious organization independent of state, (2) Regional coordination, (3) Ideological critique of Heaven's mandate.

üéØ Key Point

Emphasize religious meaning, not just economics. "Blue Heaven dead" = explicit rejection of Han's mandate.

üîó Bridge

"Han response seemed logical: authorize local forces to suppress rebellion. But this decision changed everything..."

The Fatal Response

Han Court's Decision:

Authorize generals and local elites to raise armies to suppress rebellion

The Problem:

  • Temporary decentralization becomes permanent
  • Once armed power goes local, it's hard to re-centralize
  • Regional commanders become warlords with independent armies
  • Loyalty shifts from court to personal military networks

The Han created the armies that would destroy it.

üéØ Structural Trap

Time: 2 min. Not "bad emperors"‚Äîit's structural: (1) Rebellion threatens state ‚Üí must suppress, (2) Central armies insufficient ‚Üí authorize regional, (3) Regional forces suppress but now exist with power, (4) Court tries to disband ‚Üí they refuse, (5) Permanent militarization.

üí≠ Discussion

If time: "Other examples of temporary military measures becoming permanent?" (Roman armies, various military coups)

The 189‚Äì190 Court Crisis

  • 189: Court coup, eunuchs massacred, factional fighting
  • Dong Zhuo enters with troops, seizes court and emperor
  • Key shift: Legitimacy becomes hostage‚Äîemperor is prize, not governing authority
  • 190: Coalition against Dong Zhuo fractures; civil war becomes normal
  • Luoyang devastated; court moved; symbolic center loses credibility

Court is no longer governing institution‚Äîit's a prize to be captured.

×

Eunuch Massacre (189 CE)

Court eunuchs had gained enormous power. Scholar-official factions (led by Yuan Shao) staged violent coup, slaughtering thousands.

Why it matters: Court dysfunctional‚Äîfactions settling scores with mass murder. Created power vacuum Dong Zhuo exploited. Military force now trumps political legitimacy.

×

Dong Zhuo (died 192 CE)

Frontier general who marched into capital during 189 chaos, deposed one emperor, installed child emperor, ruled as dictator.

Significance: Broke rules‚Äîshowed military force overrides court legitimacy. Created precedent: emperor as puppet. Triggered coalitions (failed). Devastated capital. Assassinated 192, but proved old system dead.

üìö 189-190 as Hinge

Time: 3 min. Han definitively ends as functional government (though formally continues to 220). Narrative: 189 violence ‚Üí Dong Zhuo enters ‚Üí 190 coalition fails ‚Üí civil war.

üéØ Key Points

Dong Zhuo important as proof system is dead (anyone with army can seize capital). Legitimacy as hostage: emperor symbolically matters but has zero power. Luoyang devastated = symbolic center broken.

üí° Zombie Dynasty

"Han didn't 'fall'‚Äîit became zombie. Formally alive, actually dead. Dynasty continues on paper to 220, but after 189-190, it's warlords fighting over corpse."

By early 3rd century, Cao Cao dominates north through superior organization:

  • Control of grain (garrison farming)
  • Strategic alliances, elite co-optation
  • Administration that extracts resources amid chaos
  • Military effectiveness through logistics

Not hero or villain‚Äîinstitutional competence in collapsing world.

×

Cao Cao (155‚Äì220 CE)

Most successful warlord-state-builder of late Han. Started as regional official, became dominant power in north by 200s.

Why he succeeded: Logistics (tuntian for grain), functioning bureaucracy, elite management (meritocracy + patronage), strategic patience (consolidate north first).

Legacy: Romance portrays as cunning villain. History shows pragmatic builder. His son Cao Pi ends Han, founds Wei (220).

×

Garrison Farming (Tuntian)

Military-agricultural system: soldiers/colonists farm near garrisons, provide food directly to armies.

Types: Military (soldiers farm), civilian (refugees/peasants farm state land, pay grain rent). Strategic placement near borders/supply routes.

Why crucial: Solves civil war's fundamental problem‚Äîhow feed armies when agriculture devastated? Creates self-sufficient military regions. Foundation for hereditary military households.

üìö Cao Cao as Transitional

Time: 2 min. By early 200s, dominates north. Important as example of state capacity under catastrophe.

üéØ Three Points

(1) Logistics wins (tuntian solves grain), (2) Administration matters (functioning bureaucracy), (3) Cultural complexity (Romance=villain, history=pragmatic builder).

üí≠ Teaching Tip

Many know Cao Cao from pop culture as villain. Complicate: "Romance written 900 years later, promotes Confucian virtue over pragmatism. Historical Cao Cao: ruthless? Yes. Effective? Also yes."

The Three Kingdoms: Periodization

Don't Confuse the Periods:

  • Breakdown: 184‚Äì220 (Yellow Turbans ‚Üí warlords ‚Üí end of Han)
  • Three Kingdoms proper: 220‚Äì280 (Wei, Shu-Han, Wu as recognized states)

Timeline:

  • 220: Cao Pi founds Wei (north)
  • 221: Liu Bei founds Shu-Han (southwest)
  • 229: Sun Quan founds Wu (southeast)
  • 263: Shu falls to Wei
  • 266: Sima clan replaces Wei with Jin
  • 280: Jin conquers Wu ‚Üí reunification

üìÖ Clarity on Periods

Time: 2 min. Students confuse "Three Kingdoms era" with entire collapse. Be precise: 184-220 = Han collapse/warlords (not yet Three Kingdoms). 220-280 = Three Kingdoms proper.

üéØ Key Dates

220 = Han ends, Wei founded. 280 = Jin reunifies. 60 years = stalemate duration.

üí° Setup

"Even when someone wins (Jin 280), took 60 years from Three Kingdoms start. Why so long? Stalemate wasn't incompetence‚Äîit was structural..."

Why Stalemate Happens

Three reasons none could quickly conquer others:

1. Geography

  • Sichuan basin hard to invade (mountains)
  • Yangzi River both highway and moat
  • Natural barriers favor defense

2. Logistics

  • Moving grain/siege equipment harder than troops
  • Supply lines stretch; armies weaken
  • Conquest requires sustained campaigns

3. Institution-Building

  • Each regime becomes just strong enough to survive
  • Administration, taxation, recruitment stabilize
  • Stalemate allows consolidation

üéØ Structural Stalemate

Time: 3 min. Not incompetence‚Äîstructural factors favor defense. (1) Geography: mountains/rivers create barriers, (2) Logistics: ancient rule‚Äîarmies on stomachs, far from base=weaker, (3) Institution-building: 60 years stalemate = 60 years state-building in each region.

üí° Big Picture

"Paradox: stalemate allows each region to develop distinct identities/power structures. By Jin reunification (280), trying to glue together three functioning states, not reconquer broken territory. That's why Jin reunification so fragile..."

Military Transformation

1. Cavalry Revolution

  • Stirrups attested early 4th century (Jin era)
  • Cavalry effectiveness accelerates
  • Sets conditions for heavier cavalry warfare

2. Military Households

  • Permanent soldier families, hereditary status
  • Families farm assigned land; state extracts rent + service
  • Creates durable military caste

3. Non-Chinese Troops

  • Steppe/frontier peoples recruited
  • Sometimes settled in strategic zones
  • Not "invasion"‚Äîintegration story that can flip
×

Cavalry Revolution

Cavalry becomes increasingly important late Han/Three Kingdoms.

Stirrups: Securely attested early 4th century (Jin). Earlier forms debated. Allow better fighting from horseback. Steppe influence: Contact with nomadic cavalry drives adaptation. Long-term: Foundation for medieval Chinese cavalry.

Don't claim "invented 300 CE"‚Äîsay "conditions developing during era."

×

Military Households

System where families have permanent military status passed down.

How: Son of soldier = soldier. Land grants. Dual obligation: grain rent + military service. Legally distinct from civilians. Why matters: Permanent military class. State maintains readiness without constant recruitment. Socially rigid. Continues through Tang.

×

Non-Han Troops

Chinese states recruit steppe/frontier peoples (Xiongnu, Xianbei, Qiang).

Why: Han manpower depleted. Frontier peoples have skills (cavalry). They need land/resources. Some are steppe refugees. Trap: You've militarized non-Han populations inside China proper. When Jin collapses (L2), these groups found northern kingdoms. This is NOT "invasion"‚Äîit's Chinese states recruiting that creates conditions for later "flip."

üìö Three Transformations

Time: 4 min. These reshape warfare/society, reinforce each other.

üéØ Key Points

(1) Cavalry: careful with stirrups (4th century contexts), cavalry becomes more important. (2) Military households: logical extension of tuntian, hereditary=rigid but effective. (3) Non-Han recruitment: **CRITICAL BRIDGE TO L2**. Chinese states recruit because Han manpower exhausted, frontier peoples have skills. Trap: militarized non-Han inside borders. When Jin collapses, they found kingdoms.

üîó Bridge

"These three changes define warfare for centuries. Also create conditions for northern kingdoms (L2). So someone finally wins in 280... except it lasts only a generation."

The Western Jin: False Dawn

Timeline:

  • 266: Sima clan replaces Wei, founds Jin
  • 280: Jin conquers Wu ‚Üí reunification
  • 291‚Äì306: War of Eight Princes
  • 316: Western Jin collapses; north lost

Why Fragile:

  • Reunification = military achievement, but governance needs stable succession + elite coordination
  • Jin relies on princes/great families ‚Üí high risk for factional warfare
  • Regional power structures from Three Kingdoms remain strong
×

War of Eight Princes (291‚Äì306)

Devastating civil war among Jin imperial princes fighting for court control.

What: After first emperor dies, succession crisis. Eight princes (Sima family) fight. 15 years internal warfare shatters state. Consequences: Administrative collapse, regions militarize, frontier regimes expand, armies exhausted, elite disillusionment. Proximate cause of Western Jin collapse and opening for northern regimes (L2).

üìö Western Jin

Time: 3 min. Jin reunification (280) looks impressive‚Äîfirst unified since Han. Lasts one generation.

üéØ Why Fails

Structural: Military conquest ‚â† stable governance. Jin inherits three functioning regional states with own power structures/elites/military. Jin governs through princes‚Äîbig mistake. Eight Princes (291-306): Imperial family fights itself. 15 years civil war destroys state capacity. Regions militarize; frontier defense collapses.

üí° Make Causal

"War of Eight Princes predictable: (1) Jin reunifies by force 280, (2) Regional structures remain, (3) Jin assigns princes to regions, (4) Succession crisis ‚Üí princes fight, (5) Internal war ‚Üí breakdown ‚Üí frontier expansion. By 316, Jin lost north, court flees south, Period of Division enters new phase."

When the Educated Give Up

The Nine Rank System

Original Intent

  • Identify talent
  • Rank by ability
  • Meritocratic standards
  • Connect court to locals

What It Became

  • Elites control evaluations
  • Birth/connections matter
  • Great families monopolize
  • Serving risky, low payoff

Result: Office access tilts toward birth/networks. Why risk life if system rigged?

×

Nine Rank System (Jiupin)

Personnel evaluation system during Cao Wei to identify/rank candidates.

Supposed to: Central appoints "impartial judges" in regions. They evaluate/rank candidates 1-9. Rankings determine office eligibility. Goal: identify talent without capital travel. Actually: Elite capture‚Äîgreat families control evaluation. Reputation networks matter most. Hereditary advantage. Barrier to outsiders. System for talent became tool for aristocratic preservation.

üìö Nine Ranks

Time: 2 min. Implemented during Wei as talent identification. Gets captured by power structures.

üéØ Gap Intent/Reality

Design: local judges rank 1-9. Goal: find talent efficiently. Problem: evaluators from/favor great families. Result: birth/connections trump ability.

üí° Accelerates Aristocratization

Great families (genealogies, land, education) consolidate power. Office becomes hereditary privilege. Talented outsiders can't break in. Makes government service less attractive even to elites.

Seven Sages of Bamboo Grove

3rd-century intellectuals, symbols of cultural withdrawal/philosophical freedom (Cao Wei/Western Jin).

Famous: Ruan Ji (poet, wild singing), Ji Kang (musician, executed for defiance), Liu Ling (notorious drinker, walked naked). Represent: Withdrawal from dangerous politics, naturalism (spontaneity over ritual), critique (Confucian failed, return Daoist authenticity), performance (wildness as elite status competition). Think: philosophical provocateurs using Daoism to critique collapsing Confucian order.

×

Xuanxue ("Study of Mysterious")

Intellectual movement late Han-Jin reengaging Daoist philosophy through commentary.

Themes: Naturalness (ziran‚Äîspontaneous virtue), non-action (wuwei‚Äîminimal governance), emptiness/presence, critique of artifice (Confucian ritual=hypocrisy). Why now: Confucian state collapsed ‚Üí ideology bankrupt. Elite withdrawal ‚Üí need philosophical justification. Daoist texts offer alternative. Xuanxue creates intellectual space for Buddhism to enter (L3)‚ÄîDaoist concepts become tools for Buddhist ideas.

üìö Seven Sages

Time: 3 min. Both real people (3rd c.) and cultural symbols. Use to illustrate withdrawal and xuanxue.

üéØ Key Points

Who: Intellectuals Cao Wei/early Jin. Famous for: wine, poetry, music, flouting conventions. Some (Ji Kang) died for defiance. Others performed withdrawal while maintaining networks. What represent: Philosophical critique (Confucian failed, return Daoist authenticity), cultural withdrawal (if state corrupt/dangerous, opt out), elite performance (wildness as cultural capital). Xuanxue: Reengagement with Laozi/Zhuangzi. Themes: naturalness, non-action, spontaneity vs. ritual.

üí° Dual Function

(1) Personal survival‚Äîpolitics risky. (2) Cultural critique‚Äîform of protest. But note privilege: only wealthy elites can afford withdrawal.

Wang Xizhi (303‚Äì361 CE)

Most celebrated calligrapher in Chinese history, "Sage of Calligraphy."

Why matters: Calligraphy becomes form of elite cultural competition when political service dangerous. Requires education, aesthetic training, resources. Expression of individual character/refinement. üí° Big Point
