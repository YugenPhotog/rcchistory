Confucianism & Its Critics

HIST 270 — Chapter 2, Lecture 2

Review Questions: Q5, Q6, Q7

📖 Lecture Overview

This lecture covers the Confucian tradition and its major critics. All four thinkers here are shi — the displaced lower aristocrats from Lecture 1 competing for patronage by offering competing "how to govern" packages. The central debate runs through all three review questions: can virtue govern, or does it require something more?

Review Questions Covered

Q5 — What were Confucius's foundational principles, and how did he redefine the junzi?
Q6 — How did Mozi use utility to challenge Confucian ideas on family, ritual, and war?
Q7 — How did Mencius and Xunzi differ on human nature, and how did that shape their approaches to government?

Four Thinkers, One Question

The shared question: After Zhou authority collapsed, what can hold society together?

Today's Thinkers

  • Confucius — virtue, ritual, the junzi (Q5)
  • Mozi — utility, universal love (Q6)
  • Mencius — human nature is good (Q7)
  • Xunzi — human nature is bad (Q7)

What They Agree On

  • The world is in disorder
  • Rulers bear responsibility for that disorder
  • There are principles that could restore order
  • Those principles can be taught and learned

📖 Roadmap Notes

The agreement list is as important as the disagreement list. All four thinkers share certain assumptions: that the political crisis has a diagnosable cause, that principles exist for addressing it, and that those principles can be communicated through teaching. This distinguishes them from pure cynics (who think nothing works) and from mystics (who think the answer is beyond rational communication). The Hundred Schools debate is a family argument within a shared framework.

Part I

Confucius and the Confucian Vision

Virtue, ritual, and the redefined gentleman — Q5

Confucius 孔子 551–479 BCE

His Life

  • Born in the state of Lu — minor aristocratic background
  • Wandered from state to state seeking a ruler willing to implement his ideas
  • Gathered disciples; compiled his teachings through conversation
  • Died believing he had failed — no ruler adopted his program

His Legacy

  • Disciples compiled his sayings as the Analects 論語
  • Most influential Chinese thinker in history
  • Shaped East Asian civilization for 2,500 years
  • The irony: became dominant ideology only after his death

📖 Understanding This Slide

The biographical irony — Confucius died believing he had failed, yet became the most influential Chinese thinker — is worth dwelling on. What does it mean to "succeed" as a philosopher? Confucius measured success by whether rulers implemented his ideas. By that measure, he failed completely. But the ideas themselves outlasted every ruler of his era. The philosophers who "succeeded" by getting hired — the Legalists who advised Qin — were discredited within a generation when their state collapsed.

The Analects: Not a systematic treatise but a collection of remembered conversations, compiled by disciples after his death. Short, often cryptic passages. Many sayings have multiple plausible interpretations — which is part of why they've supported such a rich commentarial tradition.

The Confucian Core

Ren 仁

Humaneness / Benevolence — the foundation virtue. Treating others with genuine care and respect. Not a rule to follow but a disposition to cultivate.

Li 禮

Ritual propriety — the proper performance of social roles. Not empty ceremony but the external expression of inner virtue. Ren without Li is formless; Li without Ren is hollow.

Xiao 孝

Filial piety — respect for parents and ancestors. The training ground for all other virtues. If you can't respect those closest to you, you can't respect anyone.

These three are inseparable: Ren is the spirit, Li is the form, Xiao is the practice ground.

📖 Breaking It Down

Ren is the hardest to translate precisely. "Humaneness," "benevolence," "goodness," "love" — all partial. The character combines "person" (人) and "two" (二), suggesting a fundamentally relational quality. You can't be ren alone; ren only exists in relationships. For Confucius, cultivating ren means practicing treating others the way you want to be treated — in every relationship, consistently.

Li originally referred to religious ceremonies — the proper performance of sacrifices. Confucius extended it to all social interactions: greetings, table manners, the proper way to address superiors and inferiors, funeral rites, coming-of-age ceremonies. The point is that proper form matters — it expresses and reinforces the social relationships that hold civilization together.

The relationship between Ren and Li: Confucius says "Without ren, what is ritual?" But he also insists on performing ritual correctly even when it seems burdensome. They're mutually dependent. Inner virtue needs outward expression; outward form needs inner commitment.

The Five Relationships

Society is structured by five fundamental pairs — each with specific obligations running both ways.

  • Ruler → Subject: benevolent governance ↔ loyal service
  • Parent → Child: nurture and love ↔ filial piety
  • Husband → Wife: care and provision ↔ support and respect
  • Elder sibling → Younger: guidance ↔ deference
  • Friend → Friend: the only equal relationship — mutual trust and loyalty
Four of five are hierarchical — but obligations run both ways. Authority is conditional on fulfilling its duties.

📖 Key Nuance

Students often read the Five Relationships as simply endorsing hierarchy. The crucial nuance: every relationship has obligations running in both directions. The ruler must govern benevolently for the subject's loyalty to be warranted. The parent must provide love and nurture for the child's filial piety to be owed. Authority is not unconditional — it must be earned and maintained through proper conduct. This is where the Mandate of Heaven logic reappears at the micro level of family and social relationships.

The friend-to-friend relationship is the only equal one — and it's included deliberately. Confucius values friendship as a distinct moral relationship with its own obligations of honesty and loyalty, not reducible to the other four. Good friends tell you difficult truths.

The Junzi 君子 — Q5 Answer

Original Meaning

  • Junzi literally means "son of a lord"
  • Before Confucius: a nobleman by birth
  • Status determined by heredity, not character

Confucius's Redefinition

  • The junzi is now an exemplary person by virtue, not birth
  • Anyone who cultivates ren, practices li, and fulfills their relationships properly
  • The junzi leads by moral example, not by command
  • Status determined by character, not heredity
This redefinition is quietly radical: virtue replaces blood as the basis of social authority.

📖 Q5 Core Answer

This slide delivers the direct answer to Q5. The redefinition of junzi is Confucius's most politically consequential conceptual move. In a world where hereditary aristocracy is crumbling (because Warring States competition is rewarding merit over birth), Confucius provides an alternative basis for social authority: moral cultivation. You don't need to be born into authority — you earn it by becoming a genuinely virtuous person.

This has enormous implications. It means education matters — anyone can become a junzi through proper learning and practice. It means rulers can be held to standards — they are junzi only if they actually govern with virtue. And it means the old aristocracy has no inherent claim to authority if they don't cultivate virtue.

Why "quietly radical"? Confucius is often read as conservative — praising the early Zhou, defending hierarchy, emphasizing proper ritual. But the junzi redefinition undermines hereditary aristocracy from within the framework of traditional virtue. He doesn't attack the old order directly; he redefines what makes the old order legitimate.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Confucius redefines the junzi — the exemplary person — from "nobleman by birth" to "person of virtue by cultivation."

If virtue can be cultivated through education, what does that imply about who deserves to hold political authority?

Instructor Notes

This question points toward the later development of the civil service examination system (Ch. 3) — the Han dynasty's mechanism for selecting officials based on mastery of Confucian texts rather than birth. The exam system is, in a sense, the institutionalization of Confucius's junzi ideal. The irony is that the exam system also became a new form of hereditary privilege — wealthy families could afford the education, poor families often couldn't.

Part II

Mozi and the Utility Critique

Universal love versus hierarchical love — Q6

Mozi 墨子 ca. 480–390 BCE

Background

  • Came from a family of craftsmen — not the aristocratic shi background
  • Confucius's most important early rival
  • His followers became known as defensive military specialists
  • The Mohist school was the most organized of the Hundred Schools

His Standard

  • Every idea should be evaluated by its benefit to the people and the state
  • If it doesn't benefit people → reject it, regardless of tradition
  • This utility standard is his weapon against Confucian ritual

📖 Understanding Mozi

Mozi's craftsman background is significant. He approaches problems as an engineer — what works? what produces the intended result? — rather than as an aristocrat concerned with proper form. His utility standard is genuinely radical: it subjects every practice, including sacred Confucian rituals, to cost-benefit analysis. If elaborate funeral ceremonies waste resources that could feed people, they should be simplified, regardless of what tradition demands.

The Mohist organization: The Mohist school was unusually disciplined — almost military in structure, with a leader (called the Grand Master) who directed members to help defend states under unjust attack. This made them uniquely capable of putting anti-war principles into practice rather than just arguing for them.

Jian Ai 兼愛 — Universal Love (Q6)

Confucian Position

  • Love is graded by relationship — you owe more to family than strangers
  • Filial piety is the foundation of all virtues
  • Differential love is natural and proper
  • Rituals that strengthen family bonds strengthen society

Mozi's Challenge

  • Graded love produces conflict — each family, state prioritizes itself
  • Treat everyone's parents as you treat your own
  • Treat every state as you treat your own state
  • Universal love is not just ethical — it's practically effective
Mozi's war critique follows directly: if you treated other states as your own, you would never attack them.

📖 Q6 Core Answer

This slide is the heart of Q6. Mozi's critique has three prongs against Confucianism, all using the utility standard:

1. Family ties: Confucian graded love (love family more than strangers) causes the very conflicts it claims to prevent — each family, clan, and state puts its own interests first, producing constant friction. Universal love would eliminate the partiality that drives conflict.

2. Ritual: Elaborate funeral ceremonies, extended mourning periods, and expensive sacrifices waste resources that could go to feeding and protecting people. The utility standard condemns them. Mozi advocates simplified funerals and brief mourning.

3. War: Aggressive warfare is the logical extension of graded love — you prioritize your own state over others, so you feel entitled to take their resources. Universal love eliminates the moral justification for aggression. Mozi's followers practiced what he preached by helping defend states under unjust attack.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Angus Graham's Disputers of the Tao argues that Mozi represents a genuinely different social vision from Confucianism — not just a refinement but an alternative. Where Confucius grounds ethics in natural human relationships (family first), Mozi grounds it in impartial calculation of benefit. This is the ancient Chinese precursor to utilitarian ethics.

Part III

Mencius vs. Xunzi

Is human nature good or bad? — Q7

Mencius 孟子 ca. 370–300 BCE

Human Nature is Good

  • People are born with four moral sprouts — innate moral tendencies
  • Compassion → ren / Shame → yi / Modesty → li / Right/wrong → wisdom
  • Bad behavior is the result of bad environment, not bad nature
  • Education and nurture allow the sprouts to flower

Political Implication

  • Benevolent government nurtures people's innate goodness
  • A tyrannical ruler has forfeited his right to rule — people may rebel
  • Mencius extends the Mandate of Heaven to justify popular resistance
  • The people's welfare is the highest political value

📖 Mencius's Key Moves

The four sprouts: Mencius uses a vivid example. If you see a child about to fall into a well, you feel horror and alarm — not because you calculated that saving the child is in your interest, but because you can't help it. That instinctive compassion is the sprout of ren. Mencius argues all four moral qualities are similarly innate — they just need cultivation to develop fully.

Legitimating rebellion: This is Mencius's most politically charged contribution. He says that when a ruler loses his benevolence, he has already lost the Mandate — and the people have the right to remove him. This is a significant extension of the Zhou Mandate of Heaven doctrine, which was mainly used by new dynasties to justify past conquests. Mencius makes it available as a justification for current resistance. Chinese emperors were uncomfortable with this for good reason — Mencius was periodically censored or de-canonized.

Xunzi 荀子 ca. 310–215 BCE

Human Nature is Problematic

  • Natural desires — food, comfort, pleasure — lead to conflict if unchecked
  • "If human nature were good, why would we need sages and ritual?"
  • Still Confucian: moral behavior is possible through education
  • Ritual and education correct bad nature rather than nurture good nature

Political Implication

  • Government must be strong enough to shape human behavior
  • Ritual and law work together — the carrot and the stick
  • Two of Xunzi's students: Han Feizi and Li Si — became Legalists
  • The bridge between Confucianism and Legalism

📖 Xunzi as Bridge

Xunzi is the most important bridge figure in Chinese intellectual history. He's a Confucian — he believes in ritual, education, and the junzi ideal. But his pessimistic view of human nature leads him toward conclusions that his students took to their logical extreme: if people are naturally selfish, ritual alone won't be enough. You need law and punishment. Han Feizi and Li Si took that step.

Xunzi also made the most rigorous philosophical arguments in the Confucian tradition — against prayer (the gods don't intervene in human affairs), against fatalism (hard work matters more than fate), and for the value of ritual as a psychological technology for shaping behavior. His rationalism makes him more palatable to modern readers than Mencius's idealism, but it also made him less canonized — the Confucian orthodoxy preferred Mencius's optimistic view of human nature.

Four Perspectives — Q5, Q6, Q7

Thinker Human Nature Key Concept Solution
Confucius Malleable Junzi, Ren, Li Moral example + ritual cultivation
Mozi Responds to incentives Jian ai, utility Universal love + practical reform
Mencius Fundamentally good Four sprouts Nurture innate virtue; benevolent rule
Xunzi Problematic / selfish Ritual as correction Education + strong institutions
All ask: Can virtue govern? No consensus — but the debate shaped Chinese civilization for 2,000 years.

📖 Summary Notes

The table is the take-home for Q7 (and summarizes Q5 and Q6 as well). If students can fill in this table in their own words, they've mastered the lecture's core content. The key comparison for Q7: Mencius and Xunzi agree that people can become moral through proper cultivation, but they disagree sharply on whether that cultivation is developing something already present (Mencius) or correcting something fundamentally defective (Xunzi). The difference sounds abstract but has real political implications: Mencius's government nurtures; Xunzi's government corrects.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Mencius says human nature is fundamentally good. Xunzi says it's fundamentally problematic. Both were Confucians.

Which view do you find more persuasive — and does your answer change how you think government should work?

Instructor Notes

This is genuinely a question students can engage with from their own experience. The Mencius-Xunzi debate maps onto contemporary debates about criminal justice (rehabilitation vs. punishment), education (unlock potential vs. correct deficiencies), and political philosophy (positive vs. negative liberty). The Chinese debate is 2,400 years old but the underlying question is perennial.

The physical spectrum activity from the original deck works well here: have students physically position themselves on a Mencius-to-Xunzi spectrum, then talk to someone near them. "Look around — we're divided, just like ancient China."

Respect and care for parents, elders, and ancestors. A person who cannot honor their parents cannot be trusted to honor their ruler or their subjects.
