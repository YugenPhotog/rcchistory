Part I

Setting the Stage

Who were the Mongols, and why does this chapter matter?

The Big Questions

  • How did the Mongols conquer all of China in seven decades?
  • Why did the Yuan Dynasty collapse before it reached two centuries?
  • How did Chinese people cope with foreign rule?
  • Did Mongol rule leave lasting marks on Chinese culture?
📖 Study Buddy Says
This chapter has two big threads running through it: the political/military story (the conquest) and the cultural/social story (what daily life was like under the conquerors). Keep both threads in your head as you study — the exam will test both.

The Mongols: Quick Context

What made them different?
  • Steppe nomads, cavalry-based warfare
  • First non-Chinese group to conquer all of China
  • Even non-Chinese rulers (Tanguts, Jurchens) couldn't stop them
  • Early campaigns brought massive destruction
Their transformation
  • By Khubilai's reign (1260–1294), became more sophisticated administrators
  • Mongols + Central Asians + Persians filled most government posts
  • Traditional Confucian elite had to find other occupations
📖 Study Buddy Says
Think of the Mongols' relationship with China in two phases: Phase 1 = destroy and plunder; Phase 2 = realize you can get richer by actually governing. Khubilai is the hinge point between those two phases.

Part II

Conquering the North

The Jin and Xia Dynasties fall to Chinggis Khan

Chinggis vs. Jin Dynasty

  • Campaigns began 1205, 1209; major invasion 1211
  • Jin had 150,000 cavalry + 300,000+ infantry — yet Mongol tactics frustrated them
  • Key tactic: Take a city, plunder it, then withdraw — let the Jin deal with the ruins
  • Jin court instability: general murdered emperor in 1213; capital moved to Kaifeng
⚔️ Jin lasted until 1234. By then they controlled only a province-sized territory around Kaifeng.

Yelü Qucai & the Tax Debate

  • Highly educated Khitan adviser recruited by Chinggis
  • Argued: don't turn north China into pastureland — tax farmers instead
  • Calculated revenues: 500,000 oz silver, 80,000 bolts silk, 20,000+ tons grain
  • Lost the debate — Ögödei chose Central Asian Muslim tax farmers instead
📖 Study Buddy Says
Yelü Qucai represents an early version of the argument that China works better when governed Chinese-style. The Muslim tax farmers were worse than Mongol lords for ordinary people — this is a pattern you'll see repeat itself.
🎓 Historians Weigh In
Historians like Thomas Allsen have studied how the Mongols gradually learned that extractive steppe strategies couldn't sustain revenue from a sedentary agricultural society — Yelü Qucai was ahead of his time in understanding this.

The Tangut Xia Dynasty

  • Initially submitted to Chinggis (1209) and agreed to help attack Jin
  • Xia soldiers later fought back against Mongols — personally enraged Chinggis
  • Chinggis led final siege of Xia capital in 1226 — died during the siege
  • His death was kept secret; when Xia ruler surrendered, he was immediately executed
The Mongol army then slaughtered the city's entire population. This was the Mongols' signature punishment for those who resisted after initially submitting — total annihilation as a warning to others.

The Human Cost in North China

  • Mongols plundered, not controlled — sometimes killed entire town populations
  • People who weren't slaughtered were frequently enslaved
  • Large land grants to generals = chaos, no stable governance
  • North China's economy began a downward spiral lasting centuries
Yuan Haowen (1190–1257): Chinese poet who collected Jin historical records and wrote laments about the destruction — viewing his writing as a way to preserve Chinese civilization.
Other Jin officials chose to serve the Mongols, believing Chinese administrators could shield society from the worst effects.

Part III

Conquering the South

Why did Song hold out — and why did it ultimately fall?

Song's Head Start

  • Song knew the Mongols were coming — watched Jin and Xia fall in the 1220s–1230s
  • Raised revenues, built armies, prepared defenses
  • Yangzi River and canal networks = cavalry nearly useless in the south
  • Mongols needed a navy — had to build one from scratch using Chinese shipbuilders
📖 Study Buddy Says
Song's geography was its greatest asset. Every non-Chinese group that had conquered north China had been stopped at the Yangzi. The Mongols had to solve an entirely new military problem — water warfare — before they could cross.

Khubilai Khan — The Game-Changer

  • Grandson of Chinggis; assigned control of all north China in 1251
  • Surrounded himself with Chinese advisers, especially Liu Bingzhong
  • Never learned to read Chinese but understood: China works through Chinese methods
  • Built new capital at Dadu (modern Beijing, 1264)
  • Took dynastic name Yuan ("primal") in 1271 from the Book of Changes
In 1259 Möngke died and Khubilai fought a four-year civil war to become Great Khan — after which he could finally focus fully on Song.

The Siege of Xiangyang (1268–1273)

  • Xiangyang controlled the key northern tributary of the Yangzi River
  • Five-year siege — both sides had thousands of boats and tens of thousands of troops
  • Mongol force was multiethnic: Chinese, Uighur, Persian, Jurchen, Korean
  • Muslim engineers brought catapults capable of hurling 100-lb rocks
Once Xiangyang fell in 1273, General Bayan led 200,000 troops (mainly Chinese) south. Song's resistance began to collapse.

The Fall of Song

  • Song's emperor was a child; court factions spent energy fighting each other
  • To frighten Hangzhou to surrender, Mongols ordered the total slaughter of Changzhou
  • Empress dowager surrendered; Song court taken to Beijing as hostages
  • Wen Tianxiang kept fighting as guerrilla long after any hope was gone — chose execution over serving the Yuan
Song loyalists searched secretly for the bones of Song emperors to give them proper reburial — a poignant act of loyalty and cultural preservation in defeat.

Part IV

Life Under Mongol Rule

How did ordinary Chinese experience Yuan governance?

The Four-Tier Ethnic Hierarchy

① Mongols
Top tier — all privileges
② Semu Ren
Uighurs, Persians, Central Asians — "useful people"
③ Han
Former Jin subjects — north Chinese
④ Nanren (Southerners) — former Song subjects — lowest tier
📖 Study Buddy Says
This hierarchy affected taxes (Han taxed by household; southerners by acreage), legal treatment (only Chinese were tattooed for theft), and access to government offices.

Mongol Controls on Chinese Society

Chinese were forbidden to…
  • Own weapons
  • Take Mongol names
  • Pass as Mongols
  • Assemble or congregate in public
  • Own bamboo (could make bows)
Legal asymmetry
  • Chinese fighting back against a Mongol → severe penalties
  • Mongol murdering a Chinese → pay a fine
  • Intermarriage discouraged (though it occurred)
The goal was to preserve Mongol privilege while extracting maximum wealth from China's economy.

Economic Life Under the Yuan

  • Mongols encouraged trade — they needed a productive economy to extract wealth
  • Repaired the Grand Canal (ruined during Jin conquest)
  • Converted Song paper money to Yuan currency
  • Chinese export industries — especially porcelain — thrived
Blue-and-White Porcelain: The Jingdezhen kilns invented underglaze blue decoration using cobalt blue during the Yuan — stimulated by Arab clients wanting durable, ornately designed ceramics. This innovation permanently shaped Chinese ceramics.
📖 Study Buddy Says
The contradiction at the heart of Yuan economics: the Mongols both devastated the north Chinese economy and created conditions for cultural/commercial innovation in other areas. Hold both ideas at once.

Yuan Dynasty in Decline

  • After Khubilai's death (1294), Mongol nobles fought over direction of empire
  • Most Mongols stayed in Mongolia — never integrated into China as Jurchens had
  • Renzong (r. 1311) — first Mongol emperor who could read and speak Chinese; restored limited civil service exams in 1313
  • His son Yingzong continued China-centered policies → assassinated by opposing factions
By the 1330s–1340s: factional civil wars + rampant inflation + climate cooling + deadly disease. Power devolved to local strongmen. The Yuan Dynasty collapsed by 1368.

Part V

The Chinese Educated Elite

How did scholars, artists, and writers respond to Mongol rule?

The Shrinking Path to Government Service

  • Civil service exams restored in 1315 — but heavily restricted by quotas
  • No more than 25% southerners, no more than 25% Han among passers
  • Only about 2% of bureaucratic posts filled through the exam system
  • Most Chinese who served did so as clerks, not scholar-officials
🎓 Historians Weigh In
John Dardess and others have shown that the contraction of official careers pushed the educated elite to redefine what it meant to be a Confucian scholar — leading to a flowering of local institution-building, painting, and drama that defined Chinese culture for centuries.

Zhao Mengfu: The Dilemma of Collaboration

  • Member of the Song imperial clan — grew up in privileged Hangzhou circle
  • After Song's fall, spent five years with poets, painters, calligraphers
  • When Khubilai recruited southerners in 1286, Zhao accepted — alienated friends and family
  • In the north, pressed for better treatment of officials; worked to bring down corrupt Tibetan chancellor Sangha
  • Rose to president of Hanlin Academy by 1316
📖 Study Buddy Says
Zhao Mengfu is the textbook case for debating collaboration vs. resistance. He used Mongol power to advance Chinese interests — but others never forgave him for serving at all. There's no simple verdict on his choice.

Yuan Painting: Politics in Brushstrokes

  • With official careers blocked, scholars turned to painting as a form of expression
  • Preference for archaic styles = longing for the past + critique of the present
  • Painted for restricted audiences of like-minded individuals
  • Key painters: Zhao Mengfu, Huang Gongwang, Wu Zhen, Ni Zan
Ni Zan (1301–1374) — known for spare, sketchy monochrome landscapes. His inscribed poems turn landscapes into meditations on political exile and the joys of withdrawal from public life.

Yuan Drama: The Great Surprise

  • Drama had been low-status — orally transmitted, hereditary troupes
  • Educated writers blocked from government → began writing scripts for theater companies
  • About 160 Yuan plays survive, some readable as covert protest against Mongol rule
  • Greatest dramatist: Guan Hanqing (ca. 1240–1320) — author of 60 plays, 15 survive complete
Guan Hanqing's heroines are virtuous women who act forcefully in impossible situations — widely read as veiled commentary on living under unjust Mongol power.
🎓 Historians Weigh In
Wilt Idema and others argue Yuan drama represents one of the most significant literary developments in all of Chinese history — a high art form born directly from political exclusion.

Part VI

Putting It Together

What did Mongol rule mean for China in the long run?

The Long-Term Legacy

Lasting changes
  • Province as permanent political/admin unit
  • Elite reinvented through local Confucian institution-building
  • North-south economic gap widened
  • Blue-and-white porcelain, Yuan drama = enduring cultural legacies
Attitudes shaped
  • Reinforced preference for Chinese things
  • Deepened wariness about outside world
  • Population declined (records unreliable)
  • North China's economy left far behind the south
📖 Study Buddy Says
The Mongol period is often taught as pure trauma — but don't lose sight of what it created: some of China's most distinctive cultural achievements came directly from this period of crisis and exclusion.

Key Terms for Chapter 12

  • Chinggis Khan — founder of Mongol empire
  • Ögödei — son, Great Khan who finished Jin conquest
  • Yelü Qucai — Khitan adviser, argued for taxation over destruction
  • Khubilai Khan — grandson, conquered Song, founded Yuan
  • Yuan Dynasty — Mongol dynasty in China, 1271–1368
  • Dadu — Khubilai's capital (modern Beijing)
  • Four-tier hierarchy — Mongol → Semu → Han → Southerners
  • Wen Tianxiang — Song loyalist who chose death over surrender
  • Zhao Mengfu — Song imperial clansman who served Yuan
  • Ni Zan — Yuan painter; archaic landscapes as political statements
  • Guan Hanqing — greatest Yuan dramatist
  • Blue-and-white porcelain — Yuan innovation, Jingdezhen kilns

📝 Chapter 12 Review Quiz

Q1. What military tactic did the Mongols use repeatedly against the Jin Dynasty that proved so effective?
✅ Answer: The Mongols would take a city, plunder and destroy it, then withdraw — forcing the Jin to reclaim ruins and deal with food shortages and devastation without the Mongols having to hold the territory.
Q2. Who was Yelü Qucai, and what argument did he make to the Mongol leadership about governing north China?
✅ Answer: Yelü Qucai was a highly educated Khitan adviser recruited by Chinggis Khan. He argued that the Mongols should leave the Chinese farming population in place and tax them equitably, rather than converting north China to pastureland — calculating this would produce far greater revenue.
Q3. Why did Song China hold out against the Mongols longer than the Jin or Xia Dynasties? Name at least two reasons.
✅ Answer: Song had more time to prepare (the Mongols were busy elsewhere in the 1240s–1250s); the Yangzi River and canal networks made cavalry nearly useless; Song had a navy while the Mongols had to build one from scratch; Song knew from Jin and Xia's fate what it was facing.
Q4. What was the four-tier ethnic hierarchy established by the Mongols, and what was its purpose?
✅ Answer: The four tiers were: (1) Mongols, (2) Semu Ren (Central Asians, Persians, Uighurs), (3) Han (former Jin subjects), (4) Nanren/Southerners (former Song subjects). Its purpose was to preserve Mongol privilege and prevent the Chinese from using their numerical majority to challenge Mongol power.
Q5. What cultural innovation in Chinese ceramics emerged from the Yuan period, and what drove its development?
✅ Answer: Blue-and-white porcelain, using underglaze cobalt blue decoration, developed at the Jingdezhen kilns in Jiangxi. It was driven by demand from Arab clients in the Middle East who wanted durable, finely designed ceramics suited to their aesthetic preferences.
Q6. Why did Yuan drama flourish during a period of Mongol rule, and who is considered its greatest practitioner?
✅ Answer: Because the Mongols severely restricted access to government careers for educated Chinese, talented writers turned to writing scripts for theater companies as an alternative outlet. The greatest Yuan dramatist was Guan Hanqing (ca. 1240–1320), author of sixty plays, whose virtuous heroines are often read as veiled political commentary on unjust rule.
Q7. What did Zhao Mengfu represent in terms of how Chinese intellectuals responded to Mongol rule, and why was his choice controversial?
✅ Answer: Zhao Mengfu was a member of the Song imperial clan who chose to serve the Yuan government when Khubilai recruited southern scholars in 1286. He represents the "collaborator" response — working within the system to advance Chinese interests. It was controversial because many of his friends and family refused to speak to him, viewing service to the conquerors as a betrayal of loyalty to Song.
Q8. According to the textbook, how did the Mongol period change China's cultural and political attitudes in the long run?
✅ Answer: The destructive side of Mongol rule reinforced in China a preference for Chinese things (over foreign or outside influences) and a wariness about the outside world. It also widened the north-south economic gap, declined population, and gave elites stronger incentives to maintain status through local Confucian institutions rather than government service.

Yelü Qucai (1190–1244)

A highly educated Khitan statesman who served the Mongols for twenty-five years. Born into a family that had long served the Jin dynasty, he famously told Chinggis he could not consider Jin his enemy because his ancestors had served them with loyalty. Chinggis admired his candor and took him into his entourage. Yelü spent his career trying — with limited success — to persuade the Mongols that governing sedentary agricultural populations required fundamentally different methods than steppe raiding.

×
Khubilai Khan (1215–1294)

Grandson of Chinggis and founder of the Yuan Dynasty in China. Unlike many Mongol leaders, Khubilai recognized early that China could only be effectively exploited through Chinese administrative methods. He never became fully "Chinese" — he didn't learn to read Chinese, and he maintained many Mongol customs — but he was pragmatic enough to surround himself with Chinese advisers and adopt the trappings of a legitimate Chinese emperor. His reign (1260–1294) represented the peak of Mongol power and administrative competence in China.

×
Zhao Mengfu (1254–1322)

One of the most celebrated calligraphers and painters in Chinese history, and one of its most morally contested figures. As a member of the Song imperial clan who chose to serve the Yuan government, he occupied a uniquely awkward position — both enormously influential and perpetually under scrutiny for his "betrayal" of Song loyalty. His archaic painting style, drawn on pre-Tang models, expressed both a longing for the lost dynasty and a conscious claim that Chinese aesthetic values could survive and resist political conquest.

×
Guan Hanqing (ca. 1240–ca. 1320)

The most prolific and celebrated dramatist of the Yuan period. Of his sixty plays, fifteen survive complete. His leading characters are almost always women — courtesans, widows, mothers, daughters-in-law — who act with remarkable moral courage in situations of social injustice. Historians read these heroines as indirect commentary on the situation of educated Chinese under Mongol rule: virtuous people forced to navigate an unjust system with their integrity intact. His most famous play is Snow in Midsummer, about a woman wrongly executed whose ghost petitions heaven for justice.
