Why did Mao launch the Hundred Flowers Campaign, and what were the consequences of the Anti-Rightist Campaign? This question belongs in Hundred Flowers, the Great Leap, and the Soviet Split because it clarifies how campaigns, failed feedback, famine, hukou, and international rupture reshaped socialist China. The most productive way to approach it is through causal explanation rather than a single-event narrative. Students often recognize the principal names or events but miss the machinery connecting them. For instructor preparation, the task is therefore to reconstruct the background conditions, identify the actors' available choices, and distinguish intended policy from consequences that emerged only over time.

📌 Textbook Anchor

“By the end of the campaign, the Western-influenced elite created in the 1930s was destroyed, condemned as "poisonous weeds.” — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 480 (Review Question 6 anchor). This passage supplies the question’s diagnostic evidence.

Breaking It Down

The section should begin from the institutional inheritance established in the previous lecture. Historical actors did not confront a blank slate: they inherited administrative routines, social rankings, military organizations, religious languages, commercial networks, and memories of earlier success and failure. The question is not merely what happened, but why a particular response became plausible in this inherited setting. This prevents the lecture from turning into biography or chronology and keeps attention on the institutions that made individual decisions consequential. The course's verified answer synthesis provides the essential factual spine: Mao initially encouraged criticism of the government through the Hundred Flowers Campaign in hopes of improving governance and identifying problems. This formulation should be unpacked rather than simply repeated. It states: “By the end of the campaign, the Western-influenced elite created in the 1930s was destroyed, condemned as "poisonous weeds."” Read narrowly, the sentence supplies a concrete fact students must recognize.
