Non-Chinese Rule in the North

The Big Question

When "barbarians" rule China,
who gets to define what "Chinese" means?

After 316 CE, north China was ruled by non-Chinese dynasties for nearly 300 years.

Chinese elite who remained had to decide:

  • Can we serve these rulers?
  • Are they legitimate?
  • What does our identity mean now?

üéØ Frame

Most ethnically complex period in Chinese history. "Chinese" identity revealed as process, not fixed essence. Modern relevance but focus on historical understanding.

Connection to Lecture Arc

  • Lecture 1: Han collapsed (316), Three Kingdoms stalemate, Jin failure. Non-Chinese peoples controlled north.
  • Lecture 2 (Today): Nearly three centuries when various non-Chinese dynasties ruled northern China‚Äîtransforming both conquerors AND Chinese identity.
  • Lecture 3: How Buddhism‚Äîanother "foreign" import‚Äîconquered Chinese hearts/minds during same period.

üìö Arc Logic

L1=political collapse. L2=identity complexity. L3=religious transformation. Together show complete restructuring of Chinese civilization.

Who Were the "Barbarians"?

Not a single group‚Äîdiverse peoples with different histories.

The Five Barbarian Groups (Wu Hu)

  • Xiongnu ‚Äî Remember from Han Dynasty; many settled inside China by 300 CE
  • Xianbei ‚Äî Northern nomads; would become dominant (Northern Wei)
  • Jie ‚Äî Small group; brutal conflicts both ways
  • Di ‚Äî Northwestern peoples, some agricultural
  • Qiang ‚Äî Western peoples, pastoral and agricultural

Important: These weren't racial categories‚Äîthey were political and cultural identities that shifted over time.

×

Five Barbarians (Wu Hu ‰∫îËÉ°)

Chinese classificatory label, not stable ethnographic map. These identities were fluid‚Äîfamilies could shift between categories through political alliance, marriage, or relocation.

Key point: "Hu" often means "people from north/west" or "non-Han" in documents, but same person could be labeled differently depending on context (office, marriage, residence).

üéØ Key Points

Don't memorize all five. Focus: Xiongnu (continuity from Han), Xianbei (Northern Wei). Challenge assumption that conquest was simple "barbarians vs. civilization." These are administrative/political labels as much as ethnic ones.

The Xiongnu Legacy

  • Remember them from Han Dynasty (heqin marriage alliances)
  • After confederation collapsed, many groups settled inside China
  • By 300 CE: Xiongnu had lived in border regions for generations
  • Some spoke Chinese, knew Chinese classics, used Chinese names

Case Study:

Liu Yuan, who captured Luoyang in 311, claimed descent from the Han imperial family through a marriage treaty. His dynasty was called "Han"!

×

Liu Yuan and the Disaster of Yongjia (311)

Xiongnu leader who founded Han Zhao dynasty. His forces sacked Luoyang in 311‚Äîa trauma marker for Jin collapse.

Identity complexity: Liu Yuan claimed Han imperial descent, used Chinese dynastic name, but led Xiongnu confederation. Shows how blurred lines already were.

üí° Key Point

Liu Yuan exemplifies complexity: Xiongnu leader claiming Han legitimacy. Identity was already negotiable, not fixed. The 311 Luoyang sack (Disaster of Yongjia) is major trauma marker‚Äîlike Roman sack of 410.

The Sixteen Kingdoms Era

304‚Äì439 CE: Chaos Before Consolidation

  • Name "Sixteen Kingdoms" is traditional but misleading‚Äîthere were more
  • Dynasties rose and fell quickly; average reign very short
  • Violence endemic: cities sacked, populations massacred or enslaved
  • New rulers rapidly learned: Cannot rule North China without Chinese clerks, tax systems, and Mandate of Heaven ideology

Shi Le: Complexity in Action

His Story

  • Born Jie tribesman
  • Sold into slavery as young man
  • Rose to lead mounted bandits
  • Founded Later Zhao (319‚Äì351)
  • Could not read Chinese

His Strategy

  • Employed Chinese advisors
  • Used Chinese administration
  • Patronized Buddhism
  • Governed without reading Chinese himself

Pattern: Non-Chinese rulers needed Chinese administrative expertise.
Chinese officials needed non-Chinese patrons to survive.

×

Shi Le (274‚Äì333 CE)

Jie leader, former slave, founder of Later Zhao. One of most fascinating figures of era.

Significance: Shows you could govern North China without reading Chinese, BUT only if you employed Chinese administrators. Also converted to Buddhism (Fotudeng as advisor)‚Äîwe'll see why that mattered in Lecture 3.

Ethnic violence: His successor launched persecution of ethnic Chinese, which Chinese subjects carried out "with vengeance"‚Äîviolence flowed multiple directions based on who held power.

üéØ Teaching Point

Shi Le shows: (1) Conquest didn't destroy Chinese institutions‚Äînew rulers needed them. (2) Literacy question significant‚Äîgoverned without reading Chinese himself. (3) Mutual dependence key. (4) Ethnic violence went both ways‚Äîdon't simplify.

üí° Expected

Accept: pragmatic survival, preserve culture, influence rulers. Refuse: betrayal, ancestors disapprove, collaboration with invaders. Both positions have historical support. This was REAL dilemma‚Äîsome fled south, some served, some resisted. Refusal often meant death/poverty‚Äîraise stakes.

The Northern Wei: A Different Approach

Xianbei Unification (386‚Äì439)

  • Tuoba clan of Xianbei created most durable northern dynasty
  • Founded Northern Wei in 386 from base in northern Shanxi
  • Advantage: Access to steppe cavalry AND Chinese agricultural population
  • By 439: Unified all of north China; ruled until 534

Early Strategy: Separation Model

  • Capital at Pingcheng (northern Shanxi)‚Äîcloser to steppe
  • Xianbei warriors settled nearby as herders, not farmers
  • Chinese ran bureaucracy; Xianbei ran military
  • Why? Fear of being "overwhelmed" by more numerous Chinese
×

Northern Wei (386‚Äì534)

Tuoba/Xianbei dynasty that unified north (439) and experimented with centralizing reforms.

Early phase (Pingcheng): Hybrid state‚Äîsteppe military organization + Chinese bureaucratic practices. Military effectiveness depended on cavalry skills maintained through pastoral life. Identity preservation: remain Xianbei, not become Chinese.

üìö Northern Wei as Anchor

Most durable northern dynasty (386-534). Early strategy: keep Chinese and Xianbei separate. Why separation? Military effectiveness depends on cavalry skills; fear of being overwhelmed; identity preservation. This changes dramatically under Xiaowen...

Emperor Xiaowen's Revolution

471‚Äì499 CE: Everything Changed

Born to Chinese mother (Empress Dowager Feng). Wanted to unite Chinese and Xianbei elites. Decided separation was unsustainable.

The Radical Program (493‚Äì499):

  • 1. Moved capital south from Pingcheng to Luoyang (300 miles closer to Chinese heartland)
  • 2. Banned Xianbei language and clothes at court (officials under 30 must speak Chinese)
  • 3. Encouraged intermarriage between highest Xianbei and Chinese families
  • 4. Changed surnames to Chinese ones (imperial house: Tuoba ‚Üí Yuan)
  • 5. Adopted Chinese government structures (equal-field system, bureaucratic ranks)
×

Emperor Xiaowen (r. 471‚Äì499)

Most significant figure in early medieval Chinese history, yet often overlooked.

Context: 494 capital move to Luoyang (old Han/Jin capital). 150,000 Xianbei warriors moved south. Court-driven reforms associated with "sinicization."

Modern scholarship caution: Don't frame as "they became Chinese." Frame as state centralization + elite competition. Reforms strengthened bureaucratic center, reoriented court culture, BUT produced backlash among frontier military elites.

üéØ Xiaowen's Program

One of most significant figures in Chinese history. Program controversial AMONG Xianbei‚Äîmany resisted. Note Empress Dowager Feng's influence (women's political power). Modern framing: this is governing strategy, not simple cultural conversion. Equal-field system (juntian) = state attempt to stabilize taxation by tying people to land/registers.

üéØ Historiographical Debate

This is genuine scholarly debate, not settled. Position B reflects more recent scholarship (Scott Pearce 2023). Neither "wrong"‚Äîhelp students see complexity. Some may note "Chinese culture" itself already diverse.

The Hybrid Reality

Despite Xiaowen's program, result was more complex than "becoming Chinese"

Xianbei Culture Persisted

  • Language in private/family contexts
  • Military culture retained steppe elements
  • Hunting/martial skills remained elite markers
  • Buddhism appealed to both groups

Chinese Culture Transformed

  • Northern Chinese adopted steppe elements
  • Cavalry warfare became central
  • Sitting on chairs spread (Central Asian?)
  • Diet, clothing, music showed hybrid influences

This wasn't "Chinese win" or "Xianbei win"‚Äîit was mutual transformation.

üí° Challenge Simple Narratives

Despite Xiaowen's reforms, Xianbei culture didn't disappear. Chinese culture also transformed‚Äînorthern Chinese adopted steppe elements. Challenge: "Chinese absorbed barbarians" narrative. Reality: both changed. This hybrid culture will reunify China (Sui/Tang).

Gao Huan: The Perfect Hybrid

  • Han Chinese by ancestry, but raised among Xianbei
  • Married into Xianbei family
  • Could appeal to both ethnic communities
  • Became powerful warlord in Eastern Wei sphere

His speeches to each group:

To Xianbei: "The Han are your slaves... they till and weave for you."
To Chinese: "The Xianbei are your retainers... they fight bandits so you are safe."

Both statements partially true. Both communities needed each other.

×

Gao Huan (496‚Äì547)

Han Chinese by ancestry but raised among Xianbei. Perfect example of hybrid identity in northern society.

Political genius: His speech to each group shows remarkable manipulation of ethnic tension. He tells each side they're superior, but frames superiority differently (military vs. economic). Shows he understood mutual dependence.

Significance: Represents new generation of elites who could operate in both worlds‚Äîneither purely Xianbei nor purely Chinese, but something new.

üéØ Gao Huan's Significance

Perfect hybrid‚Äîneither purely Chinese nor Xianbei. His speeches show political manipulation of ethnic tension but also reveal truth: mutual dependence. He represents new generation of elites who will reunify China. His Eastern Wei faction leads to Northern Qi.

The Divisional Militia (Fubing)

  • Created in northwest where Xianbei and Chinese most mixed
  • Multiethnic military system
  • Soldiers registered separately from regular taxpayers‚Äîhonorable status
  • Would help reunify China under Sui/Tang

Why This Matters:

The fubing system emerged from hybrid northwest society. It combined steppe cavalry traditions with Chinese administrative practices. This military system would become foundation for Sui/Tang reunification.

üìö South Briefly

Eastern Jin = refugee government at Nanjing (Jiankang). Southern dynasties: Song, Qi, Liang, Chen (constant military coups). Elite culture: genealogies, pedigree, "pure" Chinese culture claimed. Irony: "purity" claim despite military weakness. Military problem: generals repeatedly seized power; military households near-slaves.

Who Won the Identity War?

By 580 CE, north China had been ruled by non-Chinese dynasties for over 250 years. But something remarkable happened:

The lines between "Chinese" and "non-Chinese" had blurred beyond recognition.

  • The Xianbei who conquered were themselves transformed
  • The Chinese who remained incorporated steppe influences
  • New hybrid elites emerged who could operate in both worlds
  • Both north AND south would be reunified by dynasty from hybrid northwest: the Sui (581)

Who counts as Chinese? The Period of Division showed Chinese identity was never simply about blood or birth. It was about culture, language, values‚Äîand these could be adopted, adapted, and transformed.

üéØ Synthesis

By 580: lines blurred beyond recognition. Xianbei transformed, Chinese transformed, hybrid elites emerged. Answer to opening question: Chinese identity is process, not fixed essence. Conquest dynasties didn't destroy civilization‚Äîthey became part of it. Sui/Tang preview: these centuries mattered for what came next.
