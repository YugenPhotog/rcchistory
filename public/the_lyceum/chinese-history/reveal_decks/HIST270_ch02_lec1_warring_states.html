Spring and Autumn Period (770‚Äì479 BCE)

Named after the chronicle Spring and Autumn Annals covering Lu state (722‚Äì479 BCE), traditionally attributed to Confucius.

During this era, Zhou kings retained symbolic authority while powerful states took turns acting as "hegemons" (ba) who called meetings, enforced agreements, and maintained interstate order.

Key source: The Zuo zhuan (Zuo Commentary) provides detailed narrative of this period.

Chariot Warfare

During the Spring and Autumn period, states were ranked by how many hundreds of chariots they could deploy. A chariot typically carried three warriors: driver, archer, and halberdier.

Chariots were expensive - requiring horses, bronze fittings, and trained crews. This kept warfare an elite affair. Battles often began with champions challenging each other, and ceremonial rules governed conduct.

The Zuo zhuan records instances where commanders refused to attack an enemy crossing a river or forming ranks - such "honorable" conduct disappeared in the Warring States.

×

Conscription

Universal male military obligation - all adult male peasants required to serve in the army.

This was revolutionary: previously warfare was elite privilege. Now rulers needed to mobilize entire societies. This required:

  • Population registers to track men
  • Supply systems to feed armies
  • Training programs for peasant soldiers
  • Administrative systems to coordinate

The result: bureaucratic states that could project massive military force.

üìö Why This Transformation?

Two key technological changes enabled mass infantry:

  • Crossbow - A peasant with a crossbow could kill an aristocratic warrior. Democratized killing.
  • Iron weapons - Cheaper than bronze, could be mass-produced. Every soldier could be armed.

üéØ Social Implications

This is NOT just military history - it's social transformation:

  • Aristocracy loses monopoly on violence
  • Common people become militarily significant
  • States need bureaucracies to manage conscription
  • Entire societies mobilized for war

üí° Modern Comparison

Compare to WWI "total war" - similar mobilization of whole societies. Or the transition from medieval knights to standing armies in early modern Europe.

The Crossbow Revolution

The crossbow's bronze trigger mechanism was a sophisticated engineering achievement. Unlike composite bows requiring years of training, crossbows could be taught in days.

One period text claimed: "A skilled soldier with a powerful crossbow and a sharp sword was the match for a hundred ordinary men."

States like Wei equipped elite troops with crossbows that could fire multiple bolts, shoulder armor, helmets, and three days' provisions - they could march 50 kilometers in a day.

üìö Technical Details

The crossbow's bronze trigger mechanism had interchangeable parts - early standardization. Archaeological finds show mass production of identical components.

üéØ Key Insight

Technology doesn't just change HOW wars are fought - it changes WHO can fight and what social structures are needed to support warfare. Crossbow + iron = mass armies = bureaucratic states.

üí° Connection to Later History

Preview: When we get to Qin Dynasty, we'll see the Terra Cotta Army - life-sized soldiers with actual bronze weapons, showing the scale of military production.

üß† What is a ‚Äúmulti-firing crossbow‚Äù?

When people say ‚Äúmulti-firing crossbow,‚Äù they usually mean a repeating crossbow, often called the Zhuge nu (Ë´∏ËëõÂº©).

This design typically has a top-mounted magazine holding multiple bolts and a lever mechanism. When you pump the lever back and forth, it:

  • Cocks the bow
  • Drops a bolt into place
  • Releases the shot

So one lever cycle = one shot, repeated quickly until the magazine runs out.

‚è≥ Key historical point (important for Warring States)

The repeating crossbow is best documented in later periods (especially the Han dynasty and after) and is popularly associated‚Äîoften more in legend than firm proof‚Äîwith Zhuge Liang in the Three Kingdoms era. That means it is not the main technology behind the early Warring States ‚Äúcrossbow revolution.‚Äù

‚öñÔ∏è Fast, but not powerful

Repeating crossbows trade power for speed. They tend to have a shorter draw and lower penetration, making them most effective at:

  • Short range
  • Defensive volleys (e.g., from walls or fortifications)
  • Harassment rather than armor-piercing kills

By contrast, the crossbow that matters most for the Warring States period is the powerful, mechanically triggered military crossbow (often with a bronze trigger mechanism). It fired more slowly, but could deliver serious penetrating force and was suited to disciplined mass armies.

‚úÖ Safe phrasing for your slide

Bottom line: repeating (‚Äúmulti-firing‚Äù) crossbows are a later development; the Warring States impact comes mainly from high-power crossbows used by mass infantry.

Warring States crossbows had a reputation for serious stopping power, and that reputation makes sense: the key innovation was the bronze trigger mechanism, which let soldiers draw and lock a very high-tension bow without having to hold the full force with their fingers. That mechanical lock meant higher draw weights and more stored energy, so bolts hit hard. Archaeology also shows bolts designed for penetration‚Äîoften short, thick shafts with narrow metal heads that concentrate force on a small point (better for punching through protection than wide hunting heads). In this period, most ‚Äúarmor‚Äù was not solid metal plate; it was typically leather, layered hide/fabric, or lacquered lamellar (small plates laced together). Against these common defenses, a high-powered crossbow at typical battlefield ranges could be lethal, and even when metal components existed (usually for elites), protection varied and had seams and gaps that a bolt could exploit. So ‚Äúarmor-piercing‚Äù here means the ability to defeat the most common battlefield protection of the time‚Äînot some fantasy image of punching through later medieval plate armor. Historically, the big consequence was social and political: a trained infantryman with a crossbow could kill a well-equipped aristocrat at range, helping shift warfare toward mass infantry armies and toward bureaucratic states that could organize training, production, and supply at scale.

üìö Battle of Changping

This battle between Qin and Zhao in 260 BCE was one of the largest in ancient Chinese history. After a prolonged siege, Zhao's army (perhaps 400,000+ men) surrendered. According to Sima Qian's Records, Qin general Bai Qi ordered all prisoners executed to prevent future resistance.

‚ö†Ô∏è Historical Caution

The 400,000 figure may be exaggerated - ancient sources often inflated numbers. But even if half that, it represents unprecedented scale. Archaeological evidence confirms mass graves in the region.

üéØ Key Point

This isn't "gentlemanly" warfare anymore. It's existential struggle. States that lose major battles disappear entirely. This desperation explains why rulers were willing to try radical new philosophies and policies.

Life in an Age of Fear

  • States could disappear within a generation
  • Families uprooted ‚Äî refugees and displacement common
  • Peasants conscripted for years at a time
  • Old Zhou ritual norms collapsing
  • Trust between states gone ‚Üí diplomacy = manipulation

Why this matters: Philosophers aren‚Äôt debating in comfort ‚Äî they are trying to solve how to survive social collapse.

üìö Emotional & Cultural Context

Up to now students have seen structural change ‚Äî war, states, armies. This slide shifts to lived experience. For ordinary people, this period meant fear, uncertainty, and instability. A village might belong to one state this decade and another the next. Nobles could lose everything in a single campaign. Families were separated by conscription and migration.

The older Zhou world ran on shared ritual expectations ‚Äî how rulers behaved, how war was conducted, how alliances were honored. In the Warring States period those norms break down. Survival replaces honor. Deception becomes normal. Treaties are temporary tools, not moral commitments.

üéØ Why This Drives Philosophy

This emotional climate explains why thinkers obsess over order, trust, hierarchy, and moral behavior. Their ideas are not abstract speculation ‚Äî they are attempts to rebuild a world where people can predict how others will act.

The Birth of the Bureaucratic State

  • Household registration to track population and labor
  • Tax records to fund armies and state projects
  • Land surveys to measure, allocate, and control territory
  • Legal codes written and publicly enforced
  • Standardized punishments applied across regions

Key idea: States stop being family networks and start becoming administrative machines.

üìö What Changed

Earlier Zhou politics depended heavily on kinship ties and personal loyalty. In the Warring States period, rulers needed systems that worked even when personal trust did not. Mass armies required reliable food supplies, equipment production, and troop coordination. That meant governments had to count, measure, record, and enforce at a scale never seen before in China.

‚öôÔ∏è Why This Is a World-Historical Revolution

This shift marks one of the earliest large-scale examples of a state governed through impersonal administration rather than aristocratic lineage. Power increasingly flowed through records, laws, and offices instead of family prestige. That model ‚Äî rule through bureaucratic institutions ‚Äî becomes a defining feature of later Chinese empires and influences state formation across East Asia for two millennia.

In world history terms, this is comparable to the rise of Roman imperial administration or early modern European fiscal-military states. The Warring States period shows that sustained warfare can push governments to become highly organized, information-driven systems.

üéØ Connection to Philosophy

This is the environment where Legalist thinkers make the most sense. If a state is now a machine, then laws, regulations, incentives, and punishments become the tools for running it. Moral persuasion alone cannot manage millions of people ‚Äî systems and enforcement can.

üîÆ Looking Ahead

When we reach the Qin dynasty, students will see these bureaucratic tools used to unify China under centralized rule. The administrative revolution of the Warring States is the foundation that makes empire possible.

Iron Agricultural Tools

Iron plows and tools spread during the Warring States period, dramatically increasing agricultural productivity.

Consequences:

  • More land could be cultivated
  • Food surplus supported larger populations
  • Peasants could be taxed more heavily
  • States had resources for larger armies

The same iron technology that made weapons also made tools - agricultural and military revolutions were connected.

Result: A dynamic, chaotic, opportunistic society where everything is up for grabs.

üéØ Key Point

The old Zhou order is completely gone. Everything - social status, political authority, economic opportunity - is being renegotiated. This explains the URGENCY of philosophical questions.

üí° Connection

Â£´ Shi (shir) - The Scholar-Official Class

Originally Â£´ meant "knight" or "warrior" - the lowest rank of the hereditary aristocracy. During the Warring States, the term shifted to mean educated men who served as advisors, ministers, and officials.

Their expertise included:

  • Military strategy
  • Administration and taxation
  • Diplomacy and rhetoric
  • Philosophy and persuasion

Famous shi could move between states, seeking rulers who would adopt their ideas. The philosophers we study (Confucius, Mozi, Mencius, etc.) were all shi seeking patronage.

üìö Social Revolution

This is one of the most important social changes in Chinese history. The shift from hereditary aristocracy to merit-based service creates the model that persists for 2000+ years.

üéØ Key Parallel

üìö Overview

This slide gives students the ‚Äúmap of the argument.‚Äù The Hundred Schools aren‚Äôt just offering wise sayings‚Äîthey‚Äôre arguing over a handful of core questions that determine what kind of state you build and what kind of people you try to produce.

üß† Key Points

  • Human nature (xing ÊÄß): If people are naturally good, government should nurture virtue. If people are selfish or unreliable, government needs stronger controls. If people are moldable, then education, rewards, and institutions matter most.
  • Virtue/ritual (li Á¶Æ) vs. law/punishment (fa Ê≥ï): This is a clash between ‚Äúshape behavior through culture and moral example‚Äù versus ‚Äúshape behavior through rules, incentives, and consequences.‚Äù Most thinkers mix the two, but they disagree on which should lead.
  • War & diplomacy: Some argue war must be morally limited and politically justified; others treat war as an unavoidable technology of survival where results matter more than purity.
  • Hierarchy: Is social rank part of a stable order, or does it create resentment and injustice that destabilize the state?
  • Language & reality (names/rectification): If titles don‚Äôt match behavior‚Äîif ‚Äúminister‚Äù acts like a warlord, or ‚Äúlaw‚Äù is arbitrary‚Äîtrust collapses. For some schools, bad categories aren‚Äôt just academic: they cause real political breakdown.

One way to read the whole period is: everyone agrees the world is falling apart, but they disagree about why‚Äîand once you choose a ‚Äúwhy,‚Äù you automatically choose a different solution.

Major Schools (and their ‚Äúone weird trick‚Äù)

  • Confucians (Ru): order through ritual + moral cultivation (better people ‚Üí better politics)
  • Mohists (Mo): order through impartial care, anti-war stances, and merit-based advancement ‚Äî plus very practical tech and organization
  • Daoists (Dao): order by not forcing things ‚Äî skeptical of rigid social engineering
  • Legalists (Fa): order through clear laws, incentives, and punishments (treat humans as predictable)
  • Military strategists: war as a system ‚Äî deception, logistics, terrain, morale
  • ‚ÄúSchool of Names‚Äù (Mingjia): language and logic puzzles with political stakes (if names don‚Äôt fit realities, governance collapses)

üìö Overview

This slide introduces the main intellectual ‚Äúteams‚Äù students will keep encountering. Each offers a different core mechanism for producing order in a chaotic world.

üß† Key Points

  • Confucians: Focus on shaping character through ritual, education, and moral example. Good rulers cultivate virtue in themselves and set the tone for society.
  • Mohists: Argue that partiality‚Äîfavoring family, clan, or state over others‚Äîcauses conflict. They promote impartial concern, oppose aggressive warfare, and value practical skills and organization.
  • Daoists: Warn that over-planning and over-controlling make things worse. The best order may come from simplifying, reducing interference, and allowing natural processes to unfold.
  • Legalists: Assume people respond reliably to rewards and punishments. A strong state uses clear laws, strict enforcement, and administrative techniques rather than moral preaching.
  • Military thinkers: Treat warfare as a technical field. Victory depends on information, adaptability, morale, terrain, and strategy more than brute force.
  • School of Names: Explore how language shapes reality. If social roles and titles don‚Äôt match actual behavior, political and moral order break down.
  • Yin‚ÄìYang / Five Phases: See politics as part of larger cosmic rhythms. Rulers gain legitimacy by aligning with perceived natural and cosmic patterns.

üéØ Tutor-Style Framing

Think of each school as offering a different ‚Äúlever‚Äù for controlling chaos: character, care, non-interference, law, strategy, language, or cosmic alignment.
