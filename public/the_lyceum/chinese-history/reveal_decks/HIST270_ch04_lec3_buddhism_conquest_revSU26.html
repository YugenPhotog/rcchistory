Despite initial conflicts with traditional Chinese values like the ancestral lineage, why did Buddhism successfully gain widespread popularity during the Period of Division? This question belongs in Buddhism's Conquest because it clarifies how an imported religion transformed salvation, gender, state power, art, and Daoism. The most productive way to approach it is through causal explanation rather than a single-event narrative. Students often recognize the principal names or events but miss the machinery connecting them. For instructor preparation, the task is therefore to reconstruct the background conditions, identify the actors' available choices, and distinguish intended policy from consequences that emerged only over time.

📌 Textbook Anchor

“In a rough and tumultuous age, Buddhism offered an appealing emphasis on kindness, charity, the preservation of life, and the prospect of salvation.” — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 70 (Review Question 7 anchor). This passage supplies the question’s diagnostic evidence.

Breaking It Down

The section should begin from the institutional inheritance established in the previous lecture. Historical actors did not confront a blank slate: they inherited administrative routines, social rankings, military organizations, religious languages, commercial networks, and memories of earlier success and failure. The question is not merely what happened, but why a particular response became plausible in this inherited setting. This prevents the lecture from turning into biography or chronology and keeps attention on the institutions that made individual decisions consequential. The course's verified answer synthesis provides the essential factual spine: Buddhism spread rapidly because it addressed the anxieties created by centuries of warfare and instability. It offered moral teachings, salvation, charitable activities, and hope for a better future. This formulation should be unpacked rather than simply repeated. Each clause names a mechanism - a way resources were mobilized, authority was justified, social groups were incorporated or excluded, or costs were displaced onto other people. Those mechanisms explain why the development could reshape the state or society beyond the immediate episode. The textbook anchor sharpens the evidence. It states: “In a rough and tumultuous age, Buddhism offered an appealing emphasis on kindness, charity, the preservation of life, and the prospect of salvation.” Read narrowly, the sentence supplies a concrete fact students must recognize. Read analytically, it reveals what the historian has chosen as diagnostic evidence. The instructor should ask what larger structure becomes visible through this detail: who controlled land or labor, who could speak for the state, whose status changed, what resource moved, or what boundary was being enforced. The quotation is therefore both quiz alignment and a doorway into interpretation.

Why This Matters

The causal Q7 answer: Buddhism spread rapidly because it addressed the anxieties created by centuries of warfare and instability. It offered moral teachings, salvation, charitable activities, and hope for a better future.
