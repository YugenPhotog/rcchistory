📖 Roadmap Notes

The four questions of Lecture 2 converge on a single formulation that the textbook states outright and that is one of the most important interpretive keys to all of Chinese history: "The relative success of the Han form of government validated the imperial system, which drew from both Confucian rhetoric and Legalist bureaucratic methods." This is the synthesis. From Q3, the Han kept the Qin (Legalist) administrative machinery while shedding its brutality. From Q6, the Han clothed that machinery in Confucian ideology — selecting officials by Confucian learning, justifying rule in Confucian terms. The result was a hybrid that neither pure Legalism nor pure Confucianism could have produced: the bureaucratic efficiency of the Legalist state operating under the moral legitimacy of the Confucian tradition. The textbook puts it elegantly: the Zhou idea of All-Under-Heaven ruled by the Son of Heaven, supported by Confucian thinkers, "now had attached to it the structures of the centralized bureaucratic empire, indebted though these were to Legalist ideas."

📖 Understanding This Slide

Lecture 1 ended with the paradox of Qin: a system so dependent on the First Emperor's personal authority that it could not survive his death. Lecture 2 opens in the wreckage. When the First Emperor died in 210 BCE, the textbook observes with grim economy: "He had trusted no one, and at this juncture no one proved trustworthy." The chief eunuch and a younger son conspired to forge orders commanding the legitimate heir and General Meng Tian to commit suicide. The younger son took the throne as the Second Emperor, executed several brothers, and was soon manipulated into executing Li Si himself — the architect of the Qin state destroyed by the state he built.

📌 Textbook Anchor

“In theory, the Han emperor was all-powerful, but in actuality depended on his chancellor and other high officials for information and advice... Officials, graded by rank and salary, were appointed by the central government for their merit, not their birth, and were subject to dismissal, demotion, or transfer, much in the way Qin officials had been.” — Ebrey, A History of China, p. 43 (Q3 anchor passage). This passage supplies the question’s diagnostic evidence.

Breaking It Down

The textbook names the structural lesson precisely: "The Legalist institutions designed to concentrate power in the hands of the ruler made the stability of the government dependent on the strength and character of a single person." When that person was gone and his successor was weak, the entire apparatus collapsed. Rebellion erupted across the empire, and from the chaos emerged Liu Bang — a man of commoner background who would found the Han Dynasty and take the imperial title Emperor Gao. Liu Bang's commoner origins matter for Q3. Unlike the Qin First Emperor, who came from the royal house of a Warring State, the Han founder rose from nothing. According to the IRM, legend has it he first rebelled against the Qin because he feared punishment under its strict laws — a founder shaped by the very harshness he would later soften. The contrast that Q3 asks students to draw begins with the founders themselves: a Legalist prince versus a commoner rebel. The central insight of Q3 is that the Han did not reject the Qin system — they inherited it and adjusted it. The IRM states the anchor directly: "Emperor Gao did not disband the centralized government." This is the crucial fact. The bureaucratic apparatus the First Emperor built — the appointed officials, the commandery-and-county administrative divisions, the standardized script and measurements, the merit-based selection of officials — all of it survived the dynasty's fall and was taken over wholesale by the Han.

Why This Matters

The causal Q3 answer: The Han founding was an act of selective inheritance. Emperor Gao kept the Qin administrative machine — the appointed, accountable, merit-graded bureaucracy and the centralized command structure — because it worked. He discarded the Qin's brutal penal codes, crushing labor demands, and predatory taxation because they had provoked the rebellion that destroyed the Qin. The one genuine structural innovation, the mixed kingdom-commandery system, proved dangerous and was gradually reversed. The Han formula was thus "Qin efficiency without Qin cruelty" — and finding a stable basis for that formula is the story of the rest of the chapter.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The Xiongnu were the nomadic pastoral confederation to the north — the defining foreign-policy problem of the entire Han Dynasty, and the model for how every subsequent Chinese dynasty would think about its northern frontier. The textbook tells the chilling story of how the Xiongnu chief Maodun seized power: he trained his bodyguards to shoot whatever he shot, testing them by ordering them to kill his favorite horse, then his favorite concubine, executing any who hesitated. When he was satisfied of their absolute obedience, he ordered them to shoot his own father, and declared himself chief. Under Maodun, the Xiongnu defeated the Eastern Hu and the Yuezhi, united the tribes of Mongolia, and — as the Qin Dynasty collapsed — reclaimed the Ordos region.

📌 Textbook Anchor

“The Xiongnu considered this tribute, but the Han naturally preferred to consider it an expression of friendship.” — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 43 (Q4 anchor passage). This passage supplies the question’s diagnostic evidence.

Breaking It Down

The military problem the Xiongnu posed was structural, and it is central to Q4. As mounted nomads, they could not be fought the way Chinese armies fought walled cities. The textbook is explicit: "fighting nomads was not like attacking walled cities. If the Xiongnu did not want to fight the Chinese troops, they simply decamped." A nomadic enemy has no capital to capture, no territory that must be defended, no fixed point of vulnerability. This asymmetry shaped Han policy for four centuries. Q4 asks how diplomacy, tribute, and military action interacted. The Han tried both major approaches, and the chapter lets students see why neither fully worked. The early Han, after Emperor Gao suffered a humiliating military defeat, turned to appeasement — the policy later known as heqin ("peace and kinship"). The textbook describes it: the Han supplied the Xiongnu "not only with material goods but also with princesses as brides (which they hoped in time would lead to rulers with Chinese mothers)." The Han sent silk, grain, and other goods. The diplomatic fiction was delicate: "The Xiongnu considered this tribute, but the Han naturally preferred to consider it an expression of friendship." The same transaction was face-saving generosity to one side and extracted tribute to the other.

Why This Matters

The causal Q4 answer: The Han never solved the Xiongnu problem; they managed it through an oscillation between appeasement and war, neither of which fully worked. Heqin appeasement — tribute goods and marriage alliances — bought peace but funded the enemy and never stopped the raids. Emperor Wu's massive military campaigns were costly and of limited value against a mobile nomadic foe with no fixed target. What military force could achieve was incremental: consolidating vacated land through forts, officials, and settlers. The Xiongnu confrontation established the enduring template for the Chinese dynastic relationship with the northern steppe — a permanent, unsolvable frontier managed by a shifting mix of payment, force, and colonization.

Visual evidence for Q5

📖 Understanding This Slide

Q5 connects directly to Q4: Emperor Wu's aggressive Xiongnu campaigns were enormously expensive, and paying for them drove a fundamental transformation in the state's relationship to the economy. The textbook lists his revenue measures: "To pay for his military campaigns, Emperor Wu took over the minting of coins, confiscated the land of nobles, sold offices and titles, and increased taxes on private businesses." Each of these was significant, but the most consequential was the assault on private commerce.

📌 Textbook Anchor

“In 119 BCE, government monopolies were established on the production of iron, salt, and liquor, enterprises that previously had been sources of great profit for private entrepreneurs. Large-scale grain dealing also had been a profitable business, which the government now took over under the guise of stabilizing prices.” — Ebrey, A History of China, p. 43 (Q5 anchor passage). This passage supplies the question’s diagnostic evidence.

Breaking It Down

The heart of Q5 — "how did his administration alter the relationship between the centralized state and private enterprise?" — lies in Emperor Wu's monopolies. A widespread suspicion of commerce, on both moral and political grounds, made merchants an easy target for heavy taxation. Boats, carts, and shops were subjected to property taxes. But the decisive blow, in the textbook's words, was "the government's decision to enter into market competition with them by selling the commodities that had been collected as taxes." In 119 BCE, the government established monopolies on the production of iron, salt, and liquor — three of the most profitable private industries in the empire. The state simply took over enterprises that had made private entrepreneurs rich. It also moved into large-scale grain dealing under the banner of price stabilization: the government would buy grain where it was plentiful and cheap, store it or transport it to areas of scarcity, and sell it — eliminating private speculation while generating state revenue. This is the policy later formalized as the "ever-normal granary" system. The significance for Q5 is structural, not just fiscal. Emperor Wu transformed the state from a tax-collector that stood outside the economy into a direct economic actor that competed with — and displaced — private enterprise in the most lucrative sectors. This was a fundamental reordering: the centralized state now claimed not just a share of private wealth through taxation but ownership of entire industries. The tension between state economic control and private commerce that this created would be debated for the rest of Chinese imperial history. (The famous "Discourses on Salt and Iron," a court debate on whether to keep the monopolies, took place a generation later and became a classic text on the question.)

Why This Matters

The causal Q5 answer: Emperor Wu's wars against the Xiongnu drove an economic revolution. To fund them, he transformed the state from a collector of taxes into a direct competitor with — and owner of — private enterprise. The iron, salt, and liquor monopolies of 119 BCE and the state takeover of grain dealing fundamentally altered the relationship between the centralized state and private commerce: the state no longer merely taxed wealth, it appropriated whole industries. This established a permanent question in Chinese statecraft — how far should the state intervene in the economy? — that would be debated for two millennia. Q5 and Q4 are linked: the cost of empire on the frontier reshaped the economy at home.

Visual evidence for Q6

📖 Understanding This Slide

Q6 addresses the single most consequential of Emperor Wu's innovations: making Confucianism the official ideology of the Chinese state. The textbook frames it precisely: "Emperor Wu was the first Han emperor to privilege Confucian scholars within the government." The key adviser was the Confucian scholar Dong Zhongshu, whose advice to Emperor Wu the textbook explicitly compares to Li Si's advice to the First Emperor — a deliberate and telling parallel.

📌 Textbook Anchor

“Because one of the highest duties of the Confucian scholar was to admonish the ruler against misguided policies, officials whose educations imbued them with Confucian values did not comply automatically with the emperor's wishes.” — Ebrey, A History of China, p. 44 (Q6 anchor passage). This passage supplies the question’s diagnostic evidence.

Breaking It Down

Where Li Si had urged the suppression of all schools but Legalism (and the burning of books), Dong Zhongshu urged the suppression of all schools but Confucianism. The textbook quotes him: "Because the various schools of thought differ, the people do not know what to honor," and he advised that "anything not encompassed by the Six Disciplines and the arts of Confucius be suppressed and not allowed to continue further, and evil and vain theories be stamped out." The structural logic is identical to Li Si's — intellectual uniformity as a tool of state control — but the content is inverted. Confucianism now occupied the position Legalism had held under the Qin. Emperor Wu acted on the advice. He decreed that officials should be selected on the basis of Confucian virtues and established a national university to train officials in the Confucian classics. This is the institutional birth of the system that would define imperial China for two thousand years. The textbook names the significance directly: "The Han government's decision to recruit men trained in the Confucian classics marks the beginning of the Confucian scholar-official system, one of the most distinctive features of imperial China." But the passage's real sophistication lies in identifying the productive tension built into the system.

Why This Matters

The causal Q6 answer: Emperor Wu transformed Confucianism from one school among many into the official ideology of the state and the basis for selecting its officials. Dong Zhongshu's advice mirrored Li Si's — suppress rival schools, impose intellectual uniformity — but installed Confucianism where Legalism had stood. The resulting scholar-official system was imperial China's defining institution, and its genius lay in a built-in tension: Confucian officials served the emperor efficiently because of their ingrained sense of duty, yet that same duty obligated them to admonish him when he erred. The system gave the state loyal, self-disciplined administrators who were also, in principle, the emperor's conscience — a tension Emperor Wu himself could not abide, but which shaped Chinese governance for two millennia.
