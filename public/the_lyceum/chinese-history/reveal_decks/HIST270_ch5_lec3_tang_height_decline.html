Section I

Empress Wu and Female Power

How did one woman become emperor in a Confucian world that assumed only men could rule?

The Big Question

Only one woman in Chinese history declared herself emperor of her own dynasty.

How did Empress Wu rise from concubine to sovereign ruler?

And why did Tang cultural achievements peak precisely when centralized political control was fragmenting?

Empress Wu's Rise to Power

  • Entered court as a concubine to Emperor Taizong; survived by attaching herself to his heir, Gaozong
  • Convinced Gaozong to demote the empress and elevate her to that position
  • Dark legend: smothered her own infant daughter to frame the empress ‚Äî may be later propaganda
  • 665: When Gaozong suffered a stroke, Wu made state decisions "from behind the screen"

Emperor Wu of Zhou: 690‚Äì705

  • 683: Gaozong dies; Wu controls court through her sons
  • 684‚Äì690: Deposes two sons in succession for defying her authority
  • 690: Declares herself emperor of the new Zhou dynasty ‚Äî at age 65

She declared herself huangdi (emperor) ‚Äî not empress consort. She founded her own dynasty. This was unprecedented and never repeated.

Buddhism and Female Power

  • Confucianism assumed cosmic order required male rulers ‚Äî Buddhism offered alternatives
  • The Great Cloud Sutra prophesied a female universal monarch; Wu promoted it empire-wide
  • Buddhist monks became loyal allies; patronage created institutional support
  • Expanded the examination system, recruiting new men outside the old aristocracy

Section III

The An Lushan Rebellion

How did Tang's greatest strength ‚Äî its cosmopolitan, multi-ethnic military ‚Äî become its greatest danger?

The Road to Rebellion

  • Xuanzong's later reign dominated by favorite concubine Yang Guifei ‚Äî her relatives seized enormous court power
  • Frontier provinces commanded by professional, non-Chinese armies loyal to their generals, not the court
  • General An Lushan commanded over 160,000 troops across three northeastern commands
  • Bitter rivalry between An Lushan and Yang Guifei's cousin sparked open conflict

The An Lushan Rebellion: 755‚Äì763

  • 755: An Lushan rebels with his frontier army; marches south
  • 756: Luoyang falls; veterans advance on Chang'an; Xuanzong flees west
  • 756: Troops mutiny at Mawei Postal Station ‚Äî demand execution of Yang Guifei; heir apparent declares himself emperor
  • 757: An Lushan murdered by his own son; Tang calls on Uighurs to retake Chang'an ‚Äî Uighurs loot the city
  • 763: Rebellion finally suppressed after eight years
Estimated death toll: tens of millions. The most destructive rebellion in Chinese history to that point.

Yang Guifei and the Politics of Blame

  • As Xuanzong fled west, his guards mutinied and demanded the execution of Yang Guifei and her relatives
  • The emperor had no choice ‚Äî Yang Guifei was strangled at a postal station; he watched
  • Bai Juyi's "Song of Everlasting Sorrow" immortalized their tragic love as literary legend
  • Yet the poem also blamed the woman for the emperor's failures ‚Äî a persistent Confucian pattern
Note the pattern: Yang Guifei commanded no armies and made no policy ‚Äî yet she became the scapegoat for a systemic military and political failure

Section IV

Transformation, Not Simply Decline

What does it mean for an empire to "decline" ‚Äî and what counts as evidence?

After the Rebellion: A Different Tang

What Collapsed
  • Equal-field system and fubing militia
  • Centralized court control over provinces
  • Tang's ability to project military power
  • Emperor's authority over frontier generals
What Flourished
  • Poetry ‚Äî Du Fu's greatest work, Bai Juyi's entire career
  • Trade and commercial networks; salt monopoly revenue
  • Regional diversity and local cultural expression
  • Buddhist culture and Confucian intellectual revival
Regional military governors (jiedushi) held real power; the court survived by letting them govern autonomously

Late Tang Culture

  • Bai Juyi (772‚Äì846) ‚Äî accessible poetry with massive popular output; his Song of Everlasting Sorrow became a literary touchstone
  • Tang tales ‚Äî a new genre of classical fiction featuring love stories and supernatural themes
  • 845: Emperor Wuzong's Buddhist persecution ‚Äî monasteries destroyed, monks secularized ‚Äî but Buddhism survived and recovered
  • Han Yu (768‚Äì824) launched a Confucian revival, challenging Buddhist dominance and laying the groundwork for Song Neo-Confucianism
Tang cultural achievements peaked after political centralization collapsed ‚Äî culture and political power do not always move together

Tang's End: 907

  • Regional military governors effectively independent ‚Äî the court was a formality
  • Eunuchs controlled the inner court, killing and enthroning emperors at will
  • 875‚Äì884: Huang Chao Rebellion devastated the south and what remained of central authority
  • Last Tang emperor forced to abdicate to a warlord ‚Äî dynasty formally ended
  • Followed by the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period (907‚Äì960)
Tang's cultural legacy endured: poetry, legal codes, examination system, and the cosmopolitan ideal continued to define East Asia

Emperor Xuanzong (r. 712‚Äì756)

The seventh and longest-reigning emperor of the Tang dynasty. His reign is divided into two periods: an energetic early phase of administrative reform and consolidation, and a later phase characterized by political withdrawal, factionalism, and infatuation with his concubine Yang Guifei. An Lushan (703‚Äì757)

A half-Sogdian, half-Turk professional soldier who rose through Tang's multi-ethnic frontier military to command three northeastern frontier commands with over 160,000 troops ‚Äî the single largest military force in the empire. Born on the frontier, he spoke multiple languages and understood both Chinese court culture and steppe military organization. Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms (907‚Äì960)

The period of political fragmentation following the fall of the Tang dynasty. In the north, five successive short-lived dynasties controlled the old Tang heartland (Later Liang, Later Tang, Later Jin, Later Han, Later Zhou), while in the south and southwest, ten independent kingdoms emerged. The period ended when the Song dynasty (960‚Äì1279) reunified most of China. Despite the political chaos, the Five Dynasties period saw significant economic and cultural continuity ‚Äî and in southern China, considerable prosperity. The period is notable for the first large-scale use of printed books and the beginnings of the commercial revolution that would transform Song China.
