üìö Lecture Overview

This lecture examines daily life in Han China, the challenge of the Xiongnu nomads, the opening of the Silk Road, and the eventual decline and fall of the Han Dynasty.

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Describe the social structure of Han China and daily life for different classes
  • Analyze Han relations with the Xiongnu and the evolution of frontier policy
  • Explain the opening of the Silk Road and its significance for trade and cultural exchange
  • Assess the factors that led to the Han Dynasty's decline and fall

üí° Connection to Previous Lectures

Lectures 1-2 covered political history: Qin unification, Han founding, and governance. This lecture shifts to social history, foreign relations, and the dynasty's end‚Äîcompleting our picture of the Qin-Han period.

The World of Han China

The Han Dynasty governed roughly 59 million people across a vast territory‚Äîthe earliest indication of China's large population.

Today's Questions

  • What was life like for ordinary people in Han China?
  • How did the Han deal with the powerful Xiongnu confederation?
  • What were the consequences of Han expansion into Central Asia?
  • Why did the Han Dynasty eventually fall?

üìö Setting the Scene

From the textbook: "During the Western Han period, with the establishment of peace and the extension of the empire's frontiers, the Chinese population grew rapidly. The census of 2 C.E. recorded a population of 59 million, the earliest indication of the large size of China's population."

üéØ Key Context

This population figure is remarkable for the ancient world‚Äîthe Roman Empire at its height had perhaps 50-60 million people across a comparable timeframe. China's population would remain among the world's largest throughout history.

The Confucian ideal: Scholars ‚Üí Farmers ‚Üí Artisans ‚Üí Merchants

(Reality was more complicated...)

üìö Social Structure

The Confucian ideal placed scholar-officials at the top (after the emperor), followed by farmers who produced food, then artisans who made useful things, and finally merchants who merely moved goods around for profit.

üéØ Key Point

In practice, this hierarchy was often inverted‚Äîwealthy merchants could buy education for their children and rise into the scholar class, while farmers often struggled in poverty. The ideology and the reality didn't always match.

Common Farmers: The Vast Majority

Daily Life

  • Most Chinese were farmers living in villages
  • Typical family: parents, children, sometimes grandparents
  • Millet in the north, rice in the south
  • Supplemented with vegetables, pigs, chickens
  • Life was hard but generally better than under Qin

Obligations to the State

  • Land tax: 1/30 of harvest (very low!)
  • Poll tax: per person
  • Corv√©e labor: work on government projects
  • Military service: when called

Unpaid labor that subjects owed to the state:

  • Building and maintaining roads, walls, canals
  • Transporting goods for the government
  • Working on imperial construction projects
  • Typically one month per year for adult males

This was the most burdensome obligation for farmers. Under Qin, corv√©e demands had been crushing; the Han reduced them significantly.

üìö Farmer Life

From the textbook: "The bulk of the population in Han times (and even into the twentieth century) was made up of farmers living in villages."

üéØ Key Points

  • The land tax was remarkably low‚Äî1/30 of the harvest compared to much higher rates elsewhere
  • The Han learned from Qin's mistake of over-taxing and over-working the population
  • But farmers were still vulnerable to bad harvests, debt, and land loss

The Problem of Land Concentration

  • Over time, wealthy families accumulated large estates
  • Poor farmers sometimes had to sell their land
  • Became tenants (paying rent) or laborers
  • Some became slaves (through debt, crime, or capture)

A recurring pattern: Government periodically tried to limit landholding‚Äîbut powerful families resisted and reforms rarely succeeded.

Remember Wang Mang's failed land reform from Lecture 2?

Slavery in Han China

Slavery existed but was different from chattel slavery in other societies:

  • Sources: Debt bondage, criminal punishment, war captives, sale by parents
  • Scale: Probably a small percentage of the population
  • Treatment: Varied widely‚Äîsome were domestic servants, others worked in harsh conditions
  • Legal status: Could sometimes be freed or buy their freedom

Wang Mang tried to outlaw slavery‚Äîone of his reforms that failed.

üìö The Land Problem

Land concentration was a persistent problem throughout Chinese history. As wealthy families accumulated land, more farmers lost their independence. This created both economic inequality and political instability.

üéØ Key Point

This pattern‚Äîland concentration leading to peasant distress leading to rebellion‚Äîwould repeat throughout Chinese history. The Yellow Turban Rebellion that ended the Han was partly driven by this dynamic.

Merchants & Artisans

  • Confucian ideology looked down on profit-seeking
  • But successful merchants could become very wealthy
  • Government monopolies on iron and salt
  • Silk production‚Äîmostly women's work

The Scholar-Gentry

  • Families who could afford Confucian education
  • Goal: government office
  • Example: The Ban Family
  • Would dominate Chinese society for 2,000 years

  • Led the confederation in war and peace
  • Received tribute from subordinate tribes and (for a time) from China

üìö The Nomadic Way of Life

From the textbook on the Xiongnu and related peoples: "They took to riding horses before the Chinese did, and by the seventh century B.C.E., many of these groups were making the move to nomadic pastoralism. Families lived in felt tents that could be taken down and moved north in summer and south in winter in search of fresh pasture."

üéØ Key Points

  • The Xiongnu represented a fundamentally different way of life than agricultural China
  • Their mobility was both a military advantage and made them impossible to conquer permanently
  • The conflict between settled farmers and nomadic herders is a recurring theme in world history

The Xiongnu Military Advantage

Revolutionary military technology: Mounted archery‚Äîthe ability to shoot arrows while riding horseback at full gallop.

Xiongnu Advantages

  • Speed and mobility
  • Every man a warrior from childhood
  • No cities to defend (or capture)
  • Could retreat into the steppe
  • Knew the terrain

Han Advantages

  • Larger population
  • Greater resources
  • Infantry and siege weapons
  • Fortifications (walls)
  • Administrative capacity

From the textbook: "Especially awesome from the Chinese perspective was the ability of nomad horsemen to shoot arrows while riding horseback."

üìö Military Technology

Mounted archery required years of training from childhood. Xiongnu children learned to ride and shoot as naturally as Chinese children learned to farm. This gave the nomads a military edge that was extremely difficult to counter.

üéØ Strategic Problem

The fundamental problem: Han armies could win battles but couldn't win the war. They could drive Xiongnu out of a region, but the Xiongnu would simply retreat and return later. You can't conquer people who have no cities to capture.

Early Han Policy: The Heqin System

Background: The Disaster at Baideng (200 BCE)

  • Emperor Gaozu (Liu Bang) led army against Xiongnu
  • Surrounded by Xiongnu cavalry for seven days
  • Barely escaped‚Äîhumiliating defeat

The Heqin ("Peace and Kinship") Policy

  • Sent Chinese princesses to marry the chanyu
  • Provided annual "gifts" (essentially tribute)
  • Silk, grain, wine, and other luxury goods
  • Established border markets for trade
×

The Siege of Baideng (ÁôΩÁôª‰πãÂúç, 200 BCE)

A turning point in Han-Xiongnu relations:

  • Liu Bang personally led 320,000 troops against the Xiongnu
  • The chanyu Maodun pretended to retreat, drawing Liu Bang forward
  • Then surrounded him with 400,000 cavalry on a hill called Baideng
  • Trapped for seven days with no relief
  • Escaped only through diplomatic trickery (bribing Maodun's wife)

This disaster convinced the Han that they couldn't defeat the Xiongnu militarily‚Äîleading to the heqin policy.

A diplomatic arrangement that was essentially paying tribute to avoid war:

  • Chinese view: Generous gifts to barbarians, Chinese princess as bride
  • Xiongnu view: Tribute from a subordinate state
  • Both sides could interpret it in ways that preserved their dignity
  • Practically effective‚Äîreduced raiding for decades
  • But deeply humiliating to Han pride

Emperor Wu would eventually reverse this policy and pursue military confrontation.

üìö The Heqin System

From the textbook: "Even before Emperor Gao had completed the consolidation of the empire, he came to realize the threat posed by the Xiongnu. In 200 B.C.E. the Xiongnu under Maodun attacked one of the recently appointed kings. Gao agreed to make yearly gifts of silk, grain, and even a Chinese princess as a bride to surround the Han army."

üéØ Key Points

  • The heqin policy was practical but humiliating
  • It represented China paying tribute to "barbarians"‚Äîreversing the expected relationship
  • The policy bought peace but did not solve the underlying problem

Emperor Wu's Aggressive Turn

  • Emperor Wu (r. 141‚Äì87 BCE) reversed the heqin policy
  • Launched major military campaigns against the Xiongnu
  • Key generals: Wei Qing and Huo Qubing
  • Led cavalry armies deep into the steppes

Results

  • Drove Xiongnu out of Ordos region (loop of Yellow River)
  • Pushed them north of the Gobi Desert
  • Extended Han control into Central Asia

But: Campaigns were enormously expensive. The Han never fully defeated the Xiongnu‚Äîthe conflict would continue for centuries.

üìö Emperor Wu's Campaigns

Emperor Wu abandoned the humiliating heqin policy and pursued military victory. His campaigns were initially successful‚Äîpushing the Xiongnu far from the Chinese heartland.

üéØ Key Points

  • The campaigns were expensive‚Äîdraining the treasury built up by early Han frugality
  • Victory was never final‚Äîthe Xiongnu would return when Han military pressure relaxed
  • The cost of empire would contribute to later Han financial problems

üí° Long-term Pattern

Eventually (1st century BCE) the Xiongnu confederation split. Some groups surrendered and were settled inside China‚Äîa pattern that would have major consequences in later centuries.

The Decline of the Han

Problems of the Later Han (Eastern Han)

  • Land concentration: Wealthy families accumulated estates; peasants lost land
  • Eunuch vs. scholar-official factions: Bitter struggles for influence at court
  • Weak emperors: Often young, manipulated by eunuchs or consort families
  • Tax base eroded: Powerful families evaded taxes; burden fell on remaining farmers
"During the Eastern Han period, eunuchs were able to build a base of power within the palace, with the result that weak emperors became their captives rather than their masters."
‚Äî Ebrey, Cambridge Illustrated History of China

üìö Structural Problems

The problems that brought down the Han were structural‚Äîbuilt into the system itself. Land concentration created desperate peasants. Court factions undermined governance. Weak emperors couldn't control either.

üéØ Key Pattern

These problems would recur in later dynasties: the cycle of land concentration ‚Üí peasant distress ‚Üí rebellion is a persistent theme in Chinese history.
