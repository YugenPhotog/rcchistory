Political Fragmentation & the Warring States World

HIST 270 — Chapter 2, Lecture 1

Review Questions: Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4

📖 Lecture Overview

This lecture establishes the historical context that produced the Hundred Schools of Thought — the subject of Lectures 2 and 3. The core argument: the Zhou quasi-feudal system's collapse didn't just produce political chaos. It produced a new kind of world — more dangerous, more mobile, more economically complex — that created both the demand for new ideas and the class of people (the shi) capable of supplying them.

Review Questions Covered

Q1 — How did the Eastern Zhou political structure differ from Western Zhou, and what was the role of the hegemon?
Q2 — How did warfare transform from Spring and Autumn to Warring States?
Q3 — What economic and demographic transformations occurred during the Eastern Zhou?
Q4 — What was the shi class and how did they influence statecraft?

The key framing for the whole lecture: you can't understand the Hundred Schools without understanding the world that produced them. Part I

The Collapse of Zhou Authority

From feudal order to multistate competition — Q1

771 BCE — The Collapse

  • Western Zhou king killed by coalition of Rong tribes and Zhou vassals
  • Royal court flees east to Luoyang — Eastern Zhou begins
  • The nobles who helped the court escape were rewarded with more autonomy
  • The Zhou king becomes a figurehead — nominally supreme, actually powerless
The Zhou king retains ritual prestige but loses political power. No one can conquer him — but no one obeys him either.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the hinge moment connecting Chapter 1 to Chapter 2. The Zhou quasi-feudal system's structural weakness — hereditary delegation produces local powers that eventually rival the center — produced the exact outcome Chapter 1 predicted. The collapse of 771 doesn't mean "everything changed immediately." The Zhou king continued to exist for another 500 years. But his authority was hollowed out from the start.

Key distinction: Ritual prestige vs. political power. The Zhou king still performed the ceremonies that legitimized the cosmos — the sacrifices to Heaven that made the crops grow and the seasons turn. Rulers still wanted his ritual blessing. But they stopped obeying his commands. This split between symbolic authority and real power is one of the defining features of the Eastern Zhou period.

The Multistate System Emerges

Two Sub-Periods

  • Spring & Autumn (770–479 BCE) — many competing states; chivalric warfare; hegemons emerge
  • Warring States (479–221 BCE) — seven major powers; total war; survival of the fittest

How Many States?

  • Spring & Autumn: ~170 states at peak
  • By 500 BCE: competition reduces to about 20 significant states
  • By 300 BCE: 7 major powers remain
  • By 221 BCE: 1 — Qin conquers all

📖 Understanding This Slide

The two-period division (Spring & Autumn / Warring States) comes from the texts of those periods — the Spring and Autumn Annals (a historical chronicle of the state of Lu) and the Intrigues of the Warring States (organized by state rather than chronologically). Historians have used these text names as period labels ever since.

The numbers are striking: from ~170 states to 1 in about 550 years. That's a process of continuous violent elimination. Every state that survives does so by developing better military, administrative, and economic capacity than its neighbors. The "Warring States" name is literal — this is a period defined by warfare as the normal condition of political life.

The Hegemon — Q1 Answer

What the Hegemon Was

  • The leader of the most powerful state, recognized by other lords as first among equals
  • Convened leagues of states; organized collective defense against threats
  • Claimed to act on behalf of Zhou civilization — not as a new king
  • Position was earned by power, not inherited by birth

How It Differed from Zhou King

  • Zhou king: authority from ritual lineage and Heaven's mandate
  • Hegemon: authority from military strength and statecraft
  • Zhou king: permanent, hereditary position
  • Hegemon: temporary, competitive — could be displaced by a stronger rival

The hegemon system acknowledges that real power and ritual authority have separated.

📖 Q1 Core Answer

This slide delivers the direct answer to Q1. The Eastern Zhou period differed from Western Zhou in that the Zhou king retained only symbolic authority while real power was distributed across competing states. The hegemon system was the practical mechanism for organizing inter-state relations when the king could no longer enforce order.

The first hegemon was Duke Huan of Qi (685–643 BCE), whose chancellor Guan Zhong (the subject of the textbook's Biography feature) built up Qi's administrative and military capacity. The IRM notes that Confucius praised Guan Zhong for his statecraft despite his controversial loyalty record. The hegemon system was an acknowledgment of political reality: Zhou ritual prestige still had value, but actual governance required demonstrated power.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Mark Edward Lewis's Sanctioned Violence in Early China argues that the hegemon system was a transitional mechanism — it preserved the fiction of Zhou authority while acknowledging that real governance had become competitive. When even the hegemon fiction collapsed in the Warring States period, what remained was naked military competition.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

The Zhou king kept his ritual role even as his political power vanished. The hegemon held real power but needed the king's symbolic blessing.

When symbolic authority and real power are separated — which one actually governs?

Instructor Notes

This is a genuinely open question. The obvious answer is "real power governs." But the Zhou example complicates this — the ritual authority of the Zhou king was valuable enough that even the most powerful warlords wanted it. Symbols have power when enough people believe in them. The interesting follow-up: what happens when they stop believing? That's the transition from Spring & Autumn to Warring States — when even the ritual fiction became too thin to maintain.

Part II

The Warfare Revolution

From chivalric ritual to total war — Q2

Two Ways of War

Spring & Autumn Style

  • Chariots — aristocratic warfare, small armies
  • ~10,000 soldiers = large army
  • Agreed time and place — codes of honorable conduct
  • Neither side attacks if the other's ruler has been killed
  • Victory measured by prestige, not annihilation

Warring States Style

  • Mass infantry displaces chariots — armies of 200,000+
  • Crossbow, armor, cavalry from northern steppe peoples
  • Deception, maneuver, siege warfare — no rules
  • Cities get walls; armies develop siege tactics
  • Victory means elimination of rival states

📖 Q2 Core Content

Why did chariot warfare give way to infantry? Two reasons: terrain and cost. Chariots require flat ground — most of China isn't flat. And chariots require aristocratic drivers — trained from birth, expensive to maintain. Mass conscript infantry armies just needed bodies, which states could produce through census-taking and conscription. The crossbow was the great equalizer — a peasant with a crossbow could kill an aristocratic charioteer with months of training. Armor was developed in response to the crossbow.

The scale shift is staggering: 10,000 to 200,000+ in the space of a few centuries. This required completely different administrative infrastructure — feeding, supplying, and coordinating 200,000 men for a campaign requires census records, granaries, supply chains, and professional officers. The Warring States period essentially forced states to develop early bureaucracies just to survive militarily.

Cavalry: Borrowed from the northern steppe peoples (the same Rong and Di groups that had always been on the Zhou periphery). King Wuling of Zhao (325–299 BCE) is famous for adopting "barbarian dress" — trousers and riding gear — to enable cavalry use. The textbook's Documents feature on this episode is relevant here.

What Total War Required

People

  • States register populations to conscript soldiers
  • First systematic family name registration in world history
  • Bring new land under cultivation to feed armies

Revenue

  • Direct taxation of farmers replaces tribute from lords
  • Decline of serfdom — farmers need legal status to be taxed directly
  • Iron technology expands agricultural output

Administration

  • Hereditary lords replaced by appointed officials
  • Early bureaucratic structures emerge
  • Merit over birth begins to matter for state service

Military competition forced states to modernize their economies and administrations — or die.

📖 Q2 + Q3 Bridge

This slide bridges Q2 (warfare transformation) and Q3 (economic/demographic change). The key insight is that military competition was the engine of social and economic modernization. States didn't reform their economies out of enlightened self-interest — they reformed because survival required it.

Surname registration: This is genuinely significant. For most of Chinese history before this, surnames were aristocratic markers. The Warring States period produced the first systematic registration of commoner family names — because you can't conscript people you can't track. China preceded Europe in this practice by over a millennium.

Decline of serfdom: When states begin taxing farmers directly rather than through lords, farmers need a legal identity independent of their lord. The collapse of the manorial economy is driven partly by military fiscal demands. Economic freedom as a side effect of military competition is an irony worth flagging.

Part III

Economy, Society, and the Shi

The transformations that created the Hundred Schools — Q3 & Q4

Economic & Demographic Change (Q3)

Iron Changes Everything

  • Iron casting technology spreads — largest smelters employ hundreds of workers
  • Iron tools make more land cultivable
  • Iron weapons democratize warfare — bronze was expensive, iron is cheap
  • New class of wealthy merchants and industrialists emerges

Administrative Revolution

  • States survey land and tax farmers — serfdom declines
  • Population census — commoners get registered surnames
  • Cities proliferate across north China
  • Long-distance trade creates new economic networks

By 300 BCE, China looks more like a market economy than a manorial one.

📖 Q3 Full Answer

Q3 asks about three specific drivers: agriculture, administration, and metallurgy. This slide covers all three. The key conceptual move: these changes are interrelated, not independent. Iron → more agricultural output → more surplus → more trade → more wealthy merchants → more urbanization → more demand for literate administrators → more demand for the shi. The economic transformation and the intellectual flowering are causally connected.

Serfdom decline: The textbook says "direct taxation of farmers" contributed to the decline of serfdom. The mechanism: when the state taxes farmers directly rather than through lords, farmers need a legal identity. They become taxpayers rather than attached laborers. This is not liberation by ideology — it's liberation as an administrative convenience.

The Shi 士 Class (Q4)

Who Were They?

  • Lower aristocracy — too high-born to farm, too low-ranked to hold hereditary office
  • As hereditary rank meant less, talent and learning opened new paths
  • Traveled from state to state offering advice to rulers
  • Competed for government posts through demonstrated expertise

Why They Mattered

  • States needed skilled administrators, strategists, diplomats
  • Shi developed competing philosophies — each a "how to govern" package
  • Rulers who hired good shi advisors gained competitive advantage
  • Their competition for patronage drove intellectual innovation

Confucius, Mozi, Mencius, Han Feizi — all were shi.

📖 Q4 Core Answer

The shi are the social class that made the Hundred Schools possible. They occupied a structural position that created both the need and the opportunity for intellectual innovation: too educated to do manual labor, lacking the hereditary connections to hold aristocratic office, dependent on demonstrating intellectual value to secure patronage.

The market for ideas: Rulers competing for survival needed the best available advice on how to administer, tax, conscript, and govern. This created a genuine market for political philosophy. Philosophers who could demonstrate that their approach produced stronger states got hired; those who couldn't didn't. This market pressure is part of why the Hundred Schools were so practically oriented — they were competing for state employment, not writing for posterity.

Confucius as example: He spent most of his adult life traveling from state to state seeking a ruler willing to implement his ideas. He died believing he had failed. The irony is that his ideas became the dominant ideology of Chinese civilization — but not until centuries after his death, when the Qin's failure proved that pure Legalism was unstable.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Mark Edward Lewis's Writing and Authority in Early China argues that the shi's dependence on writing — on texts that could travel, be copied, and outlast their authors — is what gave their ideas such durability. Unlike oral teachings, written texts could propagate independently of their originators. The philosophy market created a written culture; the written culture preserved the philosophy.

The Hundred Schools — Why Now?

One question, many answers: After Zhou authority collapses, what can replace it?

Same Crisis

  • Political fragmentation
  • Constant warfare
  • Erosion of old hierarchies
  • New economic complexity

Different Diagnoses

  • Confucians: We lost moral order — restore it through virtue and ritual
  • Mohists: We lost impartiality — replace family loyalty with universal care
  • Daoists: We over-engineered — stop trying to fix what can't be forced
  • Legalists: We lack discipline — govern through clear laws and incentives

📖 Bridge to Lectures 2 & 3

This slide is the bridge. Everything in Lectures 2 and 3 follows from the crisis established in this lecture. Each school of thought is a different diagnosis of the same problem — and each diagnosis implies a different prescription. Students who understand the crisis are much better prepared to evaluate the competing solutions.

The "Hundred Schools" is a poetic term — there weren't literally 100 schools, and most of the figures we know about traveled similar circuits and argued with each other constantly. The label captures the diversity and intensity of the intellectual culture, not a specific count.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

The Warring States period was an age of constant warfare, political instability, and social upheaval. It was also the most intellectually creative period in Chinese history.

Is there a connection between crisis and intellectual creativity — or is it a coincidence?

Instructor Notes

This parallels the "Axial Age" question that Ebrey raises in the chapter introduction: why did China, Greece, India, and Israel all produce major intellectual and spiritual innovations in roughly the same centuries? The connection between political fragmentation, intellectual competition, and philosophical creativity is a genuine historical puzzle. Students might note similar patterns in Renaissance Italy (fragmented city-states, intense artistic and intellectual competition) or Weimar Germany (crisis producing both great art and dangerous politics).

Putting It Together

Q1: Eastern Zhou = multistate competition; the hegemon coordinated without replacing the Zhou king's ritual role — real power and symbolic authority separated.

Q2: Warfare transformed from aristocratic chariot ritual to mass infantry total war — requiring conscription, siege tactics, crossbows, and cavalry.

Q3: Military competition drove iron technology, direct taxation, population registration, and the decline of serfdom — modernizing the economy as a side effect of survival.

Q4: The shi — displaced lower aristocrats — filled the demand for skilled state administrators, competing for patronage by developing competing philosophies.

📖 Summary Slide

If students can reproduce these four answers in their own words, they've mastered Lecture 1. The answers build on each other: Q1 establishes the political context → Q2 shows what that context demanded militarily → Q3 shows the economic consequences → Q4 shows the social class that emerged to provide intellectual solutions.

Shi 士 — The Lower Aristocracy

The lower ranks of the old Zhou aristocratic order. As hereditary rank gave way to demonstrated talent, many shi competed for government posts by developing sophisticated philosophies and offering advice to rulers. They traveled from state to state — effectively as policy consultants. Confucius, Mozi, Mencius, Xunzi, and Han Feizi were all shi. Hundred Schools of Thought (諸子百家)

The poetic term for the intellectual flowering of the Warring States period (ca. 475–221 BCE), when competing philosophers traveled from state to state offering different solutions to the same crisis: how to restore order after the collapse of Zhou authority. The major schools included Confucians, Mohists, Daoists, Legalists, and military strategists. "Hundred" is figurative — the label captures diversity and competition, not a specific count.
