📖 Lecture Overview

The last lecture's thinkers all shared one assumption: society should be deliberately improved. This lecture begins with two radical departures and then widens the frame. The Daoists reject striving itself — better to align with the natural order than to fix it. The Legalists keep the goal of order but throw out virtue entirely, trusting only law, reward, and punishment. Then we step outside the schools to see the wider intellectual world: the rise of yin-yang cosmology alongside enduring spirit beliefs, and the distinctive culture of Chu, the great southern state that was both Zhou and not-Zhou. Four review questions that complete the Hundred Schools and the chapter.

Review Questions Covered

Q8 — How did Daoists respond to Confucian striving, and what was their alternative vision?
Q9 — What were the core tenets of Legalism, and why did Legalists reject Confucian virtue?
Q10 — How did yin-yang thinking interact with traditional spirit beliefs in the late Zhou?
Q11 — What was the cultural relationship between the central Zhou states and the southern state of Chu?

📖 Roadmap Notes

The first half sets up the era's two most radical political philosophies as mirror opposites: Daoism says the cure for disorder is to stop trying to impose order (wuwei), while Legalism says the cure is to impose order absolutely through impersonal law. Between them and the Confucians, students now have the full spectrum of answers to "how do you create order out of chaos." The second half deliberately steps outside the schools — yin-yang cosmology (Q10) shows how the Chinese explained nature, and Chu (Q11) shows that the "Chinese" world had a rich, distinct southern frontier. The chapter closes not with one winning philosophy but with a whole intellectual world in motion.

Part I

The Daoist Alternative

Against striving, toward the Dao — Q8

The Cure Is the Disease

  • Laozi and Zhuangzi reject Confucian and Mohist striving
  • Their claim: trying to make things better generally makes them worse
  • Rules, moral campaigns, and clever schemes create the very problems they aim to fix
While everyone else debates how to fix society, the Daoists ask whether fixing is the problem.

The Daodejing: wisdom in paradox and restraint

📖 Understanding This Slide

After two lectures of thinkers urgently proposing fixes for a broken world, the Daoists make a startling move: they question the urge to fix things at all. The texts associated with Laozi (the Daodejing) and Zhuangzi are the foundation of Daoist thought, and they turn the era's central project on its head.

📌 Textbook Anchor

"The authors of the Laozi and Zhuangzi thought striving to make things better generally makes them worse." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 30. This is the Q8 anchor.

Breaking It Down

The critique of striving: Confucians want to cultivate virtue and revive ritual; Mohists want to maximize utility; rulers want to engineer powerful states. The Daoists see all of this anxious effort as the problem, not the solution. Human attempts to impose order through laws, moral instruction, and clever contrivance tend to backfire — they multiply rules people evade, create distinctions that breed envy, and disturb a natural harmony that was working fine on its own.

The paradox: The more you legislate against theft, the more you sharpen the idea of property worth stealing. The more you preach virtue, the more hypocrisy you breed. Daoist texts delight in these reversals because they expose the hidden costs of all the earnest fixing going on around them.

Two voices: The Laozi often speaks to rulers, advising a paradoxical politics of restraint. Zhuangzi is more playful and personal, using parables and humor to loosen our grip on rigid categories and certainties. Both point the same direction: away from forcing, toward yielding.

Why This Matters

This establishes the Daoist diagnosis. The next slide gives their positive alternative — what to do instead of striving — centered on the Dao and wuwei.

Wuwei: The Power of Not-Forcing

  • Align with the Dao — the natural way underlying all things
  • Practice wuwei: effortless action, not-forcing, going with the grain
  • Prize simplicity and spontaneity over ambition and contrivance
Like water, which yields yet wears down stone — the soft overcomes the hard.

Harmony with the natural order, not mastery over it

📖 Understanding This Slide

If striving makes things worse, what's the alternative? Not passive resignation, but a different kind of action — aligned, effortless, and attuned to how things naturally flow. This is the positive heart of Daoism.

Breaking It Down

The Dao: "The Way" — the natural order or fundamental pattern underlying the cosmos. It can't be fully named or defined (the Daodejing opens by saying the Dao that can be spoken isn't the eternal Dao), but it can be aligned with. Living well means harmonizing with the Dao rather than imposing your will against it.

Wuwei: Literally "non-action," but better understood as effortless action or not-forcing. It doesn't mean doing nothing; it means acting without strain, coercion, or ego — like a skilled swimmer working with the current instead of against it, or water that flows around obstacles yet carves canyons. The ideal ruler practices wuwei by governing lightly, leaving people alone to flourish naturally rather than micromanaging them with laws and campaigns.

Simplicity and spontaneity: Daoists prize the uncarved block, the natural and unpretentious, over sophistication and ambition. They distrust the cleverness and status-seeking that drive the competitive world of Lecture 1. The goal is to recover a spontaneous attunement to nature that all the era's striving has obscured.

The contrast that defines the chapter: Set the three answers side by side. Confucians fix society through moral cultivation; Legalists (next) fix it through law; Daoists say stop trying to fix it and align with nature. The same crisis, three radically different cures.

Why This Matters

The causal Q8 answer: Daoist thinkers criticized political and moral striving as self-defeating and offered an alternative of harmony with the Dao through simplicity, spontaneity, and wuwei (non-forcing) — urging people to align with the natural order rather than actively engineer a better society.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

A.C. Graham – Disputers of the Tao

Graham read early Daoism as a profound response to the failure of the era's rational projects — a recognition that the analytic, calculating mind has limits, and that some goods come only by letting go. He stressed that wuwei is a sophisticated skill, not mere passivity: the mastery that looks effortless precisely because it no longer fights the world.

The Daoists answered an age obsessed with fixing society by saying: stop trying so hard.

Part II

The Legalist Solution

Order through law, not virtue — Q9

Rule by Law, Not by Character

  • Han Feizi: strong government depends on rigorous laws, not good rulers
  • The tools: clear laws + automatic rewards and punishments + centralized power
  • People are self-interested — so harness that with incentives, don't rely on virtue
Build a state that works even when ruled by a mediocre king — by trusting the system, not the man.

Written law and records: the machinery of the Legalist state

📖 Understanding This Slide

Legalism is the hard-nosed answer to the era's crisis, and it's the one that actually conquered China. Synthesized by Han Feizi (a student of Xunzi — remember Xunzi's view that people are born selfish), Legalism keeps the Confucian goal of order but discards the Confucian means of virtue.

📌 Textbook Anchor

"These theorists, labeled Legalists because of their emphasis on the need for rigorous laws, argued that strong government depended not on the moral qualities of the ruler... but on the institutions of the state." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 31. This is the Q9 anchor.

Breaking It Down

The core insight: The Legalists noticed a fatal weakness in Confucianism. If good government depends on a virtuous ruler, what happens when you get a bad one — and how often do sages actually sit on thrones? Their solution: design a state that produces order regardless of the ruler's character, by relying on impersonal institutions rather than personal virtue.

The three tools: Clear, public, uniformly applied laws so everyone knows the rules. A rigid system of rewards and punishments — generous rewards for desired behavior (farming, fighting), severe punishments for everything else — applied automatically and without exception, even to the high-born. And centralized authority concentrating power in the ruler and his administrative machinery, stripping it from the old aristocracy.

Human nature as raw material: Legalists took people to be fundamentally self-interested — seeking reward, avoiding pain. Rather than lamenting this (or trying to reform it, as Xunzi hoped), they exploited it: align the incentive structure so that pursuing self-interest automatically serves the state. The farmer-soldier who wants rank and land will grow grain and fight hard if those are the rewards.

Why This Matters

This slide lays out the Legalist system. The next slide draws the explicit, pointed contrast with Confucian virtue that Q9 asks for — and notes the historical stakes.

Why Virtue Wasn't Enough

The Legalist Critique

  • Virtuous rulers are rare; you can't build a state on hoping for sages
  • Moral example is too slow and weak for a violent, competitive age
  • Law applies to everyone equally; virtue is unevenly distributed

The Result: Qin

  • The state of Qin adopted Legalist methods wholesale
  • Became a ruthless, efficient war machine
  • In 221 BCE, Qin conquered all rivals and unified China
Legalism won the Warring States — but its harshness would help doom the empire it built (Chapter 3).

📖 Understanding This Slide

Q9 specifically asks why Legalists thought Confucian virtue was insufficient for governing — so let's make that argument explicit, then follow it to its world-changing consequence.

Breaking It Down

The case against virtue: The Legalist objection to Confucianism has three prongs. First, scarcity: truly virtuous rulers appear maybe once in generations, so a system requiring them will fail most of the time. Second, weakness: moral suasion is too slow and unreliable to control a large population or win a war against ruthless neighbors — in a knife fight, leading by example gets you killed. Third, partiality: virtue and ritual traditionally treated the noble and the common differently, while Legalist law could be applied uniformly to all, making it a more powerful instrument of central control. For the Legalists, betting your state's survival on the ruler's character was a luxury the Warring States could not afford.

Qin and unification: This wasn't just theory. The western state of Qin embraced Legalist reforms (associated earlier with Shang Yang and later informed by Han Feizi's synthesis): it registered and organized the population, rewarded farming and military success, punished severely, and centralized power. The result was the most formidable state of the age. In 221 BCE Qin conquered the last of its rivals and created the first unified Chinese empire — the subject of Chapter 3.

The twist to remember: Legalism's very ruthlessness, so effective for conquest, made the Qin empire brutal and brittle. It fell within a generation, and later dynasties blended Confucian virtue with Legalist institutions. Hold that tension — it's where the next chapter begins.

Why This Matters

The causal Q9 answer: Legalists like Han Feizi argued that effective government depends on clear laws, automatic rewards and punishments, and centralized institutions rather than the ruler's moral virtue — because virtuous rulers are rare and moral example too weak for a competitive age — and the state of Qin used these methods to conquer and unify China in 221 BCE.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Yuri Pines – The Everlasting Empire

Pines argues that the Legalist achievement was institutional rather than ideological: it built the administrative machinery of centralized empire that every later dynasty inherited, whatever moral language they wrapped it in. Confucianism became the empire's official creed, but Legalist institutions remained its skeleton — a synthesis Pines traces across two thousand years.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

The Daoists said govern less; the Legalists said govern absolutely. The Legalists won — and built an empire that quickly fell.

Part III

Cosmos and Spirits

Yin, yang, and the enduring spirit world — Q10

Explaining Nature: Yin and Yang

  • Natural philosophers explained the world through yin and yang
  • Not opposing forces but complementary poles — dark/light, cold/hot, passive/active
  • Their interplay explains seasons, weather, health, and change itself
A naturalistic system: the cosmos as patterned cycles, not divine whim.

Yin and yang: complementary poles, each holding a seed of the other

📖 Understanding This Slide

Q10 steps away from political philosophy to ask how late Zhou thinkers explained the natural world — and the answer, yin and yang, became one of the most enduring frameworks in Chinese thought. The key is to grasp what kind of idea it is.

📌 Textbook Anchor

"Yin and yang are complementary poles rather than distinct entities or opposing forces." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 32. This is the Q10 anchor — and that word "complementary" is the whole point.

Breaking It Down

Complementary, not opposing: This is the most common misunderstanding, so the anchor corrects it directly. Yin (associated with dark, cold, passive, female, the moon) and yang (light, hot, active, male, the sun) are not warring opposites like good and evil. They are complementary poles of a single continuum — each defines the other, each contains a seed of the other, and each gives way to the other in cycles. Night becomes day becomes night; winter yields to summer yields to winter.

What it explains: Thinkers used the waxing and waning of yin and yang to explain natural patterns — the turn of the seasons, the alternation of weather, the balance of health in the body (illness as imbalance). It's a genuinely naturalistic system: the cosmos runs on regular, intelligible cycles rather than the arbitrary will of gods. In this sense it's a step toward explaining nature through pattern and reason.

Why This Matters

This slide gives the rational, naturalistic side of Q10. But the question asks how this interacted with traditional spiritual practices — and the answer, on the next slide, is that the two coexisted rather than one replacing the other.

Reason and Spirits Side by Side

  • Naturalistic yin-yang thinking did not replace older beliefs
  • Belief in spirits, demons, exorcisms, and protective ritual remained widespread
  • Rational explanation and magico-religious practice coexisted — often in the same people
Not a march from superstition to science, but two ways of knowing held at once.

Spirits, demons, and protective rites endured alongside cosmology

📖 Understanding This Slide

It's tempting to read the rise of yin-yang cosmology as "the dawn of rational thought defeating superstition." Resist that — it's both anachronistic and wrong about what actually happened. The late Zhou evidence shows the new naturalism living comfortably alongside the old spirit world.

Breaking It Down

The persistence of spirits: Alongside yin-yang explanations of nature, belief in spirits, ghosts, and demons remained widespread and vigorous. People performed exorcisms to drive out malevolent forces, used charms and rituals to protect against demons and illness, and consulted specialists in the spirit world. These weren't fringe survivals — they were mainstream practice across society.

Coexistence, not replacement: The crucial point for Q10 is that these two frameworks coexisted. A person might explain the seasons through yin and yang and hang a charm to ward off a demon, seeing no contradiction. Recall Chapter 1's lesson that even sophisticated cultures hold beliefs we can only partly reconstruct — here we see naturalistic and magico-religious thinking braided together in the same world, sometimes the same mind.

The method lesson: History rarely moves in clean replacements where one worldview simply defeats another. More often, new ideas are added to old ones, and people live amid the mixture. The late Zhou intellectual world contained Confucian ethics, Daoist mysticism, Legalist calculation, yin-yang cosmology, and demon-quelling ritual, all at once.

Why This Matters

The causal Q10 answer: natural philosophers used yin and yang — complementary poles, not opposing forces — to explain seasonal change, weather, and health, while belief in spirits, demons, exorcism, and protective ritual remained widespread, so naturalistic explanation and traditional spiritual practice coexisted rather than one displacing the other.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Mark Edward Lewis – The Construction of Space in Early China

Lewis shows how correlative cosmology (yin-yang, and later the five phases) became a master language linking body, state, and cosmos into a single ordered system. Far from opposing religion, it gave spirit beliefs and ritual a cosmological grammar — another instance of early Chinese thought integrating rather than discarding.

Part IV

The Southern Frontier

Chu — both Zhou and not-Zhou — Q11

Chu: Inside the Zhou World, Yet Its Own

  • Chu: a powerful state on the southern frontier of the Zhou world
  • It adopted Zhou institutions and joined the multistate political order
  • Yet it kept a distinct cultural and literary identity — viewed by the north as half-foreign
The "Chinese" world had a frontier — and the frontier was creative, not just peripheral.

Chu lacquerware: a southern aesthetic all its own

📖 Understanding This Slide

The chapter closes by widening the lens beyond the northern Zhou heartland. Chu was a vast and powerful state in the Yangzi region to the south, and it complicates any simple picture of a uniform "Chinese" civilization — echoing Chapter 1's lesson (Sanxingdui) that the Zhou core was never the whole story.

Breaking It Down

Integration: Chu became thoroughly enmeshed in the Zhou political world. It participated in the interstate system as a major player, adopted many Zhou institutions, used the Chinese script, and contended for hegemony. By any political measure it was part of the late Zhou order.

Distinction: Yet Chu retained a strong, separate identity. Northern states often regarded it as semi-barbarian — its rulers at times claimed the title "king," asserting parity with the Zhou royal house. More importantly, Chu developed a distinctive culture: gorgeous lacquerware in swirling red and black, a vivid artistic style, and above all a unique literary and religious tradition rich in shamanism and myth (the later Songs of Chu / Chuci preserve this haunting, otherworldly poetry, quite unlike the northern Book of Songs).

The administrative innovation: Don't miss this — the frontier was ahead of the center in one key respect. Chu was a pioneer of the county (xian) system: rather than granting annexed land to hereditary lords, it formed conquered territory into counties and sent appointed officials to govern them. This is a crucial step toward the centralized bureaucratic empire — and it appeared first on the periphery, not in the Zhou heartland.

Why This Matters

The causal Q11 answer: Chu integrated into the Zhou political world and adopted many Zhou institutions while maintaining a distinct cultural and literary identity — pioneering the administrative innovation of counties governed by appointed officials and developing a unique tradition of lacquerware and shamanic poetry. Integration and distinctiveness coexisted, and the frontier drove innovation.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Constance Cook & John Major (eds.) – Defining Chu

This collection reframed Chu not as a barbarian fringe but as a sophisticated civilization that was central to the formation of early China — contributing administrative innovations and a distinctive religious-literary imagination. Their work exemplifies the broader scholarly move (begun with Sanxingdui in Chapter 1) to decenter the northern plain and recognize early China as genuinely plural.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

The southern state the northerners called half-barbarian was the first to invent the appointed-official county — a building block of empire.

Bridge to Chapter 3: this lecture introduced Legalism and named Qin as its great practitioner. The legendary founder of Daoism, traditionally credited with the Daodejing ("Classic of the Way and Virtue"), a short, poetic, paradoxical text. The work often addresses rulers, advising a paradoxical politics of restraint, humility, and wuwei — teaching that the soft overcomes the hard and that the wise ruler governs least.

Zhuangzi (莊子)

Both a foundational Daoist thinker and the book named for him. More playful and personal than the Daodejing, the Zhuangzi uses parables, paradoxes, and humor — the famous butterfly dream, the useless tree that survives precisely because it is useless — to loosen rigid categories and certainties. It celebrates spontaneity, the relativity of human judgments, and freedom from conventional ambition.

Han Feizi (韓非子, c. 280–233 BCE)

The great synthesizer of Legalist thought and a student of the Confucian Xunzi (whose view that human nature is selfish he carried to ruthless conclusions). The State of Qin (秦)

The westernmost major state of the Warring States period, which adopted Legalist methods wholesale: population registration, rewards for farming and military success, severe uniform punishments, and centralized authority that stripped power from the old aristocracy. These reforms made Qin the most formidable state of the age. In 221 BCE Qin conquered all rivals and founded the first unified Chinese empire — the subject of Chapter 3.

Complementary poles used to explain patterns in the natural world — not opposing forces. Yin is associated with dark, cold, passive, and the moon; yang with light, hot, active, and the sun. The State of Chu (楚)

A vast, powerful state in the Yangzi region on the southern frontier of the Zhou world.
