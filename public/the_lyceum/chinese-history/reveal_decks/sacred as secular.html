Fingarette’s Confucius

The Secular as Sacred

Final Lecture in our Confucianism Unit

PHIL 210 • World Religions

Today is our closing lecture for the Confucianism unit. The goal: use Herbert Fingarette’s interpretation to rethink what “religion” can mean. Emphasize: Confucius can be “religious” without looking like the Abrahamic template (God/creed/clergy).

Where We’ve Been (Quick Recap)

  • ren (humaneness): becoming fully human with others
  • li (ritual propriety): patterned action that shapes relationships
  • junzi (exemplary person): cultivated character made visible in conduct
  • The Dao (“Way”) is learned through practice, not discovered in private
Remind students: Confucianism is a training program for social life. It’s not “just ideas”—it’s a way to become a certain kind of person in community. Transition: Fingarette helps us see why ritual is central rather than decorative.

The Puzzle

Student question: “Is Confucianism a religion or a philosophy?”

Fingarette’s vibe: That question assumes a Western template.

Explain the “template” students often import: Religion = belief in a supernatural realm + God + salvation + clergy + worship. Confucianism doesn’t center those things, so it can look “not religious” by that definition. Fingarette: you’re looking in the wrong place for the sacred.

Herbert Fingarette (1972)

Big claim: Confucius shows the sacred can be enacted, not just believed.

  • No gods required for deep “religious” meaning
  • Sacredness can arise inside disciplined human practice
Keep biography minimal; focus on the interpretive shift. Fingarette isn’t saying “Confucius is atheist modern secular.” He’s saying “the secular can become sacred through ritualized action.”

Core Concept: li as Sacred Performance

Ritual transforms ordinary actions into meaningful performances.

  • The form of the act matters
  • The act becomes “sacred” when done properly (attention + sincerity)
  • Meaning isn’t only inside you—it’s in the shared ritual script
Define li simply: patterned ways of acting that express AND create proper relationships. Clarify “sacred”: not “supernatural”; rather, reverence-worthy, meaning-making, reality-shaping. Emphasize shared recognition: both sides understand what kind of act this is.

Fingarette’s Famous Example: The Handshake

Physical

  • Two palms clasp
  • Pressure + motion
  • That’s all physics sees

Ritual

  • Greeting
  • Respect
  • Agreement
  • Reconciliation
  • “I recognize you.”

Point: Meaning isn’t in the motion—it's in the recognized form.

A handshake “works” because both people understand what it is and participate in it. Optional quick contrast: refusing a handshake changes the relationship immediately. Scale-up: Confucius treats a huge range of social behavior this way.

Scale Up: Confucius Applies This to Social Life

For Confucius, many everyday actions can function as li:

  • Greeting elders / teachers
  • Serving parents (and speaking properly)
  • Hosting guests
  • Mourning the dead
  • Weddings / funerals
  • Posture, timing, deference, restraint

Everyday life becomes the arena of moral cultivation.

Key contrast: Confucianism does not reserve sacredness for one day a week in a special building. Sacred meaning can be distributed across daily life—if actions are performed with reverence and form.

Fingarette’s Provocative Word: “Magic”

Not paranormal. “Magic” = ritual efficacy that changes social reality.

  • A wedding doesn’t only express love—it constitutes a marriage
  • A funeral doesn’t only express grief—it re-situates the dead as an ancestor
  • A bow doesn’t only show respect—it enacts a relationship

No gods intervene—yet something real changes.

Clarify: magic here means “things happen that can’t be explained just by mechanics.” It’s closer to “performative power”—actions that make something socially real by doing it. This is the heart of “secular as sacred.”

“Isn’t Ritual Fake?” (The Western Suspicion)

Confucian reply (via Fingarette): Form doesn’t kill sincerity—form can train sincerity.

  • Feelings alone are unstable
  • Ritual disciplines attention and self-control
  • Virtue becomes reliable through repeated practice

Analogy: music/sports—discipline and form enable authentic excellence.

Many students equate authenticity with “whatever I feel in the moment.” Confucius: your feelings matter, but they need education and stability. Ritual is moral craftsmanship—training the self to become dependable for others.

Confucianism: Not Western Religion… Yet Religious

Not (narrow Western sense)

  • No creator deity at the center
  • No salvation doctrine
  • No required priestly mediation

Yet “religious” (broader sense)

  • Ultimate meaning through practice
  • Reverence and seriousness
  • Self-transformation
  • Sacred bonds in community
Offer a broad working definition: “Religious” practices structure ultimate meaning, cultivate reverence, and transform persons. Confucianism can do that without a salvation narrative or a personal God at the center.

Immanent Sacredness

Immanent = “here, in this world,” not primarily “somewhere else.”

  • Holiness appears in proper relationships
  • The Way is learned in human interaction
  • Moral order is lived—made real through conduct

The sacred emerges through perfected secular action.

Keep the vocabulary simple: immanent = here-and-now. Tie back to ren/li: humaneness becomes real through ritualized relational life. The sacred isn’t missing; it’s concentrated in ordinary life done well.

Comparison (Heuristic)

This is a simplification to highlight differences—not a “better/worse” claim.

Common Abrahamic Pattern Fingarette’s Confucian Pattern
Sacred linked to transcendent God Sacred linked to perfected human interaction
Prayer/worship central Ritual + self-cultivation central
Emphasis on belief/faith Emphasis on practice/formation
Clergy often mediates Everyone participates in sacred acts
Stress: heuristic, not stereotype. The point is category-expansion: sacred meaning can be built through practice, not only anchored in creed.

Criticisms & Limits (Scholarly Caution)

  • May underplay transcendent elements (Heaven, spirits, sacrifice language)
  • May overemphasize ritual form vs inner moral psychology
  • Focuses on elite philosophical texts more than popular practice

Balanced takeaway: best read as re-centering li, not denying all transcendence.

Keep short but honest: scholars disagree about how “secular” Confucius is. Our use of Fingarette: a lens that makes li’s meaning-making power visible. You can accept the lens without making it the whole truth.

Final Takeaway for the Unit

Confucianism challenges three common assumptions:

  • Religion = belief in supernatural beings
  • Ethics and religion are separate domains
  • Ritual is the opposite of sincerity

Fingarette’s insight: the sacred can be something we do—together—through disciplined, reverent practice.

Deliver this as the closing thesis. Reinforce: Confucius is a philosopher of sacred everyday life. The “holy” is not primarily elsewhere; it’s in human relations done right.

Capstone Discussion Prompt

Where do you see “secular-as-sacred” rituals in modern life?

  • Graduations
  • Court oaths / promises
  • Apologies + forgiveness
  • Introductions and titles
  • Memorials and funerals

Follow-up: What happens when rituals break down—or are treated cynically?

Encourage concrete examples. Push them to notice: even “non-religious” modern life is filled with meaning-making rituals. Breakdown question gets them thinking about social trust, disrespect, alienation, and fragmentation.

Exit Ticket (1–2 minutes)

  1. Define “secular as sacred” in your own words.
  2. Give one modern ritual that creates meaning (not just expresses it).
  3. Why might someone dismiss ritual as “empty”? How does Fingarette respond?
This checks comprehension fast. If collecting: you’ll see whether students understood “enactment” vs “mere symbolism.”

Wrap

Confucius, through li, suggests a radical possibility:

holiness can be built inside ordinary life—by the way we treat one another.
