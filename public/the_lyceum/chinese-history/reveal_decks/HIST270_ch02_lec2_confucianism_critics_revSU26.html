📖 Lecture Overview

The last lecture ended with the Hundred Schools — a marketplace of ideas created by desperate, competing rulers. This lecture takes up the first and most enduring tradition to enter that marketplace, and the rivals who immediately challenged it. The organizing question of the whole era is: how do you create order out of chaos? Confucius answers with moral cultivation and ritual; Mozi answers with utility and impartial care; and the two great Confucian successors, Mencius and Xunzi, split over a foundational question — is human nature good or bad? — that decides everything about how you govern. Three review questions, four thinkers, one argument about what holds a society together.

Review Questions Covered

Q5 — What were the foundations of Confucius's philosophy, and how did he redefine the "gentleman" (junzi)?
Q6 — How did Mozi use utility to challenge Confucian ideas about family, ritual, and war?
Q7 — How did Mencius and Xunzi differ on human nature, and how did that shape their views of government?

Where We're Going Today

The Confucian Vision (Q5)

  • Ren, li, and filial piety
  • The junzi redefined: virtue, not birth
  • Good government through virtuous example

The Critics (Q6–Q7)

  • Mozi: judge everything by its usefulness
  • Mencius: human nature is good
  • Xunzi: human nature is selfish

One question, rival answers: what makes people — and states — good?

📖 Roadmap Notes

Structure the lecture as a conversation, not a list of biographies. Confucius sets the terms (Q5). Mozi attacks from outside the tradition, rejecting Confucian ritual and partiality on grounds of social utility (Q6). Then the argument moves inside the Confucian tradition: Mencius and Xunzi both accept Confucius's goals but split over the nature of the raw material — are humans born good or selfish? — and that disagreement drives them toward different theories of government (Q7). The payoff is that abstract questions about human nature have concrete political consequences.

Part I

Confucius and the Moral Order

Ren, ritual, and the redefined gentleman — Q5

Order Through Virtue, Not Force

  • Confucius (551–479 BCE): a shi who taught in the age of collapse
  • Core virtues: ren (benevolence), li (ritual propriety), filial piety
  • Society held together by the five relationships — each with mutual duties
Fix society by cultivating virtue in people — starting in the family.

Confucius: a teacher seeking order in a collapsing world

📖 Understanding This Slide

Confucius (Kongzi) lived through exactly the disorder Lecture 1 described, and his philosophy is a direct response to it. Where others would later turn to law or force, his radical proposal was that social order grows from the moral cultivation of individuals — and that it starts at home.

Breaking It Down

Ren and li: The two pillars. Ren — benevolence or humaneness — is the inner disposition to care about others; it's the moral core of a good person. Li — ritual propriety — is the outer practice: the manners, ceremonies, and proper forms that express respect and structure relationships. Inner virtue and outer ritual reinforce each other; you cultivate ren partly by practicing li.

Filial piety and the five relationships: Confucius made the family the school of virtue. Filial piety — devotion to parents — is the root from which wider benevolence grows. Society is structured by five cardinal relationships (ruler–subject, parent–child, husband–wife, elder–younger sibling, friend–friend), most of them hierarchical but all involving reciprocal obligations: the superior owes care and good example, the subordinate owes loyalty and respect.

Government by example: Politically, this means good government depends on virtuous rulers. A ruler who cultivates himself and leads by moral example draws the people toward goodness without coercion — like the wind bending the grass. This is the Confucian alternative to ruling by force.

Why This Matters

This is the foundation half of Q5. The next slide delivers the single most influential move Confucius made — his redefinition of the junzi — which carries his whole ethics into a claim about social worth.

The Gentleman Remade

  • Old meaning of junzi: "son of a lord" — nobility by birth
  • Confucius's redefinition: a "true gentleman" is a person of moral cultivation
  • Nobility becomes an achievement, open in principle to anyone who cultivates virtue
A revolution hidden in a definition: worth is earned by character, not inherited by blood.

The Analects: Confucius's teachings, recorded by disciples

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is one of those moments where redefining a single word reshapes a civilization's values. The term junzi literally meant "son of a ruler" — an aristocrat by birth. Confucius kept the word but completely changed what made someone a junzi.

📌 Textbook Anchor

"Confucius urged his followers to aspire to become true gentlemen (junzi), a term that he redefined to mean men of moral cultivation rather than men of noble birth." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 26. This is the Q5 anchor.

Breaking It Down

The move: By relocating nobility from bloodline to character, Confucius made moral worth something you achieve through self-cultivation rather than something you inherit. A peasant's son who cultivates ren and masters li is more truly a junzi than a duke's son who is cruel and crude.

Why it resonated in this era: Recall Lecture 1 — this is the age of the shi, men of education but not necessarily high birth, rising on talent in a fluid social order. Confucius's redefinition gives that social reality a moral philosophy: it says the new men can be the best men. It dignifies cultivation over pedigree exactly when cultivation was becoming the path to advancement.

The long consequence: This ideal — that the truly noble person is the morally cultivated person — became the bedrock of the Confucian tradition and, eventually, of the examination system that selected officials by learning rather than birth. A definition became an institution.

Why This Matters

The full causal Q5 answer: Confucius built his philosophy on filial piety, ren, li, and the reciprocal five relationships, holding that good government depends on virtuous rulers leading by example — and he redefined the junzi as a person of moral cultivation rather than noble birth, making nobility an achievement of character.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Benjamin Schwartz – The World of Thought in Ancient China

Schwartz emphasized that Confucius was a transmitter who claimed to revive the Zhou past while in fact transforming it — the junzi redefinition being the prime case. He took an aristocratic ideal and made it ethical and universal, preserving the language of tradition while quietly revolutionizing its content.

Part II

Mozi's Challenge

Utility against ritual and partiality — Q6

Does It Actually Help People?

  • Mozi's test for any practice: is it useful to society?
  • His radical alternative to Confucian family-first ethics: universal love
  • Care for everyone impartially — not more for your own kin
Confucius asks "is it proper?" Mozi asks "does it benefit people?"

Mozi: judge every custom by its usefulness

📖 Understanding This Slide

Mozi (Mo Di) founded the first organized philosophical movement to challenge the Confucians, and he did it with a single, powerful tool: a standard of social utility. Where Confucius revered inherited tradition and ritual, Mozi asked a blunt question of every custom — does this actually benefit people? If not, scrap it.

Breaking It Down

The utility standard: Mozi judged ideas and practices by their usefulness to society — concretely, whether they increased wealth, population, and order. This is a strikingly consequentialist way of thinking, centuries before similar ideas appeared elsewhere. It gave him an external yardstick to criticize traditions the Confucians simply assumed.

Universal love: His most famous doctrine, jian'ai — impartial or universal care. Mozi argued we should care for all people equally, rather than loving our own family and state more than others. He claimed partiality was the root of social conflict: people harm outsiders to benefit their own. The Confucians found this both unrealistic and wrong — for them, graded love starting with family was the very foundation of morality. This is the central fault line of Q6.

Why This Matters

This slide establishes Mozi's method and his core disagreement with Confucius. The next slide shows the utility standard in action, demolishing specific Confucian practices.

Utility on the Attack

Mozi Rejected

  • Elaborate funerals & three-year mourning — wasteful
  • Musical performances — costly indulgence
  • Aggressive warfare — ruinous loss of life and resources

His Reasoning

  • These drain wealth that could feed and protect people
  • Ritual that doesn't help society is indefensible
  • War's costs in weapons, horses, and lives never repay
The same blade cuts ritual and war alike: if it wastes human welfare, it must go.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Here's Mozi's utility standard doing real damage to Confucian commitments. Watch how one consistent principle generates all his specific positions — that's what makes him a systematic philosopher rather than just a critic.

📌 Textbook Anchor

"Using this standard, Mozi rejected many of the rituals emphasized by Confucius's followers, especially mourning parents for three years." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 27. This is the Q6 anchor.

Breaking It Down

Against lavish ritual: The Confucians prized elaborate funerals and the three-year mourning period for parents as essential expressions of filial piety. Mozi attacked them as economically ruinous — they consumed enormous resources and labor, impoverished families, and benefited no one. By his standard, a ritual that drains society without helping it cannot be justified.

Against music: Similarly, Mozi condemned the costly musical performances the Confucians valued as cultivating refinement. To Mozi they were luxury indulgences paid for by the labor of common people who needed food, not entertainment for elites.

Against offensive war: Most powerfully, Mozi opposed aggressive warfare, cataloging its colossal costs — destroyed crops, dead soldiers and horses, ruined states — and arguing it could never produce benefits worth those losses. (The Mohists famously didn't just preach this; they organized to help defend states against aggressors.) Note how this lands in the world of Lecture 1: Mozi is the era's sharpest critic of the very warfare reshaping it.

The unifying logic: Ritual, music, and war look unrelated until you see the principle. All three fail the same test — they consume human welfare without repaying it. Mozi's critique is the utility standard applied without fear of tradition.

Why This Matters

The causal Q6 answer: Mozi judged ideas by their usefulness to society and, on that standard, rejected elaborate rituals, the three-year mourning period, and musical performances as wasteful, and opposed aggressive warfare for its enormous losses — directly challenging the Confucian reverence for ritual and graded, family-first obligation.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

A.C. Graham – Disputers of the Tao

Graham presented Mozi as China's first true logician and moralist of principle — the thinker who forced the Confucians to actually argue for traditions they had taken for granted. For Graham, the Confucian–Mohist debate sharpened Chinese philosophy as a whole, compelling every later school to defend its premises.

Mozi said we should love strangers as much as our own family — and that mourning your parents for three years was a waste.

Part III

Is Human Nature Good?

Mencius and Xunzi divide the tradition — Q7

Mencius: The Sprouts of Goodness

  • Mencius: people have an innate tendency toward goodness
  • Everyone feels instinctive compassion — the sprouts of virtue
  • Government should nurture this nature through benevolent rule
If people are born good, the ruler's job is to cultivate, not coerce.

Mencius: human nature tends toward the good

📖 Understanding This Slide

Mencius (Mengzi) was the most influential Confucian after Confucius himself, and he gave the tradition something it had lacked: a theory of human nature. His answer was optimistic — and it shaped everything downstream.

Breaking It Down

Innate goodness: Mencius argued that human nature tends naturally toward the good. His famous proof: anyone who suddenly sees a child about to fall into a well feels an immediate flash of alarm and compassion — not for reward or reputation, but spontaneously. That reflexive care reveals our true nature.

The sprouts: He described innate moral "sprouts" or beginnings — compassion, shame, courtesy, a sense of right and wrong — that grow into the full virtues if nurtured, but wither if neglected or starved by bad conditions. Virtue is natural growth, like a plant given water and light.

Benevolent government: The political conclusion follows directly. If people are innately good, the ruler's task is to provide the conditions — material security, moral example, education — in which their good nature can flourish. Mencius pressed rulers hard on the people's welfare, even endorsing the idea (rooted in the Mandate of Heaven from Chapter 1) that a tyrant who ruins the people forfeits the right to rule. Good nature plus good conditions yields a good society.

Why This Matters

This is half of the Q7 comparison. Hold Mencius's optimism in mind, because the next slide introduces a brilliant Confucian who looked at the same humanity and concluded the opposite.

Xunzi: Crooked Wood Must Be Straightened

  • Xunzi explicitly opposed Mencius: people are born selfish
  • Goodness is not natural — it's an achievement of education, ritual, and discipline
  • Government must actively reshape people through strong institutions and teaching
Same goal as Mencius — a moral society — but it must be built against the grain of human nature.

Xunzi: goodness is made, not born

📖 Understanding This Slide

Xunzi was every bit as committed to Confucian morality as Mencius — they wanted the same destination. But Xunzi started from the opposite premise about human nature, and that single disagreement sent the two Confucians down very different roads.

📌 Textbook Anchor

"Xunzi explicitly opposed Mencius's view of human nature, arguing that people are born selfish and that it is only through education that they become civilized." — Patricia Ebrey, A History of China, p. 29. This is the Q7 anchor.

Breaking It Down

Nature is bad: Xunzi argued that people are born with selfish desires — for profit, for pleasure — and that if these run unchecked, the result is conflict and chaos. Left to "nature," humans are not good. His famous image: human nature is like crooked wood that must be steamed and bent straight, or a blunt blade that must be ground sharp. Goodness is artificial in the precise sense that it must be made.

How goodness is made: The instruments are education, ritual (li), and discipline. Through sustained training in proper forms and study, people transform their raw nature and become civilized and moral. Ritual, which Mozi dismissed as waste, Xunzi defends as the essential technology of moral formation — it channels and reshapes dangerous desires into ordered conduct.

The political consequence: Because people won't become good on their own, the state must take an active, shaping role — strong institutions, clear standards, firm teaching, and authority to enforce order. This is a more directive, top-down vision than Mencius's nurturing ruler. (It's no accident that Xunzi taught two of the most important Legalists; his emphasis on institutions over innate virtue points toward the next lecture.)

Why This Matters

The full causal Q7 answer: Mencius held that people have an innate tendency toward goodness that benevolent government should nurture, while Xunzi held that people are born selfish and become moral only through education, ritual, and discipline — so their opposed views of human nature produced opposed approaches to government, nurturing versus actively reshaping. One disagreement about the raw material, two blueprints for the state.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Benjamin Schwartz – The World of Thought in Ancient China

Schwartz framed the Mencius–Xunzi split as Confucianism arguing with itself about its own foundations — a sign of the tradition's depth, not its weakness. Both answers remained live options for two millennia, with Mencius eventually winning orthodox favor while Xunzi's institutional realism quietly shaped how China was actually governed.

Confucius (孔子, Kongzi, 551–479 BCE)

The most influential philosopher in Chinese history, a shi who lived through the disorder of the Spring and Autumn period. He sought to restore social order through moral cultivation, ritual propriety, and the reciprocal duties of human relationships rather than through force. His teachings, recorded by disciples in the Analects, became the foundation of the Confucian tradition that shaped East Asian government, education, and ethics for over two thousand years.

×
Ren (仁) — Benevolence

The central virtue of Confucian ethics: humaneness, benevolence, or the genuine disposition to care for others. Ren is the inner moral core of the cultivated person, expressed outwardly through proper conduct and ritual. Confucius treated it as the highest human achievement, developed gradually through self-cultivation that begins with devotion to one's family.

×
Li (禮) — Ritual Propriety

The proper forms, manners, ceremonies, and etiquette that structure relationships and express respect. For Confucius, li was not empty formality but the outward practice through which inner virtue (ren) is cultivated and shown. Practicing ritual correctly trains the character; Xunzi later made li the central tool for reshaping selfish human nature into moral conduct.

×
Junzi (君子) — The Gentleman

Literally "son of a lord," originally meaning an aristocrat by birth. Confucius radically redefined the term to mean a person of moral cultivation regardless of birth — making nobility an achievement of character rather than an accident of blood. The junzi as cultivated moral exemplar became a central Confucian ideal and helped justify advancement by learning rather than pedigree.

×
The Five Relationships

The five cardinal relationships structuring Confucian society: ruler–subject, parent–child, husband–wife, elder–younger sibling, and friend–friend. Four are hierarchical and one (friends) is equal, but all involve reciprocal obligations — the superior owes care and good example, the subordinate owes loyalty and respect. Social harmony results when everyone fulfills the duties of their roles.

×
Mozi (墨子, c. 470–391 BCE)

Founder of Mohism, the first organized philosophical movement to challenge the Confucians. Mozi judged all practices by their usefulness to society — whether they increased wealth, population, and order. He advocated universal impartial love, condemned wasteful ritual and music, and opposed aggressive warfare. His followers were a disciplined organization that helped defend states against aggressors.

×
Universal Love (兼愛, jian'ai)

Mozi's doctrine that one should care for all people equally and impartially, rather than loving one's own family and state more than others. Mozi argued that partiality — favoring one's own — was the root cause of social conflict and war. The Confucians rejected this fiercely, holding that graded love beginning with family was the natural foundation of all morality.

×
Mencius (孟子, Mengzi, c. 372–289 BCE)

The most influential Confucian after Confucius. Mencius argued that human nature tends innately toward goodness, illustrated by the spontaneous compassion anyone feels seeing a child in danger. He taught that government should nurture people's good nature through benevolent rule, and pressed rulers on the people's welfare — affirming that a tyrant who ruins the people forfeits the Mandate of Heaven.

×
The Four Sprouts

Mencius's metaphor for the innate moral beginnings present in every person: the feelings of compassion, shame, courtesy, and the sense of right and wrong. Like seedlings, these "sprouts" grow into the full virtues (benevolence, righteousness, propriety, wisdom) when nurtured by good conditions and education — but wither if neglected or corrupted by a harsh environment.

×
Xunzi (荀子, c. 310–235 BCE)

A major Confucian who explicitly opposed Mencius, arguing that human nature is selfish and that goodness is an achievement of education, ritual, and discipline rather than something innate — "crooked wood must be straightened." His emphasis on institutions and the active shaping of behavior over reliance on innate virtue influenced his students Han Feizi and Li Si, who became leading Legalists.
