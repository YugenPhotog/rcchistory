## SLIDE 3.5

## What the Buddha Discovered
### Core Insights from His Awakening

**The Three Marks of Existence**

All conditioned things share three characteristics:

1. **<span class="key-term">Anicca</span>** (Impermanence)
   - Everything changes, nothing lasts forever
   - Even pleasant experiences end

2. **<span class="key-term">Dukkha</span>** (Suffering/Unsatisfactoriness)
   - Life contains inevitable dissatisfaction
   - Clinging to impermanent things causes suffering

3. **<span class="key-term">Anatta</span>** (No-Self)
   - No permanent, unchanging soul or essence
   - We are constantly changing processes, not fixed entities

<div id="three-marks-def" class="popup">
    <span class="popup-close">&times;</span>
    <div class="definition-term">Three Marks of Existence</div>
    <p>These three insights form the foundation of all Buddhist teaching across every tradition. Understanding impermanence, suffering, and no-self is essential to the Buddhist path.</p>
</div>

**LECTURE NOTES:**
<span class="popup-trigger" data-popup="three-marks-def">Three Marks</span> show WHAT Buddha awakened to. These appear in all Buddhist schools. Connect to Four Noble Truths (coming Day 2). Students often struggle with anattaâ€”clarify it's about process vs. substance, not nihilism.

# Growth of the Sangha
## Community of Buddhist Practitioners

### Monastic Sangha  
- Monks (<span class="key-term">bhikkhus</span>) and nuns (<span class="key-term">bhikkhunis</span>)
- Full-time practitioners following strict discipline
- Preserve and teach the dharma

### Lay Sangha  
- Householders supporting monastics
- Follow basic ethical precepts
- Gain merit through generosity

<div id="bhikkhuni-note" class="popup">
    <span class="popup-close">&times;</span>
    <div class="definition-term">Note on Women's Ordination</div>
    <p>The bhikkhuni (nuns') full ordination lineage died out in Theravada countries centuries ago and has been controversially revived in recent decades. Women's full ordination remains a contested issue in some Buddhist traditions today.</p>
</div>

**LECTURE NOTES:**
Explain symbiotic relationship: monastics preserve teachings, laypeople provide material support. <span class="popup-trigger" data-popup="bhikkhuni-note">Women's ordination</span> has complex history worth acknowledging honestly. Both groups essential to Buddhist community.

# Theravada Buddhism: Origins, Development, and Spread
## A Focus on the Tradition of the Elders

### Introduction to Theravada Buddhism
- Oldest surviving school of Buddhism  
- Claims to best preserve <span class="key-term">early teachings</span>
- Based on Pali Canon texts
- Dominant in Sri Lanka & Southeast Asia  

<div id="theravada-def" class="popup">
    <span class="popup-close">&times;</span>
    <div class="definition-term">Theravada</div>
    <p>"Teaching of the Elders." Theravada claims to preserve early Buddhist teachings, though all extant texts post-date the Buddha by centuries. The claim is about textual lineage and interpretive approach rather than word-for-word preservation.</p>
</div>

**LECTURE NOTES:**
<span class="popup-trigger" data-popup="theravada-def">Theravada</span> means "conservative in preservation," not politically conservative. All Buddhist texts come centuries after Buddhaâ€”"early teachings" more accurate than "original teachings." Modern scholarship questions simple transmission narratives.

## SLIDE 12

### Historical Development

**Second Council (c. 340 BCE)**
- Disputes over monastic discipline
- Split between conservative and progressive factions
- Multiple schools emerge from elder tradition

**Development of Theravada**
- One lineageâ€”the <span class="key-term">Vibhajjavada</span>â€”took root in Sri Lanka
- Evolved over centuries into what we now call Theravada Buddhism
- This crystallization was gradual, not sudden

**Emperor Ashoka (3rd century BCE)**
- Converts to Buddhism after violent Kalinga War
- Sends missionaries throughout Asia
- Son Mahinda brings Buddhism to Sri Lanka

<div id="ashoka-dhamma" class="popup">
    <span class="popup-close">&times;</span>
    <div class="definition-term">Ashoka's Dhamma</div>
    <p>Ashoka promoted "dhamma" (ethical governance) through rock edicts throughout his empire. His dhamma emphasized moral conduct, religious tolerance, and social welfareâ€”broader than strictly Buddhist dharma (teaching).</p>
</div>

**LECTURE NOTES:**
Second Council ~340 BCE (not 375 BCEâ€”updated dating). Theravada didn't emerge directly from Council split; developed gradually through <span class="popup-trigger" data-popup="ashoka-dhamma">Vibhajjavada lineage</span> in Sri Lanka over centuries. Compare Ashoka to Constantineâ€”both royal conversions transformed their religions. Kalinga War's brutality prompted his conversion.

## SLIDE 15

### Core Beliefs of Theravada

**The Buddha**
- Human teacher, not deity
- Showed the path to liberation
- Now in final nirvana, beyond reach

**Liberation**  
- Through individual effort
- Following ethical precepts
- Meditation and wisdom

**Monastic Ideal**
- Monks/nuns as dedicated practitioners
- Laypeople support and gain merit
- Gradual progress over many lives

<div id="lay-practice" class="popup">
    <span class="popup-close">&times;</span>
    <div class="definition-term">Lay Buddhist Practice</div>
    <p>Recent scholarship highlights sophisticated lay Buddhist practice and questions the strict monk/lay hierarchy. Merit-making represents complex soteriology, not just "spiritual consumerism." Lay practitioners can achieve advanced understanding.</p>
</div>

**LECTURE NOTES:**
Buddha as exemplar, not savior. Emphasizes self-reliance: "Be a lamp unto yourself." <span class="popup-trigger" data-popup="lay-practice">Monastic ideal</span> traditional but modern scholarship recognizes lay practice sophistication. Contrast devotional vs philosophical elements within Theravada itself.
