## Functionalist vs. Substantive Approaches

**RIGHT:**
### [POPUP_TRIGGER id="substantive"]Substantive Approach[/POPUP_TRIGGER]

**Thinkers:** Otto, Eliade, Tylor, Frazer

[POPUP id="functionalism"]
**Functionalism**

A theoretical approach that explains religion by examining what it DOES rather than what it IS. Asks: What functions does religion serve?

- Social cohesion (Durkheim)
- Psychological comfort (Freud)
- Economic control (Marx)
- Legitimating authority
- Creating meaning systems

Can be reductive if it ignores what practitioners consciously believe/intend.
[/POPUP]

[POPUP id="substantive"]
**Substantive Approach**

A theoretical approach that explains religion by examining the IDEAS and EXPERIENCES that motivate people. Asks: What do practitioners believe/experience/intend?

Focus on:
- Religious ideas and doctrines
- Phenomenology of experience
- Meaning-making
- Conscious motivations

Can miss unconscious social functions if it only looks at explicit meanings.
[/POPUP]

**Neither is more "correct"**‚Äîthey ask different questions and reveal different aspects of religious life.

### SLIDE 44: Functionalism in Action

## What Does Religion DO?

**Functionalists ask:**

‚óÜ How does religion create social cohesion?

‚óÜ How does religion provide psychological comfort?

‚óÜ How does religion legitimate authority structures?

‚óÜ How does religion mask or reinforce economic exploitation?

**Key Functionalist Thinkers:**

‚óÜ **Karl Marx** (Economics): Religion numbs people to exploitation

‚óÜ **Sigmund Freud** (Psychology): Religion meets need for father figure

‚óÜ **√âmile Durkheim** (Sociology): Religion reinforces group identity

‚óÜ **Max Weber** (Sociology): Religion influences economic systems

**Important Note:**

Functionalism doesn't necessarily dismiss or attack religion‚Äîit analyzes what religion *does* rather than whether beliefs are true.

Early functionalists (Marx, Freud) were often hostile critics. Later scholars became more neutral, using functional analysis as one analytical tool among many.

### SLIDE 47: Tylor and Frazer‚ÄîHistorical Context

## Early Substantive Theorists: [POPUP_TRIGGER id="tylor"]Edward Tylor[/POPUP_TRIGGER] (1832-1917) and [POPUP_TRIGGER id="frazer"]James Frazer[/POPUP_TRIGGER] (1854-1941)

[POPUP id="tylor"]
**Edward Tylor (1832-1917)**

British anthropologist, pioneer in cultural evolution theory. Coined term "animism" to describe belief in spiritual beings. Argued religion evolved from simple to complex forms.

Major work: *Primitive Culture* (1871). Used term "primitive" to describe "earlier stages" of cultural evolution‚Äînow rejected as ethnocentric and colonialist.
[/POPUP]

[POPUP id="frazer"]
**James Frazer (1854-1941)**

Scottish anthropologist and folklorist. Compiled massive cross-cultural study of myth, magic, and ritual.

Major work: *The Golden Bough: A Study in Magic and Religion* (13 volumes, 1890-1915). Influenced by evolutionary assumptions now considered problematic.
[/POPUP]

**Why they're here (and why this is *not* the last word):**

We place Tylor and Frazer under the **substantive** side here because they offered *content-based* accounts of what religion is (e.g., spirits, magic), even though their evolutionary assumptions are now widely rejected.

**Their Arguments:**

‚óÜ Religious belief originated as explanations of natural phenomena

‚óÜ Beliefs in spirits attempt to explain life and death

‚óÜ Myths and deities attempt to explain other natural phenomena (thunder, seasons, etc.)

‚óÜ Religion evolved from "primitive" forms to monotheism and eventually to science

**Major Work:** James Frazer, *The Golden Bough: A Study in Magic and Religion* (13 volumes!)

### SLIDE 48: Critical Context‚ÄîColonial Assumptions

## ‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL CONTEXT: Why We Study Them

[WARNING_BOX]
Tylor and Frazer used the term **"primitive"**‚Äîa problematic word reflecting **19th-century colonial assumptions**.

Their evolutionary model is now **widely rejected by scholars** because it:

‚óÜ Wrongly assumed European Christianity was the "highest" form of religion

‚óÜ Wrongly assumed indigenous traditions were "lesser" or "earlier stages"

‚óÜ Reflected colonial attitudes that justified domination and "civilizing missions"

‚óÜ Imposed Western categories (belief, monotheism, scripture) on all traditions
[/WARNING_BOX]

**We study Tylor and Frazer to understand the history of Religious Studies, NOT because their conclusions are correct.**

**What we learn from them:**

‚úÖ The importance of cross-cultural comparison (good method)

‚úÖ Attention to symbolism in myth and ritual (good insight)

‚ùå Evolutionary hierarchies are ethnocentric (bad conclusion)

‚ùå "Primitive/advanced" language is colonial (bad framing)

**SPEAKER NOTES:**
This revision is CRITICAL based on analysis. Spend 3 minutes.

Don't skip the context paragraph‚Äîit prevents students from absorbing colonialist assumptions.

Emphasize: "We learn from their mistakes as well as their insights. The discipline has evolved by recognizing and correcting these errors."

If students ask why we study them at all: "Understanding how the discipline developed‚Äîincluding its mistakes‚Äîmakes us better scholars. We see how assumptions shape conclusions."

### SLIDE 51: Disciplinary History [ASSESSMENT_BREAK]

## Theology ‚Üí Comparative Religion ‚Üí Religious Studies

**Before 19th Century:**

‚óÜ Religion studied primarily through **theology** (from within traditions)

‚óÜ Christian scholars studied Christianity; Muslim scholars studied Islam; etc.

‚óÜ Goal: Deepen faith, defend orthodoxy, interpret scripture

**19th Century: The "Science of Religion" Emerges**

‚óÜ Scholars began studying religion **comparatively** (across traditions)

‚óÜ Applied social scientific methods (history, anthropology, sociology)

‚óÜ Aimed for **descriptive** rather than **prescriptive** approach

‚óÜ Key figures: Max M√ºller (philology), Cornelius Tiele (phenomenology)

**20th Century: Institutionalization**

‚óÜ Religious Studies departments separate from theology schools

‚óÜ Development of methods: phenomenology, history of religions

‚óÜ Debates: Can you study religion "objectively"? Should you?

**21st Century: Critical Turns**

‚óÜ Postcolonial critique (acknowledging Western bias)

‚óÜ Attention to power, gender, race in religious contexts

‚óÜ New areas: digital religion, material religion, lived religion

### SLIDE 52: Historical-Critical Method

## Method #1: Historical-Critical Approach

**What It Is:**

‚óÜ Studies the process of a religion's beginnings, growth, diversity, and decline

‚óÜ Uses tools like: archaeology, textual analysis in original languages, historical contextualization

‚óÜ Treats religious texts as historical documents (when written? by whom? for what audience? in what context?)

**Example Applications:**

‚óÜ Dating biblical manuscripts

‚óÜ Tracing the development of Buddhist texts across centuries

‚óÜ Analyzing how Qur'anic interpretation changed over time

‚óÜ Understanding religious movements in their historical-political contexts

[GRID_COMPARISON]
**LEFT:**
### Strengths:

‚óÜ Grounds religion in concrete history

‚óÜ Reveals how traditions change over time

‚óÜ Uncovers influences between traditions

**RIGHT:**
### Limitations:

‚óÜ Can miss what religion *means* to practitioners today

‚óÜ May reduce religion to "just" historical phenomena
[/GRID_COMPARISON]

### SLIDE 53A: Ethnography and Fieldwork (Lived Religion as Method)

## Method Add-on: Ethnography / Fieldwork

Some scholars study religion by observing and interviewing people in real settings‚Äîhomes, temples, churches, festivals, online communities.

**Typical tools:**
- Participant observation (watching and taking fieldnotes; sometimes participating)
- Interviews (structured or informal conversations)
- Material/space analysis (objects, clothing, food, architecture)
- Ethics + consent (protecting people; avoiding harm)
- Reflexivity (noticing how *you* shape what you see)

**Key takeaway:** Ethnography helps you study **lived religion**‚Äîwhat people actually do, not only what texts or leaders say.

### SLIDE 54: Phenomenology of Religion

## Method #3: [POPUP_TRIGGER id="phenomenology"]Phenomenology of Religion[/POPUP_TRIGGER]

[POPUP id="phenomenology"]
**Phenomenology of Religion**

An approach that seeks to describe religions empathetically "in their own terms" from the standpoint of practitioners.

Key principles:
- Epoch√© (bracketing): Suspend your own judgments
- Empathy: Understand from inside perspective
- Irreducibility: Religion has its own distinct character
- Comparison: Look for universal patterns

Major figures: Rudolf Otto, Gerardus van der Leeuw, Mircea Eliade, Ninian Smart.

Critiqued for potentially essentializing religion or smuggling in theological assumptions.
[/POPUP]

**Core Principles:**

**1. Empathetic Standpoint**

Seeks to describe religions **in their own terms** from a standpoint that is empathetic with practitioners

Phenomenologists argue that religion is best approached by starting from the standpoint of practitioners‚Äîdescribing before judging‚Äîwhile recognizing that complete neutrality is impossible.

**2. [POPUP_TRIGGER id="epoche"]Bracketing (Epoch√©)[/POPUP_TRIGGER]**

Temporarily suspend your own beliefs and judgments

"Bracket" questions of truth/falsehood to focus on *what religion means to practitioners*

**Quick check:** Bracketing does **not** mean agreeing‚Äîit means suspending judgment long enough to describe carefully.

[POPUP id="epoche"]
**Epoch√© (·ºêœÄŒøœáŒÆ)**

Greek philosophical term meaning "suspension" or "holding back."

In phenomenology: The practice of temporarily setting aside (bracketing) your own beliefs, judgments, and assumptions to focus purely on describing phenomena as they appear to consciousness.

Example: Don't ask "Is God real?" Ask "What is the experience of encountering God like for this practitioner?"
[/POPUP]

**3. Irreducibility of the Sacred**

Religion cannot be fully explained by psychology, sociology, economics, or history alone

Religion has its own distinct character

**4. Comparative Focus**

Seeks out aspects of religious life that are universal or essential

Example: experience of the sacred across cultures

### SLIDE 56: Mircea Eliade [ASSESSMENT_BREAK]

## [POPUP_TRIGGER id="eliade"]Mircea Eliade[/POPUP_TRIGGER] (1907-1986): Key Phenomenologist

[POPUP id="eliade"]
**Mircea Eliade (1907-1986)**

Romanian historian of religions and University of Chicago professor. One of the most influential phenomenologists of religion in the 20th century.

Major works: *The Myth of the Eternal Return* (1949), *The Sacred and the Profane* (1959), *Patterns in Comparative Religion* (1958).

Argued that religion has irreducible essence (the sacred) that appears in universal patterns across cultures. Critiqued for essentializing and ignoring power/history.
[/POPUP]

**Major Works:**

‚óÜ *The Myth of the Eternal Return* (1949)

‚óÜ *The Sacred and the Profane* (1959)

**Eliade's Arguments:**

**1. Irreducibility of the Sacred**

Religious phenomena must be understood as uniquely and irreducibly religious

Religion expresses meaning on a *religious* plane of reference

Cannot reduce religion to psychological, social, economic, or historical factors alone

**2. Dialectic of the Sacred and Profane**

Religion involves the experience of the transcendent (sacred, infinite, eternal) manifesting through ordinary (profane, finite, temporal) phenomena

Examples: How can the infinite God appear in bread and wine (Eucharist)? How can the eternal manifest in a specific historical event?

**3. Universal Symbolic Systems**

Human beings are *homo religiosus* and *homo symbolicus* (religious and symbolic beings)

We use symbolic language to express transcendent meanings

Common symbols (water, sky, earth, center) appear across cultures with similar meanings

### SLIDE 59: Map Is Not Territory

## "Map Is Not Territory" ‚Äî [POPUP_TRIGGER id="jz-smith"]Jonathan Z. Smith[/POPUP_TRIGGER]

[POPUP id="jz-smith"]

American historian of religions, University of Chicago. Known for deconstructing taken-for-granted categories in religious studies.

[/POPUP]

**What This Means:**

All our frameworks‚Äî"world religions," "myth," "ritual," "functionalism," "phenomenology"‚Äîare **maps** we've drawn to help navigate complex terrain.

**The map is useful, but it's not the same as the actual territory.**

### SLIDE 61: Evidence and Academic Writing

## What Counts as Evidence in Religious Studies?

When you write papers or make arguments in this class, **good evidence includes:**

‚úÖ **Primary sources:**
‚óÜ Sacred texts (Bible, Qur'an, Vedas, etc.)
‚óÜ First-hand accounts from practitioners
‚óÜ Historical documents
‚óÜ Ethnographic observations

‚úÖ **Secondary sources:**
‚óÜ Scholarly books and peer-reviewed journal articles
‚óÜ Reputable news sources (for contemporary events)
‚óÜ Documentary films by credentialed scholars

‚ùå **NOT good evidence:**
‚óÜ "I think..." or "I feel..." (personal opinion without support)
‚óÜ "Everyone knows..." (unexamined assumptions)
‚óÜ Stereotypes from media or hearsay
‚óÜ Random websites without scholarly credentials

**Academic Writing Tip:**

‚ùå Instead of: "I think Buddhism is peaceful"

‚úÖ Write: "Scholar X argues that Buddhist ethics emphasize ahimsa (non-harm), as evidenced by texts such as the Dhammapada."

### SLIDE 62: Language of Respectful Disagreement

## How to Engage Critically While Respecting People

## KEY TERMS FOR DAY 3

**Students should know by end of Chapter 1:**

**Approaches:**
- **Functionalism:** Explains religion by examining effects‚Äîasks "What does religion DO?"
- **Substantive theory:** Explains religion by examining ideas/experiences‚Äîasks "What does religion MEAN?"

**Methods:**
- **Historical-critical method:** Uses archaeology, textual analysis, historical context
- **History of religions school:** Studies religion as social/cultural phenomenon; comparative
- **Phenomenology of religion:** Describes religions empathetically "in their own terms"; uses epoch√© (bracketing)

**Key Figures:**
- **Edward Tylor & James Frazer:** Early substantive theorists; evolutionary model (NOW REJECTED as colonialist)
- **Mircea Eliade:** Phenomenologist; irreducibility of sacred, universal symbolic systems

**Contemporary Approaches:**
- **Digital religion:** Study of religion in/through digital media
- **Lived religion:** How ordinary people actually practice vs. textbook ideals
- **Material religion:** Focus on objects, bodies, senses, architecture
- **"Map is not territory":** All scholarly frameworks are constructed tools, not objective truth
- **Methodological self-awareness:** Examining your own assumptions and analytical tools

**Evidence:**
- **Primary sources:** Sacred texts, first-hand accounts, historical documents
- **Secondary sources:** Scholarly books, peer-reviewed articles
