### SLIDE 3: A Note on This Framework

## ‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Awareness: The "Map is Not Territory"

The "world religions" approach was developed by British educators in the 1960s to broaden religious education beyond Christianity.

**While useful for organizing our study, scholars now recognize this framework has [KEY_TERM]limitations[/KEY_TERM]:**

‚óÜ It can make religions look more unified and bounded than they really are

‚óÜ It emphasizes beliefs and texts (a Protestant Christian model) over practices and diversity

‚óÜ It marginalizes traditions that don't fit the "Big Five" mold (indigenous religions, "folk" practices, hybrid traditions)

[WARNING_BOX]
**Think of the "world religions" paradigm as a MAP** ‚Äî useful for navigation, but remember: **the map is not the territory.**

We'll point out where the map oversimplifies as we go.
[/WARNING_BOX]

[POPUP_TRIGGER id="paradigm-critique"]World Religions Paradigm Critique[/POPUP_TRIGGER]

[POPUP id="paradigm-critique"]
**World Religions Paradigm (WRP)**

A framework developed in 1960s Britain for organizing religious studies education. Critics (Owen 2011, Cotter & Robertson 2016) note it:
- Models all religions on Protestant Christianity (emphasis on belief, texts, founders)
- Creates artificial boundaries between traditions
- Privileges certain traditions while marginalizing others
- Reflects colonial assumptions about "civilized" vs "primitive" religions

We use it pragmatically while remaining aware of its limitations.
[/POPUP]

<h3 style="color: #2c5282; margin-top: 1.5em;">üìñ Understanding This Slide</h3>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  Here's where things get interesting: we're about to use a framework (world religions) while simultaneously critiquing it. This might feel contradictory, but it's actually a sophisticated scholarly move. <h3 style="color: #2c5282; margin-top: 1.5em;">Breaking It Down</h3>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  <strong>The 1960s British origin</strong>: The "world religions" approach emerged in Britain when educators wanted to broaden religious education beyond just Christianity. This was progressive for its time‚Äîit acknowledged religious diversity. But here's the catch: the framework was built by people coming from a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestantism" target="_blank">Protestant Christian</a> background, and they unconsciously modeled it on their own tradition.
</p>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  <strong>Making religions look unified</strong>: When we talk about "Hinduism" or "Buddhism," we risk making these traditions sound like single, coherent systems with clear boundaries. In reality, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism" target="_blank">Hinduism</a> is incredibly diverse‚Äîthere's no single founder, no single sacred text, no unified set of beliefs. The label "Hinduism" itself is relatively modern and was largely imposed by outsiders. Similarly, Buddhism encompasses vastly different practices from Zen meditation in Japan to tantric rituals in Tibet.
</p>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  <strong>Emphasizing beliefs and texts</strong>: Protestant Christianity historically emphasized <em>what you believe</em> (doctrine, creeds) over <em>what you do</em> (rituals, practices). When we study other religions through this lens, we unconsciously privilege beliefs and sacred texts, which might not be what practitioners themselves consider most important. Many traditions emphasize orthopraxy (right practice) over orthodoxy (right belief).
</p>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  <strong>Marginalizing some traditions</strong>: The "Big Five" or "Big Six" world religions framework tends to center Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, and sometimes Judaism and Sikhism. But what about <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_religion" target="_blank">indigenous religions</a>, African diasporic traditions like Vodou or Candombl√©, or "folk" practices that blend multiple traditions? These get pushed to the margins or relegated to a catch-all category like "primal religions"‚Äîa problematic term that implies they're less developed.
</p>

<h3 style="color: #2c5282; margin-top: 1.5em;">Why This Matters</h3>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  The warning box on this slide uses a perfect metaphor: <strong>the map is not the territory</strong>. This comes from philosopher <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Korzybski" target="_blank">Alfred Korzybski</a>, who reminded us that representations of reality are not the same as reality itself. A map of Illinois simplifies the actual landscape‚Äîit doesn't show every tree, every hill, every house. It's useful for navigation, but you wouldn't confuse the paper map for the actual place. Same thing here: "world religions" is a useful organizing map for our course, but it simplifies and distorts the actual diversity of human religious experience.
</p>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  Throughout the semester, your professor will point out where the map oversimplifies. You'll learn to see the internal diversity <em>within</em> traditions and the blurred boundaries <em>between</em> them. This critical awareness is what separates sophisticated religious studies from simplistic stereotyping.
</p>

<h3 style="color: #2c5282; margin-top: 1.5em;">üéì Scholars Weigh In</h3>
<h4 style="margin-top: 1em;">Christopher R. Cotter & David G. Robertson ‚Äì <em>After World Religions</em> (2016)</h4>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  Cotter and Robertson are part of a movement of scholars calling for <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Religions_Paradigm" target="_blank">moving beyond the World Religions Paradigm</a>. They argue that the framework reflects colonial-era assumptions about what counts as a "real" or "civilized" religion. When European colonizers encountered indigenous traditions, they often dismissed them as "superstition" or "animism" rather than recognizing them as sophisticated religious systems. The world religions framework, even though well-intentioned, still carries some of that baggage‚Äîit privileges traditions that look like Christianity (with founders, scriptures, centralized institutions) and marginalizes traditions that don't fit that mold. For this course, we're using the framework because it's practical for a 15-week semester, but we're doing so with our eyes wide open to its limitations.
</p>

### SLIDE 6: What is a Worldview?

## Worldview = Your Lens on Reality

A [POPUP_TRIGGER id="worldview-def"]worldview[/POPUP_TRIGGER] is essentially a **lens** through which individuals interpret and interact with the world around them.

It encompasses a set of beliefs, values, and assumptions that shape how one understands reality and their place within it.

[POPUP id="worldview-def"]
**Worldview**

A comprehensive framework of beliefs, values, and assumptions through which an individual interprets all of reality. Includes views on:
- What exists (ontology)
- How we know (epistemology)  
- What's right/wrong (ethics)
- What it means to be human (anthropology)
- Origins and purpose (cosmology)

Everyone has a worldview, whether consciously articulated or not.
[/POPUP]

<h3 style="color: #2c5282; margin-top: 1.5em;">üìñ Understanding This Slide</h3>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">

<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  <strong>Worldview as a comprehensive framework</strong>: Your <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worldview" target="_blank">worldview</a> isn't just one or two beliefs‚Äîit's an entire interlocking system of assumptions, values, and convictions. It shapes how you answer big questions (What is real? How do we know things? What is good?) and small questions (How should I treat strangers? What does it mean to live a good life?).
</p>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  <strong>Shaped by culture, upbringing, experiences</strong>: Nobody is born with a worldview fully formed. You absorb it from your family, your community, your culture, your education, your personal experiences. If you grew up in a devoutly religious household, your worldview was shaped by that. If you grew up in a secular, scientifically-oriented household, your worldview was shaped by <em>that</em>. If you experienced trauma or profound joy, those experiences became part of your lens. None of this is neutral‚Äîyour worldview reflects your particular location in history, geography, culture, and society.
</p>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  <strong>No one has a "neutral" or "objective" view</strong>: This is a critical point that students sometimes resist. You might think, "I'm open-minded" or "I just look at the facts." But here's the thing: <em>what counts as a fact</em> depends on your worldview. A scientist and a mystic might both witness the same sunset, but they'll interpret it through completely different frameworks. The scientist might see photons scattering through atmospheric particles. The mystic might see the divine presence manifest in nature. Neither perspective is "view from nowhere"‚Äîboth are shaped by particular worldviews.
</p>

<h3 style="color: #2c5282; margin-top: 1.5em;">Why This Matters</h3>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  Understanding that you <em>have</em> a worldview‚Äîand that it's not neutral‚Äîis the first step toward studying other traditions fairly. If you're not aware of your lens, you'll unconsciously impose your assumptions onto everything you study. You might judge another tradition as "irrational" simply because it doesn't share your culture's assumptions about what counts as rational. Or you might romanticize a tradition by projecting your own values onto it. Awareness of your worldview creates the space for genuine understanding of others' worldviews.
</p>

### SLIDE 7: The Seven Basic Questions

### SLIDE 8: Preunderstanding

## Coming to Grips with Your Preunderstanding

[POPUP_TRIGGER id="preunderstanding-def"]**Preunderstanding**[/POPUP_TRIGGER] = the understanding of reality with which one makes sense of new experiences

‚óÜ It's the background assumptions (correct or not) that shape how we interpret new texts, experiences, and ideas

‚óÜ **Everyone** has preunderstandings‚Äîthey come from our upbringing, culture, education, personal experiences

‚óÜ **Important:** Preunderstanding *changes over time* as we learn and grow

[POPUP id="preunderstanding-def"]
**Preunderstanding**

The set of assumptions, beliefs, and frameworks you bring to any new learning experience. Includes:
- Cultural background
- Religious upbringing (or lack thereof)
- Media representations
- Family attitudes
- Previous education

Preunderstanding is unavoidable but can be examined and revised through education.
[/POPUP]

**Why Does This Matter for Studying Religion?**

If you don't recognize your own preunderstandings, you'll unconsciously judge other traditions by the standards of your own background.

<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">

<h3 style="color: #2c5282; margin-top: 1.5em;">Why This Matters</h3>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">

### SLIDE 11: Krister Stendahl's Approach

## [POPUP_TRIGGER id="holy-envy-def"]Holy Envy[/POPUP_TRIGGER]: A Posture of Appreciative Learning

Rather than dismissing or attacking what we don't understand, we approach other traditions with **curiosity and respect**‚Äîeven "envying" (admiring) what's beautiful or profound in them.

[POPUP id="holy-envy-def"]
**Holy Envy**

A term coined by Swedish Lutheran bishop and scholar Krister Stendahl (1921-2008). It describes an approach to interfaith study where you:

**Krister Stendahl (1921-2008)**

### SLIDE 13: Practice vs. Belief

## An Important Distinction: What You DO vs. What You BELIEVE

Western education often emphasizes what people **believe** (doctrines, creeds, theological ideas).

But for many traditions, what matters most is what you **[KEY_TERM]do[/KEY_TERM]**‚Äîritual, ethics, community participation.

**Examples:**

‚óÜ **Orthodox Judaism:** Emphasizes [POPUP_TRIGGER id="halakha"]halakha[/POPUP_TRIGGER] (Jewish law/practice) over specific theological beliefs

‚óÜ **Confucianism:** Focuses on ritual propriety ([POPUP_TRIGGER id="li"]li[/POPUP_TRIGGER]) and ethical relationships

‚óÜ **Many Indigenous traditions:** Emphasize maintaining right relationships with land, ancestors, spirits through practice

Jewish law derived from Torah, Talmud, and rabbinic interpretation. Governs daily life including diet (kashrut), Sabbath observance, prayer, marriage, and business ethics. Literally means "the way to walk."

### SLIDE 15: Our Methodological Approach

## Methodological Neutrality in Practice

**In Religious Studies, we don't ask if beliefs are true or false. Instead, we:**

1. **Understand** what beliefs and practices mean to practitioners

2. **Describe** religions in their own terms

3. **Analyze** how religions function in individuals' lives and societies

**This is different from:**

‚ùå **Theology** (which operates from within a tradition)

‚ùå **Anti-religious polemics** (which seek to disprove or attack religion)

‚úÖ **We're scholars, not evangelists or debunkers.**

**Describe Before Explain:**

‚óÜ First, carefully observe and describe what people do and believe *in their own terms*

‚óÜ Then, analyze or explain using theoretical frameworks

‚óÜ Think of yourself as an anthropologist or journalist first, theorist second

</p>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  <strong>Analyze function</strong>: How do beliefs and practices work in people's lives? What social functions do they serve? What psychological needs do they address? You can analyze without judging. For instance, you can study how prayer functions to create community solidarity without asking "Do prayers get answered?"
</p>

<h3 style="color: #2c5282; margin-top: 1.5em;">Why This Matters</h3>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  Students sometimes worry: "But what if something is wrong or harmful?" Valid question. Here's the key: you can critique ideas while respecting people. Academic study means engaging critically but fairly. We can analyze power dynamics, contradictions, and ethical concerns without dismissing entire traditions wholesale. For example, we can study how <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriarchy" target="_blank">patriarchal structures</a> function in various religions‚Äîdescribing the theological justifications, analyzing the impacts on women's lives, examining resistance movements‚Äîwithout saying "Religion X is evil." We describe structures, analyze impacts, and let evidence guide conclusions.
</p>

<h3 style="color: #2c5282; margin-top: 1.5em;">üéì Scholars Weigh In</h3>
<h4 style="margin-top: 1em;">Clifford Geertz ‚Äì <em>The Interpretation of Cultures</em></h4>
<p style="margin-bottom: 1em;">
  Anthropologist Clifford Geertz (1926-2006) emphasized <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thick_description" target="_blank">"thick description"</a>‚Äîdescribing religious phenomena in rich detail, attending to context, meaning, and practitioners' own understandings before rushing to theoretical explanations. His approach treats religions as "webs of significance" that people spin for themselves, requiring patient, detailed observation to understand. Think of yourself as an anthropologist first (describing carefully) and theorist second (explaining patterns).
</p>

## KEY TERMS FOR DAY 1

**Students should know by end of Day 1:**

- **World religions:** Religions that have expanded beyond their region of origin (Note: constructed framework)
- **Worldview:** A "lens" ‚Äî set of beliefs, values, assumptions shaping how a person interprets reality
- **Preunderstanding:** Background assumptions that shape how we interpret new experiences; changes over time
- **Holy envy:** Posture of appreciative learning from other traditions (Krister Stendahl)
- **Methodological neutrality:** Academic approach that describes/analyzes without claiming truth/falsehood
- **Theology:** Faith seeking understanding; study from within a tradition
- **Religious Studies:** Academic study from outside perspective using social scientific methods
- **Halakha:** Jewish law governing daily life
- **Li:** Chinese ritual propriety/etiquette (Confucianism)
