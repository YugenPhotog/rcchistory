Defining Religion

Multiple perspectives, no single answer

Section transition. We've examined religion's building blocks. Now we shift to how major thinkers have attempted to DEFINE religion itself. Important frame: The definitions you're about to see aren't competing to be THE right answer. They're different lenses highlighting different aspects. Marx emphasizes economics/society. Freud emphasizes psychology. Durkheim emphasizes social cohesion. Otto emphasizes experience. Each captures something important while leaving something out.

Why Is Defining Religion So Hard?

  • Encompasses vast diversity (Buddhism vs. Christianity vs. indigenous traditions)
  • Some traditions don't use the concept "religion" (it's a Western category)
  • Boundaries are fuzzy (Is Confucianism religion or philosophy? Both?)
  • Different definitions serve different purposes (legal, academic, popular)

No single definition satisfies everyone—different definitions highlight different aspects.

We'll look at several major approaches...

Why is defining religion so challenging? Religion encompasses vast diversity—compare Buddhism (non-theistic, focused on ending suffering) with Christianity (theistic, focused on salvation). Some traditions don't even use the category "religion" (it's Western). Boundaries are fuzzy. And different definitions serve different purposes. Don't feel frustrated that there's no single definition. If brilliant scholars disagree after centuries, you're not expected to have THE answer on Day 2! The goal is understanding multiple approaches and learning when to use which.

“Religion… is the belief in Spiritual Beings.”

— Edward B. Tylor, Primitive Culture (1871)

Edward B. Tylor (1832–1917)

Why Tylor matters here: Tylor gives a classic “minimal definition” that is short, memorable, and easy to test. He’s one of the founders of anthropology of religion, and he wanted a definition broad enough to compare religions cross-culturally.

Definition type: Substantive (it defines religion by what it is—a certain kind of belief), not by what it does socially or psychologically.

Classroom stress test: This definition immediately raises useful questions: Does non-theistic Buddhism count? What about Confucianism? What about “spiritual but not religious”? What about civil religion?

Mini-bio: English anthropologist associated with early evolutionary theories of culture. Most famous for Primitive Culture (1871), where he argues that religion and culture can be studied comparatively as human phenomena.

“A religion is a unified system of beliefs and practices… which unite into one single moral community.”

— Émile Durkheim, French sociologist of religion (1915)

Émile Durkheim (1858–1917)

Major work: The Elementary Forms of Religious Life (1912).

Durkheim’s functional definition: Focuses on what religion does socially: it distinguishes sacred from ordinary life, organizes shared practices, and binds people into a moral community.

Key elements to emphasize:

  • Sacred things (set apart, protected, treated with special rules)
  • Beliefs + practices (not just ideas, but actions)
  • Moral community (“a Church” broadly construed—community, not necessarily a building)

Provocative claim: When communities worship gods, they are also (in a sense) venerating their own shared values and social order—religious symbols represent society’s moral authority.

Classroom bridge: Civil religion and national rituals can function “religiously” in Durkheim’s sense (flags, memorials, pledges, sacred days, taboos).

“What grows out of, and gives expression to, experience of the holy in its various aspects.”

— Rudolf Otto, German scholar of religion (1917)

Rudolf Otto (1869–1937)

Major work: The Idea of the Holy (1917).

Otto’s claim: Religion is rooted in encounters with the numinous—a distinctive kind of experience (mysterium tremendum et fascinans): awe + fear + fascination before “the wholly Other.”

Why Otto matters here: He resists reduction. Against Marx/Freud/Durkheim, Otto insists religion can’t be fully explained away as economics, psychology, or social cohesion—there’s an irreducible experiential core.

Pedagogy angle: Otto helps students distinguish substantive approaches (what religion IS) from functionalist approaches (what religion DOES).

“An illusion deriving its strength from the fact that it falls in with our instinctual desires.”

— Sigmund Freud (1927)

Sigmund Freud (1856–1939)

Major works on religion: Totem and Taboo (1913); The Future of an Illusion (1927); Moses and Monotheism (1939).

Freud’s critique (function): Religion meets deep psychological needs—security, meaning, protection from anxiety. His provocative claim is that belief often functions like an adult extension of childhood dependence.

“Illusion”: Not simply “false.” Freud means a belief energized by wish-fulfillment—its appeal comes from how well it fits our desires, not from evidence.

Takeaway question for students: “What emotional needs does this belief or ritual meet?” (Even if we reject specific Freudian mechanisms, the question can still be analytically useful.)

“Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world, and the soul of soulless conditions. It is the opium of the people.”

— Karl Marx, A Contribution to the Critique of Hegel’s Philosophy of Right (1843)

Karl Marx (1818–1883)

Context: Marx wasn’t defining what religion is so much as explaining why it persists and what function it serves under conditions of inequality.

Key idea: The “opium” metaphor is often flattened into “religion is bad.” In the full line, religion is also a genuine expression of suffering (“sigh of the oppressed”)—a comfort in an unjust world.

Functionalist angle: Like later social scientists, Marx pushes the question: “What does religion do socially?” Even without being Marxist, students can ask: “Who benefits?” “What social conditions sustain this?”

Mini-bio: German philosopher, economist, and revolutionary socialist. Co-authored The Communist Manifesto (1848). Author of Das Kapital (1867–1894).

“A set of symbolic forms and acts which relate a person to the ultimate conditions of his existence.”

— Robert Bellah (1964)

Robert Bellah (1927–2013)

Why students usually like this definition: It’s broad without being empty. It highlights symbols (myth, doctrine, images) and acts (ritual, practices), so it doesn’t privilege “belief” in a Christian-centric way.

“Ultimate conditions”: This is the killer phrase. It works for theistic traditions and non-theistic traditions: salvation, liberation from suffering, harmony, cosmic order, meaning in a godless universe—different “ultimates,” same analytical slot.

Connection note: “Ultimate” language matches your course framing: religion need not equal “God,” but it does point toward what a tradition treats as most real / most important / most binding.

Mini-bio: American sociologist of religion. Known for “civil religion” and broad, comparative approaches. Major works include Tokugawa Religion (1957), Habits of the Heart (1985), Religion in Human Evolution (2011).

How Your Textbook Defines Religion

Religion is a pattern of beliefs and practices that expresses and enacts what a community regards as sacred and/or ultimate about life.

Instructor Notes

Why this definition works: It’s broad enough to include theistic and non-theistic traditions, and it avoids reducing religion to “belief” alone by pairing beliefs with practices.

Two anchor terms to keep using:

  • Sacred: set apart, treated with special care, protected by taboos or rules (can be objects, times, spaces, texts, people, or ideals)
  • Ultimate: what a tradition treats as most real/important/binding (salvation, liberation, harmony, cosmic order, justice, etc.)

Teaching move: When students struggle with “Is X a religion?”, have them test it with this definition: Does it have (1) beliefs + practices, and (2) a community orientation toward something sacred and/or ultimate?

Beliefs: Sacred Narrative

Beliefs answer those existential questions, usually in the form of Myth

In Religious Studies, “myth” is a technical term meaning:

  • A foundational sacred story conveying a tradition’s core meanings
  • Stories about origins, destiny, divine beings, cosmic order
  • Often symbolic rather than literal history

Myth (Religious Studies)

Sacred narrative: a foundational story about origins, destiny, or cosmic meaning that conveys a tradition’s core teachings.

Examples: Genesis creation accounts, Buddhist Jataka tales, Hindu epics, indigenous creation stories.

Note: Many practitioners dislike the term because in everyday English “myth” implies falsehood.

×
Critical Clarification

In Religious Studies, calling something a “myth” is not the same as calling it a lie.

The term refers to a story’s status and function in a community: it is sacred, authoritative, and worldview-shaping.

A myth may be:

  • historically accurate
  • historically inaccurate
  • impossible to verify historically

What makes it “myth” in this academic sense is that it answers big questions and orients life toward what a community treats as sacred or ultimate.

Critical point: when we say “myth,” we do not mean “false story.” We mean sacred narrative: foundational stories that convey a tradition’s core meanings. “Myth” in academic usage is neutral. Genesis creation, Buddha’s enlightenment, Hindu epics: these are “myths” in academic terms. We are not declaring them false; we are identifying their sacred and meaning-making role. Be sensitive to how practitioners hear this term. Inside the academy, “myth” is standard. Outside, “sacred narrative” or “founding story” may reduce misunderstanding. Scholar note: Mircea Eliade emphasized that myths make the sacred past present again. When a community ritually recites a creation myth, they symbolically participate in the original sacred event.

Critical Clarification

⚠️ CRITICAL: Myth does not equal false

In everyday English, “myth” implies fiction. That’s not how we use it here.

A myth can be historically true, historically false, or unverifiable. What matters is that it is sacred and meaning-making.

Practices: Putting Belief Practice

Beliefs are embodied in Ritual:

  • actions that connect the individual and the community to the sacred reality; symbolic actions in worship, meditation, or other religious activities.
  • Patterned, repeated actions (not spontaneous)
  • Symbolic (actions mean more than their literal function)

What are some examples of rituals?

×
Ritual

Symbolic, patterned actions performed in religious contexts. Rituals follow prescribed forms, carry meaning beyond literal actions (bread becomes body, water purifies), and connect practitioners to sacred/ultimate reality.

Examples: prayer, meditation, pilgrimage, rites of passage, seasonal celebrations.

×
Puja (पूजा)

Hindu worship ritual involving offerings to a deity. May include: flowers, incense, food, water, light (oil lamps), and mantras. Can be performed at home or in temples. Creates connection between devotee and divine.

×
Salat (صلاة)

Islamic ritual prayer performed five times daily at prescribed times. Involves purification (wudu), physical postures (standing, bowing, prostrating), and recitation of Qur'anic verses. One of the Five Pillars of Islam.

Rituals are sacred ACTIONS—symbolic, patterned behaviors connecting individuals and communities to sacred reality. Key insight: rituals aren't "empty traditions" or "meaningless repetition." They do real psychological, social, and spiritual work. When Catholics consume Communion, they're not just eating—they're participating in Christ's body (transubstantiation) or remembering sacrifice (memorial). When Buddhists meditate in lotus position, they're embodying enlightenment. Functions of ritual: mark transitions, create community bonds, make abstract beliefs tangible, provide structure for encountering the sacred. Scholar: Catherine Bell emphasized that rituals create "ritualized bodies"—people learn religion not just through ideas but through physical practice. Often involve the reenactment of sacred time or space Experienced as having the power to make one an actual participant in the original event. Past and present dissolve into each other. Rituals are what ties the life of the individual into the great cosmic drama, serving the highest power that gives life meaning and purpose.

Worship: Prayer, liturgy, puja, salat

Meditation: Buddhist zazen, Hindu dhyana, contemplative prayer

Rites of Passage: Baptism, bar/bat mitzvah, vision quests, weddings

Seasonal: Passover, Ramadan, Diwali, Christmas

Ritual: Entering Sacred Time

  • Often involves the reenactment of sacred time or sacred space
  • Experienced as having the power to make the participant present in the original event
  • Past and present dissolve into each other
  • Ritual ties the life of the individual into a larger cosmic drama, connecting to what gives life meaning and purpose
This slide deepens the definition: rituals are not just repeated actions; they create sacred participation. Key teaching idea: - Ritual is a technology for time and meaning. - It collapses the ordinary timeline. - Participants experience “then” as “now.” Examples you can use: - Passover: not only remembering Exodus, but ritually re-entering it. - Eucharist/Communion: not merely symbolic in many traditions; participation in sacred reality. - Hajj: reenacting sacred geography tied to Abrahamic memory. - Buddhist rituals: sacred presence via disciplined bodily practice. Beliefs and Practices

  • Myth and ritual tie individuals to:
    • The Sacred
    • The Community
    • The Cosmos
  • This is accomplished through a complex of stories and ritual obligations that express a morality or sacred way of life.
This is the “big picture” slide: myth and ritual do not just communicate ideas; they bind people. Tie/bind is the key verb: - to the Sacred (ultimate reality, holy power, the divine, the numinous) - to the Community (shared identity, shared norms, shared memory) - to the Cosmos (a meaningful order: where we came from, where we are going, how life fits together) “Complex of stories and ritual obligations” means: beliefs + practices are packaged together as a way of life. Religion is not just what you think; it’s what you do, with others, in a world that has meaning. Transition: Next slide focuses on COMMUNITY as a core feature of religion.

Community

  • A belief system is always shared: its ideals and rituals are practiced by a group.
  • Religions attempt to pass down beliefs and practices from generation to generation.
    • Ethnic Religion: a religion that originated with and or appeals to a specific ethnicity (religious community and ethnicity are coterminous)
    • Universal Religion: a religion that has broken out of its ethnic origins and claims a truth that is universally applicable (religious community is multi-ethnic)
The key idea: religion is not just private belief. It is social and transmitted. Community does at least four jobs: 1) preserves the stories (myth) and practices (ritual) 2) teaches newcomers (children, converts) how to “do” the religion 3) enforces norms (what counts as sacred behavior vs taboo) 4) provides identity: a sense of “we” across time Ethnic vs Universal: - Ethnic religion: linked to a peoplehood (often tied to land, ancestry, language, shared history). The religion is one strand of the group’s identity. - Universal religion: explicitly missionary or expansive, able to travel across cultures. It claims the message applies to all humans, not only one ethnicity. Important nuance: Even universal religions develop local cultures, and even ethnic religions can have outsiders who join. The categories describe the dominant orientation, not an iron law. Transition idea for next slide (if you want it): “If religion is communal, how does it mark insiders and outsiders?”

Sacred and/or Ultimate

  • The textbook defines sacred as: “what is considered most holy and important, whether in this world, in a supernatural world that transcends this one, or both.”

Why “sacred” can be tricky

“Holy and important” is a start, but it can smuggle in assumptions (especially the idea that “sacred” always means a supernatural being).

Some traditions locate what is “ultimate” in:

  • an impersonal reality (e.g., Dharma, Dao, Brahman)
  • ethical order and harmony (e.g., Confucian moral cultivation)
  • practice and liberation rather than worship of a god (e.g., many forms of Buddhism)

That’s why we pair sacred with ultimate.

Talking points: - “Sacred” can mean set apart, protected, powerful, taboo, worthy of reverence. - “Ultimate” widens the lens beyond supernaturalism. - This sets up Otto as one pathway to “the sacred,” but not the only one. German theologian and philosopher of religion. Major work: The Idea of the Holy (1917).

Slide 1 is the conceptual claim. Otto is not defining religion by beliefs or institutions. He’s saying: the sacred begins as experience. Sacred vs. Ultimate

Sacred

  • Most holy, set apart
  • Treated with reverence, awe, special care
  • Objects (holy books), places (Mecca), times (Sabbath), beings (God, ancestors)

Durkheim: "Things set apart and forbidden"

Ultimate

  • Broadens concept beyond "holy"
  • Works for non-theistic traditions
  • Examples: secular humanism, some Buddhism, Confucianism

Concerns: ultimate reality, ultimate values, ultimate meaning

Why both terms matter: "Sacred" works well for theistic traditions; "Ultimate" helps us analyze non-theistic or philosophical traditions without imposing Western categories.

×
Émile Durkheim (1858-1917)

French sociologist, founder of sociology of religion. Major work: The Elementary Forms of Religious Life (1912).

Argued religion creates social cohesion by uniting people around shared sacred symbols. In Durkheim's theory, when we worship God, we're really worshiping society itself.

This distinction is about intellectual precision and respect. Sacred: From Durkheim—things "set apart and forbidden," treated with special reverence. The Qur'an is sacred (never placed on floor), the Sabbath is sacred (set apart for rest), Jerusalem is sacred (a holy place). Ultimate: But what about traditions that don't emphasize "the sacred" the same way? Some Buddhism doesn't worship a creator God. Confucianism is more about ethics than encountering the holy. Secular humanism has no sacred texts or places. For these, "ultimate" is more precise—ultimate reality, ultimate values, ultimate meaning. Having both terms lets us analyze any tradition on its own terms. When studying Islam, talk about the sacred. When studying Zen Buddhism, talk about ultimate reality.

Re-Defining Religion

  • religion is often traced to the ancient Latin word religio (ree-LIG-ee-oh), from the verb religare, meaning “to bind” or “to tie fast.”
  • It can mean a constraint on behavior: doing what is good and avoiding what is evil.
  • Religio can also mean a way of life that binds people together in a group and orients them to the sacred.
  • It can entail holy awe: mysterium tremendum et fascinans toward the gods or sacred power.
This is your conceptual pivot slide: religion as binding. Use it to connect earlier material: - Myth and ritual bind individuals to the sacred, the community, and the cosmos. - “Religion” as religare gives a memorable etymological hook for that same idea. Suggested closing line: “So, religion is not just a list of beliefs. It’s a binding system of meaning and practice.” Next step (if you want): Have students test the definition on a non-obvious example (sports fandom, nationalism, vegan ethics, etc.) to clarify boundaries: what counts as sacred/ultimate, and what doesn’t.

A Better Definition of Religion?

“Religion is a communal way of life that binds people, through sacred stories and ritual practices, to what they take to be sacred and/or ultimate, shaping moral order and often evoking experiences of holy awe (the numinous).”

Use this as the capstone definition slide. It deliberately synthesizes: - beliefs + practices (myth + ritual) - community transmission - religio/religare = binding/tie-fast - sacred and/or ultimate (cross-tradition) - Otto’s numinous (holy awe, Wholly Other)
