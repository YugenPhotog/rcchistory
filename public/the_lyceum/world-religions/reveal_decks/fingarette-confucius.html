The Puzzle

Question students often ask:

"Is Confucianism a religion or a philosophy?"

Fingarette's answer:

That question assumes a Western template.

This is the central puzzle we're addressing today: How should we categorize Confucianism? Explain the common Western assumption: In many Western contexts, "religion" is often defined around: - Belief in a supernatural realm - A creator God or gods - Doctrines of salvation or afterlife - Institutional clergy and houses of worship - Faith as primary religious activity Confucianism doesn't fit this mold neatly: - No personal creator deity at the center - Focus on this-worldly ethics rather than salvation - Emphasis on practice over belief - No priestly class mediating between humans and the divine Fingarette's move: Instead of forcing Confucianism into Western religious categories, he suggests we expand our understanding of what "religious" can mean. Confucianism can be "religious" in a different way‚Äîthrough practice that generates sacred meaning. Discussion prompt: Why do we tend to use Western categories as the default when analyzing non-Western traditions?

Confucius shows how the sacred can be enacted rather than believed.

  • No gods required for "religious" meaning
  • Sacredness can arise within disciplined human practice

Herbert Fingarette (1921-2018) was an American philosopher who specialized in ethics, philosophy of mind, and Chinese philosophy. His book "Confucius: The Secular as Sacred" (1972) is a slim volume‚Äîonly about 80 pages‚Äîbut it had enormous impact on Western Confucian scholarship. Keep biographical details minimal; focus on the interpretive shift Fingarette introduced: Before Fingarette: Western scholars often saw Confucius as either: 1. A secular ethicist with no real religious dimension, OR 2. Someone whose ritual practices were mere convention or social control After Fingarette: Recognition that ritual practice itself can be the locus of sacred meaning, without requiring supernatural beliefs. Stress: Fingarette is not merely saying "Confucius is secular" or "Confucius has no religious dimension." Instead, he's arguing that "the secular can become sacred" through proper ritual practice. Key insight: We don't need to choose between "religion" and "philosophy" as if they're mutually exclusive categories. Confucius offers a third option: sacred practice grounded in human relationships. Teaching tip: Point out that Fingarette wrote this during the early 1970s when American interest in Asian philosophies was growing, but understanding was still limited by Western assumptions.

Li (Á¶Æ) ‚Äî Ritual Propriety

Patterned ways of acting that express and create proper relationships. Li includes formal ceremonies (weddings, funerals) but also everyday acts like greeting elders, serving guests, or maintaining proper posture. The form matters because it shapes both the actor and the social reality.

This slide introduces Fingarette's central interpretive move: understanding li (ritual) as the key to Confucian "religiousness." Define li in accessible terms: Patterned ways of acting that express and create proper relationships. This includes: - Formal ceremonies (weddings, funerals, ancestor rites) - Semi-formal occasions (hosting guests, official meetings) - Everyday interactions (how you greet someone, how you defer to elders) Clarify what "sacred" means in this context: - NOT "supernatural" or "otherworldly" - RATHER "reverence-worthy," "meaning-making," "reality-shaping" - Sacred = that which deserves our serious attention and proper performance Key insight: The act becomes sacred not because it connects us to gods or spirits (though it might also do that), but because when done properly‚Äîwith both correct form AND sincere intention‚Äîit creates and maintains meaningful human relationships. Two requirements: 1. Sincerity (inner attitude of genuine care and attention) 2. Recognition (shared understanding that this act means what it means) Without sincerity, ritual becomes empty formalism. Without recognition, ritual loses its social effectiveness. Example to share: Think of a graduation ceremony. The physical acts (walking across stage, receiving diploma, moving tassel) are simple. But when performed with proper solemnity and shared recognition, they actually constitute someone as a graduate. That's the "magic" of ritual. Teaching tip: Ask students to think of a time when they participated in a meaningful ritual. What made it meaningful? The physical actions, the shared understanding, or both?

This is Fingarette's most famous example, and it's pedagogically brilliant because it's so accessible. Emphasize the contrast between two ways of describing the SAME physical event: Physical description: If an alien or a scientist with no cultural knowledge observed a handshake, they'd see muscles contracting, hands moving, physical contact. Nothing about that physical description explains why handshakes matter. Ritual description: When we understand a handshake AS A HANDSHAKE, we recognize it as a culturally meaningful act. But note: it doesn't have just ONE meaning. Context matters: - After an interview: "We have an agreement" - After an argument: "We're reconciling" - At a funeral: "I share your grief" - Meeting someone new: "I acknowledge you as a social equal" Key insight: The meaning is not hiding inside the physical act, waiting to be discovered. The meaning is CREATED by the shared recognition that "this is a handshake" and "handshakes mean X in this context." Emphasize shared recognition: A handshake only "works" because both parties understand the act as that kind of act. If one person thinks they're shaking hands in agreement and the other thinks it's just a greeting, the ritual fails to do its work. Variants to discuss: - Handshake vs. bow vs. fist bump vs. high-five - Same physical space, different ritual meanings - Refusing to shake someone's hand = powerful statement precisely because we all know what handshake means Connect to Confucius: This is the basic structure of ALL ritual action in Confucianism. The physical acts of li (bowing, serving, speaking with deference) create and maintain social reality through shared recognition. Teaching tip: Demonstrate by extending your hand to a student volunteer. Ask: "What am I doing?" They'll say "offering to shake hands." Then ask: "How do you know? Did I say anything?" Point out the immediate social recognition.

Scaling Up: Confucius Applies This to Social Life

For Confucius, many actions can function as li:

  • Greeting elders properly
  • Serving parents
  • Hosting guests
  • Mourning the dead
  • Wedding ceremonies
  • Funeral rites
  • Proper posture
  • Respectful speech

Everyday life becomes the arena of moral cultivation.

Now we scale up from the handshake example to see how Fingarette reads Confucius. Point out: Confucianism doesn't reserve "the sacred" for: - A weekly worship service - A special sacred space (like a temple or church) - Extraordinary mystical experiences - Moments of private prayer or meditation Instead, sacredness is distributed across daily life through repeated, disciplined practice of li. Explain each category: Family rituals: How you greet your parents in the morning, how you serve them at dinner, how you defer to their authority‚Äîthese aren't just practical acts. When done properly, they constitute and maintain the parent-child relationship itself. Social rituals: How you welcome guests, how you introduce people to each other, how you show respect to teachers or elders‚Äîthese acts create and maintain social hierarchy and harmony. Life-cycle rituals: Weddings don't just celebrate marriage, they constitute it. Funerals don't just acknowledge death, they transform the deceased into an ancestor. Properly performed ritual actually changes social reality. Embodied rituals: Even posture and tone of voice matter. How you hold your body when addressing an elder, how you modulate your voice in different contexts‚Äîthese are all part of li. The key Confucian insight: You don't become virtuous by thinking the right thoughts in private, then expressing those thoughts in public. Rather, you become virtuous by repeatedly practicing the right forms, which gradually train your inner disposition. Fingarette's contribution: Helping Western readers see that this isn't "empty formalism" or "mere etiquette." It's a sophisticated understanding of how meaning, virtue, and social reality are constructed through shared practice. Discussion prompt: In American culture, we tend to value "authenticity" and "being yourself." How might a Confucian respond to that ideal? Is there a tension between ritual and authenticity?

Fingarette's Provocative Term: "Magic"

Fingarette describes li as having "magic" efficacy:

When ritual is done correctly, social reality changes:

  • A wedding constitutes a married couple
  • A funeral constitutes an ancestor relationship
  • A bow constitutes respect and role-recognition

No gods intervene‚Äîyet real transformation occurs.

This slide introduces Fingarette's most controversial and memorable claim: that ritual has "magical" power. CRITICAL CLARIFICATION - What "magic" does NOT mean: - Not paranormal forces - Not supernatural intervention - Not violation of natural laws - Not mystical or occult practices What "magic" DOES mean - Performative power: - Actions that bring social facts into being - Words and deeds that change reality through shared recognition - Transformation that occurs through the act itself, not through some external force Philosophers call these "performative utterances" or "speech acts": - "I now pronounce you married" ‚Äî if said by the right person in the right context, it MAKES you married - "I promise to..." ‚Äî the act of saying it creates the obligation - "I apologize" ‚Äî when sincere, it accomplishes reconciliation Fingarette's point: Confucian ritual works the same way. When performed correctly (with proper form and sincere intention), li literally creates and maintains social reality. Examples to emphasize: Wedding: Before the ceremony, two individuals. After the ceremony, a married couple with new rights, responsibilities, and social standing. What happened? Not just an announcement of pre-existing fact. The ritual MADE them married. Funeral: The deceased becomes an ancestor, changing from a corpse to an ongoing presence in family life, worthy of offerings and respect. The ritual accomplishes this transformation. Bow: When you bow to an elder, you're not just expressing pre-existing respect. You're CONSTITUTING the relationship of elder and junior, superior and subordinate. The act creates the reality. Key insight: This is why getting the form right matters so much. If the ritual is performed incorrectly or insincerely, it doesn't accomplish its transformative work. It's not that gods punish you‚Äîit's that the social reality simply doesn't get created. Compare to: Saying "checkmate" in chess. The word doesn't just describe the board state; in the right context, it changes the game's status. That's the kind of "magic" Fingarette means. Teaching tip: Ask students if they've ever been in a situation where ritual went wrong (awkward handshake, forgot someone's name at introduction, inappropriate laughter at funeral). What happened? The social reality didn't get properly established, and everyone felt the failure.

Not Empty Formalism: Why Form Matters

Common Western Suspicion

"Ritual is fake; authenticity is inner feeling."

Assumes form and sincerity are opponents

Confucian Reply

  • Form can educate feeling
  • Ritual trains attention and sincerity
  • Virtue becomes reliable through repeated practice

Analogy: Musicianship or athletics‚Äîdiscipline and form do not eliminate authenticity; they enable it.

üì∏ Image needed: "musician practicing instrument scales technique"
Suggested caption: Like musical practice, ritual discipline enables rather than opposes genuine expression
This slide addresses a major Western objection to Confucian emphasis on ritual form. Explain the common Western suspicion (especially in modern American culture): - "Just be yourself" - "What matters is what's in your heart" - "Ritual is empty if you don't really mean it" - "Formal behavior is fake; casual behavior is authentic" This view assumes sincerity and form are OPPONENTS: - Inner feeling = real, authentic, true self - Outer form = fake, artificial, imposed by society - Therefore: maximize spontaneity, minimize ritual Confucian reply (as Fingarette reads it): 1. Form can educate feeling: You don't wait until you spontaneously feel respectful toward elders, THEN bow. Rather, you practice bowing, and over time, the feeling of respect develops. The form trains the inner disposition. 2. Ritual trains attention: Performing ritual carefully makes you notice what you're doing, whom you're doing it with, and why it matters. This cultivates mindfulness and sincerity. 3. Virtue becomes reliable: If virtue depended on spontaneous feeling, it would be unpredictable. Some days you'd feel generous, other days stingy. Ritual practice makes virtue stable and reliable. Make the key point: Sincerity and form are not opponents‚Äîthey're partners. Neither alone is sufficient. You need both: - Form without sincerity = empty, mechanical, ineffective - Sincerity without form = unguided, inconsistent, socially ineffective Use the musicianship analogy: - Beginner pianist: Playing scales feels mechanical, not "authentic" - Advanced pianist: Technical mastery enables genuine musical expression - Same with ritual: Initial practice feels artificial, but mastery enables genuine virtue Athletic analogy: - Athlete must practice proper form (shooting technique, swimming stroke) - Through repetition, proper form becomes "second nature" - Peak performance combines technical precision with spontaneous flow Confucian cultivation works the same way: - Begin with careful attention to ritual form (even if it feels awkward) - Through practice, form shapes inner character - Eventually, proper action becomes natural, spontaneous, sincere Address potential objection: "But what if someone goes through the motions without meaning it?" Confucian response: That's a failure of practice, not a failure of the method. Just as a musician who practices carelessly won't improve, someone who performs ritual carelessly won't cultivate virtue. The practice must be sincere, but sincerity is itself cultivated through practice‚Äînot a prerequisite for it. Teaching tip: Ask students about learning any skill (musical instrument, sport, foreign language). Did mastery feel "authentic" at first? When did it start feeling natural? How did discipline relate to spontaneity?

Confucian "Religiousness" Without Western Religion

Not "Religion" in Narrow Western Sense

  • No creator deity at center
  • No salvation doctrine
  • No priestly mediation
  • No transcendent realm

"Religious" in Broader Sense

  • Ultimate meaning through practice
  • Reverence and seriousness
  • Self-transformation
  • Sacred bonds within community

Fingarette expands categories rather than forcing Confucius into an Abrahamic mold.

This slide presents Fingarette's central contribution: helping us see how Confucianism can be "religious" without conforming to Western religious templates. Left column - What Confucianism lacks (from narrow Western perspective): 1. No creator deity at center: Confucius rarely discusses Tian (Heaven), and when he does, it's more as a moral order than a personal God. No creation stories, no divine commandments, no personal relationship with deity. 2. No salvation doctrine: No concern with afterlife, no path to heaven or enlightenment, no escape from suffering or sin. Focus is on this-worldly flourishing. 3. No priestly mediation: No special class of people who have unique access to the sacred. Everyone participates directly in ritual. Scholars interpret texts, but don't mediate divine grace. 4. No transcendent realm: The sacred is found here, in human relationships, not in a separate supernatural world. Right column - What Confucianism has (broader understanding of "religious"): 1. Ultimate meaning through practice: Li provides comprehensive meaning structure. Properly performed ritual gives life depth, purpose, and significance. Not peripheral to life, but constitutive of it. 2. Reverence and seriousness: Confucian practice requires careful attention, proper attitude, sincere engagement. This is the tone of religious devotion, even without supernatural beliefs. 3. Self-transformation: Not just following rules, but becoming a different kind of person. The junzi (exemplary person) represents a transformed self through cultivation. This is parallel to religious ideas of spiritual development. 4. Sacred bonds within community: Family relationships, teacher-student bonds, friendships‚Äîthese aren't just social conveniences. When properly cultivated through li, they become sacred bonds that connect individuals to something greater than themselves. Provide a bridging definition of "religious": - NOT just "belief in supernatural beings" - RATHER: "practices that structure ultimate meaning, cultivate reverence, and transform the self" By this broader definition, Confucianism is thoroughly religious, even though it doesn't look like Christianity, Judaism, or Islam. Emphasize Fingarette's contribution: He's not saying "Confucianism isn't really religious" or "we should reclassify it as philosophy." Rather, he's saying "we need to expand our understanding of what religious can mean." This matters for religious studies as a discipline: If we only recognize as "religious" those traditions that look like Western monotheism, we miss the diverse ways humans create sacred meaning. Compare to how we might define "art": If we only counted Renaissance paintings as "art," we'd miss sculpture, photography, film, performance, installation. Similarly, if we only recognize Western-style religion, we miss other forms of sacred meaning-making. Teaching tip: Ask students to propose other definitions of "religion" that might be broader than "belief in god(s)." What elements seem essential? What elements are culturally specific?

Immanent vs. Transcendent

Immanent: Present within the material world, inherent in nature and human relationships. Sacred meaning arises from proper engagement with this world.

Transcendent: Beyond or outside the material world, existing in a separate supernatural realm. Sacred meaning comes from connecting to something beyond this world.

Confucianism emphasizes immanent sacredness‚Äîfinding the holy in human relationships rather than in a transcendent God.

This slide introduces the philosophical concept of immanence vs. transcendence‚Äîcrucial for understanding Fingarette's interpretation. Keep "immanent" simple: "Here, in this world, within ordinary human relationships." Explain the contrast: TRANSCENDENT SACREDNESS (common in Western monotheism): - God exists in a realm beyond the physical world - Sacred meaning comes from connecting to this transcendent reality - Prayer, worship, mystical experience reach "up" or "out" to the divine - This world is often seen as fallen, corrupt, or merely preparatory - Ultimate fulfillment comes in heaven/afterlife, not here IMMANENT SACREDNESS (Confucian, as Fingarette reads it): - Sacred meaning arises from properly cultivated human relationships - No need to transcend this world to find holiness - Ritual action here and now creates sacred reality - This world, properly ordered, IS the locus of ultimate meaning - Fulfillment comes through social harmony and virtue, not escape from world Tie back to earlier course themes: - Relational ethics: You become virtuous through relationships, not in isolation - Role-based responsibility: Your identity comes from your place in social networks - Cultivation over time: Virtue is built through repeated practice in daily life Fingarette's key insight: The phrase "secular as sacred" captures this perfectly. What we might dismiss as merely "secular" (ordinary social interactions, daily routines, family relationships) is actually the domain of the sacred when properly approached. This doesn't mean Confucius denies anything beyond human society. References to Tian (Heaven), ancestors, and spirits do appear. But the emphasis is on how sacredness manifests in human relationships, not on a transcendent realm separate from our social world. Compare to other traditions: - Buddhism: Also emphasizes practice in this world, but often with goal of transcending it (nirvana) - Christianity: This world matters, but ultimate meaning comes from relationship with transcendent God - Confucianism: This world, properly ordered through ritual, IS where ultimate meaning is realized Example to share: Imagine two ways of thinking about family dinner: 1. Transcendent view: "Mealtime is when we pause from worldly concerns to give thanks to God who transcends this meal" 2. Immanent view: "Mealtime, when done right (with proper seating, serving order, conversation), IS sacred. The meal itself, properly performed, constitutes family bonds and embodies the Way." Fingarette is arguing Confucius takes the second view. Teaching tip: Ask students whether they find one view more appealing than the other. Does anything feel lost in the immanent view? What might be gained?

Criticisms & Limits

Important for a final lecture to acknowledge scholarly debate

Common critiques of Fingarette:

  • May underplay transcendent elements (Tian, spirits, sacrifice language)
  • May overemphasize ritual form vs inner moral psychology
  • Focuses on elite philosophical Confucianism more than popular practice

Balanced takeaway: Fingarette is most convincing as a re-centering of li, not a denial of all transcendence.

Tian (Â§©) ‚Äî Heaven

An important concept in Confucian thought, often translated as "Heaven." Can mean: (1) the sky/cosmos, (2) a moral order governing the universe, (3) a quasi-personal force that responds to human virtue, or (4) fate/destiny. Confucius refers to Tian but doesn't elaborate on its nature, leaving room for various interpretations‚Äîfrom naturalistic to more theistic readings.

Keep this section brief but clear: scholarly interpretations disagree, and Fingarette's reading isn't the final word. It's pedagogically important to show students that: 1. Scholars debate interpretations 2. No single reading exhausts a complex tradition 3. Criticisms don't invalidate insights‚Äîthey refine them Critique #1: May underplay transcendent elements Evidence: The Analects does mention Tian (Heaven), spirits (shen), and sacrificial rites. Some passages suggest Confucius believed Heaven had moral awareness and responded to virtue. Later Confucian thinkers (Mencius, Xunzi) developed more elaborate metaphysics. Fingarette's response (implicit): He's not denying these elements exist in the texts. Rather, he's arguing they're not the center of Confucius's ethical program. Even if Heaven exists, the path to virtue runs through human ritual practice, not through worship or prayer to Heaven. Scholarly debate: Some argue Fingarette reads Confucius too much through Western philosophy of language (Austin, Searle) and speech act theory, potentially missing indigenous Chinese metaphysics. Critique #2: May overemphasize ritual form vs inner psychology Evidence: The Analects also emphasizes inner states like ren (humaneness), yi (righteousness), xin (trustworthiness). Later Confucians (especially Mencius) focus heavily on inner moral psychology‚Äîcultivation of innate good tendencies. Fingarette's response: He's not denying inner states matter. His point is that inner and outer develop together‚Äînot that outer form is all that matters. Critique #3: Elite philosophical vs popular practice Evidence: Fingarette reads the Analects, which represents elite philosophical reflection. Popular Chinese religion has always included ancestor spirits, fortune/misfortune from supernatural forces, divination, etc. Most practitioners would not recognize themselves in Fingarette's purely humanistic reading. Fingarette's response: He's interpreting Confucius's thought as recorded in the Analects, not describing all of Chinese religious practice. These are related but distinct projects. BALANCED TAKEAWAY for students: Fingarette's interpretation is most valuable as: - A corrective to purely secular readings that miss the sacred dimension - A way to expand Western categories of "religion" - A highlighting of ritual's central role in Confucian ethics But it shouldn't be taken as: - The only legitimate reading of Confucius - A complete description of Chinese religious practice - A claim that Confucianism has no transcendent elements at all The pedagogical value remains: Students learn that sacred meaning can be enacted socially and immanently, not only believed supernaturally and transcendently. Teaching tip: Remind students that scholarly debates aren't about "who's right," but about which interpretive lens is most illuminating for which purposes. Fingarette gives us one powerful lens, but not the only one.

  • Ethics and religion are separate domains
  • Ritual is the opposite of sincerity

The sacred can be something we do‚Äîtogether‚Äîthrough disciplined, reverent practice.

Deliver this as the "closing thesis" that ties together the entire unit and today's lecture. Three challenges Confucianism poses to common Western assumptions: 1. "Religion = belief in supernatural realm" Common assumption: For something to count as "religion," it must involve: - Belief in gods, spirits, or supernatural forces - Doctrines about afterlife, salvation, or transcendent reality - Faith as the central religious act Confucian challenge: Religion can center on practice that creates sacred meaning in THIS world, among humans, through ritual. Supernatural beliefs may be present, but they're not the defining feature. Implication for students: When studying world religions, don't assume Western Christian template is universal. "Ethics and religion are separate domains" Common assumption (especially modern Western): - Religion = worship, prayer, belief in God, church attendance - Ethics = secular moral reasoning, rules for living, social norms - You can be ethical without being religious - You can be religious without being especially ethical Confucian challenge: In Confucianism, ethics IS religion. There's no separation. Becoming virtuous through ritual practice IS the religious life. No additional "religious" layer on top of ethics. Implication: In some traditions, the separation we take for granted doesn't exist. Moral cultivation is spiritual cultivation. 3. "Ritual is the opposite of sincerity" Common assumption (especially in American expressive individualism): - Authenticity = spontaneous, from the heart, unrehearsed - Ritual = scripted, artificial, imposed from outside - To be genuine, you must act spontaneously, not ritually Confucian challenge: Ritual practice cultivates sincerity. Form and feeling develop together. Disciplined practice makes virtue reliable and authentic. Implication: Authenticity isn't always about spontaneity. Sometimes discipline and form enable the deepest authenticity. This is what you want students to remember from the Confucianism unit: A sophisticated tradition that creates sacred meaning through disciplined social practice, challenging many Western assumptions about what religion must be. Teaching tip: Emphasize that understanding Confucianism doesn't require agreeing with it or adopting it. The goal is to appreciate its internal logic and see how it expands our categories.
