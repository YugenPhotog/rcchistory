World Religions Paradigm (WRP)

A framework developed in 1960s Britain for organizing religious studies education. Critics (Owen 2011, Cotter & Robertson 2016) note it:

  • Models all religions on Protestant Christianity
  • Creates artificial boundaries between traditions
  • Privileges certain traditions while marginalizing others
  • Reflects colonial assumptions

We use it pragmatically while remaining aware of its limitations.

We're using a framework while critiquing it‚Äîa sophisticated scholarly move. Like a GPS: useful for navigation but simplifies reality. Key limitations: - Makes "Hinduism" or "Buddhism" look unified when they're incredibly diverse - Protestant emphasis on belief over practice doesn't apply to all traditions - Marginalizes indigenous religions, "folk" practices, hybrid traditions Scholar: Cotter & Robertson (2016) argue the framework reflects colonial-era assumptions about "real" vs "primitive" religions.

Worldview and Preunderstanding

Understanding the lenses we use

Transition slide. We're shifting from the framework we'll use (world religions paradigm) to examining the lenses through which we see the world. Before you can fairly study other people's worldviews, you need to understand your own. Like examining the glasses you've been wearing your whole life.

Worldview = Your Lens on Reality

A worldview is essentially a lens through which you interpret and interact with the world.

  • Shaped by culture, upbringing, experiences
  • Encompasses beliefs, values, and assumptions
  • No one has a "neutral" or "objective" view

Worldview

A comprehensive framework of beliefs, values, and assumptions through which an individual interprets all of reality. Includes views on:

  • What exists (ontology)
  • How we know (epistemology)
  • What's right/wrong (ethics)
  • What it means to be human (anthropology)
  • Origins and purpose (cosmology)

Everyone has a worldview, whether consciously articulated or not.

The lens metaphor is powerful: just as glasses shape what you see, your worldview shapes how you interpret everything. Everyone has a worldview‚Äîyou can't NOT have one. You can only become more aware of what yours looks like. A scientist and a mystic watching the same sunset interpret it through completely different frameworks. Neither is "view from nowhere." Scholar: James W. Sire (The Universe Next Door) argues most people's worldviews are largely unconscious‚Äîoperating in the background without awareness.

Every Worldview Answers These Questions

1. What is ultimate reality? (God, gods, cosmic principles, emptiness?)

2. What is the origin and nature of the universe?

3. What is the origin and nature of humanity?

4. Does human life have a purpose?

5. How do humans acquire knowledge?

6. What is the basis of ethics and morality?

7. What happens to humans after death?

Over the semester, we'll see how various traditions have historically answered these questions!

These seven questions come from worldview analysis. Notice Question 1 is carefully phrased: "ultimate reality" rather than assuming God‚Äîthis allows for traditions with non-theistic answers like Brahman (Hinduism), ≈ö≈´nyatƒÅ/emptiness (Buddhism), or the Dao (Daoism). A Christian might answer Q1 "God"; a Buddhist "emptiness and interdependence"; a scientific materialist "matter and energy governed by natural laws." Key insight: YOUR answers to these questions shape how you'll initially perceive other traditions. If you're a materialist, you might struggle with mystical experiences. Recognizing your own answers is the first step.

Coming to Grips with Your Preunderstanding

Preunderstanding = the understanding of reality with which one makes sense of new experiences

  • Background assumptions (correct or not) that shape interpretation
  • Everyone has preunderstandings‚Äîfrom upbringing, culture, education
  • Preunderstanding changes over time as we learn and grow

Why does this matter?
If you don't recognize your preunderstandings, you'll unconsciously judge other traditions by your own standards.

×
Preunderstanding

The set of assumptions, beliefs, and frameworks you bring to any new learning experience. Includes:

  • Cultural background
  • Religious upbringing (or lack thereof)
  • Media representations
  • Family attitudes
  • Previous education

Preunderstanding is unavoidable but can be examined and revised through education.

Preunderstanding is your starting point‚Äîassumptions you bring before learning begins. Like wearing glasses: you can't see without them, but you need to know you're wearing them. Example: If you grew up Christian, you might assume "religion" means having a personal relationship with God. But Buddhism often focuses on practice over belief and doesn't worship a personal God‚Äîthose assumptions won't fit. Good news: preunderstanding changes. As you learn this semester, your assumptions will shift. Scholar: Hans-Georg Gadamer emphasized all understanding begins with preunderstanding. Rather than eliminating assumptions (impossible), bring them into dialogue with what you're studying.

Recognizing Your Own Preunderstanding

Take 2 minutes to reflect silently:

1. What religious or spiritual tradition (if any) did you grow up with?

2. What assumptions about "religion" might come from that background?

3. What have you heard (from family, media, friends) about traditions different from your own?

You don't need to share‚Äîthis is for your own awareness.

This reflection helps students get specific about their preunderstanding. The goal isn't to share but to honestly identify concrete assumptions. By naming assumptions explicitly, students take the first step toward temporarily setting them aside when studying other traditions. Example: If you grew up hearing "Hinduism worships many gods and that's wrong," you can recognize that as a preunderstanding shaped by monotheistic assumptions‚Äînot objective truth. Then you can ask "What does multiplicity of divine forms mean to practitioners?"

Holy Envy

Our Class Posture

üí¨ Discussion Prompt:

ASSESSMENT BREAK - Slide 10 of approximately 16 Before defining "holy envy" abstractly, students experience it concretely: thinking about how THEY would want their own tradition approached. The Golden Rule applies to religious studies: approach other traditions the way you'd want yours approached. If someone studied your tradition only through scandals without understanding the beauty, you'd say they're missing the point.

Holy Envy: Appreciative Learning

Rather than dismissing what we don't understand, we approach other traditions with curiosity and respect.

Krister Stendahl's Three Rules:

  • Compare best with best ‚Äî not your best with their worst
  • Compare only equals ‚Äî insiders with insiders, outsiders with outsiders
  • Leave room for holy envy ‚Äî find something to appreciate

Holy Envy

A term coined by Swedish Lutheran bishop Krister Stendahl (1921-2008). An approach to interfaith study where you:

  • Appreciate what's admirable in other traditions
  • Learn from their insights and practices
  • Remain open to being enriched by difference

Without necessarily converting or abandoning your own commitments.

×
Krister Stendahl (1921-2008)

Swedish Lutheran bishop, New Testament scholar, and Harvard Divinity School dean. Pioneer in Jewish-Christian dialogue. Developed "holy envy" to promote respectful interfaith learning.

His approach emphasizes letting each tradition define itself rather than imposing outsider categories.

Holy envy is the core posture for the entire semester. Rule 1: Don't compare the Sermon on the Mount with ISIS beheadings. Compare ideals with ideals, practices with practices. Rule 2: Compare insider accounts with insider accounts. Let practitioners speak for themselves. Rule 3: Find something to genuinely appreciate. A Christian can appreciate Buddhist meditation. An atheist can admire Islamic architecture. This doesn't require conversion‚Äîjust generosity. Holy envy creates conditions for genuine learning. When you're defensive, you can't really hear what another tradition is saying.

A Note on Labels

Many religious labels were created by outsiders, not practitioners themselves:

  • "Hinduism" ‚Äî British colonial term. Practitioners use Sanatana Dharma or identify by tradition (Shaiva, Vaishnava)
  • "Buddhism" ‚Äî Western term. Practitioners say Buddhadharma or identify by lineage
  • "Shinto" ‚Äî Modern term to distinguish "native" Japanese practices from Buddhism

Labels shape understanding. Outsider categories can obscure internal diversity.

Devotees of Shiva, one of Hinduism's major deities. Shaivism emphasizes asceticism, meditation, and philosophical inquiry. Includes traditions like Kashmir Shaivism and devotional bhakti movements.

Vaishnava

Devotees of Vishnu and his avatars (especially Krishna and Rama). Vaishnavism emphasizes devotion (bhakti), scripture study, and ethical living. Largest Hindu denomination globally.

Critical awareness: many labels we use were created by outsiders‚ÄîWestern scholars, missionaries, colonial administrators‚Äînot practitioners themselves. "Hinduism": British colonial officials created an umbrella term for diverse practices in India. Many practitioners identify with Sanatana Dharma (eternal law/truth) or their specific tradition. "Buddhism": Western term. Practitioners would say Buddhadharma or identify by lineage (Theravada, Mahayana, Vajrayana). We'll use these labels pragmatically but note their limitations throughout the semester. Scholar: Jonathan Z. Smith argued "religion" itself is a scholarly construct. Classifications always reflect the perspectives of those doing the classifying.

What You DO vs. What You BELIEVE

Western education often emphasizes beliefs (doctrines, creeds). But for many traditions, what matters most is what you do.

Practice-Focused

  • Orthodox Judaism: Halakha (law/practice)
  • Confucianism: Li (ritual propriety)
  • Indigenous traditions: Right relationships through practice

Belief-Focused

  • Protestant Christianity: Personal faith, doctrine
  • Emphasis on creeds and confessions
  • "What do you believe?"

Jewish law derived from Torah, Talmud, and rabbinic interpretation. Governs daily life including diet (kashrut), Sabbath observance, prayer, marriage, and business ethics. Literally means "the way to walk."

Li (Á¶Æ)

Chinese concept meaning ritual propriety, etiquette, or ceremony. In Confucianism, li shapes social harmony through correct performance of roles and relationships. Includes everything from state ceremonies to everyday manners.

Critical distinction! Don't assume every tradition works like Protestant Christianity. Orthodox Judaism: An Orthodox Jew who observes Shabbat, keeps kosher, and follows rabbinic law is authentically Jewish even with doubts about God's existence. Meanwhile, someone who "believes in God" but doesn't practice might be seen as less authentically Jewish. Confucianism focuses on li (ritual propriety) and ethical relationships. The question isn't "Do you believe Confucius was divinely inspired?" but "Do you practice filial piety? Do you maintain proper relationships?" Many American students‚Äîeven non-religious ones‚Äîhave absorbed Protestant assumptions that "religion is about what you believe in your heart." This doesn't describe most traditions historically or globally. Scholar: Talal Asad argues the modern Western concept of religion as private belief is itself a product of Protestant Christianity and Enlightenment secularism.

Theology vs. Religious Studies

Theology

"Faith seeking understanding"

  • Operates from within a tradition
  • Asks: "What is true?"
  • Seeks to clarify or defend teachings

Example: A Christian theologian exploring the Trinity

Religious Studies

"Methodological neutrality"

  • Operates from outside
  • Asks: "What do people believe?"
  • Seeks to understand and describe

Example: A scholar analyzing how groups understand the Trinity

In this class, we do Religious Studies, not theology.

×
Methodological Neutrality

An academic approach that brackets questions of truth/falsehood to focus on describing and analyzing religion. Scholars don't ask "Is this true?" but rather "What does this mean to practitioners? How does it function?"

Note: This is NOT the same as personal neutrality‚Äîscholars can have religious commitments, but in academic work they employ neutral methods.

This distinction defines what kind of course this is. Theology is "faith seeking understanding" (St. Anselm). It operates from within a tradition, asking "What is true? How should we live according to our faith?" Religious studies operates from outside, using methodological neutrality. Scholars might be practicing believers personally, but in academic work they bracket truth/falsehood questions. Example comparison: - Theologian: "What is the true nature of the Trinity?" - Religious studies: "How do different Christian groups understand the Trinity differently? What historical factors shaped these differences?" This class won't change your beliefs or convert you. You can be religious, non-religious, anything. The key is temporarily adopting methodological neutrality‚Äîdescribing traditions on their own terms.

Methodological Neutrality in Practice

In Religious Studies, we don't ask if beliefs are true or false. Instead:

1. Understand what beliefs and practices mean to practitioners

2. Describe religions in their own terms

3. Analyze how religions function in individuals' lives and societies

‚ùå Not theology (which operates from within a tradition)

‚ùå Not anti-religious polemics (which seek to attack religion)

‚úÖ We're scholars, not evangelists or debunkers.

Making the approach concrete and practical. We're neither evangelists (promoting religion) nor debunkers (attacking religion). We're scholars trying to understand how religion works. "Describe before explain": Start by carefully observing what people actually do and believe, THEN move to theoretical analysis. When studying karma: Don't dismiss it as superstition or embrace it as truth. Ask: What does karma mean to practitioners? How does it shape ethical behavior? What comfort or motivation does it provide? Can we critique harmful ideas? Yes‚Äîbut critique ideas while respecting people. Analyze power dynamics, contradictions, ethical concerns without dismissing entire traditions wholesale. Scholar: Clifford Geertz emphasized "thick description"‚Äîdescribing phenomena in rich detail, attending to context and meaning, before rushing to theoretical explanations.
