Defining Religion

Multiple perspectives, no single answer

Section transition. We've examined religion's building blocks. Now we shift to how major thinkers have attempted to DEFINE religion itself. Important frame: The definitions you're about to see aren't competing to be THE right answer. They're different lenses highlighting different aspects. Marx emphasizes economics/society. Freud emphasizes psychology. Durkheim emphasizes social cohesion. Otto emphasizes experience. Each captures something important while leaving something out.

Why Is Defining Religion So Hard?

  • Encompasses vast diversity (Buddhism vs. Christianity vs. indigenous traditions)
  • Some traditions don't use the concept "religion" (it's a Western category)
  • Boundaries are fuzzy (Is Confucianism religion or philosophy? Both?)
  • Different definitions serve different purposes (legal, academic, popular)

No single definition satisfies everyone—different definitions highlight different aspects.

We'll look at several major approaches...

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide is basically saying: “Defining religion sounds simple until you actually try.” It’s like trying to write one definition of “game” that works for chess, football, Minecraft, and tag—each fits, but in different ways. The goal here isn’t to find the perfect definition; it’s to understand why the category is slippery and why scholars use different definitions for different tasks.

Breaking It Down

Encompasses vast diversity: “Religion” has to cover traditions that look nothing alike. For example, some forms of Buddhism don’t focus on a creator God, while Christianity centers God, salvation, and scripture. Meanwhile, many indigenous traditions are deeply tied to land, ancestors, and community practices. When one word tries to cover all of that, it starts to stretch like a rubber band.

Some traditions don’t use the concept “religion”: This is a big “category problem.” The modern Western idea of “religion” often assumes things like: a distinct “religious sphere,” private belief, and membership in a named tradition. But many cultures didn’t carve life up that way—ritual, law, ethics, politics, and family life were all tangled together. That’s why scholars warn that “religion” can be a Western category that we sometimes impose on other societies rather than neutrally “discover.”

Boundaries are fuzzy: The classic example is Confucianism. Is it a religion (rituals, ancestors, moral cultivation, harmony with Heaven)? Is it a philosophy (ethical teaching, social order, education)? The annoying-but-true answer is: it depends what definition you’re using. If your definition requires worship of a personal God, Confucianism might not qualify. If your definition focuses on ritual, moral formation, and ultimate order, it might.

Different definitions serve different purposes: This is where the “why are we defining this?” question matters. A legal definition might be needed for religious freedom cases (what counts as “religion” in court?). An academic definition might be designed for comparison across cultures. A popular definition might match everyday assumptions (“religion = belief in God”). One definition can be useful in one setting and misleading in another.

No single definition satisfies everyone: That’s not a failure of your intelligence—it’s the reality of the topic. Each definition highlights one aspect (belief, ritual, community, experience, power, meaning) and downplays others. So the trick is to treat definitions like tools: pick the tool that matches the job.

Why This Matters

This slide sets up the logic of the next section: we’re about to tour multiple major approaches to defining religion, and you’re going to see them disagree. That disagreement is productive. It forces you to ask two smart questions whenever someone defines religion: (1) What part of religion are they emphasizing? and (2) What are they leaving out? Once you start thinking that way, you can analyze any tradition more fairly—without squeezing everything into a single Western-shaped box.

🎓 Scholars Weigh In

Wilfred Cantwell Smith – The Meaning and End of Religion

Smith argues that “religion” as a fixed thing (like a bounded system) is a relatively modern way of talking, and it can distort what people are actually doing. Applied to this slide: when we try to define religion as a single “essence,” we may miss the lived reality—practice, devotion, community, and changing historical forms. His takeaway is basically: focus more on faith and practice than treating “religion” as a rigid object.

Talal Asad – Genealogies of Religion

Asad pushes the point that “religion” isn’t a neutral label—it’s shaped by history and power (especially in Western modernity). Applied to this slide: when we say “some traditions don’t use the concept ‘religion,’” Asad would add that the category can get exported through colonialism, law, and institutions—and then it starts reorganizing how people describe themselves. His warning: always ask who gets to define religion and what social consequences follow.

Jonathan Z. Smith – Imagining Religion

Smith famously emphasizes that “religion” is not a self-evident thing in the world; it’s a scholarly category we use to compare and interpret human life. Applied to this slide: the fuzziness and diversity aren’t bugs—they’re clues that definitions are strategic choices. The job of the scholar isn’t to find the one “true” definition, but to make careful comparisons and be honest about what your definition is designed to illuminate.

“Religion… is the belief in Spiritual Beings.”

— Edward B. Tylor, Primitive Culture (1871)

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide introduces one of the most famous “starter definitions” in the academic study of religion: Edward B. Tylor’s idea that religion is basically belief in spiritual beings. Think of it like a minimalist recipe: it uses as few ingredients as possible so it can apply to lots of different traditions. The upside is clarity; the downside is that anything you leave out might be exactly what matters in some religions.

Breaking It Down

Edward B. Tylor (1832–1917): Tylor is often called a founder of modern anthropology—the comparative study of human cultures. In Primitive Culture (1871), he argued that you can study religion as a human phenomenon across societies, not just as “my tradition vs. yours.” Quick reference: Tylor and Primitive Culture.

“Religion… is the belief in Spiritual Beings.”: This is a substantive definition—meaning it defines religion by its content (what it is “made of”), especially belief in non-material persons or forces (gods, spirits, ancestors, angels, etc.). If you’re looking for the “essence” of religion, Tylor says the essence is belief in spirits.

Why Tylor matters here: He wanted a definition that was short, memorable, and “portable”—something you could carry across cultures for comparison. That’s why this is sometimes called a minimal definition: it tries to be broad enough to include many traditions while still being specific enough to test.

Definition type: Substantive: Notice what this does not include: ritual, ethics, community, sacred texts, institutions, emotions, experiences, or practices. It’s “belief-first.” That’s important because a lot of later scholars critique exactly this tendency: defining religion as primarily belief (often in a way that resembles Protestant Christianity’s emphasis on belief).

Classroom stress test: This definition is useful because it instantly creates test cases. Non-theistic Buddhism: some forms don’t center a creator God—so do they count? (Many Buddhists do affirm non-human beings like devas, bodhisattvas, and spirits, but not always in the same way as “God-belief.”) Confucianism: focused on ethics and ritual order—does “spiritual beings” capture what’s central there? “Spiritual but not religious”: might be belief-heavy but institution-light—does that make it “religion” by Tylor’s definition? Civil religion: nationalism can look religious (flags, martyrs, sacred stories), but it may not involve “spiritual beings.” Reference point: civil religion.

Mini-bio: Tylor worked in an era when many European scholars used evolutionary models of culture—imagining societies moving from “simple” to “advanced.” That background matters because it shaped how early anthropology talked about “primitive” religion (a term we treat very critically today). The key takeaway: even when a definition is useful, it comes from a historical moment with its own assumptions.

Why This Matters

This slide is your first big example of how definitions act like filters. Tylor’s filter highlights belief in spirits—and it’s great for comparing traditions where spirits, gods, or ancestors play an obvious role. But it can also miss traditions where the “center of gravity” is practice, ethics, community, or liberation (rather than belief in spiritual beings). So, the point isn’t “Tylor is right” or “Tylor is wrong.” The point is: you’re learning how different definitions include some things clearly and leave other things blurry.

🎓 Scholars Weigh In

Talal Asad – Genealogies of Religion

Asad would caution you that “belief-first” definitions often reflect a specifically modern Western (and often Protestant-influenced) way of imagining religion—as primarily inner belief rather than embodied practice. Applied to Tylor: his definition looks clean and universal, but it smuggles in an assumption about what counts as “the core” of religion. Asad’s big move is to ask: how did law, empire, and modern institutions shape what scholars came to call “religion” in the first place?

Émile Durkheim – The Elementary Forms of Religious Life

Durkheim goes almost the opposite direction: instead of defining religion by spiritual beings, he emphasizes the social function—how religion creates community, shared meaning, and a sense of the sacred (what a group treats as set apart and powerful). Applied to this slide: Durkheim helps you see what Tylor leaves out. Even if a tradition talks about spirits, what often “does the work” of religion is the way rituals and symbols bind people into a moral community. Quick reference: Durkheim.

Clifford Geertz – Religion as a Cultural System

Geertz would nudge you to see religion less as a single belief and more as a whole system of symbols that shapes how people experience the world. Applied here: Tylor gives you a simple “yes/no” test (do you believe in spiritual beings?), while Geertz is interested in how religion produces a lived worldview—how it makes reality feel meaningful, emotionally compelling, and “real.” That’s a different kind of definition, and comparing them is exactly the skill this lecture is building.

“A religion is a unified system of beliefs and practices… which unite into one single moral community.”

— Émile Durkheim, French sociologist of religion (1915)

📖 Understanding This Slide

Durkheim is offering a totally different way to define religion than Tylor. Instead of starting with “What do people believe?” he starts with “What does religion do for a group?” Think of it like this: Tylor treats religion like a set of ideas in your head; Durkheim treats it like a social glue that helps a community hold together through shared meanings, rituals, and moral rules.

Breaking It Down

Émile Durkheim (1858–1917): A major founder of sociology, which studies how societies work—institutions, norms, group behavior, and social bonds. In The Elementary Forms of Religious Life (1912), he argued that religion is one of the key ways societies create shared identity and shared authority. Quick reference: Durkheim and Elementary Forms.

Functional definition: This is the key move. A functional definition explains religion by what it does (its role or function), not just what it “is.” For Durkheim, religion organizes life by separating what’s sacred from what’s ordinary, and it binds people together into a moral community.

Sacred things: In Durkheim’s vocabulary, “sacred” means “set apart.” Sacred things have special rules: you treat them differently, protect them, handle them carefully, and you don’t casually mess with them. The opposite is the profane, meaning everyday life—normal, regular, not set apart. (This is the famous “sacred/profane” divide.) Helpful overview: sacred–profane dichotomy.

Beliefs + practices: Notice the quote includes both. Durkheim wants you to see that religion isn’t just “ideas” people agree with—it’s also what people do: rituals, holidays, taboos, gatherings, chants, prayers, pilgrimages, etc. Practices are how the group reinforces the sacred and keeps the community “in sync.”

Moral community (“a Church” broadly construed): Durkheim uses “church” in a broad sense: a community bound by shared sacred symbols and shared moral expectations. It does not have to mean a Christian church building. His point is that religion is not merely private—it’s social. It creates an “us.”

Provocative claim: Durkheim suggests that when people worship gods, they are also (in a way) honoring the moral authority of the community itself—its shared values, rules, and social order. That doesn’t mean “religion is fake.” It means religious symbols can represent something bigger than any individual: the power of the group and its norms.

Classroom bridge: civil religion: Here’s where Durkheim gets sneaky-useful. Some national rituals can function “religiously” because they create sacred symbols and moral community—flags, memorials, pledges, national holidays, moments of silence, taboo acts (like burning a flag), etc. Even if there’s no explicit god-talk, the pattern can look Durkheimian. Quick reference: civil religion.

Why This Matters

Durkheim helps explain why defining religion is hard: different definitions spotlight different “centers of gravity.” Tylor’s definition is great if you want to compare beliefs about spirits across cultures. Durkheim’s definition is great if you want to understand how religion shapes groups—identity, belonging, moral rules, and social cohesion. Once you learn to see both, you can ask a sharper question: when we call something “religion,” are we talking about belief, practice, community, or all three?

🎓 Scholars Weigh In

Robert N. Bellah – “Civil Religion in America”

Bellah takes Durkheim’s “religion binds a moral community” idea and applies it to the nation-state. His point is that countries can develop a kind of shared sacred story and ritual life—founding myths, sacred texts (like constitutions treated with near-reverence), martyrs, holy days, and symbolic spaces (memorials, cemeteries). This connects directly to the slide’s “moral community” line: even outside temples and churches, communities still build “sacred” meaning that tells people who they are and what they owe each other.

“What grows out of, and gives expression to, experience of the holy in its various aspects.”

— Rudolf Otto, German scholar of religion (1917)

📖 Understanding This Slide

Otto is trying to capture something many religious people say is at the heart of religion: a powerful experience of “the holy.” Instead of starting with beliefs (like Tylor) or social functions (like Durkheim), Otto starts with a felt encounter—something that hits you as bigger than you, strange, and deeply real. Think of it like standing under a sky full of stars: you can describe the astronomy, but you can also feel awe—and Otto is interested in that “awe” side of the human experience.

Breaking It Down

Rudolf Otto (1869–1937): A German scholar of religion best known for The Idea of the Holy (1917). He wanted language that takes religious experience seriously instead of treating it as “just” politics, “just” psychology, or “just” social glue. Quick reference: Rudolf Otto and The Idea of the Holy.

“Experience of the holy”: Otto claims religion grows out of encounters with what feels holy—not ordinary, not everyday, not under your control. For him, religion isn’t just a list of doctrines; it’s a response to something perceived as sacred.

The numinous: Otto’s key term. The numinous is a distinctive kind of experience—like “the holy” as a feeling. He describes it with the famous phrase mysterium tremendum et fascinans: mysterium (mystery; it’s beyond you), tremendum (it can feel overwhelming, even scary), and fascinans (it pulls you in; it’s captivating). In plain language: it’s the mix of awe + fear + attraction. Helpful overview: numinous.

Resists reduction: Otto is pushing back against explanations that treat religion as nothing but economics, psychology, or society. He’s saying: even if those factors matter, there’s still an “irreducible” core experience that religious language is trying to express—what he calls “the wholly Other” (something radically different from normal life).

Pedagogy angle: Otto makes it easier to see the difference between definitions. He’s more substantive (religion is rooted in the holy/numinous experience), while someone like Durkheim is more functionalist (religion does social bonding and creates moral community).

Why This Matters

Otto is important because he forces a big question: if we define religion only by beliefs or social roles, do we miss what many believers think is the “engine” of religion—encounter, awe, devotion, and transformation? Even if you personally don’t buy Otto’s claim, he gives you a serious way to talk about religious experience without automatically dismissing it as irrational. That’s a key skill in a world religions course: understanding traditions from the inside and analyzing them from the outside.

🎓 Scholars Weigh In

Mircea Eliade – The Sacred and the Profane

Eliade builds on a very Otto-like idea: humans experience reality as divided between the sacred and the ordinary. Applied to this slide, Eliade helps you see Otto’s point more clearly: religion often begins when people experience something as “set apart” and then organize life around it—stories, rituals, places, and calendars. Otto explains the feeling of the holy; Eliade shows how that feeling can shape whole worlds of meaning and practice.

“An illusion deriving its strength from the fact that it falls in with our instinctual desires.”

— Sigmund Freud (1927)

📖 Understanding This Slide

Freud is not trying to define religion as “holy” (Otto) or as “community glue” (Durkheim). He’s asking a different question: Why do religious beliefs feel so compelling to people? His answer is psychological: religion can meet deep emotional needs—especially the need for security, comfort, and meaning when life is frightening or uncertain.

Breaking It Down

Sigmund Freud (1856–1939): The founder of psychoanalysis, famous for explaining human behavior through unconscious desires, fears, and childhood development. He wrote several works on religion, especially The Future of an Illusion (1927). Quick reference: Freud and The Future of an Illusion.

“An illusion…”: Freud’s word illusion is easy to misunderstand. He does not mean “a dumb lie people fall for.” He means a belief that gets its power from wish-fulfillment—it feels true because it fits what we deeply want (protection, justice, a caring parent-like figure, life having purpose). That’s why he says it “derives its strength” from our desires rather than from evidence.

Function (what religion does psychologically): In Freud’s view, religion can reduce anxiety by offering explanations and reassurance: the world isn’t random, suffering isn’t meaningless, someone is in control, morality is backed by cosmic authority, and death isn’t the end. You don’t have to agree with him to see the analytical move: he’s treating religion as a response to human vulnerability.

Takeaway question: “What emotional needs does this belief or ritual meet?” That question can still be useful even if Freud’s specific theory feels too harsh or too narrow. It’s like a flashlight you can point at religious practices: what anxieties, hopes, or fears does this address?

Why This Matters

Freud is one of the major “explain religion without assuming it’s true” thinkers. In this section of the lecture, you’re learning that some definitions treat religion as belief, some as social function, and some as experience—Freud adds another layer: religion as psychological coping. Whether you agree or disagree, he helps you practice a key academic skill: separating (1) what a tradition says about itself from (2) how an outsider might explain why it works for people.

🎓 Scholars Weigh In

William James – The Varieties of Religious Experience

James gives you a useful contrast to Freud. He also takes psychology seriously, but he’s more interested in describing religious experience as people actually live it (conversion, peace, struggle, moral transformation) rather than dismissing it as wish-fulfillment. Applied here: Freud pushes you to ask what needs religion meets; James pushes you to notice what religion can do inside a person’s life—sometimes in ways that look like growth, courage, or moral change.

“Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world, and the soul of soulless conditions. It is the opium of the people.”

— Karl Marx, A Contribution to the Critique of Hegel’s Philosophy of Right (1843)

📖 Understanding This Slide

Marx isn’t mainly asking “What is religion?” He’s asking “Why does religion show up so strongly in societies with suffering and inequality?” His famous “opium” line is often quoted like a cheap insult, but on this slide you can see something more complicated: religion is both a real expression of pain and a kind of relief from that pain.

Breaking It Down

Karl Marx (1818–1883): A German philosopher and economist associated with critiques of capitalism and class inequality. He’s best known for The Communist Manifesto (1848, with Friedrich Engels) and Das Kapital. Quick reference: Marx and “opium of the people”.

“Sigh of the oppressed creature”: Marx starts with empathy. Religion is a “sigh” because people are hurting—economically, politically, socially. It’s a human response to real suffering, not just a random superstition.

“Heart of a heartless world”: This is Marx saying religion can provide meaning, hope, community, and moral warmth in a world that often feels cold and unfair. So he recognizes religion’s comfort function.

“Opium of the people”: In the 1800s, opium was commonly used as a painkiller. Marx’s metaphor is: religion can dull pain and help people endure suffering. The critique is that pain relief can also keep people from challenging the conditions causing the pain. In other words: comfort can become a tool of social stability.

Functionalist angle: Marx pushes you to ask: “What does religion do in a society?” and “Who benefits?” That doesn’t automatically mean “religion is evil.” It means religion is connected to social and economic realities, not floating above them.

Why This Matters

Marx gives you a “power and inequality” lens. If Durkheim highlights how religion unites a moral community, Marx asks whether that unity sometimes supports an unjust system. This is a core theme in religious studies: religion can be a force for comfort and meaning—and also be entangled with power. Learning to hold both possibilities at once is part of becoming a strong analyst.

🎓 Scholars Weigh In

Max Weber – The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism

Weber is a helpful “neighbor” to Marx because he also links religion to economics—but differently. Marx tends to treat religion as shaped by material conditions (poverty, inequality, class conflict). Weber shows how religious ideas can also shape economic behavior (work ethic, discipline, ideas about calling). Applied here: Weber helps you see that the religion/economy relationship can go both directions—religion can reflect society, and religion can also reshape society.

“A set of symbolic forms and acts which relate a person to the ultimate conditions of his existence.”

— Robert Bellah (1964)

📖 Understanding This Slide

Bellah is trying to give a definition that’s broad enough to work across many traditions, without reducing religion to just “belief in God.” His definition highlights two things: (1) religion uses symbols and (2) religion involves actions. And then it points toward the deepest question religions wrestle with: what are the “ultimate conditions” of human life—what’s most real, most important, and most binding?

Breaking It Down

Robert Bellah (1927–2013): An American sociologist of religion known for comparative definitions and for the idea of civil religion (how nations can develop “religion-like” rituals and symbols). Quick reference: Bellah.

“Symbolic forms”: This includes myths, stories, doctrines, sacred images, metaphors, and teachings—basically, the meaning-making language of a tradition. Symbols are powerful because they don’t just give information; they shape how you see reality (who you are, what matters, what’s wrong with the world, what “salvation” or “liberation” would look like).

“Acts”: Religion is not just ideas—it’s practices: prayer, ritual, meditation, ethical disciplines, festivals, pilgrimage, charity, taboos, and daily habits. This is a big upgrade from belief-only definitions because it fits traditions where practice is central.

“Ultimate conditions of existence”: This is the killer phrase. It’s a way to talk about religion without assuming “God” is the center. In theistic traditions, the “ultimate” might be God and salvation. In Buddhism, it could be liberation from suffering (nirvana). In Confucian traditions, it might be harmony, cultivation, and order under Heaven. Different ultimates—same slot in the definition.

Why students usually like this definition: It feels flexible without being vague. It covers beliefs, practices, meaning, and what a tradition treats as most real/most important. It also helps you compare traditions fairly, instead of treating Christianity as the default template.

Why This Matters

Bellah is useful as a “bridge” definition. After Tylor (belief), Durkheim (community and practice), Otto (experience), Freud and Marx (psychological and social functions), Bellah gives you a definition that can hold multiple dimensions at once: symbols + acts + ultimacy. That makes it especially good for a world religions course where you need a definition that doesn’t automatically exclude half the planet.

🎓 Scholars Weigh In

Clifford Geertz – Religion as a Cultural System

Geertz helps you see why Bellah’s “symbols and acts” language is so useful. Geertz argues religion works like a meaning system: symbols and rituals shape a worldview (how reality is) and an ethos (how you should live). Applied here: Bellah’s definition is basically a student-friendly version of that insight—religion uses symbols and practices to connect people to what they think is ultimately true and ultimately important.

How Your Textbook Defines Religion

Religion is a pattern of beliefs and practices that expresses and enacts what a community regards as sacred and/or ultimate about life.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide gives you the “working definition” your textbook wants you to use for the course. It’s designed to be broad enough to cover lots of traditions without forcing everything to look like Christianity. Think of it like a good pair of hiking boots: not fancy, but reliable—something you can use again and again as we travel across very different religious landscapes.

Breaking It Down

Religion is a pattern of beliefs and practices: “Pattern” is the key word here—it means religion isn’t just one idea or one ritual. It’s a repeating set of meanings (beliefs) and actions (practices) that hang together over time. The definition deliberately pairs beliefs (ideas, stories, teachings) with practices (rituals, habits, ethics, worship, meditation), so religion isn’t reduced to “what you think” only.

…that expresses and enacts what a community regards as sacred and/or ultimate about life: This is the “target” of religion in the definition—religion points toward what a group treats as most important and most real. Notice it says community, not just “individual.” Religion often lives in shared life: traditions, rituals, moral expectations, sacred spaces, calendars, and group identity.

Sacred: Sacred means “set apart.” Sacred things have special rules: they’re treated with care, protected by taboos, and marked off from everyday life. Sacred can apply to objects (a relic), times (holy days), spaces (temples), texts (scripture), people (saints), or even ideals (a nation’s symbols can sometimes become “sacred”). Helpful reference: sacred.

Ultimate: “Ultimate” is a smart word because it works across theistic and non-theistic traditions. It means what a tradition treats as most binding or most real—the deepest “what’s it all about?” answer. That could be salvation, liberation from suffering, harmony, cosmic order, justice, enlightenment, union with God, or something else. (Different religions fill in the blank differently.) Helpful reference: ultimacy.

How to use this definition: When you get stuck on “Is X a religion?”, use it like a checklist: (1) Do you see beliefs + practices (not just opinions)? (2) Is there a community shaped by those patterns (even if loosely)? (3) Do those beliefs/practices relate people to something sacred and/or ultimate? If the answer is mostly “yes,” it probably fits. If it’s “sort of,” that’s not failure—that’s the point of the course: boundaries are fuzzy.

Why This Matters

This definition is a “bridge” between the approaches you just saw. It agrees with Durkheim that religion includes practices and a community (not just belief). It also leaves space for Otto’s idea that people experience something “holy,” while still being usable in academic analysis. Most importantly, it helps you compare traditions fairly: instead of assuming religion = “God-belief,” it asks what a tradition treats as sacred or ultimate and how it expresses that through lived patterns.

🎓 Scholars Weigh In

Émile Durkheim – The Elementary Forms of Religious Life

Durkheim helps you understand why this textbook definition puts beliefs and practices together and emphasizes community. He argued religion isn’t just private thoughts—it’s a shared system that marks some things as sacred and binds people into a moral community. So when you see “pattern” + “community” + “sacred,” you’re seeing a very Durkheim-friendly way of talking: religion is lived and social, not just a set of statements you agree with.

Myth (Religious Studies)

Sacred narrative: a foundational story about origins, destiny, or cosmic meaning that conveys a tradition’s core teachings.

Examples: Genesis creation accounts, Buddhist Jataka tales, Hindu epics, indigenous creation stories.

Note: Many practitioners dislike the term because in everyday English “myth” implies falsehood.

Critical Clarification

In Religious Studies, calling something a “myth” is not the same as calling it a lie.

The term refers to a story’s status and function in a community: it is sacred, authoritative, and worldview-shaping.

A myth may be:

  • historically accurate
  • historically inaccurate
  • impossible to verify historically

What makes it “myth” in this academic sense is that it answers big questions and orients life toward what a community treats as sacred or ultimate.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide is teaching you a crucial “translator skill” for Religious Studies: the word myth means something different in the classroom than it does in everyday conversation. In daily English, “myth” often means “fake.” In Religious Studies, myth means a sacred story that helps a community explain reality and orient life—kind of like the operating system running in the background of a tradition.

Breaking It Down

Beliefs: Sacred Narrative: Here the focus is on beliefs as answers to big “human questions” (origins, purpose, what’s wrong with the world, what the good life is, what happens after death). Religions often answer these questions through stories rather than through abstract arguments.

“Myth” as a technical term: In Religious Studies, a myth is a foundational sacred story that conveys a tradition’s core meanings. It’s “foundational” because it explains how things are supposed to be understood, and it’s “sacred” because the community treats it as authoritative and worldview-shaping. Helpful reference: myth.

Foundational sacred story: This means the story isn’t just entertainment. It functions like a “north star” for meaning—explaining what the tradition thinks is ultimately real and what matters most.

Origins, destiny, divine beings, cosmic order: These are classic myth topics because they answer “big picture” questions. Origins: Where did the world come from? Why are humans here? Destiny: Where is life headed? What is the final goal? Divine beings: What kinds of spiritual powers exist (Gods, spirits, bodhisattvas, ancestors)? Cosmic order: What is the structure of reality—chaos vs. order, good vs. evil, suffering vs. liberation?

Often symbolic rather than literal history: “Symbolic” doesn’t mean “meaningless.” It means the story can communicate truth through imagery, metaphor, and narrative patterns rather than functioning like a modern history textbook. Sometimes a myth could be historically accurate; sometimes not; sometimes it’s simply not the kind of thing that can be verified. The key issue in Religious Studies is its status and function in a community.

Durkheim helps you see sacredness as a social fact—communities mark certain things as sacred, and then treat them differently. Helpful reference: sacred/profane.

Ultimate: “Ultimate” broadens the concept beyond “holy.” It’s the category you use when a tradition is centered less on sacred objects/places and more on ultimate reality, ultimate values, or ultimate meaning. The slide gives examples like secular humanism, some Buddhism, and Confucianism—cases where “holy place” language may not be the best fit, but the tradition still makes serious claims about reality, ethics, and meaning.

Concerns: ultimate reality, ultimate values, ultimate meaning: This line is basically three versions of the same question: What is reality really like at the deepest level? What matters most (ethically)? What gives life meaning and direction? Even non-theistic traditions can have strong answers here.

Why both terms matter: The slide’s bottom line is the payoff. If we use only “sacred,” we risk forcing everything into a theistic template. If we use only “ultimate,” we can get too abstract and miss how concrete sacredness is in many traditions. Using both terms gives you a flexible toolkit: you can describe Islam’s sacred spaces and texts with “sacred,” and you can describe Zen Buddhism’s focus on reality/awakening with “ultimate,” without doing violence to either.

Practical classroom move: The notes give a nice example of how to “switch lenses” respectfully: studying Islam → sacred text, sacred time, sacred place; studying Zen → ultimate reality, ultimate meaning, ultimate practice. Same course, different vocabulary depending on the tradition’s center of gravity.

Why This Matters

This distinction is about intellectual honesty and respect. It prevents two common mistakes: (1) assuming religion always means “holy things + God,” and (2) assuming non-theistic traditions are “not really religion.” Sacred and ultimate let you compare traditions without flattening them. You’re basically learning to ask: what does this community treat as set apart—and what does it treat as ultimately real or meaningful?

Re-Defining Religion

  • religion is often traced to the ancient Latin word religio (ree-LIG-ee-oh), from the verb religare, meaning “to bind” or “to tie fast.”
  • It can mean a constraint on behavior: doing what is good and avoiding what is evil.
  • Religio can also mean a way of life that binds people together in a group and orients them to the sacred.
  • It can entail holy awe: mysterium tremendum et fascinans toward the gods or sacred power.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide is a conceptual pivot: instead of treating religion as “a list of beliefs,” it frames religion as a kind of binding—a way people get tied to meanings, practices, communities, and what they treat as sacred or ultimate. The Latin word-hunt here is basically a memory hook: religion as something that binds you—your behavior, your identity, your community, and your sense of what matters most.

Breaking It Down

Religio and religare: The slide notes that “religion” is often traced to the Latin religio, linked to religare (“to bind” or “to tie fast”). Whether or not you care about Latin, the idea is useful: it captures what we’ve been seeing—religion binds people to a way of life. Quick reference: religio.

Helpful nuance: The “bind” explanation is common, but it’s not the only proposal. Some ancient writers (and later scholars) connect religio to a different Latin idea: careful “re-reading” or “scrupulous attention” (relegere)—basically, religion as careful observance. You don’t need to memorize the debate; just notice that both options point toward religion as practice + obligation, not just private opinion.

Constraint on behavior: The slide says religion can mean a constraint—doing good and avoiding evil. This is the “religion as moral seriousness” angle. Many traditions come with expectations: habits you should cultivate, actions you should avoid, obligations you owe to others, and ideals you’re supposed to live up to.

Way of life that binds people together: This connects directly to your earlier slides on myth, ritual, and community. Religion isn’t only what you think; it’s what you do with others over time—shared stories, repeated rituals, identity, and belonging. “Binding” here means the tradition holds a group together and orients life toward what it treats as sacred.

Holy awe: The slide adds the emotional/experiential dimension: religion can involve holy awe—mysterium tremendum et fascinans (Otto’s phrase). That’s the feeling of being both drawn in and overwhelmed by sacred power: fascination + trembling. Helpful reference: mysterium tremendum.

Connecting the dots: Notice what this “binding” frame does—it gathers multiple pieces you’ve already learned: myth binds you to meaning, ritual binds you through embodied practice, community binds you through shared identity, sacred/ultimate binds you to what a tradition treats as most real and most important.

Quick classroom test: If you want to stress-test this idea, try a non-obvious example (sports fandom, nationalism, vegan ethics, etc.). Ask: does it actually “bind” people through shared practices and obligations—and does it treat something as sacred/ultimate? Sometimes the answer is “sort of,” and that fuzziness is the whole point: boundaries can be tricky.

Why This Matters

This matters because it gives you a practical way to recognize religion without reducing it to “belief in God.” The “binding” approach explains why religion shows up as a whole system—stories, rituals, moral expectations, community identity, and powerful experiences. It also helps you see why religion can be such a durable force: things that bind people tend to stick around.
