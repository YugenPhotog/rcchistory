Axial Age (c. 800‚Äì200 BCE)

Karl Jaspers's term for the period when foundational religious and philosophical traditions emerged simultaneously across Eurasia. Confucius in China, the Buddha in India, Zoroaster in Persia, Hebrew prophets in Israel, and early Greek philosophers all asked similar questions about ethics, society, and meaning‚Äîapparently independently.

This slide bridges the documentary to the lecture. The Axial Age framing helps students see Confucius as part of a global intellectual moment, not isolated Chinese thought.

Oracle Bones (Áî≤È™®)

Turtle shells or cattle scapulae inscribed with questions, then heated until they cracked. Diviners interpreted crack patterns as ancestral guidance. Over 150,000 fragments survive, providing our earliest examples of Chinese writing and insight into Shang religious concerns.

Oracle bones give us our earliest Chinese writing AND insight into Shang concerns. Note: human sacrifice existed in many ancient societies (Mesopotamia, Mesoamerica) ‚Äî avoid exoticizing.

Chinese Writing

Cultural Continuity

  • Logographic system ‚Äî characters represent words/morphemes
  • Same text readable across dialects and centuries
  • Created cultural unity despite political fragmentation
  • Spread to Korea, Japan, Vietnam

Significance: A reader today can access texts from 3,000 years ago. Imagine if modern English speakers could read Old English without translation.

This is genuinely remarkable for cultural continuity. The classics Confucius studied are still readable (with training) today. No other civilization has this unbroken written tradition.

The Zhou Conquest (1046 BCE)

A Legitimacy Problem

The Zhou faced a dilemma: How do you justify overthrowing a dynasty?

Their answer: Tianming ‚Äî the Mandate of Heaven

Shang Claim

"Our ancestors give us authority"

Power based on lineage
Static legitimacy

Zhou Innovation

"Heaven grants authority to the virtuous"

Power based on moral performance
Conditional legitimacy

This is revolutionary. The Zhou essentially invented the idea that rulers can lose the right to rule through moral failure. This will shape Chinese political thought for 3,000 years.

Tian (Â§©) ‚Äî Heaven

Not the Western "Heaven"

Tian is not equivalent to the Judeo-Christian God:

  • Impersonal moral force, not a personal deity
  • Does not answer prayers or intervene directly
  • Operates through natural and social order
  • Can be discerned through observation, not revelation
×
Tian (Â§©) ‚Äî Heaven

An impersonal cosmic-moral power that governs through natural patterns rather than personal will. Closer to "Nature" or "the way things ought to be" than to a god who answers prayers. Tian sets the moral order of the universe but does not intervene in daily affairs.

This distinction is crucial for comparative religion students. When Confucius speaks of "Heaven," he's not praying to a deity ‚Äî he's invoking a moral order built into reality. Compare to Dharma, Logos, Natural Law.

The Mandate of Heaven

How It Works

  • Heaven grants authority to virtuous rulers
  • Mandate can be lost through misrule
  • Signs of lost mandate: natural disasters, social chaos, rebellions
  • Successful rebellion = proof the old dynasty lost mandate
‚ö†Ô∏è Key insight: This concept justifies BOTH obedience to good rulers AND rebellion against bad ones.

The catch: You only know the mandate transferred if the rebellion succeeds. Losers were just criminals.

Ask students: Is this circular reasoning? (Yes, somewhat.) But it provided a framework for legitimate regime change that Europe lacked until much later. Compare to "divine right of kings" which had no built-in termination clause.

Discussion: Mandate of Heaven

In pairs, discuss (3 minutes):

  1. How does the Mandate of Heaven differ from European "divine right of kings"?
  2. What are the benefits and dangers of this concept?
  3. Can you think of modern parallels ‚Äî ideas that justify both obedience and resistance?
Good comparative thinking exercise. Modern parallels might include: democratic legitimacy (consent of governed can be withdrawn), constitutional limits on power, impeachment mechanisms.

Zhou Decline and Crisis

The World Falls Apart

  • Zhou royal power weakens after 770 BCE
  • Spring and Autumn Period (770‚Äì476 BCE): Nominal Zhou rule, real power with regional lords
  • Warring States Period (475‚Äì221 BCE): Open warfare, no pretense of unity
  • Traditional rituals and relationships breaking down

The Question: How do you restore order when everything is collapsing?

This is the crisis that produces Confucius, Laozi, and all the "Hundred Schools" of Chinese philosophy. Chaos was the mother of invention. Similar dynamic to how Renaissance emerged from plague/war chaos.

Confucius's Project

Restoration, Not Revolution

From the documentary, you know Confucius saw himself as a transmitter, not an innovator.

His diagnosis: Society collapsed because people forgot how to be properly human.

His prescription: Restore the relationships, rituals, and virtues of the early Zhou "golden age."

"I transmit but do not innovate. I trust in and love the ancient ways."

‚Äî Analects 7.1

But here's the thing: Confucius WAS innovative. By systematizing and teaching this tradition, by making it accessible through education rather than birth, he transformed it. He's like someone who claims to just be "restoring the Constitution" while fundamentally reinterpreting it.

The Analects (Lunyu Ë´ñË™û)

What Kind of Text Is This?

  • Not written by Confucius himself
  • Compiled by disciples over generations
  • Collection of sayings, dialogues, anecdotes
  • No systematic treatise ‚Äî wisdom in fragments

Think of it as: Collected tweets from a master teacher, curated by students who sometimes disagreed about what he meant.

×
Analects Compilation

The text developed in layers over perhaps 200 years. Earlier sections (Books 3-9) are considered most reliable. Later sections show influence of competing schools. Think: sayings compiled by followers, not a planned book.

This matters for interpretation. The Analects isn't a systematic philosophy textbook. It's situational wisdom. Different passages can seem contradictory because they addressed different contexts.

Ren (‰ªÅ) Etymology

The character visually suggests "what happens between two people." Ren is not a private virtue ‚Äî it only exists in relationship. You cannot practice Ren alone. It's the moral quality that emerges when we engage properly with others.

Ren is the sun; other virtues orbit around it. But it's not abstract "be nice." It's specific: How do you treat THIS person in THIS situation appropriately? A person of Ren knows.

Practicing Ren

The Golden Rule, Confucian Style

"Do not impose on others what you yourself do not desire."

‚Äî Analects 15.24

This is shu (ÊÅï) ‚Äî reciprocity or empathy.

Combined with zhong (Âø†) ‚Äî loyalty/doing one's best ‚Äî these form Confucius's "one thread" connecting all his teachings.

Compare to Jesus's Golden Rule, Hillel's formulation, Kant's categorical imperative. Confucius's version is "negative" (don't do X) rather than "positive" (do unto others). Some scholars see this as more cautious ‚Äî you might not know what others want, but you know what you don't want.

Li (Á¶Æ) ‚Äî Ritual Propriety

More Than Ceremonies

Li encompasses:

  • Religious rituals and ceremonies
  • Social etiquette and manners
  • Proper behavior in any situation
  • The "grammar" of social interaction

Key insight: Li is not about rigid rule-following. It's about knowing the appropriate action for each context.

×
Li (Á¶Æ) ‚Äî Ritual Propriety

Originally meant religious sacrifice. Confucius expanded it to all proper social behavior. Think: knowing when to be formal vs. casual, how to show respect, what's appropriate at a funeral vs. a wedding. It's social competence, not robotic rule-following.

Modern analogy: You know to silence your phone in class, speak differently to your professor than your roommate, dress differently for an interview than a party. That's Li ‚Äî social competence, not robotic rule-following.

Li in Action

Social Grammar

Without Li

  • Awkward, offensive, chaotic
  • Everyone guessing what's appropriate
  • Relationships strained
  • Society fragments

With Li

  • Smooth, respectful, harmonious
  • Shared expectations
  • Relationships nurtured
  • Society coheres

The goal: Li becomes second nature ‚Äî you don't calculate, you just know.

Li is like language fluency. A fluent speaker doesn't think about grammar rules ‚Äî they just speak correctly. A person of Li doesn't calculate proper behavior ‚Äî they embody it.

Virtue Application

Scenario (5 minutes):

Your friend copies answers during an exam. You see it happen. Using Ren (benevolence) and Li (propriety), how do you respond?

Consider:

  • Your relationship to your friend
  • Your role as a student
  • What a person of Ren would do
  • What's appropriate (Li) in this context
This is deliberately difficult ‚Äî values in tension. A good Confucian response wouldn't be simple "report them" or "ignore it." It might involve private confrontation, concern for friend's character development, recognition of your own responsibilities.

Xiao (Â≠ù) ‚Äî Filial Piety

The Root of All Virtue

Xiao means:

  • Respect and care for parents
  • Obedience to parental wishes
  • Care for parents in old age
  • Proper mourning after death
  • Honoring ancestors

"Filial piety and brotherly respect are the root of Ren."

‚Äî Analects 1.2

This is where Western students often push back. "Blind obedience to parents?" But Xiao is more complex ‚Äî it includes duty to gently correct parents who err. And the logic: if you can't maintain your first relationships (family), how can you maintain any others?

Why Xiao Matters

The Confucian Logic

  1. Family is where you first learn relationships
  2. If you fail at family, you'll fail everywhere
  3. Social order is built on family order
  4. A state is just a large family

Family-State Analogy

Critical question: What happens when family loyalty conflicts with other duties?

×
Family-State Analogy

Confucians saw the state as family writ large. The emperor is "father" to the people. This cuts both ways: rulers must care for subjects like children, AND subjects owe obedience like children. It creates mutual obligations, not just top-down authority.

The Analects includes a famous passage where Confucius seems to prefer a son covering up his father's crime over reporting it. This troubles modern readers. Confucius's point: destroying family bonds destroys the foundation of all social trust. Complicated.

This is often criticized as patriarchal and authoritarian. Fair. But note: the superior has DUTIES to the inferior. A ruler who doesn't care for subjects has violated the relationship. This isn't "obey whoever's in charge" ‚Äî it's "everyone has role-specific obligations."

Hierarchy and Mutuality

The Deal Goes Both Ways

Superior's Duties

  • Benevolence
  • Protection
  • Guidance
  • Model virtue

Inferior's Duties

  • Respect
  • Obedience
  • Support
  • Learn virtue

The catch: If superiors fail their duties, the relationship breaks down. A tyrannical ruler is not really a ruler. A cruel father is not really a father.

This connects to "Rectification of Names" (next slide). Confucius isn't defending any hierarchy ‚Äî he's defending proper hierarchy where everyone fulfills their role. Abusive authority is illegitimate authority.

Rectification of Names

Zhengming (Ê≠£Âêç) ‚Äî Be What You Claim to Be

"Let the ruler be a ruler, the minister a minister, the father a father, and the son a son."

‚Äî Analects 12.11

This means: Live up to your titles.

  • A "father" who abandons his children isn't really a father
  • A "ruler" who exploits his people isn't really a ruler
  • Names should match reality
This is quietly radical. It means authority figures can be judged against the standards their titles imply. A bad king isn't a king at all ‚Äî he's just a tyrant using the wrong name. Compare to modern critiques: "That's not real democracy" or "That's not real Christianity."

Confucian Government

Rule by Virtue, Not Force

"Guide them with government orders, regulate them with penalties, and the people will seek to evade the law and be without shame. Guide them with virtue, regulate them with ritual, and they will have a sense of shame and become upright."

‚Äî Analects 2.3

The ideal: Rulers so virtuous that people naturally follow.
Laws are a sign of failure.

This sets up contrast with Legalism (which we'll mention briefly). Legalists said: human nature is selfish, so use rewards and punishments. Confucians said: human nature is improvable, so model virtue. This debate echoes in modern political philosophy.

Discussion: Confucian Order

Small groups (5 minutes):

  1. What problems might arise in a strictly hierarchical social system?
  2. How does "mutual obligation" address some of these problems?
  3. Could Confucian principles work in a democratic society? How would they need to adapt?
Push for nuanced thinking. Critiques: patriarchy, stifling of dissent, conformity. Defenses: clear expectations, mutual care, social stability. Modern adaptation: retain emphasis on relationships and virtue, drop rigid hierarchy?
