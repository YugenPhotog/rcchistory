A religion practiced by millions in one region (like Shinto in Japan) isn't a "world religion" by this definition because it hasn't spread significantly beyond its homeland.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

We're doing something sophisticated here: using a framework while simultaneously critiquing it. Think of this like using a GPS for navigation‚Äîit's incredibly useful, but it also simplifies reality. Breaking It Down

The world religions framework was developed in 1960s Britain by educators trying to teach about religion in an increasingly diverse society. While useful, it has significant limitations that scholars now recognize.

Limitation #1: False unity‚ÄîThe framework makes "Hinduism" or "Buddhism" look like unified entities when they're actually incredibly diverse. There's more diversity within "Hinduism" than between some versions of Hinduism and some versions of Buddhism.

Limitation #2: Protestant bias‚ÄîThe framework emphasizes beliefs and texts (following a Protestant Christian model) rather than practices, experiences, or community. This doesn't work well for traditions like Judaism or Confucianism that emphasize practice over creed.

Limitation #3: Marginalization‚ÄîBy focusing on the "Big Five" (Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, Buddhism, Judaism), the framework marginalizes indigenous traditions, new religious movements, and traditions that don't fit neatly into categories.

Why This Matters

The metaphor "the map is not the territory" comes from philosopher Alfred Korzybski. A map helps you navigate, but confusing the map for reality leads to misunderstanding. We'll use the world religions framework pragmatically‚Äîas a starting point for organization‚Äîwhile remaining aware that real religious life is far messier, more fluid, and more interesting than any framework can capture.

  • What's right/wrong (ethics)

üìñ Understanding This Slide

The lens metaphor is powerful: just as glasses shape what you see, your worldview shapes how you interpret everything. If you're wearing rose-colored glasses, the world looks rosy‚Äîbut that doesn't mean the world IS rosy; it means you're seeing it through a particular lens.

Breaking It Down

Everyone has a worldview‚Äîyou can't NOT have one. You can only become more or less aware of it. Your worldview includes your assumptions about: What exists? What's real? What matters? How do we know things? What's right and wrong? These aren't just abstract philosophical questions‚Äîthey shape your everyday decisions and reactions.

Worldviews can be secular or religious: A committed atheist has a worldview (philosophical naturalism), just as a committed Christian has a worldview (Christian theism). Neither position is "neutral"‚Äîboth are comprehensive frameworks for interpreting reality.

The goal isn't to abandon your worldview‚Äîthat's impossible. The goal is to become conscious of it so you can recognize when it's shaping your interpretation, and to develop the intellectual flexibility to temporarily inhabit other worldviews to understand them from within.

Why This Matters

Understanding worldviews helps you recognize that when people disagree about religion, they're often operating from fundamentally different starting assumptions. Arguments about whether miracles are possible, for instance, often stem from different worldviews about what kind of universe we live in. Recognizing this doesn't resolve disagreements, but it helps you understand WHY people see things so differently.

James W. Sire ‚Äì The Universe Next Door

Sire argues that every worldview attempts to answer fundamental questions about reality, and that understanding these questions helps us recognize the deep structures underlying religious and philosophical differences. He identifies seven basic worldviews (Christian theism, deism, naturalism, nihilism, existentialism, pantheistic monism, and postmodernism), each offering different answers to questions about ultimate reality, human nature, death, and meaning.

Every Worldview Answers These Questions

1. What is ultimate reality? (God, gods, cosmic principles, emptiness?)

2. What is the origin and nature of the universe?

3. What is the origin and nature of humanity?

4. Does human life have a purpose?

5. How do humans acquire knowledge?

6. What is the basis of ethics and morality?

7. What happens to humans after death?

Over the semester, we'll see how various traditions have historically answered these questions!

üìñ Understanding This Slide

These seven questions come from worldview analysis and provide a framework for understanding how different religious traditions answer life's big questions. Notice that Question 1 is carefully phrased as "ultimate reality" rather than assuming "God"‚Äîthis allows for traditions with non-theistic ultimate realities like Buddhism or Daoism.

Breaking It Down

Question 1 (Ultimate Reality): Is there a God? Multiple gods? An impersonal force? Nothing beyond the physical universe? Different traditions give radically different answers.

Question 2 (External Reality): Is the physical world real and important, or is it illusion (maya) as some Hindu philosophies suggest?

Question 3 (Human Nature): Are humans fundamentally good, evil, both, neither? Created in God's image? Mere matter? Buddha-nature waiting to be realized?

Questions 4-7 build on these foundations, asking about death, knowledge, ethics, and history/meaning. As we study different traditions, you'll see how their answers to these questions form coherent systems‚Äîchange one answer and you often have to change others.

Why This Matters

These questions help you recognize the systematic nature of worldviews. Religious traditions aren't just random collections of beliefs‚Äîthey're comprehensive attempts to answer the deepest questions humans ask. Understanding this prevents you from cherry-picking individual beliefs out of context and helps you see how beliefs form coherent wholes.

Holy Envy

A term coined by Swedish Lutheran bishop Krister Stendahl (1921-2008). An approach to interfaith study where you:

  • Appreciate what's admirable in other traditions
  • Learn from their insights and practices
  • Remain open to being enriched by difference

üìñ Understanding This Slide

Holy envy is the core posture for the entire semester. It means recognizing and appreciating what's beautiful, true, or compelling in traditions you don't personally practice. It's the opposite of both defensive superiority ("my tradition has all the answers") and dismissive condescension ("all religion is silly").

Breaking It Down

Rule 1: Fair comparison‚ÄîDon't compare the Sermon on the Mount with ISIS beheadings. Compare ideals with ideals, practices with practices, ordinary believers with ordinary believers. Cherry-picking the best from your tradition and the worst from others is intellectually dishonest.

Rule 2: Inside-out understanding‚ÄîTry to understand traditions from the perspective of committed practitioners, not from outside critics. Ask "What does this mean to people who practice it?" before asking "Is this true?"

Rule 3: Appreciation without appropriation‚ÄîYou can admire the beauty of Islamic daily prayer or Hindu puja without feeling obligated to practice them yourself or without treating them as consumer products you can casually adopt.

Why This Matters

Holy envy creates the intellectual and emotional space for genuine learning. It means you can study Buddhism without becoming Buddhist, appreciate Islamic art without converting to Islam, and recognize wisdom in traditions you don't personally follow. This posture‚Äîrespectful curiosity combined with honest appreciation‚Äîmakes serious scholarship possible.

Breaking It Down

"Hinduism" was coined by British colonizers to describe the diverse religious practices of India. Many Hindus today use the term, but historically practitioners called their tradition Sanatana Dharma (eternal dharma) or identified with specific sampradayas (lineages). "Hinduism" as a unified religion is partly a colonial construct.

"Buddhism" comes from Western scholars. Practitioners in different regions call it Buddha-Dharma, the teachings of the Buddha. They often identify more with specific schools (Zen, Theravada, Pure Land) than with "Buddhism" as a whole.

"Confucianism" is a Western academic term. In Chinese, it's Rujia (School of Scholars) or Ruxue (teachings of the scholars). Confucius himself is Kongzi in Chinese‚Äî"Confucius" is a Latinization created by Jesuit missionaries.

Why this matters: These labels aren't neutral. They reflect particular historical encounters (often colonial) and shape how we think about traditions. Being aware that categories are constructed helps us use them more carefully.

Why This Matters

Understanding the history of religious labels helps you recognize that "world religions" as discrete, bounded categories is partly an artifact of how Westerners encountered and categorized non-Western traditions. Real religious life is messier‚Äîtraditions overlap, borrow from each other, and resist neat categorization. Use the labels as organizing tools, but don't mistake them for reality.

×
Methodological Neutrality

An academic approach that brackets questions of truth/falsehood to focus on describing and analyzing religion. Scholars don't ask "Is this true?" but rather "What does this mean to practitioners? How does it function?"

Note: This is NOT the same as personal neutrality‚Äîscholars can have religious commitments, but in academic work they employ neutral methods.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

This distinction defines what kind of course this is. Understanding the difference between religious studies and theology is essential for setting appropriate expectations and approaches.

Breaking It Down

Theology is "faith seeking understanding" (St. Anselm's famous definition). It operates from within a tradition, asking "What is true? How should we live according to our faith?" A Christian theologian asks "What is the true nature of the Trinity?" A Muslim theologian asks "How do we understand tawhid (divine unity)?" Theology is first-person engagement: "What do WE believe?"

Religious studies operates from outside, using bracketing or methodological agnosticism. Scholars might be practicing believers personally, but in academic work they bracket truth/falsehood questions. Religious studies asks third-person questions: "What do THEY believe? How does this practice function? What historical factors shaped this tradition?"

Example comparison:

‚Ä¢ Theologian: "What is the true nature of the Trinity?"
‚Ä¢ Religious studies: "How do different Christian groups understand the Trinity differently? What historical factors shaped these differences? How does Trinitarian belief function in Christian worship and identity?"

Why This Matters

This class won't try to change your beliefs or convert you to anything. You can be religious, non-religious, agnostic, atheist, spiritual-but-not-religious‚Äîyou're equally welcome. The key is temporarily adopting methodological neutrality: describing traditions on their own terms, analyzing how religion functions, understanding what practices mean to practitioners. This isn't relativism ("all beliefs are equally valid")‚Äîit's methodological bracketing for the purpose of understanding.

Smart argued that religious studies should practice "methodological agnosticism"‚Äînot making assumptions about whether religious claims are true or false, but describing and analyzing how religion works. This doesn't mean scholars can't have personal religious commitments; it means those commitments don't determine scholarly conclusions. A Buddhist scholar can study Christianity rigorously, and a Christian scholar can study Buddhism fairly.

Methodological Neutrality in Practice

In Religious Studies, we don't ask if beliefs are true or false. Instead:

1. Understand what beliefs and practices mean to practitioners

2. Describe religions in their own terms

3. Analyze how religions function in individuals' lives and societies

‚ùå Not theology (which operates from within a tradition)

‚ùå Not anti-religious polemics (which seek to attack religion)

‚úÖ We're scholars, not evangelists or debunkers.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

This slide makes the methodological approach concrete and practical. We're neither evangelists (promoting religion) nor debunkers (attacking religion). We're scholars trying to understand how religion works in human lives and societies.

Breaking It Down

"Describe before explain": Start by carefully observing what people actually do and believe, THEN move to theoretical analysis. Don't rush to explain away or reduce religion to something else (psychology, economics, power dynamics). First understand it on its own terms.

Example: Studying karma: Don't dismiss karma as superstition or embrace it as cosmic truth. Ask: What does karma mean to practitioners? How does belief in karma shape ethical behavior? What comfort or motivation does it provide? How has the concept changed across different Buddhist and Hindu contexts?

Can we critique harmful ideas? Yes‚Äîbut critique ideas while respecting people. You can analyze power dynamics in religious institutions, point out contradictions in doctrine, or discuss ethical concerns without dismissing entire traditions wholesale. Critical analysis isn't the same as dismissive condescension.

Why This Matters

Methodological neutrality doesn't mean moral neutrality. You can study how religious ideas function while still maintaining ethical standards. The key is distinguishing between describing what people believe (which requires bracketing judgment) and evaluating the ethical implications of those beliefs (which requires moral reasoning). Good scholarship does both‚Äîbut in the right order.
