Functionalist vs. Substantive Approaches

Functionalist Approach

Question: "What does religion DO?"

Focus: Social, psychological, economic functions

Thinkers: Marx, Freud, Durkheim

Substantive Approach

Question: "What does religion MEAN?"

Focus: Ideas, experiences, intentions

Thinkers: Otto, Eliade, Tylor, Frazer

Neither is more "correct"‚Äîthey ask different questions and reveal different aspects.

×
Functionalism

A theoretical approach that explains religion by examining what it DOES rather than what it IS. Asks: What functions does religion serve?

  • Social cohesion (Durkheim)
  • Psychological comfort (Freud)
  • Economic control (Marx)
  • Legitimating authority

Can be reductive if it ignores what practitioners consciously believe/intend.

×
Substantive Approach

A theoretical approach that explains religion by examining the IDEAS and EXPERIENCES that motivate people. Asks: What do practitioners believe/experience/intend?

Focus on: religious ideas/doctrines, phenomenology of experience, meaning-making, conscious motivations.

Can miss unconscious social functions if it only looks at explicit meanings.

Spend 2-3 minutes. Frame as complementary, not competing. Functionalism asks: What does religion DO? (create cohesion, provide comfort, legitimate authority) Substantive asks: What does religion MEAN? (ideas, experiences, intentions) Next slides will unpack each with concrete examples.

What Does Religion DO?

Functionalists ask:

  • How does religion create social cohesion?
  • How does religion provide psychological comfort?
  • How does religion legitimate authority structures?

Key Functionalist Thinkers:

Karl Marx (Economics): Religion numbs people to exploitation

Sigmund Freud (Psychology): Religion meets need for father figure

√âmile Durkheim (Sociology): Religion reinforces group identity

Max Weber (Sociology): Religion influences economic systems

Note: Functionalism doesn't necessarily dismiss religion‚Äîit analyzes what religion does rather than whether beliefs are true.

Spend 2-3 minutes. Emphasize the QUESTION: "What does this DO?" Early functionalists (Marx, Freud) were often hostile critics. Later scholars became more neutral, using functional analysis as one analytical tool among many. Make clear: You can use functionalist analysis without being anti-religious. Many religious scholars use it!

What Does Religion MEAN?

Substantive theorists emphasize:

  • Conscious human intention, emotion, and agency
  • What practitioners think they're doing and why
  • The content of religious ideas and experiences
  • Irreducibility of religious meanings (can't be "explained away")

Key Substantive Thinkers:

Edward Tylor & James Frazer (19th c.): Religion as explanation of phenomena

Rudolf Otto: Religion emerges from numinous experience

Mircea Eliade: Religion cannot be reduced to social/psychological factors

Spend 2 minutes. Emphasize the QUESTION: "What does this MEAN to practitioners?" Preview: "We'll discuss problematic aspects of Tylor/Frazer shortly, and then dive deeper into phenomenology."

Example: A person fasts during Ramadan.

Edward Tylor (1832-1917)

British anthropologist, pioneer in cultural evolution theory. Coined term "animism" to describe belief in spiritual beings. Argued religion evolved from simple to complex forms.

Major work: Primitive Culture (1871). Used term "primitive" to describe "earlier stages"‚Äînow rejected as ethnocentric and colonialist.

×
James Frazer (1854-1941)

Scottish anthropologist and folklorist. Compiled massive cross-cultural study of myth, magic, and ritual.

Major work: The Golden Bough (13 volumes, 1890-1915). Influenced by evolutionary assumptions now considered problematic.

Spend 2 minutes on this slide, then CRITICAL CONTEXT next slide. Set up: "Tylor and Frazer were hugely influential, but we need to understand the problematic assumptions they brought..."

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Context: Why We Study Them

‚ö†Ô∏è Tylor and Frazer used the term "primitive"‚Äîreflecting 19th-century colonial assumptions.

Their evolutionary model is now widely rejected because it:

  • Wrongly assumed European Christianity was the "highest" form
  • Wrongly assumed indigenous traditions were "lesser stages"
  • Reflected colonial attitudes justifying domination
  • Imposed Western categories on all traditions

We study them to understand the history of Religious Studies, NOT because their conclusions are correct.

‚úÖ What we learn:

Cross-cultural comparison (good method)

Attention to symbolism (good insight)

‚ùå What we reject:

Evolutionary hierarchies (ethnocentric)

"Primitive/advanced" language (colonial)

This revision is CRITICAL. Spend 3 minutes. Don't skip the context‚Äîit prevents students from absorbing colonialist assumptions. Emphasize: "We learn from their mistakes as well as their insights. The discipline has evolved by recognizing and correcting these errors." If students ask why we study them at all: "Understanding how the discipline developed‚Äîincluding its mistakes‚Äîmakes us better scholars."

Method #1: Historical-Critical Approach

What It Is:

  • Studies the process of a religion's beginnings, growth, diversity, decline
  • Uses: archaeology, textual analysis, historical contextualization
  • Treats religious texts as historical documents

Example Applications:

  • Dating biblical manuscripts
  • Tracing Buddhist text development across centuries
  • Analyzing how Qur'anic interpretation changed over time

Strengths:

Grounds religion in concrete history; reveals how traditions change

Limitations:

Can miss what religion means to practitioners today

Spend 2 minutes. Frame as ONE tool among many. Quick example: "The Dead Sea Scrolls discovery (1947) revolutionized understanding of early Judaism and the context for early Christianity. Historical-critical method made that possible."

Method #2: History of Religions

(Religionswissenschaft)

What It Is:

  • Studies religion as a social and cultural phenomenon
  • Comparative approach: looks for patterns across traditions
  • Interdisciplinary: anthropology, sociology, history, philology

Examples:

  • Comparing creation myths across cultures
  • Tracing how Buddhism adapted: India ‚Üí China ‚Üí Japan
  • Analyzing how Christianity changed in new cultural contexts
Spend 1.5 minutes. Don't dwell too long‚Äîstudents just need basic awareness. Note: This is comparative but historical (differs from phenomenology which emphasizes empathy/experience).

Method Add-on: Ethnography / Fieldwork

Some scholars study religion by observing and interviewing people in real settings‚Äîhomes, temples, churches, festivals, online communities.

Typical Tools:

  • Participant observation ‚Äî watching, taking fieldnotes, sometimes participating
  • Interviews ‚Äî structured or informal conversations
  • Material/space analysis ‚Äî objects, clothing, food, architecture
  • Reflexivity ‚Äî noticing how you shape what you see

Key takeaway: Ethnography helps you study lived religion‚Äîwhat people actually do, not only what texts or leaders say.

This connects to "lived religion" from Day 2. Ethnography is how scholars access what people actually DO rather than just what texts prescribe. Ethics matter: protecting people, avoiding harm, getting consent.

Method #3: Phenomenology of Religion

Core Principles:

1. Empathetic Standpoint

Describe religions in their own terms from practitioners' perspective

2. Bracketing (Epoch√©)

Temporarily suspend your own beliefs and judgments

3. Irreducibility of the Sacred

Religion cannot be fully explained by psychology, sociology, economics alone

4. Comparative Focus

Seeks universal or essential aspects of religious life

Phenomenology of Religion

An approach that seeks to describe religions empathetically "in their own terms" from the standpoint of practitioners.

Key principles: Epoch√© (bracketing), empathy, irreducibility, comparison.

Major figures: Rudolf Otto, Gerardus van der Leeuw, Mircea Eliade, Ninian Smart.

Critiqued for potentially essentializing religion or smuggling in theological assumptions.

Greek philosophical term meaning "suspension" or "holding back."

Phenomenology in Practice

Imagine: You're watching someone meditate.

Other Approaches Say:

Psychologist: "They're reducing anxiety through controlled breathing."

Sociologist: "They're performing group identity as a Buddhist."

Historian: "This practice developed in ancient India."

Phenomenologist Says:

"Wait‚Äîlet's first understand what the meditator thinks is happening."

‚óÜ What's their experience from the inside?

‚óÜ What do they believe they're doing?

‚óÜ How do they describe the goal/purpose?

The phenomenologist temporarily brackets external explanations to really listen to the practitioner's own perspective.

This concrete example is CRITICAL. Spend 3 minutes. Ask: "Does this make sense? Phenomenology is about understanding experience from the inside, in the terms practitioners themselves use." Acknowledge the challenge: "Total bracketing is impossible‚Äîwe all have assumptions. But the EFFORT to bracket makes us better listeners and more accurate describers."

Mircea Eliade (1907-1986)

Key Phenomenologist

Eliade's Arguments:

  • Irreducibility of the Sacred: Religious phenomena must be understood as uniquely religious
  • Dialectic of Sacred/Profane: The transcendent manifests through ordinary phenomena
  • Universal Symbolic Systems: Common symbols (water, sky, earth) appear across cultures

Major Works: The Myth of the Eternal Return (1949), The Sacred and the Profane (1959)

Mircea Eliade (1907-1986)

Romanian historian of religions and University of Chicago professor. One of the most influential phenomenologists of religion.

Major works: The Myth of the Eternal Return, The Sacred and the Profane, Patterns in Comparative Religion.

Phenomenology: Strengths and Critiques

Strengths:

‚úÖ Takes practitioners seriously on their own terms

‚úÖ Attends to religious experience, not just structures

‚úÖ Resists reductive explanations

‚úÖ Comparative insights across traditions

Critiques of Eliade:

‚ùå Accused of "crypto-theology"‚Äîsmuggling in religious assumptions

‚ùå May essentialize religion (assume universal core)

‚ùå Can ignore power dynamics, history, context

‚ùå "Bracketing" harder than it sounds

Contemporary Approach: Most scholars today use multiple methods‚Äîhistorical, sociological, phenomenological‚Äîrecognizing each has strengths and limitations.

Critical Awareness

Examining our own tools

Transition: "We've examined theories and methods. Now a meta-question: How do we think critically about our OWN analytical frameworks?"

"Map Is Not Territory"

Jonathan Z. Smith wrote: "Map is not territory."

All our frameworks‚Äî"world religions," "myth," "ritual," "functionalism," "phenomenology"‚Äîare maps we've drawn to help navigate complex terrain.

The map is useful, but it's not the same as the actual territory.

üì∏ Image needed: "antique world map with blank spaces"
Suggested caption: Historical map showing the constructed nature of cartography

As we study, ask: Who created this category? What does it highlight and hide? How might practitioners describe their tradition differently?

This methodological self-awareness separates academic study from tourism or prejudice.

×
Jonathan Z. Smith (1938-2017)

American historian of religions, University of Chicago. Known for deconstructing taken-for-granted categories in religious studies.

Major work: Map Is Not Territory (1978). Argued that "religion" is a scholarly construct, not a natural kind.

Influenced postmodern and critical approaches to religious studies.

This is NEW content. Spend 2-3 minutes. Critical thinking skill: examine your own tools, not just the object of study. Brings course full circle back to "world religions paradigm" critique from Day 1. Example: "When we call something a 'myth,' we're using OUR category. The tradition itself might call it 'revealed truth,' 'sacred history,' or have no single label."

Why "Maps" Matter Socially

Definitions of "religion" can shape real lives because institutions use them.

Examples of Power at Work:

  • Governments decide what counts as a religion for legal recognition
  • Schools, courts, prisons decide which practices get protected
  • Media labels some groups "religion," others "cult," "superstition," or "culture"

Analytic move: Ask who benefits from a definition‚Äîand who gets excluded.

This shows why the "map" question isn't just academic‚Äîit has real-world consequences. Example: Native American traditions weren't legally protected as "religion" in the US until 1978 (American Indian Religious Freedom Act). The category "religion" was defined in ways that excluded them.

Digital Religion

The study of how religion operates in and through digital media‚Äîsocial media, streaming, apps, virtual communities, online rituals.

Key questions: How do online and offline religious lives blend? Does virtual community = real community? How do algorithms shape religious identity?

What Counts as Evidence?

‚úÖ Good Evidence:

Primary sources:

Sacred texts, first-hand accounts, historical documents, ethnographic observations

Secondary sources:

Scholarly books, peer-reviewed articles, reputable news sources

‚ùå NOT Good Evidence:

"I think..." or "I feel..." (personal opinion without support)

"Everyone knows..." (unexamined assumptions)

Stereotypes from media or hearsay

Random websites without scholarly credentials

‚ùå Instead of:

"I think Buddhism is peaceful"

‚úÖ Write:

"Scholar X argues that Buddhist ethics emphasize ahimsa (non-harm), as evidenced by texts such as the Dhammapada."

This is NEW content. Spend 2 minutes. Sets expectations for assignments. Students need explicit instruction on evidence‚Äîmany come from high school where "I think" was acceptable.

How to Engage Critically While Respecting People

You may encounter beliefs or practices you find strange, wrong, or troubling. That's okay.

‚ùå Instead of this...

"That's crazy/stupid/backwards"

"They're wrong"

"I can't believe anyone would..."

"That doesn't make sense"

‚úÖ Try this...

"From an outsider's perspective, this seems..."

"A critic might argue..."

"This challenges my assumptions about..."

"This operates according to a different logic..."

‚úÖ Analyze power dynamics, critique ideas with evidence, note contradictions, disagree respectfully

‚ùå Blanket dismissals, assuming your worldview is superior, reducing people to stereotypes

This is NEW content. Spend 2-3 minutes. Models how to be critical without being dismissive. Empowers students to engage rather than staying silent out of fear of offense. Example: "You can say 'This text reflects patriarchal assumptions common in ancient contexts' without saying 'This religion hates women.'"
