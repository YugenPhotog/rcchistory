Begin with the assumption students already carry without knowing it. Most modern people ‚Äî especially in the West ‚Äî believe there is a true self inside: our authentic personality, our real feelings, our private identity. Roles ‚Äî student, employee, child, friend ‚Äî often feel like performances. Something we put on and take off. The common belief is this: the inner self is primary. Social behavior either expresses it or hides it. This is sometimes called the "expressive" model of selfhood. It underlies therapy culture, social media authenticity discourse, and most pop psychology. Teaching tip: Ask students: "Have you ever felt like your 'real self' was different from the role you were playing?" They will all say yes. That shared assumption is exactly what Confucius is going to challenge.

Fingarette's Reversal

Confucius sees the self differently

There is no private soul behind social roles.

Herbert Fingarette argues that the self is not something hidden inside the individual.

The person exists in and through relationships.

Identity is relational, not private.

Herbert Fingarette (1921‚Äì2018)

American philosopher at UC Santa Barbara. His 1972 book Confucius: The Secular as Sacred transformed Western understanding of Confucian ethics. Fingarette argued that Confucius presents a radical alternative to both Western individualism and classical virtue ethics ‚Äî a fully relational account of the human person.

Fingarette's argument is the philosophical engine of this entire lecture. He argues that Confucius does not see social roles as performances layered over a private self. Instead: the person IS the pattern of roles they enact in relation to others. Who you are is not defined by private feelings or hidden motives. Who you are is defined by the pattern of conduct you enact with other people. This is a complete reversal of the modern picture. Modern model: Inner self ‚Üí produces ‚Üí outward behavior Confucian model: Enacted behavior in relationships ‚Üí constitutes ‚Üí the self Teaching tip: Use the pop-up to briefly introduce Fingarette before moving on. Students often ask "who said this?"

The Goal: Becoming Fully Human

Confucius is not concerned with self-expression. His goal is the formation of the junzi ‚Äî the exemplary person.

  • Keeping commitments
  • Showing appropriate respect
  • Fulfilling responsibilities
  • Acting rightly toward others

Character is not hidden. Character is what other people reliably experience.

Junzi ÂêõÂ≠ê

Often translated "gentleman," "exemplary person," or "superior person." In Confucian thought, the junzi is not a social rank but a moral achievement ‚Äî a person who has cultivated ren (humaneness) and consistently expresses it through li (ritual propriety) in all relationships. The junzi is recognizable by conduct, not by private virtue.

Confucius is not asking: "Have you found your authentic self?" He is asking: "Have you become the kind of person your relationships call you to be?" The junzi ‚Äî exemplary person, superior person, fully realized human ‚Äî is the goal of Confucian self-cultivation. What defines a junzi is not inner emotion, private intention, or hidden virtue. The junzi is defined by consistent conduct: ‚Äî keeping commitments ‚Äî showing respect ‚Äî fulfilling responsibilities ‚Äî acting appropriately toward others Critical line for students: "In Confucian thought, character is not something hidden. Character is what other people reliably experience." This is deeply different from the modern idea that someone might be "a good person inside" despite behaving badly. Confucius would say: if you consistently behave badly, that IS your character. Use the pop-up to define junzi. Students often need to see the Chinese characters alongside the English translation.

Roles Are Not Masks

Acting differently in different situations is not hypocrisy

Students often worry: if I adjust my behavior for different people, am I being fake?

The exemplary person knows how to act appropriately in each relationship. This flexibility reflects moral awareness, not dishonesty.

Ren ‰ªÅ ‚Äî Humaneness / Benevolence

The central virtue in Confucian ethics ‚Äî often translated as humaneness, benevolence, or goodness. Ren is not an abstract principle but a disposition that is only realized in concrete relationships. You cannot possess ren in isolation; it is enacted in how you treat other people.

×
Li Á¶Æ ‚Äî Ritual Propriety

The ceremonial norms, ritual practices, and codes of appropriate conduct that structure relationships in Confucian society. Li includes everything from formal ceremonies to everyday greetings. For Confucius, li is not mere convention ‚Äî it is the form through which ren (humaneness) becomes visible in the world.

Students often flag this as a contradiction: "If I act differently with different people, doesn't that make me a hypocrite?" Confucius would say the opposite. The exemplary person knows that the appropriate way to speak to a professor, a close friend, and someone who is grieving are all different. This flexibility is not inauthenticity. It is moral sensitivity ‚Äî the ability to express ren (humaneness) through the appropriate form of li (ritual propriety) for each relationship. The word "appropriate" is key. Li is not rigid conformity; it requires judgment. Modern analogy: We all know it is not rude to speak differently at a job interview than at a bar with friends. Confucius is making the same observation ‚Äî but extending it into a full account of moral formation. Use both pop-ups here: ren and li are the two key terms for the entire Confucianism unit.

How the Self Is Formed

In Confucian thought, character grows through practice

Modern assumption:
Inner character ‚Üí produces ‚Üí outward behavior

Confucian view:
Outward behavior, repeated ‚Üí shapes ‚Üí character

The self is not discovered.
The self is cultivated through repeated participation in relationships.

üì∏ slide-self-formation-practice.jpg

slide-self-formation-practice.jpg

This is the key slide ‚Äî the deepest difference between modern psychology and Confucian thought. Modern thinking assumes: inner character produces outward behavior. ‚Äî "I'm a generous person, so I give to charity." ‚Äî "I'm honest, so I tell the truth." Confucius inverts this: ‚Äî You do not act respectfully because you already possess a respectful nature. ‚Äî You become respectful by practicing the forms of li again and again. Over time, these practices become natural. The habits become dispositions. The dispositions form the person. Aristotle made a similar argument in the Nicomachean Ethics: we become just by doing just acts, brave by doing brave acts. The Confucian version is more socially embedded ‚Äî it is always about practices within relationships, not individual virtuous acts. Key line for students: "The self is not discovered. The self is cultivated through repeated participation in relationships." Teaching tip: Connect to muscle memory ‚Äî athletes and musicians practice forms until they become natural. Confucian self-cultivation works the same way, but morally.

Fingarette's Most Challenging Claim

There is no hidden moral identity behind your behavior.

Your daily actions are not simply expressing who you are.

They are forming who you are.

There is no separate backstage self that exists apart from your behavior.

The pattern of roles you live out is the person you are becoming.

üì∏ slide-no-backstage-self.jpg

slide-no-backstage-self.jpg

This is Fingarette's most provocative claim ‚Äî and the one students resist most. There is no private moral identity separate from your conduct. Your daily actions are not simply expressing who you are ‚Äî they are forming who you are. There is no separate backstage self that exists apart from your behavior. The modern temptation: "I'm a good person inside, even if I sometimes act badly." Confucius: That is a consoling fiction. The pattern of what you actually do IS who you are becoming. This is why li ‚Äî ritual and proper conduct ‚Äî is not superficial for Confucius. Ritual is not mere ceremony layered over the real you. Ritual IS the training ground of the self. Fingarette's phrase: "Confucius sees the entire world of human conduct as a vast system of li ‚Äî of ceremony and ritual ‚Äî and the moral life as the task of learning to perform this ceremony well." Teaching tip: Ask: "If there's no backstage self ‚Äî no private moral identity ‚Äî is there such a thing as being a good person in secret?" Let students wrestle with this before moving to the closing slide.
