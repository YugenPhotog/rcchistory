Part I

Terminology & Theory

What is history, and how do we study it?

üìñ Section Overview

This section establishes the basic vocabulary students need to engage with historiographical debates. We'll define "history" and "historiography" and explore how historians think about their craft.

What is History?

üìñ Facilitator Instructions

üéØ Common Student Responses

Students often say things like "what happened in the past" or "studying old events." Use these as springboards to introduce the more nuanced definitions on the following slides.

Defining History

"This then is 'history': a true story of something that happened long ago, retold in the present. The past is brought to life once more..."
"History is a story about the past that is significant and true."

üìñ Understanding This Slide

These definitions highlight two crucial aspects of history: it involves narrative (storytelling) and claims to truth. History isn't just random facts‚Äîit's organized into meaningful stories that help us understand the past.

üí° Key Insight

Notice the phrase "retold in the present." History is always written by people living NOW, looking backward. This means our present concerns shape how we tell stories about the past.

History as Narrative

  • Narrative ‚Äî "True Stories"
  • History tells stories about the past
  • "The Past is a foreign country"
  • History begins and ends with questions
×

Narrative

A narrative is a connected account of events‚Äîa story with a beginning, middle, and end. Unlike a simple list of facts, a narrative explains why things happened and how they connect to each other.

Historians don't just collect facts; they organize them into meaningful stories that help us understand causation and change over time.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

This slide introduces the concept of history as narrative‚Äîorganized storytelling about the past. The phrase "the past is a foreign country" comes from novelist L.P. Hartley and reminds us that people in the past thought, felt, and acted differently than we do today.

üí° Key Point

History is a process, not just a collection of facts. It begins with questions (Why did this happen? What changed?) and ends with new questions generated by our investigations.

üìö Source

Arnold, John H. History: A Brief Insight. Sterling Publishing, 2000.

What is Historiography?

-ology: The principles, theory, and history of historical writing

-graphy: Writing of history based on the critical examination of sources

üîë Core Concept

"History is an argument about the past!"

üìñ Understanding This Slide

Historiography is the study of how history is written. It examines the methods historians use, the theories that guide their interpretations, and how historical understanding has changed over time.

üí° Key Insight

The phrase "history is an argument about the past" is crucial. Historians don't simply report facts‚Äîthey make arguments about what happened, why it mattered, and what it means. Different historians can look at the same evidence and reach different conclusions.

üéØ Student Takeaway

Understanding historiography helps you become a critical consumer of historical claims. You can ask: What is this historian arguing? What evidence do they use? What other interpretations exist?

Part II

Conceptual Frameworks

How does history "work"?

üìñ Section Overview

Now we move from definitions to the big theoretical frameworks historians use to explain historical change. Students will learn about different "engines of change"‚Äîthe forces that drive historical development.

Why Do Historians Revise?

  • Each new generation writes their own history
  • History is always written from a particular vantage point
  • New questions emerge; priorities change
  • New information comes to light
üí° Critical Thinking

Students must understand historiography to analyze and evaluate historical thought!

üìñ Understanding This Slide

Historical interpretation isn't static‚Äîit changes over time. Each generation asks different questions based on their own concerns. For example, historians in the 1950s during the Cold War emphasized different aspects of American history than historians today.

üí° Examples

Cold War era: Historians emphasized consensus, American exceptionalism, and the triumph of democracy.

Today: Historians pay more attention to marginalized voices, conflict, and questioning national myths.

üéì Scholarly Context

Peter Novick's That Noble Dream (1988) traces how the American historical profession's commitment to "objectivity" has been debated and contested throughout the 20th century.

Conceptual Frameworks

What Are They?

An inter-connecting set of ideas that shapes and supports how we perceive history

The central question: What is the engine of change?

üìñ Understanding This Slide

A conceptual framework is like a lens through which historians view the past. It provides a set of assumptions about what matters most in driving historical change.

üí° Analogy

Think of it like glasses with different colored lenses. An economist looking at history might see everything through the lens of material conditions and markets. A cultural historian might focus on ideas, beliefs, and symbols. Same past, different emphases.

üéØ Key Question

The "engine of change" question asks: What drives historical development? What forces cause societies to transform over time?

Assumptions About Change

Linear

Moving in a straight line through time

Evolutionary

Evolving and changing over time

Progressing

Moving forward; getting "better"

üìñ Understanding This Slide

Many Western historians have assumed that history moves in a linear direction (past ‚Üí present ‚Üí future), that it evolves (things change), and that it progresses (things get better over time).

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Point

These assumptions are not universal! Many cultures view history as cyclical rather than linear. And the idea that things always "progress" toward improvement is contested‚Äîthink about the environmental destruction or warfare of the modern era.

ü§î Discussion

Is history really "progress"? For whom? These assumptions often reflect the perspectives of dominant groups.

Rational Human Model

Core Assumption

Most people set rational goals for themselves and seek to achieve them through the exercise of reason and logic.

  • Engine of Change: Human Agency
  • Attacked by determinist theories, such as Marxism
×

Determinist Theories

Determinist theories argue that history is shaped primarily by forces beyond individual human control‚Äîwhether economic systems, social structures, or psychological drives.

These theories challenge the Rational Human Model by arguing that individuals are constrained by larger historical forces (economics, class, culture) that limit their "rational" choices.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

The Rational Human Model assumes that individuals are the primary drivers of historical change. People make rational decisions, and history is the sum total of these individual choices.

üí° Example

This framework might explain the American Revolution by focusing on the rational calculations of founders like Jefferson and Madison‚Äîthey weighed the costs and benefits of independence and made a reasoned choice.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critique

Critics argue this model overestimates individual freedom and underestimates how social structures (class, race, gender, economics) shape what choices are even available to people.

Determinism

Core Assumption

History is shaped primarily by forces beyond individual human agency

Human choices matter, but are constrained by deeper forces:

  • Karl Marx ‚Äî material conditions / class conflict
  • Sigmund Freud ‚Äî unconscious drives / repression
×

Karl Marx (1818-1883)

German philosopher and economist who argued that material conditions‚Äîespecially the relationship between those who own the means of production and those who labor‚Äîare the primary engine of historical change.

For Marx, ideas and politics are shaped by underlying economic structures. History moves through stages (feudalism ‚Üí capitalism ‚Üí socialism) driven by class conflict.

×

Sigmund Freud (1856-1939)

Austrian neurologist and founder of psychoanalysis who argued that unconscious drives‚Äîespecially repressed desires and childhood experiences‚Äîshape human behavior.

Applied to history, Freudian analysis suggests that cultural and political developments may be driven by psychological forces that people aren't consciously aware of.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

Determinism argues that larger forces‚Äîeconomic systems, social structures, psychological drives‚Äîshape history more than individual choices. People still make decisions, but those decisions are constrained by factors beyond their control.

üí° Key Distinction

Determinism doesn't mean people have no agency. It means their agency operates within limits set by larger structures. Think of it like a chess game: you can choose your moves, but you can't change the rules of chess.

üéì Scholarly Context

Marx's famous quote: "Men make their own history, but they do not make it as they please; they do not make it under self-selected circumstances, but under circumstances existing already."

Great Man Theory

Core Assumption

Every so often, a great* man will arise and alter the course of history.

  • A form of the Rational Human Model
  • Focuses on exceptional individuals as engines of change
  • *"Great" meaning significant or influential‚Äînot necessarily "good"

üìñ Understanding This Slide

The Great Man Theory was popularized by Scottish philosopher Thomas Carlyle in the 19th century. It argues that history is shaped primarily by the actions of exceptional individuals‚Äîleaders, geniuses, heroes‚Äîwhose unique qualities allow them to bend the course of events.

üí° Examples

Traditional biographies of figures like Napoleon, Lincoln, or Churchill often reflect this framework‚Äîtreating history as the story of great individuals who shaped their eras.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critiques

  • Ignores the contributions of ordinary people
  • Often focuses exclusively on elite white men
  • Underestimates structural forces that enable or constrain leaders
  • The "great men" were shaped by their times too!

üìù Check Your Understanding

Quick Reflection

Which framework seems most convincing to you?

  • Rational Human Model (individual choices drive change)
  • Determinism (larger forces constrain choices)
  • Great Man Theory (exceptional individuals shape history)
  • Some combination of these?

üìñ Facilitator Notes

This is a good moment to pause and check student understanding. You can do this as a quick show of hands, a think-pair-share, or a brief written reflection.

üí° Discussion Points

Most historians today use some combination of these frameworks. The question is always: what's the right balance between individual agency and structural forces for understanding any particular historical event?

Key Takeaways

  • History = narrative accounts of the past that claim truth and significance
  • Historiography = the study of how history is written and interpreted
  • History is constantly revised as perspectives and questions change
  • Conceptual frameworks shape how historians understand causation
  • Different frameworks emphasize different engines of change
