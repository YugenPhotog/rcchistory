Hoover and FDR's Folly

The Great Depression and the Limits of Policy

HIST 102 · Chapter 25 · Lecture 1

📖 Understanding This Slide

Welcome to one of the most contested arguments in American economic history. Notice the subtitle: "The Limits of Policy." This lecture is not going to tell you that Hoover was a villain and FDR was a hero — that's the story most of you already know. Instead, it's going to do something harder and more interesting: it's going to make both men more complicated, and more human, than the myth allows.

Why This Matters

The title uses the word "Folly" — a deliberate provocation. A folly is not a crime; it's a mistake made by someone who should have known better. The lecture argues that both Hoover and FDR made consequential errors — not because they were evil or incompetent, but because neither man fully understood the nature of the catastrophe he was inhabiting. Understanding that distinction between moral failure and historical tragedy is the intellectual payoff of this lecture.

Section I

The Myth Students Bring In

History is not a morality play — it is a stress test

📖 Understanding This Slide

This opening section sets the intellectual ground rules for the entire lecture. The phrase "stress test" is precisely chosen: engineers use stress tests to find where a structure breaks under pressure. The Great Depression was exactly that kind of test — it exposed the breaking points in the governing assumptions that both Hoover and FDR brought to Washington.

Why This Matters

Most students arrive in this classroom having already been told who the good guys and bad guys are. The danger of that framework is that it stops you from asking the most historically productive question: why did each man make the choices he made? When you replace moral judgment with historical understanding, you get a much richer — and more usable — account of the past.

The Story You've Heard

The Villain
  • Hoover was heartless and inactive
  • Watched the nation suffer and did nothing
  • Caused the Great Depression
The Hero
  • FDR saved the nation
  • The New Deal ended the Depression
  • Bold action = recovery
This lecture dismantles that framework — in both directions

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the myth laid out in its most vivid form — and it's a myth that contains just enough truth to be dangerous. The lecture doesn't deny that Hoover failed and FDR achieved important things. What it insists on is that this simple villain/hero binary prevents you from understanding why either outcome happened.

Breaking It Down

The Villain narrative: As you'll see shortly, Hoover was actually quite active — he rejected his Treasury Secretary's advice to do nothing, ran the largest peacetime deficit in U.S. history to that point, and launched more public works projects than the previous thirty years combined. The "do-nothing" label is almost entirely false as a description of policy.

The Hero narrative: FDR did end the psychological crisis of abandonment — and that was genuinely important. But as the unemployment numbers will show, the Depression was still producing 14.6% unemployment in 1940, after seven years of New Deal programs. The Depression did not end because of the New Deal.

Why This Matters

The bottom line of this slide — "dismantles that framework in both directions" — is the key phrase. The lecture is not defending Hoover and attacking FDR. It is complicating both, because the truth is more useful to you as a historical thinker than the myth.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Joan Hoff Wilson – Herbert Hoover: Forgotten Progressive

Wilson was one of the first historians to push back hard against the do-nothing myth, arguing that Hoover was actually a progressive technocrat whose governing philosophy was genuinely innovative — but simply overwhelmed by a crisis far larger than anything his framework anticipated. Her rehabilitation of Hoover's pre-presidential record is now standard in the historiography.

The Governing Premise

  • Both men inherited philosophies built for different eras
  • Neither fully understood the crisis they inhabited
  • Our goal: replace moral judgment with historical understanding

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the single most important idea in the lecture, and it's worth reading the callout box twice: "History rewards fit between ideas and moments — not good intentions." Think of it like a key and a lock. Hoover had a perfectly good key — but the Depression had changed the lock. His philosophy wasn't wrong in some abstract sense; it was wrong for this specific, unprecedented crisis.

Breaking It Down

Philosophies built for different eras: Both Hoover and FDR were formed as thinkers and leaders in the Progressive Era of the 1910s, when the economic challenges were fundamentally different — trusts, monopolies, financial instability, but no large-scale demand collapse. Neither man had a ready intellectual framework for what an industrial economy looks like when consumer spending simply stops.

Neither fully understood the crisis: This is a key phrase. It doesn't say they were incompetent or malicious — it says the crisis was genuinely unprecedented. The Great Depression was unlike any previous American downturn in scale, duration, and mechanism. Economists were still arguing about what was causing it while it was happening.

Why This Matters

The goal stated at the bottom — replace moral judgment with historical understanding — is the methodology of good history. When you ask "was this person good or bad," you stop learning. When you ask "why did this person make this choice, given what they knew and believed," you start learning. That shift in question is what this lecture is training you to make.

What the Standard Story Gets Right

  • Banking reform — FDIC ended the bank-run terror; no Depression-scale failures since
  • Infrastructure — TVA, Hoover Dam, roads, bridges, rural electrification still in use
  • Social Security (1935) — America's first federal old-age pension; permanent safety net
  • Federal responsibility — "Washington must act" became the new American consensus
These are real, measurable, durable — the overwhelming majority of scholars still recognize them

📖 Understanding This Slide

Before the lecture dismantles the myth, it wants to be scrupulously fair. The standard story does get important things right — and this slide is where the lecture acknowledges them directly. The critique that follows does not erase these genuine achievements.

Breaking It Down

FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation): Created in 1933, the FDIC insures bank deposits — meaning if your bank fails, your money is protected up to a limit. Before the FDIC, bank failures wiped out depositors entirely. Bank runs — where everyone panicked and tried to withdraw at once — were a self-fulfilling catastrophe. The FDIC broke that mechanism. It still exists and still works today.

Social Security (1935): America had no federal pension system before this. Older Americans who couldn't work faced destitution. Social Security created a permanent floor. It remains the single largest federal expenditure and the most popular government program in American history.

Federal responsibility: Perhaps the most lasting change of all. Before 1933, Americans broadly accepted that the federal government had no role in fighting economic downturns. After 1933, that changed permanently — every subsequent president, regardless of party, has been expected to respond to economic crises with federal action.

Why This Matters

The lecture's intellectual integrity depends on this slide. It would be easy to construct a one-sided critique of the New Deal. Instead, the lecture insists you hold both truths simultaneously: the New Deal produced lasting, genuinely beneficial structural reforms AND it failed to end the Depression. Both things are true. Learning to hold competing truths in tension is what historical thinking actually demands.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Having acknowledged the New Deal's real achievements, the lecture now introduces the questions that complicate the hero narrative. The four bullet points are essentially the lecture's research agenda — they tell you what to look for as the argument unfolds. And the number at the bottom — 14.6% unemployment in 1940 — is the anchor fact you should carry through the entire lecture.

Breaking It Down

14.6% unemployment in 1940: This is seven years after FDR took office. Seven years of New Deal programs, alphabet agencies, infrastructure spending, and regulatory reform. And still nearly one in seven American workers was unemployed. This number is the single most important counterweight to the "FDR saved us" narrative. Keep it in mind.

Who paid the political price? The New Deal coalition depended on Southern Democrats, who controlled key committee chairmanships in Congress. To keep their support, FDR allowed New Deal programs to discriminate against Black Americans — through exclusions from Social Security coverage, discriminatory implementation of the AAA, and the absence of anti-lynching legislation. The political cost was borne by the most vulnerable members of the supposed beneficiary class.

Why This Matters

The photograph of the Chicago soup kitchen — famously organized by Al Capone — captures the human reality behind the statistics. These are not abstractions. Real people were standing in real lines, in a real American city, in the middle of the supposedly transformative New Deal era. The 14.6% figure is not just a number — it represents millions of families still without income after seven years of promised recovery.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

If a policy creates lasting structural reforms but does not end the crisis it was designed to solve — does it succeed or fail?

What would "success" even mean here?

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is a genuine philosophical puzzle, not a trick question. The New Deal created the FDIC, Social Security, the SEC, and the Wagner Act — all of which are still operating and benefiting Americans today. It also left 14.6% of Americans unemployed in 1940. How do you evaluate that record?

Thinking It Through

One approach: judge by the stated goal. If the goal was to end the Depression, the New Deal failed. Another approach: judge by lasting institutional change. By that measure, the New Deal was enormously successful. A third approach: ask who benefited and who didn't. The New Deal benefited white industrial workers enormously while systematically excluding Black agricultural workers and domestic servants. "Success" looks very different depending on whose experience you center.

There is no single correct answer here — and that's the point. History doesn't offer clean verdicts. It offers competing frameworks for evaluation, each of which reveals something true and hides something else.

Section II

Herbert Hoover

The Last President of the Old Moral Economy

📖 Understanding This Slide

The subtitle — "The Last President of the Old Moral Economy" — is doing a lot of work. The word "moral" is key: Hoover's governing philosophy wasn't simply an economic theory. It was a theory about character, citizenship, and the right relationship between individuals and government. He genuinely believed that federal relief would corrupt the moral fiber of Americans. Understanding that belief is essential to understanding his choices.

Why This Matters

The phrase "last president" is also significant. Hoover stood at the end of a long tradition of American governance that assumed the federal government's job was to keep the field level, not to intervene in the economy on behalf of the suffering. After him, that tradition was gone. He was not an anomaly — he was the culmination of an entire era of American political thought.

Who Was Herbert Hoover?

  • Born 1874 — Quaker family, orphaned at 9
  • Made a fortune in global mining engineering
Not a politician by instinct — a technocrat who believed problems could be solved
📸 Herbert Hoover portrait

Herbert Hoover · c. 1928 · NARA · Public Domain

📖 Understanding This Slide

The man the Depression would destroy was, by 1920, the most celebrated American alive. This is not hyperbole — contemporaries genuinely said it. Understanding the Hoover who entered the White House in 1929 requires you to set aside everything you know about the Hoover who left in 1933 in disgrace.

Breaking It Down

Quaker family, orphaned at 9: Hoover's Quaker upbringing shaped everything about his governing philosophy. Quaker faith emphasizes community service, voluntary cooperation, personal responsibility, and the moral dangers of dependency. These were not political talking points for Hoover — they were religious convictions.

10 million Belgians: During World War I, Belgium was under German military occupation and facing mass starvation. Hoover organized the Commission for Relief in Belgium, navigating the demands of both the German occupiers and the British naval blockade to deliver food to a civilian population. He did this as a private citizen, not a government official. The scale of the operation was staggering — and it made him an international hero.

"The Great Engineer": Click the popup trigger to read more. The nickname reflects both his professional background and his reputation as a man who could apply rational, systematic problem-solving to any challenge. He was seen as the embodiment of American pragmatic genius.

Why This Matters

The man who "did nothing" during the Depression was the man who had personally fed ten million people during a world war. The contradiction between those two images is the central puzzle of Section II. His failure during the Depression was not a failure of compassion — it was a failure of framework.

Hoover's Social Thought

  • Directed the American Relief Administration — 4 million tons to 23 countries
  • Wilson's war-team — absorbed philosophy of forceful government planning
  • "The Great Engineer" — the most admired man in the world, 1920
  • Commerce Secretary under Harding and Coolidge — transformed a backwater agency into Washington's most dynamic department
The most activist Commerce Secretary in history — not an opponent of government action
📸 U.S. Food Administration poster · 1917

U.S. Food Administration · 1917 · Public Domain · Wikimedia Commons

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide complicates the standard picture of Hoover as a passive, laissez-faire ideologue who refused to act. The record before 1929 shows the opposite: Hoover was one of the most energetic, interventionist administrators in American government. He believed deeply in organized, coordinated action — he just believed the right vehicle for it was voluntary cooperation, not federal coercion.

Breaking It Down

American Relief Administration: After World War I, Hoover directed the ARA, a U.S. government agency tasked with delivering food relief to a shattered Europe. Between 1919 and 1923 the ARA delivered more than four million tons of supplies to 23 war-torn countries, including post-revolutionary Russia — where it fed nearly 11 million people daily during a catastrophic famine. This was one of the largest humanitarian operations in history to that point, and Hoover ran it with characteristic efficiency.

Wilson's war-team: Hoover served as U.S. Food Administrator during World War I under Woodrow Wilson — a role that put him at the center of the wartime government's massive effort to coordinate and direct American agricultural production. He saw firsthand what organized federal effort could accomplish. The difference between Hoover and FDR was not that Hoover opposed government action — it was that Hoover believed voluntary coordination was morally and practically superior to legal compulsion.

Commerce Secretary — backwater to powerhouse: Under both Harding and Coolidge, Hoover transformed the Commerce Department from what contemporaries called a "sleepy backwater responsible for lighthouses and fisheries" into the most dynamic agency in Washington. He created sub-departments, standardized industries, regulated early aviation and radio, and coordinated economic data across the entire federal government. Colleagues dubbed him "Secretary of Commerce and Under-Secretary of all other departments." He was, by any measure, a builder of federal capacity — not its enemy.

Why This Matters

The "Food Will Win the War" poster beside this slide is Hoover's own product — the U.S. Food Administration's campaign asked American families to voluntarily reduce consumption to feed Allied troops and civilians. It worked. Millions of families observed "Wheatless Mondays" and "Meatless Tuesdays" because Hoover persuaded them to — without a single coercive law. That success confirmed his deepest conviction: that voluntary action, properly organized, was more powerful than compulsion. The Depression would test whether that conviction had limits.

Hoover's Governing Philosophy

What He Believed
  • Voluntarism — cooperation, not coercion
  • Associationalism — business, government, civic bodies working together
  • Cash relief to individuals would corrupt self-reliance — loans to banks and public works did not
  • Democratic citizenship required self-reliance
In Context
  • These were mainstream progressive ideas of the 1910s–20s

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide does something most textbooks refuse to do: it takes Hoover's governing philosophy seriously on its own terms, and then refuses to let the Depression's outcome settle the question of whether he was wrong. The standard story treats Hoover's laissez-faire instincts as obviously discredited by events. The revisionist reading — supported by serious economic historians — argues that the interventions themselves prevented us from ever finding out whether self-correction was possible.

Breaking It Down

Voluntarism (click the popup): The idea that social problems should be solved through voluntary cooperation — neighbors helping neighbors, businesses pledging to maintain wages, civic organizations running relief efforts. Hoover believed this was morally superior to federal coercion because it preserved individual dignity and democratic self-government.

Associationalism (click the popup): As Commerce Secretary, Hoover had used trade associations, industry groups, and voluntary standards bodies to coordinate economic activity without direct government mandates. It worked — standardizing products, reducing waste, improving efficiency across industries. His pre-1929 record genuinely warranted the enormous expectations Americans placed on him.

The Depression of 1920–21: This is the historical precedent that anchors the revisionist argument. In 1920–21, the United States experienced a severe deflationary crash — unemployment hit roughly 11.7%, industrial production collapsed, prices fell sharply. The Harding administration and Treasury essentially did nothing. Within eighteen months the economy had fully recovered and entered the prosperity of the 1920s. If the same non-interventionist approach had been applied in 1929, would the outcome have been similar? The question cannot be answered — but it cannot be dismissed either.

Mellon's liquidationist advice: Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon counseled Hoover to "liquidate labor, liquidate stocks, liquidate the farmers, liquidate real estate" — allow the crash to purge the malinvestments of the 1920s, let wages and prices find their natural bottom, and let the market reorganize itself. Hoover rejected this advice and intervened actively: wage pledges, the RFC, public works spending, and ultimately the catastrophic Revenue Act of 1932. FDR then doubled down on intervention with the NRA's price floors, the AAA's crop destruction, and punitive taxation of corporate investment. Both men refused to let the market clear. The liquidationist position is easy to caricature as callous — but the serious economic argument behind it deserves honest engagement.

"Whether 1929 would have self-corrected — we cannot know": This is the epistemically honest position. The interventions that followed the crash — Smoot-Hawley, the Revenue Act, the NRA codes, the Fed's contraction of the money supply — create an insurmountable counterfactual problem. We cannot run the experiment without the interventions. What we can say is that the mechanisms that transformed a sharp recession into a decade-long depression were substantially policy-induced, not structurally inevitable.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "the interventions make the counterfactual permanently inaccessible" — is the most important analytical sentence on this slide. It reframes the standard narrative: the Depression's severity does not prove that laissez-faire was wrong, because we never had laissez-faire. We had Hoover's interventionism, then FDR's interventionism, and the Depression lasted through both. Students who absorb that point are thinking like historians, not consuming a morality play.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Milton Friedman & Anna Schwartz — A Monetary History of the United States (1963)

Friedman and Schwartz's landmark study argued that the Depression became the Great Depression primarily because the Federal Reserve contracted the money supply by roughly a third between 1929 and 1933 — a catastrophic policy failure, not a structural inevitability. On this reading, the crash itself was not uniquely uncorrectable; it was the Fed's intervention — tightening credit precisely when the economy needed liquidity — that turned a severe recession into a decade-long catastrophe. Ben Bernanke acknowledged this directly at Friedman's 90th birthday in 2002, telling him: "You're right, we did it — we won't do it again." Friedman's analysis implicitly rehabilitates the instinct behind Mellon's advice (don't intervene destructively) while indicting the specific institution that intervened most harmfully.

Ellis Hawley — Herbert Hoover as Secretary of Commerce

Hawley showed that associationalism was a genuinely innovative governing philosophy — a "third way" between laissez-faire capitalism and European-style state socialism. His work explains why so many contemporaries expected Hoover to be a transformative president: the pre-1929 record genuinely warranted that expectation. Read alongside Friedman, Hawley's Hoover looks less like a man with the wrong ideas and more like a man whose ideas were overtaken by an institutional failure — the Fed — that his philosophy had no mechanism to address.

Murray Rothbard — America's Great Depression (1963)

Rothbard made the most systematic case that Hoover was not a do-nothing president but an interventionist one — and that his interventions, particularly his pressure on businesses to maintain wages above market-clearing levels, prevented the price and wage adjustments that would have allowed recovery. Rothbard's Austrian framework is more heterodox than Friedman's monetarism, but his core empirical point — that Hoover actively intervened and that those interventions prolonged the downturn — is broadly accepted by revisionist historians across the political spectrum.

  • Stock bought on margin — up to 90% borrowed
  • Consumer spending slows → margin calls → forced selling
  • Self-reinforcing collapse through October–November 1929

📸 Wall Street 1929

Wall Street, October 1929 · Public Domain

📖 Understanding This Slide

The Wall Street Crash of 1929 was not a random event — it was the inevitable result of a decade of speculation built on borrowed money. Think of it like a house of cards: each card was individually lightweight, but the structure required every card to stay in place. When consumer spending slowed and the bottom cards started slipping, the entire structure collapsed at once.

Breaking It Down

Buying on margin: In the 1920s, you could buy $100 worth of stock for as little as $10 of your own money — the other $90 was borrowed from your broker. This was called buying "on margin." It worked magnificently when prices rose. But when prices fell, your broker issued a "margin call" — demanding you repay the loan. To get cash, you had to sell stock, which drove prices down further, which triggered more margin calls, which forced more selling. A feedback loop of ruin.

Hoover did not cause the crash: This point is important. The speculative bubble was built throughout the 1920s, under Calvin Coolidge's administration and with the Federal Reserve's cooperation. Hoover had actually expressed concern about speculative excess before taking office. Blaming him for the crash itself is historically inaccurate.

Why This Matters

The key phrase is "his response would shape the decade." Hoover inherited a crisis not of his making — but what he chose to do about it, and what he refused to do, would determine whether a serious recession became the Great Depression. That's where his historical responsibility lies.

Hoover's Response

The Interventions
  • Rejected Mellon's "liquidationist" advice
  • Called business leaders to White House — secured voluntary wage pledges
  • Ran a $2.7 billion peacetime deficit — largest in American history to that point
The Counterfactual
  • The Depression of 1920–21 self-corrected — without federal intervention
  • Mellon: liquidate, let prices find bottom — both Hoover and FDR refused
  • Whether 1929 would have self-corrected we cannot know

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide does two things simultaneously: it demolishes the "do-nothing" myth about Hoover, and it refuses to let the Depression's outcome settle whether intervention was the right response. The left column is the evidence against the caricature. The right column is the question the evidence raises. Hold both columns in view at the same time — they belong together.

Breaking It Down

Rejected Mellon's "liquidationist" advice (click the popup): Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon argued the Depression should be allowed to run its course — let banks fail, let prices fall, let the market clear itself. Hoover found this morally intolerable and refused. This decision is usually presented as obviously correct. The right column asks whether it was.

Voluntary wage pledges: Hoover convened major business leaders and extracted promises to maintain wages rather than cutting them. This reflected his voluntarist philosophy — but it worked only as long as businesses remained solvent. As the Depression deepened, the pledges collapsed and the wage floors Hoover had maintained became a mechanism for unemployment: businesses that couldn't cut wages simply laid workers off instead.

Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC): A federal lending institution that pumped $1.2 billion into banks and railroads to prevent collapse. This was genuine federal intervention — institutional, not individual — consistent with Hoover's distinction between lending to businesses and providing a dole to individuals. FDR not only kept the RFC but expanded it massively after taking office.

$2.7 billion peacetime deficit: Hoover ran deficit spending — the opposite of what the "do-nothing" myth implies. The scale was simply insufficient given the magnitude of the collapse. Whether larger deficits would have helped or whether they would have crowded out private investment is a separate question the slide leaves open.

The Depression of 1920–21: In 1920–21, the United States suffered a severe deflationary crash — unemployment hit roughly 11.7%, industrial production collapsed, prices fell sharply. The Harding administration and Treasury essentially did nothing. Within eighteen months the economy had fully recovered and entered the prosperity of the 1920s. This is the empirical foundation of the revisionist argument: downturns had self-corrected before, recently, and without federal management.

"We cannot know": Mellon's advice was rejected. Hoover intervened. Then FDR intervened far more aggressively. The Depression lasted through both sets of interventions. That sequence does not prove Mellon was right — but it does mean we never ran the experiment he proposed. The counterfactual is permanently closed. Responsible historical analysis acknowledges that closure rather than pretending the Depression's severity proves intervention was necessary.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — more public works under Hoover than in the previous thirty years combined — is the empirical core of the lecture's revisionist case. He was not passive. He intervened actively and at unprecedented peacetime scale. The Depression outlasted his interventions anyway, and then outlasted FDR's far larger ones too, until the war spending that neither man was willing to replicate in peacetime finally ended it. That is the sequence the rest of the lecture will follow.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide puts specific dollar figures on Hoover's interventions — and those figures are the most effective single argument against the "do-nothing" myth. More than $4 billion in federal money moved into the economy under Hoover before FDR took the oath of office. For context, the entire federal budget in 1929 was roughly $3.1 billion. Hoover was spending at a scale that had no peacetime precedent. The question the slide raises but does not answer: was it the right kind of spending, directed at the right targets, in the right sequence?

Breaking It Down

Agricultural Marketing Act (1929) and the Federal Farm Board: Passed within months of Hoover taking office — before the crash — the AMA created the Federal Farm Board with a $500 million revolving fund to stabilize agricultural prices through cooperative marketing and commodity purchases. It was the largest peacetime federal intervention in agriculture to that point. It failed to stop the collapse of farm prices because it had no mechanism to reduce production — a problem the New Deal's AAA would later address through the politically and morally catastrophic method of crop destruction.

Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC): Created in January 1932 on the model of the War Finance Corporation Hoover had used during WWI, the RFC was authorized to lend $1.2 billion to banks, railroads, agricultural credit corporations, and insurance companies. The theory was correct — preventing institutional collapse would prevent the cascading bank failures that were destroying savings and credit. The execution was politically toxic: critics pointed out that the RFC was bailing out large financial institutions while individual Americans starved. Charles Dawes, former Vice President, resigned from the RFC board in June 1932 and immediately received an $80 million RFC loan for his Chicago bank — a scandal that permanently damaged Hoover's reputation on relief.

Emergency Relief and Construction Act (1932): Passed in July 1932, this was Hoover's closest approach to the kind of direct federal relief he had long resisted. It authorized $2 billion to states and localities for self-liquidating public works — roads, bridges, public buildings — and $750 million in business loans. Hoover signed it reluctantly, insisting the public works loans must be self-liquidating (i.e., they had to generate revenue to repay the federal government). That constraint severely limited what could actually be built with the money.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — over $4 billion in federal intervention before FDR took office — reframes the entire Hoover-versus-FDR narrative. FDR did not arrive at a federal government that had stood aside and watched. He arrived at one that had already spent at unprecedented scale and still failed to end the Depression. That context makes the New Deal's continuation of Hoover's programs (the RFC, public works, farm price supports) look less like bold new departure and more like an escalation of a strategy that was already not working. Keep that framing in mind as Section III develops.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Here is one of the most pointed ironies of the Hoover–FDR story: some of the most iconic symbols of New Deal achievement were actually initiated under Hoover. The dam that bears his name is the most obvious example. Construction began in 1931 under his authorization, using his RFC funding. It was completed in 1936 — and became part of the New Deal's celebrated legacy.

Breaking It Down

Hoover Dam: One of the largest engineering projects in human history, Hoover Dam on the Colorado River was authorized by the Boulder Canyon Project Act, which Hoover signed in 1928. Construction began in 1931. It employed thousands of workers during the Depression — exactly the kind of relief Hoover's critics said he refused to provide.

"Celebrated as New Deal achievements": Political credit for infrastructure projects goes to whoever is in office when the ribbon is cut, not when the ground is broken. FDR benefited enormously from this dynamic — inheriting projects Hoover had started and presiding over their completion as symbols of his own administration's energy and purpose.

Why This Matters

This slide is not an argument that Hoover deserves more credit than FDR. It's an argument that the historical record is more complicated than the myth. Acknowledging that Hoover Dam was Hoover's project doesn't diminish what FDR built — it just gives you a more accurate picture of the 1930s as a whole.

Walter Lippmann, 1935

"The policy initiated by President Hoover in the autumn of 1929 was something utterly unprecedented in American history. The national government undertook to make the whole economic order operate prosperously . . . The Roosevelt measures are a continuous evolution of the Hoover measures."
— Walter Lippmann, 1935

📖 Understanding This Slide

Let the quote land before you say anything. Lippmann was not a conservative critic writing from the right — he was the most influential liberal public intellectual in America in 1935, a man who had supported Wilson, championed progressive causes, and written sympathetically about FDR's early presidency. When he says the Roosevelt measures are a continuous evolution of the Hoover measures, he is not mounting a partisan attack. He is making a historical observation from inside the progressive tradition. That is what makes it devastating.

Breaking It Down

"Utterly unprecedented in American history": Lippmann is crediting Hoover, not condemning him. The intervention was unprecedented because no previous American president had treated a financial panic as a problem requiring the federal government to manage the entire economic order back to prosperity. Every prior president had let the market clear. Hoover decided the federal government was responsible for the outcome. That decision — not FDR's — is where the modern interventionist state begins.

"The national government undertook to make the whole economic order operate prosperously": This is a precise description of what distinguishes 1929 from every prior American depression. In 1893, in 1907, in 1920 — the federal government did not accept responsibility for economic outcomes. Hoover accepted that responsibility in October 1929. Everything that followed — RFC, public works, farm price supports, and then the entire New Deal apparatus — flows from that acceptance.

"A continuous evolution": Lippmann is collapsing the Hoover-versus-FDR narrative that both men's political operations worked hard to construct. Hoover needed to be seen as the responsible steward undone by circumstances; FDR needed to be seen as the bold innovator who broke with his failed predecessor. Lippmann — writing in 1935, with the full record available — said neither story was true. The New Deal was Hoover's program, enlarged.

Why This Matters

This quote is the bridge between Section II and Section III. Everything the lecture has established about Hoover — the RFC, the Agricultural Marketing Act, the $4 billion in federal intervention, the public works program — is now confirmed by a contemporary liberal witness as the actual origin point of the New Deal. When FDR takes the stage in Section III, students should be carrying Lippmann's observation with them. The question is not whether FDR innovated — it is whether his enlargement of Hoover's program succeeded where Hoover's version had failed. The unemployment figures on slide 26 will answer that.

Walter Lippmann — Interpretations 1933–1935

Lippmann (1889–1974) was the dominant political journalist and public intellectual of the interwar period — co-founder of The New Republic, foreign policy adviser to Woodrow Wilson at Versailles, and the writer whose columns reached more educated Americans than any other single voice. His 1922 book Public Opinion remains a foundational text in political theory. He was emphatically not a conservative. His observation that the New Deal was a continuous evolution of Hoover's program carries particular weight precisely because it comes from someone with every ideological incentive to draw a sharp line between the two presidencies — and who refused to do so.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The previous slides established that Hoover intervened actively and at unprecedented scale. This slide explains why those interventions failed — and the answer is not that he did too little. It is that his right hand was destroying what his left hand was building. The RFC pumped $1.2 billion into the economy; the Revenue Act simultaneously drained purchasing power back out. The public works program tried to create jobs; Smoot-Hawley destroyed the export sector that employed millions. Hoover did not fail because he stood aside. He failed because his interventions were internally contradictory and, in the case of Smoot-Hawley, catastrophically wrong.

Breaking It Down

Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930): This is Hoover's most consequential single policy decision — and one of the most consequential in American economic history. The Smoot-Hawley Act raised tariff rates on more than 20,000 imported goods to historically high levels. More than 1,000 economists — an extraordinary show of professional consensus — signed a public petition urging Hoover to veto it. He signed it anyway in June 1930, apparently persuaded that protecting domestic industry was consistent with his associationalist philosophy. The retaliation was immediate and severe. Canada, Britain, France, Germany, and dozens of other trading partners raised their own tariffs. American exports, which had been $5.4 billion in 1929, fell to $2.1 billion by 1932 — a collapse of 61%. A financial crisis that had been severe but potentially manageable became a global trade catastrophe. The Smoot-Hawley decision is the clearest example of Hoover's interventions actively deepening the Depression rather than ameliorating it.

Revenue Act of 1932: If Smoot-Hawley was Hoover's worst external policy decision, the Revenue Act of 1932 was his worst domestic one. In the depths of the worst deflationary contraction in American history, Hoover raised taxes — dramatically. The top marginal income tax rate nearly tripled from 25% to 63%. Corporate taxes rose. Estate taxes rose. A broad new excise tax structure was imposed. Hoover's reasoning was internally coherent within his governing framework: balanced budgets signal fiscal responsibility, which restores investor confidence, which enables recovery. The economic logic was precisely backwards. In a deflationary spiral, withdrawing money from the economy through taxation accelerates the contraction. The Revenue Act of 1932 is now considered by economic historians across the political spectrum — Keynesian and monetarist alike — as one of the most destructive fiscal decisions in American history. It is the mirror image of the New Deal's later contradictions: just as FDR's undistributed profits tax would discourage the corporate investment the recovery needed, Hoover's income tax hike withdrew the consumer demand the economy was desperately short of.

Why This Matters

The bottom line reframes the entire Hoover failure narrative. The standard story is that Hoover failed because he did too little. The revisionist reading — supported by the evidence on this slide — is that he failed because he did the wrong things. The RFC and the public works spending were genuine attempts to stabilize the economy. Smoot-Hawley and the Revenue Act gutted both. By 1932, Hoover was simultaneously the most interventionist peacetime president in American history and the author of two of the most economically destructive policies of the era. That combination — not passivity — is the real verdict on his presidency. And as the next section will show, FDR's record on contradictory interventions is not as different as the standard narrative suggests.

A note on the "psychological failure" framing: The original version of this slide treated Hoovervilles and the Bonus Army as evidence of a psychological failure co-equal with the policy disasters. That framing has been deliberately removed. Hoover's inability to project empathy was a political consequence of his policy failures and his temperament — it was not a cause of the Depression's severity. The Hoovervilles existed because of Smoot-Hawley and the Revenue Act and the RFC's insufficient scale, not because Hoover failed to emote convincingly. Treating optics as causally equivalent to policy mistakes reproduces the progressive narrative this lecture is designed to complicate.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is a question about historical memory — which is distinct from historical fact. We've just established that the "do-nothing" label is empirically false. So why does it persist? That question is actually more interesting than the factual correction, because it reveals how Depression-era politics shaped what Americans chose to remember.

Thinking It Through

The myth persisted for several reasons. First, FDR's political team actively cultivated it — the contrast with a "do-nothing" Hoover made FDR's activism look more heroic and necessary. Second, Hoover's public persona was cold and technocratic; he never built the emotional connection with ordinary Americans that might have complicated the caricature. Third, the sheer scale of suffering under his watch made the myth feel true even when the policy record contradicted it.

What does this tell us about historical memory? It tells us that memory is shaped by political necessity, emotional resonance, and narrative simplicity — not primarily by the factual record. The story that gets told is often the story that serves someone's interests, feels emotionally true, and fits into a comprehensible moral framework.

The subtitle is everything: "Confidence Before Solutions." FDR's greatest gift was not economic expertise — he had almost none. 📖 Understanding This Slide

The previous slides established Hoover's actual record: more active, more interventionist, and more consequential than the standard narrative allows. This slide explains why none of that mattered politically. By 1932 Hoover had become the Depression personified — his name attached to its every visible symptom with savage popular ingenuity. The psychological failure discussed earlier was not a cause of the Depression's severity, but it was a cause of Hoover's political annihilation. And that annihilation had consequences: it handed FDR a political mandate to do something, anything, and gave the New Dealers the "do-nothing Hoover" myth they needed to justify interventions far more radical than Hoover's own.

Breaking It Down

Hoovervilles: Click the popup for details. Shantytowns built from scrap lumber, cardboard, and tin appeared in vacant lots, parks, and riverbeds in every major American city. By 1932 there were Hoovervilles in Seattle, New York's Central Park, St. Louis, and dozens of other cities. The name was not affectionate. It was an act of public attribution — these are the president's villages, this is the president's poverty. The naming itself was political: it fixed responsibility on a specific individual for conditions that had structural and policy causes far beyond any one man's decisions.

Hoover flags and Hoover blankets: The vernacular humor of the Depression was relentless and specific. A "Hoover flag" was an empty trouser pocket turned inside out — the universal gesture of having nothing. A "Hoover blanket" was a newspaper used for warmth by someone sleeping outdoors. A "Hoover hog" was an armadillo, hunted for food in the South. A "Hoover wagon" was a car pulled by mules because its owner couldn't afford gasoline. The catalog of Hoover-named deprivations was exhaustive and cumulative — each addition reinforced the association between the president's name and the experience of destitution.

The Bonus Army: Click the popup. In the summer of 1932, approximately 20,000 World War I veterans marched on Washington to demand early payment of service bonuses Congress had promised for 1945. They camped across the Anacostia River from the Capitol in a sprawling shantytown. In July, after Congress refused their request, Hoover ordered the Army to clear them. General Douglas MacArthur — with Major Dwight Eisenhower and Major George Patton in attendance — used infantry, cavalry, and tanks to disperse the veterans, burning their camp. Several veterans were killed and hundreds injured. The images of the United States Army attacking its own veterans on the streets of the capital destroyed whatever remained of Hoover's popular standing.

The "do-nothing" myth and its political function: This is the slide's analytical payoff. FDR's 1932 campaign attacked Hoover simultaneously for spending too much and doing too little — a contradiction that worked because the campaign's goal was not coherence but attribution. By 1933 the New Dealers had settled on a simpler narrative: Hoover had done nothing, faith in markets had produced catastrophe, and only bold federal intervention could rescue the country. This narrative was politically indispensable because it justified everything the New Deal wanted to do. If Hoover had genuinely done nothing, then the New Deal's interventions were a necessary departure from a failed experiment. But Hoover had not done nothing — he had spent more, borrowed more, and intervened more than any peacetime president before him. The "do-nothing" myth required active construction, and it served a specific political purpose: it created the permission structure for an administrative expansion of federal power that no accurate account of Hoover's record could have justified.

Why This Matters

The bottom line captures the essential tragedy: the man who had organized the greatest humanitarian relief operations in history — feeding millions in Belgium, in post-war Europe, in famine-stricken Russia — could not escape the image of a man indifferent to suffering at home. That image was partly his own fault (the Bonus Army), partly the result of genuine policy failures (Smoot-Hawley, the Revenue Act), and partly a political construction built by skilled opponents. All three causes were real. But the third — the deliberate myth-making — had the longest historical consequences. The "do-nothing Hoover" is still the standard textbook account. It was never true. And its persistence has distorted how Americans understand both the Depression and the New Deal ever since.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Murray Rothbard — America's Great Depression (1963)

Rothbard was among the first historians to systematically document that Hoover was not a laissez-faire do-nothing but an active interventionist whose programs established the template the New Deal would enlarge. His central empirical point — that Hoover's wage maintenance program, RFC, and public works spending were the direct predecessors of New Deal policy — is now broadly accepted across the historiographical spectrum, even by historians who reject Rothbard's Austrian economic framework. The "do-nothing" myth, Rothbard showed, was a political invention, not a historical description.

Amity Shlaes — The Forgotten Man (2007)

Shlaes traces the construction of the do-nothing myth through FDR's 1932 campaign and the early New Deal's communications strategy. Her account of how the Roosevelt administration's media operation — including the nascent FSA photography program — worked to fix the Depression's image on Hoover personally is essential for understanding how myth functions in democratic politics. The myth was not incidental to the New Deal; it was the ideological precondition for it.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The election of 1932 was not a mandate for a specific program — it was a mandate for change. FDR won in a landslide not because he offered a coherent economic plan, but because he wasn't Hoover, and because Americans were desperate enough to try anything.

Breaking It Down

"Bold persistent experimentation": This phrase from FDR's campaign is both inspiring and revealing. It's not a policy platform — it's a temperamental stance. He was saying: I will try things, and if they don't work, I'll try other things. Given that economists disagreed sharply about what was causing the Depression and how to fix it, this was perhaps the most honest position available.

Attacked Hoover for spending too much: Here's the irony students often miss. During the campaign, FDR attacked Hoover for running deficits and expanding government. He promised to balance the budget. Once in office, he did the opposite — but his fiscal instincts remained genuinely conservative throughout the 1930s, which contributed directly to the 1937 recession.

No master plan: The bottom line is crucial and counterintuitive. The New Deal was improvised. Different advisors pushed different solutions; many contradicted each other. FDR's genius was political and psychological, not economic. He surrounded himself with competing "Brain Trust" intellectuals and played them against each other, picking and discarding programs by instinct.

Why This Matters

Understanding that FDR had no master plan helps explain why the New Deal contained so many internal contradictions — the NRA raised prices while the AAA reduced supply; relief spending stimulated the economy while tax increases contracted it. These weren't mistakes of execution. They were the predictable output of an improvisational presidency operating without a coherent theoretical framework.

  • Emergency Banking Act drafted, passed, and signed within hours

📖 Understanding This Slide

If you want to understand what FDR was genuinely good at, this is your best example. The Bank Holiday was not a complex economic reform — it was a masterful act of psychological intervention. By closing all the banks simultaneously and reopening only the sound ones, FDR transformed the signal banks sent to depositors: instead of "we might fail," the message became "we have been certified safe by the federal government."

Breaking It Down

Bank holiday: On March 4, 1933 — his first day in office — FDR inherited a banking system in cascading collapse. By March 6, he had declared a national bank holiday: all banks closed. This stopped the runs cold, because you can't run on a bank that isn't open.

Emergency Banking Act — within hours: Click the popup for details. The speed was itself a statement — FDR was demonstrating that the government could act decisively. Congress passed the bill in hours without even having printed copies to read. The optics of speed were as important as the content.

Deposits flooded back: When FDR delivered a Fireside Chat explaining the situation on March 12, and banks reopened the following Monday, depositors returned their money. The panic that had built for three years evaporated almost overnight. This is the clearest proof that FDR's confidence theory was correct — at least in this instance.

Why This Matters

The Bank Holiday is FDR's most unambiguous success because the problem — a self-fulfilling panic — matched perfectly with the solution — a credible federal guarantee. When problem and solution align this cleanly, decisive action works. The rest of the New Deal's more mixed record is partly explained by the fact that the other problems were harder.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the lecture's prosecutorial summary — the point where the specific policy failures of the previous slides resolve into a general indictment of both administrations. The Lippmann quote established continuity between Hoover and FDR. This slide explains what that continuity produced: a decade-long depression that outlasted both men's interventions because the interventions themselves were part of the problem. This is the hardest claim in the lecture, and it should land hard.

Breaking It Down

"Meddlesome activism impeded natural recovery": This is the revisionist core, and it requires careful handling with students who will instinctively resist it. The claim is not that suffering should have been ignored. It is that deflation, while brutal, performs a necessary economic function — it liquidates bad investments, realigns wages and prices with productive capacity, and creates the conditions for genuine recovery. The 1920–21 depression self-corrected in eighteen months precisely because the government did not prevent the adjustment process from running. Both Hoover and FDR, by maintaining artificially high wages (Hoover's voluntary pledges; NRA minimum wages), destroying supply to raise prices (AAA), and cartelizing industry (NRA), prevented the price and wage adjustments that would have allowed the economy to find its floor and rebound. They were treating symptoms while blocking the cure.

"Confused and contradictory": The internal contradictions are the most damning evidence against both administrations' economic competence. Hoover ran the RFC to inject liquidity while simultaneously passing the Revenue Act to drain it. FDR's NRA raised prices for consumers while the administration simultaneously worried about insufficient purchasing power. The AAA destroyed food to raise farm prices while relief agencies struggled to feed the hungry. The undistributed profits tax penalized companies for retaining the earnings they needed to invest in recovery. These were not minor inconsistencies — they were systematic contradictions built into programs designed by political coalitions rather than coherent economic theory.

"Attacked business and Wall Street — then expected private investment": This contradiction runs through the entire New Deal period. FDR's rhetoric consistently vilified "economic royalists," "money changers," and the business community. His tax policy — particularly the undistributed profits tax and the top rate of 79% under the Wealth Tax — made clear that the administration regarded business profit as suspect. And yet recovery required private investment. Businesses that believed the administration was hostile to their survival and their profits did not invest. The result was the investment drought that kept unemployment above 14% through 1940.

"Politics infused every policy": The New Deal coalition — Northern urban labor, Southern agrarian Democrats, Western progressives — had internally contradictory economic interests. Southern Democrats would not support programs that treated Black and white workers equally; hence the racial exclusions in AAA, NRA, and Social Security. Urban labor wanted high wages; hence the NRA codes. Western farmers wanted high commodity prices; hence the AAA's crop destruction. None of these priorities were determined by what would most efficiently end the Depression. They were determined by what would hold the coalition together through the next election cycle.

"Relief channeled away from the poorest": This is the most morally corrosive finding. AAA payments went to landowners, not tenant farmers — and the mechanization those payments funded displaced hundreds of thousands of Black sharecroppers who had no other economic option. NRA codes established lower wage floors for "Negro occupations." Social Security explicitly excluded domestic workers and agricultural laborers — the categories covering roughly 65% of Black Southern workers. The people most devastated by the Depression received the least from the programs nominally designed to help them, because their political powerlessness made their interests expendable in coalition management.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "both administrations prolonged what they claimed to be curing" — is the most direct statement of the lecture's thesis. It will be uncomfortable for students raised on the standard narrative. Press them on it: if both administrations intervened massively and the Depression lasted a decade, what does that tell us about the relationship between the interventions and the outcome? The answer is not that intervention is always wrong. It is that incoherent, politically motivated, internally contradictory intervention — intervention designed to manage a coalition rather than solve a problem — can make things worse even when undertaken with genuine urgency and genuine compassion. That is the New Deal's real lesson, and it applies far beyond the 1930s.

The National Recovery Administration

The Logic
  • Competition was driving wages and prices into a deflationary spiral
  • Fix prices from above — restore producer profitability
  • Profitable producers would then rehire workers
  • Struck down by the Supreme Court in 1935 — Schechter Poultry v. United States
What It Did
  • Suspended antitrust law — industries allowed to collude legally
  • Industry codes set minimum prices — illegal to sell below the fixed rate
  • Companies required to cut output to maintain price floors
  • Wagner Act boosted union power — wages pushed above market-clearing levels
The federal government legalized cartels — and called it recovery

📖 Understanding This Slide

The NRA is one of the most revealing episodes in New Deal history because its internal logic is so transparent — and so wrong. The theory was that falling prices were the problem, so the solution was to prevent prices from falling. What that actually meant in practice was that the federal government suspended the antitrust laws it had spent decades building and handed industry associations the power to set minimum prices. This is cartelization with federal teeth, justified as recovery policy.

Breaking It Down

Suspended antitrust law: The Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) and the Clayton Act (1914) had prohibited exactly the kind of collective price-setting the NRA's industry codes mandated. The NRA did not reform those laws — it simply suspended them for industries participating in the code system. Businesses that had been prosecuted for colluding to fix prices were now legally required to do so. This was presented as a pragmatic emergency measure; it was, in substance, the federal government endorsing and enforcing private monopoly.

"Illegal to sell below the mandated price": The enforcement was real and intrusive. A tailor in New Jersey was prosecuted in 1934 for pressing a suit for 35 cents when the NRA code set the minimum at 40 cents. The Schechter brothers — Brooklyn poultry dealers — were prosecuted for selling chickens at prices below the NRA code. Their case reached the Supreme Court, which unanimously struck the NRA down in May 1935 as an unconstitutional delegation of legislative power to private industry associations.

Wagner Act and union wages: The Wagner Act (1935) — the National Labor Relations Act — gave unions the legal framework to organize, bargain collectively, and strike, backed by a new federal enforcement body. Union membership surged. Wages in unionized industries rose. This is usually presented as an unambiguous good; the NRA critique slide that follows explains why, in the context of a depression requiring employment expansion, it had a different effect than intended.

Why This Matters

The Supreme Court's verdict — unanimous, including FDR's own appointees — was that the NRA had delegated legislative power to private industry associations without constitutional authority. The constitutional objection happened to align with the economic one: an agency that let industries write their own pricing rules was not regulating the economy in the public interest. It was handing the economy to the largest and most organized players in each industry. The next slide shows what that cost.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide follows the NRA's consequences to the people who bore them. The left column traces the damage to the price and output structure — how cartelization and minimum wages interacted to prevent the employment expansion the NRA claimed as its goal. The right column traces the racial and class distribution of those costs — who specifically was displaced when the NRA made labor more expensive and output more restricted. The answer is the same in both columns: the poorest workers, and disproportionately Black workers.

Breaking It Down

Higher prices reduced purchasing power: The NRA's core self-contradiction is stark: it was attempting to stimulate economic recovery by raising the prices Americans paid for goods, at a time when the Depression had already drastically reduced American incomes. Higher prices for industrial goods meant consumers bought less. Buying less meant lower sales. Lower sales meant less production. Less production meant fewer workers needed. The mechanism the NRA intended to generate employment instead reduced it.

Large firms wrote the codes: The industry associations that drafted the NRA codes were dominated by the largest firms in each sector. The minimum prices they set were calibrated to protect large producers' margins — and were set high enough to be manageable for large firms but ruinous for small competitors who operated on thinner margins. The NRA accelerated industrial concentration, eliminating the small competitors whose price competition had been keeping costs down for consumers.

NRA racial exclusions: Many NRA industry codes established explicitly lower wage floors for "Negro occupations" — but even these depressed floors were higher than what Black workers had previously earned in an unregulated market, which meant employers substituted white labor or machinery rather than pay even the discounted Black wage floor. The union closed shop provisions made the exclusion structural: in industries where union membership was required for employment, and where AFL unions barred Black membership, Black workers were simply locked out of entire sectors. The Wagner Act's empowerment of collective bargaining institutionalized this exclusion with federal authority.

Why This Matters

The bottom line is the lecture's sharpest formulation of the NRA's failure: a program designed to raise wages and restore employment that raised prices, cut output, and excluded the poorest workers. This is not a minor implementation problem — it is the program working as designed, serving the interests of organized labor and large industry at the expense of the unorganized, the marginal, and the racially excluded. The Supreme Court ended it in 1935. The workers it had displaced did not recover until the war.

The Logic
  • Farm prices had collapsed because supply was too high
  • Reduce supply → raise prices → restore farm income
  • Farmers with income would spend into the broader economy
What It Did
  • Paid farmers to destroy crops and reduce acreage
  • 10 million acres of cotton plowed under

Scarcity as a deliberate policy instrument — enforced by the federal government

📖 Understanding This Slide

The AAA is the most viscerally disturbing element of the early New Deal — and the most revealing about the administration's governing logic. The program paid farmers to destroy food at a moment when millions of Americans were going hungry. The economic theory behind it was not irrational; the political and moral consequences were indefensible. This slide establishes what the program did and why. The next slide follows the consequences.

Breaking It Down

10 million acres plowed under / 6 million pigs slaughtered: These figures are not abstractions. In the summer of 1933, the AAA paid Southern cotton growers to plow under roughly a quarter of the standing crop. In the Midwest, it paid hog farmers to slaughter approximately six million pigs — many of them under market weight, their carcasses processed into fertilizer and lard rather than food. This happened while the Federal Emergency Relief Administration was simultaneously trying to feed unemployed Americans in the cities. The left hand of the New Deal was destroying what the right hand was desperately trying to provide.

The logic: Farm commodity prices had collapsed catastrophically between 1929 and 1932 — cotton fell from 17 cents a pound to 6 cents; wheat from $1.03 a bushel to 38 cents. The AAA's analysis was correct that oversupply was driving prices down. Its solution — pay farmers to reduce that supply — was internally coherent as agricultural economics. The problem was that it treated the farm sector in isolation from the broader economy: raising food prices helped farmers but hurt the urban consumers, including unemployed industrial workers, who needed cheap food precisely because they had no income.

Supreme Court: The Court struck down the original AAA in January 1936 in United States v. Butler, ruling that using federal taxing power to regulate agricultural production was an unconstitutional invasion of state authority. FDR responded with a revised AAA in 1938 that achieved similar goals through different constitutional mechanisms — the crop destruction logic survived the Court's intervention essentially intact.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — scarcity as a deliberate policy instrument — is the precise description of what both the NRA and the AAA were. Both programs used the authority of the federal government to restrict supply in order to raise prices. The NRA restricted industrial output; the AAA restricted agricultural output. In both cases the theory was that higher prices would restore producer profitability and thus employment and recovery. In both cases the practice produced the opposite: higher costs for consumers, reduced output, and fewer jobs. The next slide shows who specifically paid.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The previous slide established what the AAA destroyed. This slide follows the money — who received the benefits, and what the political logic behind their distribution was. Both trails lead to the same conclusion: the program worked as designed, and the design served the coalition that built it, not the rural poor it claimed to help.

Breaking It Down

Landowners, not tenants — 500,000 displaced: The AAA paid farmers to reduce acreage. In the South, "farmers" meant landowners. Sharecroppers farmed land they didn't own in exchange for a share of the crop — they received nothing from AAA payments. When landowners took acres out of production, they evicted the sharecroppers farming them, and used AAA payments to mechanize whatever acreage remained. The Southern agricultural economy was structured around Black labor; when the AAA restructured Southern agriculture, Black workers bore the overwhelming burden. An estimated 500,000 African Americans lost jobs as a direct result.

Consumers worse off — including farmers: The AAA succeeded in raising agricultural prices. But raised food and commodity prices hurt everyone who bought them — including the farmers who were supposed to benefit. A cotton farmer who received an AAA payment for reducing his acreage then paid higher prices for corn, wheat, and manufactured goods whose prices had been raised by NRA codes. The program transferred income to agricultural producers at the expense of everyone who consumed agricultural products — a category that included most agricultural producers themselves.

Subsidies tracked electoral maps: Economic historians have found consistent patterns of political favoritism in AAA payment distribution — swing states and politically important constituencies received disproportionately large payments relative to agricultural output. Federal relief administered through state and local Democratic Party structures created systematic incentives to reward electorally valuable constituencies. The New Deal was simultaneously a relief program and a political machine, and when those functions conflicted, the political machine won.

The political price: Southern Democrats controlled key Senate committees through the seniority system. FDR needed their votes for every piece of New Deal legislation. The price they extracted was systematic exclusion of Black Americans — discriminatory local administration, Social Security occupational exemptions covering 65% of Black Southern workers, and no federal anti-lynching legislation. FDR never supported an anti-lynching bill. Those who paid for the coalition's cohesion were those with no political leverage to resist.

Why This Matters

The Rothstein photograph shows an Oklahoma farmer and his sons fleeing a Dust Bowl storm. It became iconic of Depression-era suffering — notice who it depicts: a white farm family. The racialized dimension of agricultural displacement was largely invisible in the era's iconic imagery, which is itself a product of the same political management this slide describes. The FSA photography program systematically underrepresented Black rural poverty because it served a coalition that depended on making that poverty invisible. The archive reflects the coalition, not the reality.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Ira Katznelson — Fear Itself: The New Deal and the Origins of Our Time

Katznelson's landmark study argues the New Deal was a Faustian bargain: transformative social legislation purchased at the price of Southern white supremacy. The racial exclusions built into Social Security, the AAA, and labor law were not incidental but structural — the deliberate cost of building a coalition capable of passing reform at all. His argument forces a reckoning with whether progressive reform and racial justice were compatible in 1930s America — and arrives at the uncomfortable conclusion that FDR chose the coalition over the people the coalition was supposed to serve.

FDR Tripled the Tax Burden

  • After Prohibition repeal — heavy excise taxes on liquor, tobacco, gasoline (regressive)

📖 Understanding This Slide

Here is perhaps the sharpest economic paradox of the New Deal era: while FDR was spending federal money to stimulate the economy on one side of the ledger, his tax policies were withdrawing money from the economy on the other. The net effect was significantly smaller than the gross spending figures suggest.

Breaking It Down

Regressive excise taxes: When Prohibition ended in 1933, the federal government immediately imposed heavy excise taxes on alcohol, tobacco, and gasoline. These taxes fall proportionally harder on lower-income people (who spend a larger share of their income on these goods). The same administration that was providing relief to the poor was taxing them regressively to pay for it.

Revenue Act of 1935 — 79% top rate: Politically, this was brilliant. Taxing the wealthy during the Depression was enormously popular and helped FDR neutralize radical critics like Huey Long. Economically, a 79% top rate on investment income sent a clear signal to wealthy investors: the government regards your profits as a target. This contributed to the investment drought that kept unemployment elevated.

Undistributed-profits tax (1936): This may be the most economically damaging tax of the era. Companies that retained earnings to fund future investment — rather than paying them out as dividends — were taxed on those retained earnings. This actively penalized companies for saving to invest. Given that corporate investment was already near collapse, this was fiscal malpractice.

Why This Matters

The bottom line captures the essential tension: these taxes were politically progressive (they fell on the wealthy and powerful, satisfying the Democratic base's demand for redistribution) but economically contractionary (they reduced investment, withdrew purchasing power, and discouraged the private sector activity needed for genuine recovery). You can hold both of those truths at once — and the lecture asks you to.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The previous slide established what FDR's tax policy looked like on paper — the rates and the names of the acts. This slide explains what it produced in practice. The two columns trace the damage in two directions simultaneously: downward through consumer spending, and upward through business investment. Both channels contracted. Both were necessary for recovery. The tax structure hit both at the same time.

Breaking It Down

Regressive excise taxes: When Prohibition was repealed in 1933, the Roosevelt administration immediately imposed heavy excise taxes on alcohol. Similar taxes on tobacco and gasoline followed. These taxes were politically easy — sin taxes face less public resistance than income taxes — but they were economically regressive, falling disproportionately on working-class and poor Americans who spent a higher proportion of their income on these goods. In an economy already suffering from collapsed consumer demand, taxing the consumption of the people with the highest propensity to spend was precisely backwards.

The undistributed profits tax (1936): This is the most economically destructive single tax measure of the New Deal period. The theory behind it was that corporations were hoarding profits rather than distributing them to shareholders or investing them productively. The tax was intended to force that money into circulation. The actual effect was to penalize companies for retaining the retained earnings they needed as internal capital for expansion and hiring. A firm that wanted to build a new facility or hire additional workers — using its own accumulated profits rather than borrowing — now faced a federal tax penalty for doing so. Corporate investment, already deeply depressed, fell further. The tax was quietly repealed in 1938 after its damage became undeniable.

Why This Matters

The bottom line captures the central paradox of New Deal fiscal policy: the administration needed private investment to drive job creation and recovery, while its tax structure made private investment economically irrational. A 79% top marginal rate told any entrepreneur or corporate manager that the government would claim the vast majority of any above-average return. The undistributed profits tax told any firm that retaining earnings for future investment would be penalized. These signals did not encourage the risk-taking that generates employment. They discouraged it systematically, while the administration publicly blamed business for failing to invest.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The Roosevelt Recession is the single most damning economic indictment of New Deal management. After four years of programs, the economy had partially recovered to 14% unemployment by 1937. Then FDR made a set of decisions that sent it back to 19%. This recession was not caused by an external shock — it was caused by the administration's own fiscal choices.

Breaking It Down

Balanced-budget orthodoxy: Remember that during the 1932 campaign, FDR had attacked Hoover for deficit spending. Despite running deficits himself, FDR was never ideologically comfortable with them. By 1937, with unemployment falling, he convinced himself the emergency had passed and cut federal spending sharply to balance the budget.

Social Security payroll tax: This is the cruel irony. The Social Security Act was passed in 1935 but began collecting payroll taxes in January 1937, before paying out any benefits. This meant it was withdrawing billions of dollars in purchasing power from workers' paychecks at the exact moment FDR was also cutting federal spending. Two contractionary forces hit simultaneously.

Industrial production fell 33% in thirteen months: This is a staggering number. American industrial output declined by a third — faster than at any point in the original 1929–1933 collapse. Millions of people who had found work under the New Deal lost their jobs again.

Why This Matters

"Not inherited — policy-induced." This is a devastating verdict. Hoover's failures can partly be excused by the unprecedented nature of the crisis and the absence of theoretical tools to understand it. The 1937 recession cannot be so excused — it happened after four years of experience, was predicted by Keynesian economists within the administration, and was caused directly by FDR's decisions. This is where "folly" in the lecture's title most clearly applies to FDR.

Section IV

What Actually Ended the Depression

And what that tells us about the limits of peacetime policy

📖 Understanding This Slide

The final section delivers the lecture's most challenging conclusion. It's not simply "the war ended the Depression" — that's the fact. The harder question is: why did it take a war to achieve the scale of stimulus that ended it? What does that tell us about the relationship between democratic governance, political constraints, and economic catastrophe?

Why This Matters

The phrase "limits of peacetime policy" is carefully chosen. It doesn't say the New Deal was wrong or that FDR was incompetent. It asks whether the political conditions required for adequate fiscal response to a catastrophic depression can actually be achieved in a democratic society short of existential emergency. That's a structural question — and it's still relevant today.

What Actually Ended the Depression

  • Unemployment still 14.6% in 1940
  • A decade of programs — insufficient scale
Wartime Mobilization

📖 Understanding This Slide

The contrast between these two columns is the lecture's empirical conclusion in its most concentrated form. The New Deal spent up to 10% of GDP and left 14.6% unemployment after a decade. War mobilization spent 40% of GDP and produced under 2% unemployment in three years. The gap between those outcomes is a measure of the scale problem that peacetime democratic politics could not solve.

Breaking It Down

10% vs. 40% of GDP: GDP (Gross Domestic Product) measures the total value of all goods and services produced in a country in a year. When the federal government spends 10% of GDP, it's making a significant intervention. When it spends 40%, it is essentially taking over the direction of the entire economy. That's what wartime mobilization did — and nothing less than that scale was sufficient to absorb the unemployment the Depression had created.

Industrial production more than doubled: Between 1940 and 1944, American industrial output more than doubled. Factories that had been idle or running at fraction capacity were running three shifts. Women entered the workforce in unprecedented numbers. The unemployment problem that had seemed intractable for a decade was solved in months — not by clever policy, but by the sheer volume of government demand for war production.

Why This Matters

The bottom line is the lecture's most challenging claim: the scale required for recovery was only politically possible under existential emergency. In peacetime, democratic constituencies resist the levels of taxation, deficit spending, and government control of economic activity that would have been required. In wartime, those constraints dissolve — and the economy responds accordingly. This is not a comforting conclusion. It suggests that the New Deal's failure to end the Depression was not primarily a failure of intelligence or will, but a structural limit of democratic governance itself.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Robert Higgs – Crisis and Leviathan

Higgs argues that war and national emergencies systematically expand the size and reach of government in ways that partially persist after the emergency ends — a "ratchet effect." The New Deal and WWII together permanently shifted the baseline of American federal government activity. His analysis helps explain why wartime mobilization succeeded where peacetime intervention did not: crisis politics removes the normal constraints on government action.

The New Deal's Deepest Legacy

The New Deal's greatest achievement was not what it did — it was what it made normal.
  • Before 1933: federal government had no role in fighting recessions
  • After 1933: "Washington must act" became permanent American consensus
  • Every subsequent recession has been met with federal fiscal and monetary response
  • This consensus survived every conservative resurgence — 1950s, 1980s, 2010s

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the lecture's most generous assessment of FDR's historical significance — and it's a genuinely important insight. The New Deal's most durable achievement wasn't any specific program. It was a permanent transformation of what Americans expect from their federal government in times of economic crisis.

Breaking It Down

Before 1933 — no federal role: This is difficult to imagine today, but it's historically accurate. Before Hoover (and largely despite Hoover), the prevailing American consensus was that economic downturns were natural market corrections that the federal government had no business interfering with. The idea that Washington should spend money, provide relief, and regulate the financial system to fight recessions was genuinely radical before 1933.

Survived every conservative resurgence: Eisenhower in the 1950s, Reagan in the 1980s, Tea Party Republicans in the 2010s — none of them repealed Social Security, abolished the FDIC, or returned to pre-New Deal fiscal orthodoxy. Even the most conservative postwar administrations accepted the basic New Deal framework of federal responsibility for economic stabilization. That is the measure of how completely it transformed American political culture.

Why This Matters

The callout box captures it perfectly: the New Deal's greatest achievement was making federal economic intervention normal. Every time the federal government responds to a recession with stimulus, every time the Fed cuts interest rates in a downturn, every time Congress passes relief legislation — they are acting within a framework that FDR's New Deal made permanent. That is a legacy that outlasted every specific program, every political reversal, and every historical critique.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

If the scale of spending required to end the Depression was only possible under wartime conditions — what does that tell us about the structural relationship between democracy and economic catastrophe?

Is this a failure of policy — or an inherent limit of democratic governance in peacetime?

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the lecture's deepest question — and there is no clean answer. It's asking you to think about democracy itself as a system, not just about the specific choices made in the 1930s. If democratic societies can only mobilize adequate resources to fight catastrophic economic collapse when the alternative is literal military defeat, then democratic governance has a structural vulnerability that no amount of better policy design can fix.

Thinking It Through

Consider the evidence: In peacetime, political coalitions resist the tax levels, deficits, and government control necessary for adequate fiscal response. In wartime, those constraints dissolve because the existential stakes override normal political calculation. The New Deal spent 10% of GDP and left 14.6% unemployment. The war spent 40% of GDP and ended unemployment almost entirely.

Is this a "failure of policy"? You could argue that better-designed New Deal programs — more spending, less contradictory price manipulation, no 1937 austerity reversal — could have come closer to achieving wartime-scale results in peacetime. Keynesian economists in the administration made this argument in real time.

Or is it an "inherent limit"? You could argue that democratic publics will never voluntarily accept the economic disruption that adequate anti-depression spending requires — the deficits, the taxation, the government direction of private economic activity — unless their survival is directly threatened. On this view, the New Deal's limitations were not failures of design but features of democratic politics under normal conditions.

Both positions are defensible. The question is asking you to think at that structural level — and to connect the 1930s to questions about democratic governance that remain unresolved today.

The Verdict

Both administrations intervened. Both prolonged the crisis. The war ended what a decade of programs could not.
  • Hoover: intervened actively — then destroyed his own program with Smoot-Hawley and the Revenue Act
  • FDR: enlarged Hoover's interventions — added confused, contradictory, politically captured programs that prolonged unemployment
  • The war ended the Depression — at fiscal scale neither man would permit in peacetime, and for reasons neither could claim credit for
  • The New Deal's most durable product was not recovery — it was the story of recovery
Why did Americans dance, escape to the movies — and board ships for Stalin's Russia?

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the lecture's closing argument — and it should land without softening. The standard verdict on the Depression era is that Hoover failed and FDR saved democracy. This lecture has built a different case: that both men intervened actively, that both sets of interventions were internally contradictory and politically captured, that neither ended the Depression, and that the war — not the New Deal — finally achieved the fiscal scale the crisis required. The verdict slide states that case directly. Do not retreat from it in the closing moments.

Breaking It Down

Hoover — intervened, then undermined himself: The lecture has established that Hoover was the most interventionist peacetime president in American history to that point — RFC, Agricultural Marketing Act, public works, deficit spending. He then signed Smoot-Hawley over the objections of 1,000 economists and passed the largest peacetime tax hike in American history in the middle of a deflationary collapse. His positive interventions and his catastrophic ones were simultaneous. The verdict is not "too little" — it is "actively self-defeating."

FDR — enlarged what wasn't working: Walter Lippmann said it in 1935: the Roosevelt measures were a continuous evolution of the Hoover measures. FDR kept the RFC and expanded it. He continued public works at larger scale. He added the NRA's cartelization, the AAA's crop destruction, punitive taxation of corporate investment, and the undistributed profits tax — each of which either raised prices on the poor, discouraged the private investment recovery required, or transferred resources away from those most in need toward those most politically useful. Unemployment was still above 14% in 1940. That is the record.

The war ended it — for reasons neither man could claim: Defense spending from 1940 to 1943 achieved what neither administration's programs had approached: it injected demand at a scale large enough to actually end mass unemployment. Unemployment fell from 14.6% in 1940 to 1.9% in 1943. The mechanism was not New Deal policy — it was the emergency appropriation of wartime necessity, at a fiscal scale that peacetime democratic politics had refused to permit precisely because the political costs of that scale were too high. FDR did not choose wartime spending as an economic recovery strategy. He chose it because the country was at war.

"The story of recovery": This is Lecture 2's subject. The New Deal's most consequential long-run product was not the FDIC or Social Security — it was the cultural and political narrative that government intervention had saved America from the Depression. That narrative was produced deliberately, through FSA photography, fireside chats, federal arts programs, and the management of public memory. It was powerful enough to survive the unemployment data. Lecture 2 examines how it was constructed — and what it cost.

"Board ships for Stalin's Russia": Between 1931 and 1934 an estimated 10,000 Americans emigrated to the Soviet Union, drawn by Soviet propaganda about full employment and industrial progress at a moment when American capitalism seemed to have collapsed. This was a tiny fraction of the population — but it is historically significant as evidence of how badly the Depression had damaged faith in American institutions. The people who made that journey believed, based on available evidence, that socialist planning had solved the problem that democratic capitalism could not. Most were disillusioned within a few years. Some were caught in Stalin's purges and never came home.

Why This Matters

The closing question — why did Americans dance, escape to movies, and emigrate to the Soviet Union? — is the bridge to Lectures 2 and 3. A government that spent a decade intervening massively and still could not end mass unemployment created a specific psychological and cultural condition: people who needed relief from a reality that official policy kept promising to fix and never did. How ordinary Americans responded to that condition — through popular culture, through political radicalism, through the construction of private meaning in public crisis — is the subject of what follows.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Robert Higgs — Depression, War, and Cold War (2006)

Higgs's revisionist economic history argues that the New Deal did not merely fail to end the Depression — it actively prolonged it by creating what he calls "regime uncertainty": a business environment in which the rules governing property rights, taxation, and regulation were so unstable and hostile that private investment could not recover. His analysis of why the wartime boom did not simply continue into the postwar period (it did, once wartime controls were lifted) is essential reading for anyone who wants to understand why the Depression lasted as long as it did. Higgs represents the most rigorous economic case for the argument this lecture has been building.

Amity Shlaes — The Forgotten Man: A New History of the Great Depression (2007)

Shlaes's revisionist narrative history reconstructed the Depression from the perspective of those who were harmed by New Deal programs rather than helped by them — small businessmen crushed by NRA codes, Black workers displaced by AAA acreage reductions, taxpayers funding a bureaucracy that could not deliver recovery. Her central figure, "the forgotten man," is the ordinary American whose interests were sacrificed to the political needs of the New Deal coalition. Shlaes is a more accessible entry point to this historiography than Higgs and is well suited to undergraduate reading lists.

Voluntarism

The political philosophy that social problems should be solved through voluntary cooperation among citizens, businesses, and civic organizations — without coercive government intervention. Hoover believed that direct federal relief would undermine individual initiative, weaken local institutions, and corrupt the character of democratic citizenship. In his view, Americans helping Americans freely was both more effective and more morally sound than government compulsion.

Associationalism

Hoover's signature governing philosophy: the organized cooperation of business, government, and civic institutions through voluntary trade associations, industry groups, and coordinating bodies. As Commerce Secretary, he had used this approach to standardize industrial practices, reduce waste, and improve economic efficiency without direct government mandate. He believed the state's role was to facilitate and coordinate private actors — not to replace them.

Liquidationism

The economic position, associated with Treasury Secretary Andrew Mellon, that the Depression should be allowed to "liquidate" itself: let insolvent banks fail, let wages fall, let prices fall, let overextended businesses go bankrupt, and allow the market to clear. In theory, this deflationary process would eventually restore equilibrium at a lower price level. Hoover found this advice morally unacceptable and economically dangerous — though some later economic historians have argued that preventing liquidation prolonged the Depression by keeping misallocated resources in place.

Passed within eight hours of being submitted to Congress on March 9, 1933 — setting a speed record for major legislation. The act gave the President authority to regulate banking transactions and foreign exchange, required banks to be examined before reopening, and provided for the issuance of Federal Reserve bank notes backed by bank assets. When Roosevelt delivered a Fireside Chat explaining the legislation on March 12, deposits began flooding back into reopened banks the following Monday. It is widely considered the most immediately effective single piece of New Deal legislation.

National Recovery Administration (NRA)

Established by the National Industrial Recovery Act of 1933, the NRA required industries to establish "codes of fair competition" setting minimum wages, maximum hours, and minimum prices. It explicitly suspended antitrust law, allowing competing businesses to collude on prices and output. The NRA's Blue Eagle symbol became ubiquitous in 1933–1934. Critics on the left argued it gave too much power to big business; critics on the right objected to government price-fixing. The Supreme Court struck it down unanimously in Schechter Poultry Corp. v. United States (1935), ruling that Congress had unconstitutionally delegated legislative power to private trade groups.

Agricultural Adjustment Administration (AAA)

Created by the Agricultural Adjustment Act of 1933, the AAA aimed to raise farm prices by reducing agricultural supply. It paid farmers to take land out of production and, in the emergency summer of 1933, to destroy existing crops and livestock — including six million pigs and ten million acres of cotton. Critics argued that deliberately destroying food while people went hungry was morally indefensible. The Supreme Court struck down the AAA's processing taxes in United States v. Butler (1936), but Congress passed a modified version under the Soil Conservation and Domestic Allotment Act the same year.

American Relief Administration (1919–1923)

Created by Congress in February 1919 with a $100 million budget — boosted by another $100 million in private donations — the ARA was the successor to Hoover's wartime U.S. Food Administration. In the immediate aftermath of World War I, the ARA delivered more than four million tons of relief supplies to 23 war-torn European countries. It later extended operations into post-revolutionary Russia, where it fed nearly 11 million people daily during the famine of 1921–23. Hoover served as program director throughout. The scale and efficiency of the operation cemented his international reputation as the greatest humanitarian administrator of his generation.

Federal Farm Board

Created by the Agricultural Marketing Act of 1929, the Federal Farm Board was given a $500 million revolving fund to stabilize farm prices by purchasing surplus commodities and supporting agricultural cooperatives. It was the largest peacetime federal intervention in American agriculture to that point. The Board ultimately failed because it had no authority to limit production — it could buy surplus crops, but farmers simply grew more surplus in response to the price support. The problem of overproduction that destroyed farm prices in the early 1930s was one the Board could slow but not solve. The New Deal's Agricultural Adjustment Administration later addressed the production problem directly — by paying farmers to destroy crops and reduce acreage, a policy with its own severe costs.

Hoovervilles

Shantytowns built by unemployed and homeless Americans during the Depression, constructed from scrap lumber, cardboard, packing crates, and corrugated tin. They appeared in vacant lots, public parks, and riverbanks in virtually every major American city. The largest, in Seattle's tidal flats, housed over a thousand residents. The name — coined by Democratic National Committee publicity director Charles Michelson — was a deliberate act of political attribution, fixing responsibility for Depression poverty on the president personally. The word entered common usage almost immediately and has remained the standard term for Depression-era shantytowns ever since.

The Bonus Army (1932)

In the spring and summer of 1932, approximately 20,000 World War I veterans — calling themselves the Bonus Expeditionary Force — marched to Washington to demand early payment of service bonuses Congress had authorized in 1924 but scheduled for payment in 1945. They camped in vacant buildings and a large shantytown across the Anacostia River. When the Senate rejected the bonus bill in June, most veterans left, but several thousand remained. In late July, Hoover ordered the Army to clear them. General Douglas MacArthur, commanding infantry and cavalry with six tanks, drove the veterans from their camp and burned it. The use of military force against unarmed veterans on American streets — widely photographed and reported — was politically catastrophic for Hoover and became one of the defining images of his presidency.
