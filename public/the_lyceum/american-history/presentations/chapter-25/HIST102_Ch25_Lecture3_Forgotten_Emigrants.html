Welcome to one of the most unsettling stories in American history — one almost nobody talks about. This lecture follows thousands of Americans who, during the depths of the Great Depression, made a fateful choice: they packed up and moved to the Soviet Union, believing it offered something America could not. For many of them, that decision would cost them everything.

Breaking It Down

The subtitle — "Americans Who Voted With Their Feet" — signals something important. Voting with your feet means making a political statement not through protest or the ballot box, but by physically leaving. These weren't tourists or adventurers. They were workers, engineers, and idealists who looked at America in 1931 and said: this system has failed us.

The phrase "what the Soviet Union did with them" is quietly ominous. It promises that this story does not end well — and prepares you to think about the gap between what people were promised and what they actually received.

Why This Matters

This lecture sits at the intersection of two of the 20th century's great disasters: the American Great Depression and Stalinist terror. By following Americans into the Soviet Union, we can hold both systems up to the light simultaneously — and ask hard questions about which kind of failure is worse, and why.

Section I

Exit as a Historical Category

Why did thousands of Americans choose to leave — and what does that choice mean?

Beyond Protest

  • Depression produced three responses: voice, loyalty, and exit
  • Most protests assumed continued membership in the U.S. — reform from within
  • Exit is neither apathy nor despair — it is a reasoned verdict on a failed system
  • Emigration to the USSR: the ultimate vote of no confidence
📸 Great Depression breadline photograph

New York Breadline, c. 1930–32 · Public Domain

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide introduces a conceptual framework that will anchor the entire lecture. When a system fails you, you have three basic options: fight to change it from inside, endure it, or leave. The Americans who emigrated to the USSR chose the third option — and this slide argues that choice was not weakness or apathy, but a deliberate political judgment.

Breaking It Down

The three responses — voice, loyalty, and exit — come from economist Albert O. Hirschman's 1970 book Exit, Voice, and Loyalty. Voice means staying and demanding change: strikes, protests, voting. Loyalty means staying and enduring: holding on and hoping it gets better. Exit means leaving: physically removing yourself from the failing system.

Most Depression-era Americans chose voice or loyalty. They organized unions, voted for FDR, wrote letters to Congress. The emigrants we're studying chose exit — and the slide insists this was not apathy or despair. It was a "reasoned verdict on a failed system." They were, in effect, conducting a vote by leaving.

The phrase "ultimate vote of no confidence" captures something powerful. If you leave a country to live under a rival ideology, you are making the most extreme political statement possible — not a protest march, but an emigration.

Why This Matters

Understanding exit as a political act (rather than personal failure or naive idealism) lets you take these emigrants seriously as historical actors. They weren't fools. They were workers making rational calculations — and the lecture will show that their calculations were not entirely wrong, only incomplete in ways they couldn't have known.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Tim Tzouliadis – The Forsaken: An American Tragedy in Stalin's Russia

Tzouliadis's book is the foundational account of this story. He argues that these emigrants have been "doubly forsaken" — first by their own government, which abandoned them to Soviet terror rather than demand their return, and then by history, which forgot them. His framing aligns closely with what this slide is doing: treating exit as a meaningful act that deserves serious historical attention, not a curiosity to be dismissed.

The Rational Calculus

  • Emigrants were skilled tradespeople — engineers, machinists, auto workers, miners — not mostly political idealists
  • Industrial unemployment reached nearly 25%; millions of experienced workers faced prolonged joblessness with no safety net
  • In 1931, Soviet Amtorg ran open advertisements in U.S. newspapers for 6,000 skilled positions
  • The response was overwhelming: over 100,000 applications in just eight months

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide destroys the stereotype of the Soviet emigrant as a starry-eyed radical. The people who applied to work in the USSR were not mostly Communist Party members or political dreamers — they were skilled tradesmen facing structural unemployment in a crisis that showed no end in sight. Their decision was, by the evidence available to them, entirely rational.

Breaking It Down

Engineers, machinists, auto workers, miners: These were the backbone of American industrial production. They had skills that were in zero demand in the U.S. in 1931 — but in enormous demand in the Soviet Union. This matters because it frames emigration not as political zealotry but as labor migration. These people went where the work was, just as immigrants had always done.

25% unemployment is a staggering number. One in four working Americans had no job. And Hoover's position — that federal relief was constitutionally impermissible — meant no government safety net was coming.

FDR takes office March 1933 is a crucial timeline marker. The peak of Soviet recruitment, 1931–1932, happened before the New Deal existed. The emigrants weren't rejecting FDR's policies. They left before those policies were even on the table. This is vital for understanding why their decision seemed rational at the time.

Amtorg, the Soviet trade agency, placed help-wanted ads in American newspapers. This was not underground recruitment. It was open, legal, and publicized. The 100,000 applications for 6,000 jobs is one of the lecture's most striking statistics — it tells you how desperate the situation was.

Why This Matters

The moral and historical weight of this story depends on you understanding that these were ordinary people making ordinary economic decisions in extraordinary circumstances — not ideological fanatics. That makes what happened to many of them not just tragic, but unjust in a particular way: they were punished for a rational choice.

What 100,000 Applications Means

The Soviet offer required no ideological conversion

It only required the perception that the USSR could deliver what the U.S. could not

Amtorg ultimately hired roughly 10,000 Americans that year

The scale of the surplus tells us how permanent the crisis felt

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide asks you to do some historical math — and what you find is revealing. 100,000 applications for 6,000 jobs means the Soviet offer was not some fringe attraction for radicals. It was a mass phenomenon. The slide's key interpretive move is separating ideological conversion from economic calculation.

Breaking It Down

"No ideological conversion required" is the central insight here. You didn't have to believe in Communism to apply. You just had to believe that the Soviet Union could offer stable employment when America could not. That's a much lower bar — and in 1931, for many workers, it was a bar the USSR genuinely cleared.

Roughly 10,000 Americans hired that year — a number larger than most people would guess. This wasn't a handful of political eccentrics. It was a mid-sized city's worth of workers transplanted to the Soviet industrial heartland.

The slide's logic is elegant and bleak: the 90,000 who weren't hired tell us as much as the 10,000 who were. The surplus of applicants signals how permanent the crisis felt to those living through it. When 100,000 people are willing to emigrate to the Soviet Union, you know the American economy has lost not just its capacity but its credibility.

Why This Matters

One of the recurring traps in studying the Depression is treating it as a temporary disruption that people could see would end. They couldn't. For workers in 1931, the Depression looked like the permanent future. The Soviet offer looked like a reasonable alternative. Understanding this prevents us from projecting our hindsight onto people who had no access to it.

The Scale and Geography of the Exodus

1931–1935: Where they came from, and where they went

Where They Came From

American Crisis
  • Detroit, Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania coal country, Midwest farm machinery towns
  • Ford cut workforce from 128,000 → 37,000 by 1931
  • Steel at 12% capacity
  • Not marginal workers — the skilled core of American industry
Soviet Demand
  • Stalin's First Five-Year Plan, 1928–1932
  • Massive new industrial facilities — no existing infrastructure
  • Technical knowledge resided in the American workforce
  • The Depression made exactly the right labor available
The same Depression that emptied American factories filled Soviet construction sites

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide shows you the structural logic of the emigration with precision. It wasn't random. The specific workers who left were those produced by the specific industries the Depression hit hardest — and those were exactly the workers Stalin's Five-Year Plan desperately needed. The Depression and Soviet industrialization were, in a grim sense, made for each other.

Breaking It Down

The American side of the ledger is stark: Ford cut its workforce from 128,000 to 37,000 by 1931 — a 71% reduction. Ford had been the symbol of American industrial abundance. Its collapse signaled that this wasn't a temporary downturn. Steel at 12% capacity meant the engine of American heavy industry was essentially idle.

These weren't peripheral workers losing marginal jobs. The slide emphasizes they were the skilled core of American industry — exactly the people whose skills were most transferable to Soviet construction projects.

On the Soviet side: Stalin's First Five-Year Plan (1928–1932) demanded the rapid construction of a modern industrial economy essentially from scratch. The USSR had the ambition and the state resources — but not the technical workforce. American industrial workers carried precisely the knowledge that Soviet planners needed.

The bottom line captures it perfectly: "The same Depression that emptied American factories filled Soviet construction sites." This isn't a coincidence — it's a structural relationship worth understanding deeply.

Why This Matters

This slide resists the temptation to moralize prematurely. It simply maps supply and demand across two economies in crisis and surplus simultaneously. Historical understanding requires this kind of structural analysis before rendering judgment. The workers weren't naïve — the math genuinely worked in their favor, at least on the surface.

The Showcase Projects

  • "American Village" nearby — baseball diamonds included
  • One match drew 25,000 Soviet spectators
Magnitogorsk Steel
  • Designed by Albert Kahn — same architect as Ford River Rouge

📖 Understanding This Slide

These three facilities were the crown jewels of Soviet industrialization — and American workers built them. This slide illustrates the scale and ambition of what Americans were recruited to do, and helps explain why the early experience for many emigrants was genuinely exciting. These were not marginal postings; they were the most consequential construction sites in the world at that moment.

Breaking It Down

The Gorky Auto Plant was literally a Ford Model A factory transplanted to Soviet soil. The "American Village" with baseball diamonds tells you that the Soviets were not just importing labor — they were importing a way of life to keep those workers functioning. A baseball game drawing 25,000 Soviet spectators suggests something stranger: American workers had become cultural curiosities in their own right.

Magnitogorsk was designed by Albert Kahn — the same Detroit architect who designed Ford's River Rouge plant, then the largest industrial complex in the world. The fact that both capitalist and socialist mega-projects used the same architect is a detail that cuts against simple ideological narratives. Industrial modernity had its own logic that transcended ideology.

The Stalingrad Tractor Plant served the collectivization program — the forced reorganization of Soviet agriculture that was, simultaneously, causing mass starvation in Ukraine. American engineers running those production lines were, without knowing it, contributing to a catastrophe they had no knowledge of.

Why This Matters

These showcase projects explain why the initial years could feel rewarding. Americans were doing genuinely important work on an epic scale. The disillusionment came later — and slowly, as the political environment shifted around them while they were focused on building things.

Magnitogorsk Steel Combine Construction, c. 1931–33

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide holds two things in tension simultaneously: the genuine relief and even euphoria many emigrants felt in their first months — and the ominous John Scott quotation that tells you exactly what was underneath it. The slide is teaching you how to read historical experience at multiple levels at once.

Breaking It Down

Housing, wages, work, no breadlines — for someone who had spent years in unemployment, this was not nothing. The psychological dimension is important: "the relief of purposeful labor after enforced idleness." Being unemployed in the Depression wasn't just financially devastating; it was psychologically corrosive. Having a job, a purpose, and a place to sleep represented a real improvement for many of these workers, at least initially.

The word euphoria is deliberately chosen. Some accounts describe genuine excitement — the sense of participating in something historically significant, of building a new world. This helps explain why many emigrants defended the Soviet experiment even when conditions deteriorated: they had invested not just their labor but their sense of purpose.

Then comes John Scott's quotation from Behind the Urals — and the temperature drops. The construction "went on with a disregard for individual human life — not because the administration was deliberately cruel, but because it had been told to build a steel mill regardless of cost." This is a crucial distinction: systemic cruelty versus personal malice. The Soviet industrial machine wasn't staffed by sadists. It was run by bureaucrats following orders, and the orders didn't include accounting for human welfare.

Why This Matters

Scott's testimony is our primary source window into what this experience was actually like — and he's notable for his restraint and honesty. He doesn't romanticize or condemn wholesale. He observes. That observational stance — bearing witness without predetermined conclusions — is itself a historical and ethical act worth studying.

Section III

Three Americans

Herman, Sgovio, and Scott — the same decision, divergent fates

Victor Herman

"The Boy Who Fell Out of the Sky"
  • Left Detroit 1931, age 16, with his Ford assembly-line father
  • Champion parachutist; celebrity at Soviet state events
  • 1938: Arrested for refusing to renounce U.S. citizenship
  • Kolyma gold mines — 18 years in the Gulag
Returned to the United States in 1976 — 45 years after leaving Detroit
📸 Soviet aviation/parachute imagery, 1930s

Soviet Aviation Sport, 1930s · State propaganda context

📖 Understanding This Slide

Victor Herman's story is almost impossible to believe — and yet it is meticulously documented. He arrived in the Soviet Union as a teenager, became a genuine celebrity, and was then arrested, not for anything he did, but for refusing to sign a document. His 45-year odyssey — from Detroit Ford worker's son to Soviet Gulag prisoner to American returnee — is one of the most extreme versions of the emigrant experience.

Breaking It Down

Herman arrived in 1931 at age 16, brought by his father who worked on the Ford assembly line. He was not himself a political actor — he was a teenager who went where his family went. This matters because it removes the "he made his bed" argument that sometimes gets applied to adult emigrants. Herman was a child when the decision was made.

His rise to champion parachutist and state-sponsored celebrity tells you something important about how the Soviet system initially used American workers: as propaganda assets. Herman wasn't just an employee — he was proof that the USSR could attract talented young Americans. The state put him on display.

Then 1938: arrested for refusing to renounce U.S. citizenship. Not for espionage. Not for sabotage. For declining to sign a piece of paper. This is the pivot that defines the Terror: the crime was not action but loyalty to an outside identity. His American citizenship was itself the offense — because it implied a connection to a foreign power.

Kolyma: 18 years in the most lethal sector of the Gulag. He returned to the U.S. in 1976 — 45 years after leaving Detroit. He was 16 when he left; he was 61 when he came home. His entire adult life was consumed by this.

Why This Matters

Herman's trajectory from celebrated athlete to Gulag prisoner shows how completely the Soviet state's relationship to foreigners could reverse — and how little the individual's actual behavior mattered. He went from state asset to state enemy without changing anything about himself. What changed was the political category he was placed in.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Tim Tzouliadis – The Forsaken: An American Tragedy in Stalin's Russia

Tzouliadis devotes significant attention to Herman's case as emblematic of the broader pattern. He argues that the U.S. government's failure to intervene — even when it had documentation of American citizens being arrested and executed — represents a deliberate diplomatic choice to sacrifice individual citizens on the altar of Soviet-American relations. Herman survived in part because of his extraordinary will, but Tzouliadis makes clear that survival was not the norm.

Thomas Sgovio

The Artist Who Drew the Gulag
  • Italian-American artist from Buffalo; emigrated 1935 — driven by political idealism, not economic desperation
  • 1938: Arrested outside the U.S. embassy while renewing his passport
  • Sixteen years at Kolyma and in internal exile
  • Survived by secretly drawing portraits on cigarette paper scraps
Drawings now housed at the Hoover Institution, Stanford — one of the only visual records from inside the Gulag
📸 Sgovio Gulag drawing — Hoover Institution Archives

Thomas Sgovio, Gulag Portrait Drawing · Hoover Institution, Stanford

📖 Understanding This Slide

Thomas Sgovio is perhaps the most haunting figure in this lecture — not because his suffering was worse than others, but because he left behind evidence. His drawings on cigarette paper scraps from inside Kolyma are one of the only visual records of Gulag life made from within it. They are extraordinary documents of resistance, survival, and witness.

Breaking It Down

Unlike Herman, Sgovio's emigration in 1935 was driven by political idealism, not economic desperation. He was an artist who believed in the Soviet project. This distinction matters: he went to the USSR because he wanted to, because he believed in what it represented. The betrayal he experienced was therefore of a different character — ideological as well as personal.

Arrested outside the U.S. embassy while renewing his passport — this detail is almost theatrical in its cruelty. The act of seeking American documentation, of reaching for his legal identity, was itself treated as evidence of foreign espionage. The embassy gate had become a trap.

Sixteen years at Kolyma and in internal exile. And through it all, he drew — secretly, on cigarette paper scraps. This is not a small thing. Drawing in the Gulag was dangerous. It required materials, concealment, and the ongoing conviction that what you were recording was worth the risk. Sgovio chose to bear witness even when no one might ever see it.

His drawings are now archived at the Hoover Institution at Stanford — one of the most significant repositories of Soviet-era historical documentation in the United States. The survival of his art is itself a kind of miracle.

Why This Matters

Sgovio's case raises a question about art and testimony. In the absence of photography, journalism, or official records, a prisoner's drawings become primary historical sources. They show us faces that would otherwise be entirely lost. In that sense, his survival — and the survival of his drawings — is an act of historical resistance against the Soviet state's effort to make its atrocities invisible.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Anne Applebaum – Gulag: A History

Applebaum's Pulitzer Prize-winning history draws extensively on survivor testimony and art to reconstruct the interior life of the Gulag. She argues that acts of cultural creation — drawing, writing poetry in one's head, performing plays in secret — were forms of psychological resistance that helped prisoners maintain their sense of self. Sgovio's drawings fit directly into this pattern. Applebaum also emphasizes how deliberately the Soviet state sought to erase individual identity within the camp system, making any act of self-expression a political act by default.

John Scott

The Welder Who Bore Witness
  • Wisconsin welder; Magnitogorsk 1931 — lived as Soviet workers lived
  • Married a Russian woman; worked up to foreman; survived the purges
  • Protected by his technical utility
  • Behind the Urals (1942): neither celebration nor condemnation — observation
Later: Time magazine, Council on Foreign Relations — the analyst who lived to write
📸 Behind the Urals book cover / Magnitogorsk photograph

Behind the Urals, John Scott, 1942

📖 Understanding This Slide

John Scott is the émigré who got out — and his survival wasn't luck, it was utility. The Soviet state needed his welding skills more than it feared his foreign identity. That calculus — technical usefulness as a shield — is one of the lecture's central arguments about how the Terror actually worked. And because Scott survived, he could write: his book Behind the Urals (1942) became one of the most important eyewitness accounts of Soviet industrial life ever published.

Breaking It Down

Wisconsin welder, Magnitogorsk 1931: Scott wasn't a political figure or an intellectual. He was a skilled tradesman who went to work. He lived as Soviet workers lived — not in a foreign enclave, but embedded in the industrial community. He married a Russian woman, worked his way up to foreman, and experienced the site from the inside. This gives his testimony unusual credibility.

Protected by technical utility: When the purges began, the calculus was simple. If you were a skilled welder the plant needed, arresting you was counterproductive. Scott was too operationally valuable to become a victim. This is a deeply unsettling survival mechanism — his life was preserved not by right, but by economic calculation.

Behind the Urals (1942): "neither celebration nor condemnation — observation." Scott refused the easy narratives available to him. He didn't write an anti-Soviet exposé, nor a pro-Soviet apologia. He described what he saw with as much accuracy as he could muster. That stance — the deliberate refusal of propaganda in either direction — is itself a historical and moral achievement.

His postwar career at Time magazine and the Council on Foreign Relations shows that his Soviet experience gave him analytical credibility that shaped American understanding of the USSR for decades.

Why This Matters

Scott represents what might be called the historian's ideal: bearing witness without predetermined conclusions. He had every reason — personal, political, professional — to shape his account in a direction. He chose not to. In the context of this lecture, his testimony is all the more valuable because it comes from someone who was neither a victim nor a perpetrator, but an observer who stayed present long enough to see both the construction and the terror.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This synthesis slide forces you to think comparatively about three people who made essentially the same decision and ended up in radically different places. The question it poses — what explains the difference? — is not rhetorical. There is a real answer, and it tells you something important about how totalitarian systems operate.

Breaking It Down

Herman was a victim of the national operations — the NKVD's systematic targeting of ethnic and national groups as presumed foreign agents. His crime was refusing to renounce U.S. citizenship: he insisted on having an identity outside the Soviet system. That insistence was intolerable. Result: 18 years at Kolyma.

Sgovio was arrested at the embassy threshold — the physical act of seeking American documentation became evidence of espionage. His crime was proximity to an American institution at the wrong historical moment. Result: 16 years at Kolyma.

Scott was protected by technical utility. The state needed his welding skills more than it feared his foreign identity. His fate was not determined by his behavior, his loyalty, or his character — but by whether the NKVD's cost-benefit calculation came out in his favor. Result: survived, returned, published.

The bottom line is the sharpest line in the lecture: "Status depended not on rights — but on the state's current needs." This is the defining characteristic of totalitarian systems: there are no rights in the liberal sense. There is only utility to the state, and utility is always contingent, always revocable.

Why This Matters

This comparison is a microcosm of the lecture's larger argument. The Soviet system was not arbitrary in a random sense — it had its own logic. But that logic had nothing to do with individual rights, justice, or even loyalty to the Soviet cause. It was purely instrumental. Understanding this helps explain both why the system was so efficient at terror and why it was so morally catastrophic.

Section IV

From Guests to Enemies

The logic of the purges — how the reclassification happened

The Reclassification of Foreigners

  • As early as 1932–33: Soviet state begins confiscating foreign workers' passports for "safekeeping"
  • Without passports — workers cannot leave
  • Short-term contracts became indefinite obligations — through administrative procedure, not explicit coercion
  • Any complaint about conditions was actionable as anti-Soviet agitation
The Americans had not changed. The Soviet state's relationship to them had.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide explains the mechanism by which invited guests became trapped prisoners — and the answer is deeply unsettling because it happened gradually, through administrative procedure, without dramatic confrontation. Nobody declared war on the Americans. The state simply changed the rules around them, one bureaucratic step at a time.

Breaking It Down

Confiscating passports for "safekeeping" beginning 1932–33: This is the hinge moment. Without passports, workers could not travel internationally or leave the country. The language — "safekeeping" — is characteristically Soviet in its bureaucratic euphemism. The state was not imprisoning the workers; it was merely holding their documents. The effect was identical to imprisonment, but the framing preserved deniability.

Short-term contracts becoming indefinite obligations: Workers had arrived under specific contract terms. Those terms simply stopped being honored — not through overt coercion, but through administrative drift. The bureaucracy that had brought them there now made leaving impossible through inaction and paperwork.

Complaints actionable as anti-Soviet agitation: This completes the trap. You cannot leave. You cannot demand your rights under your contract. Any attempt to assert your interests as a foreign worker is now a criminal act. The Americans had been converted from contractors into hostages without a single dramatic moment of coercion.

The bottom line is the key interpretive point: "The Americans had not changed. The Soviet state's relationship to them had." This is how reclassification works. The individual does nothing different. The state redefines the category.

Why This Matters

Understanding how this happened through bureaucratic procedure rather than dramatic decree is essential to understanding totalitarianism. It is not always a sudden crackdown. Often it is a slow administrative tightening that only becomes visible when it is too late. This pattern — gradual reclassification through bureaucratic procedure — recurs throughout 20th-century authoritarian history.

The National Operations

  • Americans reclassified as "Trotskyist spies" or agents of Wall Street and Roosevelt
  • Hundreds arrested at or near U.S. embassy gates while renewing documents
  • Many executed at the Butovo firing range outside Moscow
  • 141 Finnish-Americans shot en masse; buried at Sandarmokh
They had left the U.S. because they had no confidence in Roosevelt — now labeled his agents

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide delivers one of the lecture's most devastating ironies. The Americans had emigrated precisely because they rejected capitalism and had no faith in the Roosevelt administration. The Soviet state then arrested them as Roosevelt's agents. The paranoid logic of the Terror transformed their act of anti-American protest into evidence of American espionage.

Breaking It Down

"Trotskyist spies" or agents of Wall Street and Roosevelt: These labels tell you everything about how the Terror operated. The charges didn't have to be plausible — they had to be politically functional. "Trotskyist spy" and "Wall Street agent" were categories that justified arrest and execution, not descriptions of actual behavior. The Americans' actual politics were irrelevant.

Arrested at or near U.S. embassy gates while renewing documents: The embassy had become a surveillance site. Americans seeking legitimate consular services — renewing passports, registering for repatriation — were arrested at the moment they reached for their American identity. The act of being American, officially documented, was itself a provocation.

Butovo firing range: Many American citizens were executed here, approximately 15 miles south of Moscow, between 1937 and 1938. At least 20,760 people were shot and buried at Butovo during the Terror. American names appear in those lists.

141 Finnish-Americans shot en masse; buried at Sandarmokh: The national operations targeted specific ethnic groups through systematic quota-driven campaigns. Finnish-Americans were a distinct category — their dual identity (American citizens, Finnish ethnicity) made them doubly suspicious in the NKVD's paranoid calculus.

Why This Matters

The irony in the bottom line is not accidental — it's the lecture's moral core. These people rejected America, and America then failed to protect them. The Soviet state labeled them American agents, when they had come precisely to escape American capitalism. There is no comfortable position to occupy in this story. Everyone — the emigrants, the American government, the Soviet state — behaved in ways that produced catastrophe.

The Embassy That Wouldn't Help

  • Ambassador Joseph Davies instructed to maintain functional relations with Stalin
  • Reported show trial confessions as "beyond reasonable doubt" genuine
  • Americans who trusted the embassy as a lifeline found it a dead end
  • Diplomatic calculation vs. the lives of U.S. citizens
📸 Ambassador Joseph Davies photograph

Ambassador Joseph Davies, Moscow, 1937–38

📖 Understanding This Slide

Joseph Davies is the American official who should have been the last line of defense for U.S. citizens trapped in the Soviet terror — and he wasn't. His story raises uncomfortable questions about institutional failure, diplomatic calculation, and the gap between what officials know and what they choose to report.

Breaking It Down

Davies was instructed to maintain functional relations with Stalin: This is critical context. He wasn't acting independently — he was implementing FDR's policy of engaging rather than confronting the Soviet government. The strategic logic was real: the U.S. needed to understand the USSR, and diplomatic relations required not antagonizing Stalin. The cost of that policy was paid by American citizens who needed their embassy to fight for them.

Reported show trial confessions as "beyond reasonable doubt" genuine: Davies attended the Moscow show trials in person. He was a lawyer. He had the training to recognize that confessions extracted under duress without defense counsel were not evidence of guilt. His report to Washington that the proceedings were credible was either a catastrophic failure of professional judgment or a deliberate diplomatic accommodation — historians disagree about which.

"Americans who trusted the embassy as a lifeline found it a dead end": This is the human consequence of the diplomatic calculation. Workers who had been trapped, whose passports had been confiscated, who were now facing arrest, turned to their embassy for help. The embassy was oriented toward maintaining Soviet-American relations, not protecting individual citizens. The bureaucratic priorities were irreconcilable with the human crisis.

Why This Matters

Davies's case is an example of institutional failure that feels especially bitter because it was not malicious — it was structural. He was doing his job as he understood it. The problem is that his job, as defined by FDR's foreign policy, made protecting American citizens in the USSR a secondary concern. When governments make that kind of trade-off, the people sacrificed are always the most vulnerable — the workers and emigrants who have no political leverage, no public profile, and no powerful advocates.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Tim Tzouliadis – The Forsaken: An American Tragedy in Stalin's Russia

Tzouliadis is withering on Davies's record. He documents how the embassy received detailed reports of American citizens being arrested and in some cases executed — and how those reports were systematically downplayed or buried in diplomatic correspondence. His argument is that the failure was not just Davies personally but a systemic State Department indifference to the welfare of working-class Americans who had no political constituency to advocate for them. Davies's memoir Mission to Moscow (1941) and its Hollywood adaptation (1943) then laundered his reputation during wartime, further obscuring the record.

Section V

The Mechanisms of the Great Terror

Not a convulsion — a quota-driven administrative operation

The Great Terror

A period of intense political repression and mass arrests in the Soviet Union, 1936–1938

Also known as the Great Purge or Yezhovshchina (after NKVD chief Nikolai Yezhov)

  • Targeted perceived enemies within the Communist Party, the military, ethnic minorities, and ordinary citizens
  • Combined public show trials with secret, quota-driven arrests and executions
  • Resulted in roughly 700,000–1.2 million deaths and over 1.5 million sent to the Gulag
  • The Americans we have discussed were caught in this machinery — reclassified from useful workers to suspected “foreign agents”
Not random chaos — a deliberate, bureaucratic campaign of state terror

Order No. 00447 and the Troika System

  • Terror officially dated 1936–1938; peak intensity July 1937–November 1938
  • Nikolai Yezhov (NKVD head) implemented via secret troikas — three-man tribunals, no defense, no appeal
  • Order 00447 (July 1937): regional arrest quotas — Category 1 (execution) / Category 2 (Gulag)
  • Original targets: 259,450 arrests; 72,950 executions
📸 NKVD Order 00447 or troika tribunal document

NKVD Order No. 00447, July 1937 · Soviet State Archive

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide shifts the lecture from individual stories to the machinery that produced them. Order 00447 is not a piece of ideological rhetoric — it is a bureaucratic document. It specifies arrest targets by region, divides victims into two categories, and assigns numerical quotas. Understanding it means understanding that the Great Terror was not a spontaneous eruption of violence but a planned, administered operation run with the logic of industrial production.

Breaking It Down

The Terror's official dates: 1936–1938; peak July 1937–November 1938. The period is sometimes called the Yezhovshchina — the era of Yezhov — after the NKVD chief who implemented it. Knowing the dates matters: this was a defined operational period, not an indefinite state of chaos.

Nikolai Yezhov implemented the terror through troikas — three-man extrajudicial panels consisting of the regional NKVD chief, the regional party secretary, and the regional prosecutor. No defense counsel. No appeal. No accused present. A troika could sentence hundreds to death or the Gulag in a single session. This was not a judicial system; it was an execution assembly line.

Order 00447: Signed July 1937, it specified regional arrest quotas — Category 1 (execution) and Category 2 (Gulag term of 8–10 years). The original targets: 259,450 arrests and 72,950 executions. These numbers were not final — Stalin repeatedly approved increases. The original targets were a floor, not a ceiling.

Why This Matters

The quota system is morally significant because it makes the mechanism of killing legible. Regional NKVD chiefs were given targets to fill. If they ran out of obvious suspects, they found new ones. The production mentality — meeting quotas, requesting increases, competing for approval — turned mass murder into a bureaucratic performance review. This is what Hannah Arendt meant by the "banality of evil": the horror is not in the malice of individual actors but in the institutional normalization of atrocity.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Oleg Khlevniuk – Stalin: New Biography of a Dictator

Khlevniuk, drawing on post-1991 Soviet archives, demonstrates that Stalin was personally involved in approving and expanding the Terror quotas — this was not a bureaucratic machine operating autonomously. Stalin signed death lists, reviewed individual cases, and pushed for increases when regional targets were met. Khlevniuk's work dismantles the argument that Stalin was unaware of or uninvolved in the operational details. The Terror had a specific author at its center.

The Kulak Operation: Final Count

  • Stalin personally approved repeated quota increases throughout 1937–38
  • Operation 00447 alone: 669,929 arrested by November 1938
  • Of those: 376,202 executed — more than half
  • Source: Soviet archives opened after 1991 — considered reliable by historians
The bureaucracy of killing: indistinguishable in its organizational logic from any other Soviet production goal

📖 Understanding This Slide

If the previous slide introduced the machinery, this slide shows you the output. The numbers here come from Soviet archives opened after 1991 — meaning these are not estimates or propaganda figures from either side. They are the state's own records of what it did to its own people. The scale is staggering; the mechanism that produced it is the point.

Breaking It Down

Stalin personally approved repeated quota increases throughout 1937–38: This is the critical evidentiary point from the archives. The Terror did not expand because regional officials went rogue. Stalin reviewed the numbers, found them insufficient, and signed orders for more arrests and more executions. The escalation was top-down.

Operation 00447 alone: 669,929 arrested; 376,202 executed. More than half of everyone arrested under this single order was executed. These numbers exceed the original targets by more than two and a half times — the result of Stalin's repeated quota approvals. And this is only one operation within the broader Terror.

Source: Soviet archives opened after 1991: The slide's note that these figures are "considered reliable by historians" is significant. Soviet archives have been contested as sources, but these operational records — created for internal bureaucratic purposes, not public consumption — are generally accepted as accurate. The state had no reason to inflate its own killing numbers in internal documents.

The bottom line — "The bureaucracy of killing: indistinguishable in its organizational logic from any other Soviet production goal" — is the lecture's most precise formulation of the Terror's nature. Production targets, regional quotas, central oversight, performance review. The same organizational logic that built Magnitogorsk also filled mass graves at Butovo.

Why This Matters

The parallel between industrial production quotas and killing quotas is not a rhetorical flourish — it is a structural observation. The Soviet state applied the same managerial framework to all of its operations, including mass murder. This is why the Terror is so difficult to comprehend morally: it doesn't look like hate crime or individual cruelty. It looks like administration. And that is precisely what makes it so historically significant.

The Moscow Show Trials

  • Aug. 1936 — Trial of the Sixteen: Zinoviev, Kamenev — "plotting with Trotsky"; all executed
  • Jan. 1937 — Trial of the Seventeen: sabotage, espionage for Germany and Japan
  • Mar. 1938 — Trial of the Twenty-One: Bukharin, Rykov, Yagoda; prosecutor Vyshinsky demanded "shoot the mad dogs"
Confessions extracted through torture, sleep deprivation, and threats against families

📖 Understanding This Slide

The Moscow Show Trials were public theater designed to accomplish two things: eliminate Stalin's potential rivals within the Communist Party, and demonstrate to the world that the Soviet Union was under constant attack from internal enemies. They were also the mechanism through which Ambassador Davies reported the confessions as genuine — making this slide directly connected to the previous section.

Breaking It Down

August 1936 — Trial of the Sixteen: Grigory Zinoviev and Lev Kamenev were among Lenin's closest collaborators — Old Bolsheviks who had made the Revolution. Their charges: plotting with Trotsky. Their fates: execution. The fact that the Revolution was now consuming its own architects tells you something about how thoroughly Stalin had transformed the political landscape.

January 1937 — Trial of the Seventeen: Sabotage and espionage charges, now with Germany and Japan added as the foreign powers behind the alleged conspiracies. The geographic scope of the alleged plots expanded with each trial.

March 1938 — Trial of the Twenty-One: Nikolai Bukharin — once called "the darling of the Party" by Lenin — sat in the dock alongside Rykov and Yagoda (the former NKVD chief). Prosecutor Vyshinsky's demand to "shoot the mad dogs" from the courtroom floor became one of the defining images of Stalinist justice.

Confessions extracted through torture, sleep deprivation, and threats against families: The confessions that so impressed Davies were not voluntary. They were produced through systematic psychological and physical destruction of the defendants. Many broke after days or weeks without sleep, after being shown evidence that family members would be arrested if they didn't cooperate.

Why This Matters

The show trials matter to this lecture in two ways. First, they were the public face of the Terror — what Western observers saw and reported on. Second, they demonstrate how the system manufactured guilt: it didn't find enemies, it created them through a production process of accusation, coercion, and confession. The Americans caught up in the national operations were processed through the same machinery, just outside the public spotlight.

The Red Army Purge and Total Scale

The Army
  • Nearly ⅔ of all general-grade officers arrested
  • 780 of 1,863 highest-ranking officers executed
  • 3 of 5 Soviet marshals shot
  • Army facing Hitler in 1941 had been decapitated three years earlier
The Overall Scale
  • 681,692 official executions, 1937–38
  • Total deaths (incl. custody): 700,000 – 1.2 million
  • More than 1.5 million arrested and sent to the Gulag
  • Source: Memorial archives + Khlevniuk

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide presents the total scale of the Terror — and adds one more dimension that has direct consequences beyond the Soviet Union's borders: the destruction of the Red Army's officer corps. The military purge is not just a humanitarian statistic. It is a strategic catastrophe that will reverberate four years later when Hitler invades.

Breaking It Down

Nearly two-thirds of all general-grade officers arrested; 780 of 1,863 highest-ranking officers executed; 3 of 5 marshals shot: These numbers represent the systematic decapitation of the Soviet military establishment. The senior officers who had professional knowledge, operational experience, and strategic understanding were eliminated in a two-year window.

"Army facing Hitler in 1941 had been decapitated three years earlier": When Germany invaded the Soviet Union on June 22, 1941, the Red Army was commanded largely by officers who had been promoted rapidly to fill positions vacated by purge victims. The catastrophic Soviet losses in the first months of Operation Barbarossa — millions of men captured or killed, entire army groups destroyed — were in significant part a consequence of the purges. Stalin's Terror created the conditions for his near-defeat.

Total scale: 681,692 official executions; 700,000–1.2 million total deaths; 1.5+ million to the Gulag: Source: the Memorial archives and Oleg Khlevniuk's scholarly work. Memorial compiled the most comprehensive database of Terror victims before its forcible dissolution by Russian courts in 2021–22 — an act widely interpreted as the Putin government's effort to restrict historical reckoning with Stalinist atrocities.

Why This Matters

The military purge connects the Terror to World War II in ways that are often underappreciated. Stalin's elimination of his most competent officers nearly cost him the war he had been building his military to fight. It is a reminder that political repression and military capacity are not separate domains — and that the internal logic of totalitarian paranoia can produce external strategic catastrophes.

Section VI

Comparative Failure

Visibility vs. concealment — the structural difference that mattered

Two Kinds of Failure

American Capitalism
  • Failure was visible — Hoovervilles, breadlines, Dust Bowl documented in newspapers and FSA photographs
  • Unemployment figures debated publicly
  • Governing party voted out in 1932
  • Crisis acknowledged; New Deal enacted
Soviet Socialism
  • Failure was concealed — Ukrainian famine 1932–33 denied entirely
  • Reporting famine internally was prosecutable
  • Great Terror operated at night, in secret, under classified quotas
  • Western correspondents rewarded for falsifying accounts
Both systems produced mass suffering — one allowed pressure for reform; the other required terror to maintain concealment

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the pivot slide — where the lecture makes its comparative argument explicit. Both American capitalism and Soviet socialism produced mass human suffering in the 1930s. The question the slide poses is not which system was more just in theory, but what structural difference determined how each system responded to its own failure. The answer is visibility versus concealment.

Breaking It Down

American failure was visible: Hoovervilles were photographed by FSA photographers and published in newspapers. Unemployment figures were debated in Congress and reported publicly. Dorothea Lange's photographs, John Steinbeck's novels, Congressional hearings — the Depression was extensively documented, and that documentation created political pressure. The governing party was voted out in 1932. The New Deal was enacted. The system responded to acknowledged failure.

Soviet failure was concealed: The Ukrainian famine of 1932–33 killed somewhere between 2.5 and 7 million people. The Soviet government denied it entirely. Reporting the famine internally was a prosecutable offense. Walter Duranty's dismissal of famine reports as anti-Soviet propaganda helped shield the Soviet government from Western scrutiny. The Great Terror operated under classified quotas, at night, in secret. Western correspondents who reported accurately were expelled; those who falsified accounts were rewarded with access.

The bottom line holds both truths simultaneously: "Both systems produced mass suffering." This prevents the slide from becoming an apologia for American capitalism. The comparison is not "America good, USSR bad." It is: one system allowed — even required — public pressure for reform; the other used terror to maintain the concealment that made reform impossible.

Why This Matters

This comparative framework is the lecture's central analytical contribution. It gives you a way to think about the relative merits and failures of different political systems without either naive celebration or false equivalence. The question is not perfection — both systems failed catastrophically. The question is what structural features determine whether failure can be acknowledged, pressured, and potentially reformed.

Walter Duranty and Journalistic Complicity

  • New York Times Moscow correspondent — most prominent Western journalist in the USSR
  • Dismissed famine reports as anti-Soviet propaganda
  • Won the Pulitzer Prize for his reporting in 1932
  • Correspondents who reported accurately were expelled; Duranty was given access
His Pulitzer has never been revoked — the New York Times acknowledged flawed reporting but declined to return the award
📸 Walter Duranty photograph

Walter Duranty, New York Times Moscow Bureau, c. 1932

📖 Understanding This Slide

Walter Duranty is the most consequential journalist you've probably never heard of. As the New York Times's Moscow correspondent in the early 1930s, he shaped how millions of Americans understood the Soviet Union — and he did so by systematically falsifying his reporting. His case raises questions about journalism, institutional accountability, and what it means to bear witness responsibly.

Breaking It Down

The most prominent Western journalist in the USSR: Duranty had access, prestige, and the platform of the New York Times. When he reported, people believed him. His dismissal of famine reports as "anti-Soviet propaganda" — in articles published while millions were dying in Ukraine — helped maintain Western ignorance of the Holodomor at a critical moment when international pressure might have made a difference.

Won the Pulitzer Prize for his reporting in 1932: The prize was awarded for a series of articles — the very articles that historians now cite as among the most egregious examples of journalistic falsification in the 20th century. He won journalism's highest honor for work that was, in significant part, propaganda.

Accurate reporters were expelled; Duranty was given access: This is the structural incentive that explains why Duranty behaved as he did. The Soviet government controlled access to the country. Journalists who reported accurately were removed; journalists who reported favorably were rewarded with continued access, interviews, and exclusive stories. Duranty's falsification was rational within the incentive structure the Soviet state had constructed.

The Pulitzer has never been revoked: The New York Times acknowledged that Duranty's reporting was flawed — but declined to return the prize. The Pulitzer board reviewed the case in 2003 and decided not to rescind the award. This ongoing debate is itself historically significant.

Why This Matters

Duranty's case is the mirror image of Thomas Sgovio's. Sgovio bore witness to the truth at enormous personal risk, on scraps of cigarette paper that almost no one would ever see. Duranty had a global platform, the New York Times, and the Pulitzer Prize — and used them to conceal the truth at no personal risk at all. The comparison is not just ironic. It asks you to think about what witness and testimony actually require, and what happens when those with the greatest institutional power to speak choose instead to silence.

What the New Deal Actually Did

  • Did not end the Depression — wartime mobilization did
  • Did not equal Soviet socialism in full-employment promise
  • Did: make failure survivable without terror
  • FSA photographs, fireside chats, letters to Eleanor Roosevelt — all forms of documented, legible crisis
The people who stayed participated in a system that retained the capacity to criticize itself

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the lecture's thesis slide — the payoff for everything that came before. It revises the standard narrative of the New Deal not by diminishing it, but by reframing what its actual achievement was. The New Deal did not end the Depression. It did something arguably more important: it kept American democracy functional during a crisis severe enough that it might not have survived.

Breaking It Down

"Did not end the Depression — wartime mobilization did": This is historically accurate and important. The Depression's unemployment figures remained high through the late 1930s. What ended it was World War II and the massive government spending that came with it. The New Deal did not solve the economic crisis.

"Did not equal Soviet socialism in full-employment promise": The USSR, through its command economy and willingness to use forced labor, achieved something close to full employment in the 1930s. On a narrow economic metric — employment — the Soviet model appeared to outperform American capitalism. This is why the emigration happened.

"Did: make failure survivable without terror": Here is the actual achievement. The New Deal created a political framework in which the crisis could be acknowledged, documented, debated, and partially addressed — without the government resorting to mass executions of its own citizens to maintain its legitimacy. FSA photographs, fireside chats, letters to Eleanor Roosevelt — all of these are forms of documented, legible crisis that fed back into the political system as pressure for change.

The bottom line completes the argument: "The people who stayed participated in a system that retained the capacity to criticize itself." That capacity — to name failure, to document it, to use it as political leverage — is the structural difference between democratic and totalitarian responses to catastrophe.

Why This Matters

This reframing of the New Deal has implications beyond the 1930s. It suggests that what democracies need to protect, above all else, is not prosperity but the mechanisms through which failure can be acknowledged and reformed. A system that can name its problems has a path forward. A system that must conceal them to survive requires terror to do so.

The Exchange They Made

The emigrants made a rational calculation based on available evidence

The Soviet Union offered work. The United States did not. They were right.

What they could not know — and what the Durantys were suppressing —

The full employment came at the cost of a totalitarian apparatus that would consume many of them

They traded uncertainty for certainty.

For most, the exchange was catastrophic.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide brings the lecture back to the human level after the structural analysis of the previous slides. It asks you to hold two truths simultaneously: the emigrants were right to make the calculation they made, given the information available to them — and the exchange was catastrophic. Both statements are true. Neither cancels the other.

Breaking It Down

"Rational calculation based on available evidence": This returns to the lecture's opening insistence that we take the emigrants seriously as historical actors. They were not fools or fanatics. The Soviet Union offered work. The United States did not. By the evidence available in 1931, the exchange looked reasonable.

"What they could not know — and what the Durantys were suppressing": This is the connection between the Duranty slide and the emigrants' fate. The information that would have complicated or reversed the calculation — the Holodomor, the scale of political repression, the coming Terror — was being actively suppressed by the journalists who had the platform to report it. The emigrants made their decision in an information environment that had been deliberately corrupted.

"They traded uncertainty for certainty." This is a beautiful encapsulation of the exchange. America offered uncertainty — unemployment that might end, a political system that might reform, a future that was genuinely unclear. The USSR offered certainty — a job, a place to sleep, a defined role. The problem is that the certainty came with a totalitarian apparatus that would eventually define many of them as enemies.

"For most, the exchange was catastrophic." The red text is deliberate. Not all of them suffered equally — Scott survived, and others did too. But most who were trapped in the USSR during the Terror years faced imprisonment, execution, or years of internal exile. The final verdict on the exchange is unambiguous.

Why This Matters

The moral weight of this slide is in its refusal to condemn the emigrants while honestly assessing the outcome. They made a rational choice in a corrupt information environment, and they paid a catastrophic price. The appropriate historical response is neither "they should have known better" nor "they were entirely victims of circumstances beyond their control." It is something more complex — and more honest.

The Open Question

Is the visibility of failure sufficient?
  • Documented suffering is not the same as remedied suffering
  • FSA photographs did not feed anyone
  • The New Deal's acknowledgment of crisis did not end it
  • The great distinction between American and Soviet failure was visibility vs. concealment
What does visibility actually require to become justice?

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the lecture's closing interrogation — not an answer, but a question it leaves you with deliberately. The argument for democratic systems over totalitarian ones has rested on the concept of visibility: the ability to acknowledge failure, document it, and use it as political pressure. This slide asks: is visibility actually enough? Does documentation lead to justice, or does it just make suffering legible without remedying it?

Breaking It Down

"Documented suffering is not the same as remedied suffering": This is the honest limit of the lecture's comparative argument. Yes, American capitalism was more visible in its failures than Soviet socialism. But visibility did not automatically produce justice. The FSA photographs of sharecroppers and Dust Bowl migrants are among the most powerful images in American history — and the people in those photographs remained poor, displaced, and suffering.

"FSA photographs did not feed anyone": A deliberately stark formulation. Dorothea Lange's Migrant Mother is an immortal image. The woman in it was still hungry when it was taken. Documentation and remedy are not the same thing. Visibility creates the conditions for political pressure — but political pressure does not automatically produce material relief.

"The great distinction was visibility vs. concealment": This is the lecture's sustained argument, restated at the close. The American system's advantage over the Soviet system was structural, not moral: it allowed the naming of failure, which created the possibility of reform. That possibility was not always realized. But it existed. In the Soviet system, it did not.

"What does visibility actually require to become justice?": This is the question the lecture ends on — and refuses to answer definitively. It is asking you to think about the gap between acknowledgment and action, between documentation and remedy, between naming a problem and solving it.

Why This Matters

This open question extends beyond the 1930s into every contemporary debate about whether documentation, journalism, photography, and public record-keeping are sufficient responses to systemic injustice. The lecture has made the case for visibility over concealment — and then, honestly, acknowledged that visibility alone is not enough. What comes after visibility is the question every democratic society must answer for itself.

Key Terms

  • Exit / Voice / Loyalty — Hirschman's framework
  • Amtorg — Soviet trade agency, recruiter
  • First Five-Year Plan
  • Kolyma — deadliest Gulag site
  • Yezhovshchina — the Terror's Russian name
  • Troika system — three-man tribunals
  • National Operations — ethnic targeting
  • Holodomor — Ukrainian famine, 1932–33

📖 Understanding This Slide

These eight terms are the vocabulary you need to master to discuss this lecture's material accurately. Each one has a popup definition in the presentation, but here's a quick guide to why each term matters — and how they connect to each other.

Breaking It Down

Exit / Voice / Loyalty: Hirschman's framework from his 1970 book. These are the three responses available when a system fails you. The lecture uses "exit" as its central interpretive category for understanding why Americans emigrated.

Amtorg: The Soviet trade agency that placed American newspaper ads for 6,000 industrial positions in 1931 and received over 100,000 applications. Remember: this was public, legal recruitment — not clandestine radicalism.

First Five-Year Plan: Stalin's 1928–1932 industrialization program that created the demand for American workers. It was also running simultaneously with the collectivization that produced the Holodomor.

Kolyma: The most lethal sector of the Gulag system, in northeastern Siberia. Where both Herman and Sgovio were sent. Anne Applebaum's Gulag: A History is the scholarly standard.

Yezhovshchina: The Russian name for the Terror, named after NKVD chief Nikolai Yezhov. It refers specifically to 1936–1938. Remember: Yezhov was himself arrested and executed by the same machinery he built.

Troika system: The three-man extrajudicial tribunals that processed cases at industrial speed — no defense, no appeal, no accused present. The mechanism through which Order 00447's quotas were fulfilled.

National Operations: The parallel NKVD campaigns targeting ethnic minorities as presumed foreign agents. This is the specific mechanism through which most American emigrants were arrested — their national identity was itself the charge.

Holodomor: The engineered famine in Ukraine, 1932–33, that killed between 2.5 and 7 million people. Denied by the Soviet government and by Duranty. Now classified as genocide by many nations. Running simultaneously with the recruitment of American workers for Soviet industrial projects.

Why This Matters

These terms connect to each other in a chain: Amtorg recruited workers during the Five-Year Plan; those workers eventually became targets of national operations; those operations were administered through troikas during the Yezhovshchina; survivors ended up at Kolyma. Knowing the terms is necessary — but seeing how they interlock is what historical understanding actually looks like.

Hoover Institution on War, Revolution, and Peace

A public policy think tank and research library at Stanford University, founded by Herbert Hoover in 1919. The Hoover Institution holds one of the most significant collections of primary source materials on Soviet history in the United States, including documents from the post-1991 opening of Soviet archives and the archives of organizations such as Memorial (the Russian human rights group dissolved by court order in 2021). Thomas Sgovio's Gulag drawings are held in the Hoover Institution's archives.

Andrei Vyshinsky (1883–1954)

Soviet prosecutor who served as chief state prosecutor in all three Moscow Show Trials. His courtroom theatrics — including the demand to "shoot the mad dogs" at the 1938 trial — became emblematic of Stalinist judicial procedure. Vyshinsky later represented the Soviet Union at the Nuremberg Tribunals (where he helped prosecute Nazi war criminals for crimes against humanity) and served as Soviet Foreign Minister 1949–53. The irony of his Nuremberg role was not lost on Western observers.

Memorial (Международное общество "Мемориал")

A Russian human rights organization founded in 1989 to document Soviet-era repression. Memorial compiled the most comprehensive databases of Gulag victims and executed persons, drawing on post-1991 archive access. It was forcibly dissolved by Russian courts in December 2021–January 2022 — a decision widely interpreted as the Putin government's attempt to restrict historical reckoning with Stalinist atrocities. Memorial's records and methodology remain the scholarly standard for Terror casualty figures.
