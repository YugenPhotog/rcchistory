The Great Society
and the War on Poverty

Intentions vs. Results

HIST 102: U.S. History Since 1877  |  Chapter 29, Lecture 1

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze the ideological foundations of the Great Society and War on Poverty
  • Evaluate economic and social outcomes of major programs
  • Explain unintended consequences on family structure and community cohesion
  • Assess the gap between stated goals and measurable long-term results
  • Argue a position on whether federal expansion accelerated or impeded Black economic progress

Section I

The Eisenhower Baseline

What did market-driven progress actually look like?

The 1950s: A Statistical Reality

  • Real wages rose steadily through the decade
  • Homeownership climbed from 55% → 62% (1950–1960)
  • Poverty rate fell from ~32% → ~22% (1950–1960)
  • Progress driven by private-sector wages, education, migration — not redistribution
The Eisenhower baseline is not nostalgia — it is a measurable counterfactual

Eisenhower in the Oval Office · ca. 1955 · Public Domain · Wikimedia Commons

Black Progress Before 1964

The Numbers
  • Black poverty: 87% (1940) → 47% (1960)
  • Two-parent Black families: ~78% in 1960
  • Achieved under legal segregation
  • No Great Society — no federal anti-poverty programs
The Drivers
  • Family stability and two-parent households
  • Great Migration to higher-wage northern labor markets
  • Entrepreneurship within Black communities
  • Educational investment across generations
Key question: Did federal programs after 1964 accelerate or disrupt this trajectory?

Fiscal Restraint as Governing Principle

  • Balanced the federal budget in 3 of 8 years
  • Federal spending kept below 19% of GDP
  • Resisted expanding the New Deal welfare architecture
  • Used federal power targeted and constitutionally grounded: Interstate Highway System, Little Rock desegregation
"Public policy could itself become the captive of a scientific-technological elite."
— Eisenhower, Farewell Address, 1961
Eisenhower anticipated Hayek's warning: central planners cannot possess the dispersed knowledge that decentralized institutions hold

Two Governing Philosophies

Eisenhower
  • Targeted, constitutionally grounded federal action
  • Pay-as-you-go financing
  • Resist open-ended programmatic expansion
  • Epistemic humility: markets and families know more than planners
Johnson
  • Comprehensive expert-engineered social outcomes
  • Deficit spending accepted as tool of progress
  • Launched during economic expansion — not crisis
  • "Mystical belief" in planners' capacity to engineer society
The Great Society was discretionary, not countercyclical — its incentive effects, not its emergency necessity, must be evaluated

Black poverty fell from 87% to 47% between 1940 and 1960 — before the Civil Rights Act and before the Great Society. What forces drove that progress, and what does that trajectory imply about the role of federal intervention in economic advancement?

Section II

The Great Society's Architecture

Vision, programs, and intellectual foundations

The Political Moment of 1964

  • JFK assassination gave Johnson enormous legislative capital
  • Michael Harrington's The Other America (1962) argued 40–50 million Americans were invisible to mainstream prosperity
  • Postwar liberal confidence: Keynesian tools could engineer social outcomes
  • Architects: Shriver, Heller, and Council of Economic Advisers social scientists

"This administration today, here and now, declares unconditional war on poverty in America. I urge this Congress and all Americans to join with me in that effort."

Wars have victory conditions — this announcement would haunt every subsequent evaluation of program outcomes

Core Legislation: 1964

  • VISTA — domestic Peace Corps analog; Head Start — pre-K for low-income children

Section III

Outcomes vs. Promises

Poverty trends, family collapse, and bureaucratic expansion

The Poverty Data — Read Carefully

The Claim
  • Official poverty rate: 19% (1964) → 11% (1973)
  • Great Society advocates cite this as proof of program success
−8%
poverty rate drop, 1964–1973
Two Critical Rebuttals
  • Economy grew at >4% GDP annually — reduction would have occurred regardless of new programs
  • Poverty stalled after early 1970s: has oscillated between 11–15% ever since, despite trillions spent
  • The pre-1964 secular decline did not resume
Victory was redefined by counting income support itself as flourishing — a permanent floor, not a ladder

The Family Structure Collapse

  • 1960: ~78% of Black children in two-parent households
  • 1980: fallen to ~56%
  • 2000: fallen to ~38%
  • Timing of collapse coincides precisely with AFDC expansion
The "man-in-the-house" rules made the rational economic response to welfare eligibility staying separated

Moynihan's Warning (1965)

  • Assistant Secretary of Labor Daniel Patrick Moynihan delivered The Negro Family: The Case for National Action
  • Argued instability was product of policy incentives, not character — historical trauma compounded by welfare rules punishing intact families
  • Pilloried as "blaming the victim" — critics suppressed the empirical substance
  • His predictions proved accurate in every measurable dimension
"The Negro community has been forced into a matriarchal structure which, because it is so out of line with the rest of American society, seriously retards the progress of the group as a whole."
— Moynihan, The Negro Family, 1965

Pruitt-Igoe: A Pattern, Not an Anomaly

  • St. Louis: 33 towers, 2,870 units — opened 1954 as model of modern public housing
  • By mid-1960s: crime-ridden and structurally deteriorating
  • Demolished 1972–1976 — less than 20 years after opening
  • Failure was not architectural — it was social and economic
Concentrating poverty + removing mixed-income community + welfare rules discouraging male presence = predictable outcome

Bureaucratic Expansion and Its Costs

Scale of Growth
  • Federal domestic programs: ~40 (1960) → over 400 (1970)
  • OEO alone: 2,000+ employees, $1B+ annual budget by late 1960s
  • Administrative overhead consumed significant fractions of appropriated funds
Structural Problems
  • Community action agencies: organizational dysfunction, political patronage, documented corruption
  • Legal Services expanded welfare rolls through court advocacy — creating constituencies for dependency
  • Programs designed to maximize enrollment, minimize accountability
Nixon did not dismantle the Great Society — he expanded it, demonstrating the institutional momentum comprehensive welfare architectures generate

Section IV

Why the Architecture Failed

Incentive distortion, Hayek's warning, and the Moynihan moment

Hayek's Warning: The Epistemic Problem

  • Friedrich Hayek (1945): central planners cannot possess the dispersed, local, tacit knowledge necessary to plan social outcomes
  • The price system solves this by transmitting information without requiring anyone to possess it completely
  • Great Society architects substituted their abstract models for the rich information of decentralized social processes
  • Mistaking the symptom (low income) for the cause, and responding without modeling behavioral consequences
The Central Methodological Error:

Defining poverty as an income threshold made income transfer the obvious solution — without modeling the behavioral consequences of how people respond to those transfers

Planners vs. Local Knowledge

The Theory
  • "Maximum feasible participation" — poor communities design their own uplift
  • Social scientists identify causes of poverty with sufficient precision to design reliable interventions
  • Washington expertise can engineer behavior in millions of individuals across diverse local circumstances
The Reality
  • CAPs became vehicles for political organizing by activist organizations disconnected from communities
  • Conflicts with elected local governments → political backlash
  • Nixon dismantled the OEO — but expanded the underlying programs
  • Political durability of dependency outlasted administrative reform

Incentive Structures Work Regardless of Intent

  • AFDC "man-in-the-house" rules: able-bodied male present = family ineligible for benefits
  • Rational economic response: stay separated to preserve eligibility
  • Moynihan predicted this in 1965 — was dismissed as "blaming the victim"
  • Programs that punish household formation do not need to intend these outcomes to produce them
The institutions that produced pre-1964 progress were weakened precisely when federal policy claimed to be strengthening them
≠ Intent
Incentive effects operate
independent of intentions

This is not a story of bad faith — it is a story about what happens when planners do not model behavioral responses

What the Great Society Did Achieve

Genuine Accomplishments
  • Medicare substantially improved health outcomes for elderly Americans
  • Head Start produced modest early-childhood educational benefits
  • Civil Rights Act (1964) & Voting Rights Act (1965) — constitutionally significant advances in equal citizenship
The Honest Assessment
  • Head Start gains largely dissipate by third grade in follow-up studies
  • Civil Rights legislation is politically inseparable from, but analytically distinct from, the War on Poverty programs
  • Programs that failed their own ambitious targets imposed costs compounding over six decades
The honest position: the Great Society accomplished some things; the programs designed to engineer social outcomes through redistribution failed their own targets

Closing Synthesis

The Costs of Hubris

What the evidence demands and what the tragedy deserves

The Great Society's Deepest Lesson

  • Architects were sincere, intelligent, operating within the dominant intellectual paradigm — results diverged from promises anyway
  • Pre-1964 Black economic progress — achieved under conditions that should have made it impossible — is the most powerful analytical datum
  • Families, churches, migration networks, and small businesses produced two generations of advancement without federal engineering
  • Institutions that actually lift people were crowded out by programs designed to replace them
The Central Failure:

When epistemic humility required by good governance is displaced by ideological certainty that experts can improve on decentralized institutions, results diverge from promises

Welfare Reform and the Verdict

1996 Reform
  • Most significant retrenchment of the Great Society architecture
  • Replaced entitlement with conditionality
  • Sharp decline in welfare caseloads followed
  • Short-term improvements in employment and earnings among former recipients
What It Proved
  • Behavioral assumptions embedded in original programs had been wrong
  • Incentive structures, restored, could revive self-sufficiency
  • Political durability of Great Society programs: took 30 years to achieve reform
  • Whether institutional and cultural damage is fully reversible remains open

The Enduring Question

Did the Great Society accelerate the pre-1964 trajectory of progress?

Or did it interrupt the institutions — families, communities, churches, markets — that were actually producing it?

The data on poverty stagnation, family collapse, and welfare dependency after 1965 makes this a defensible analytical claim — not merely ideological assertion

The republic survived. The open question: did reform come soon enough?

The Other America (1962)

Michael Harrington's influential book argued that 40–50 million Americans lived in a "culture of poverty" invisible to middle-class society — locked out of the general affluence of the 1950s. The book was enormously influential on Kennedy and Johnson administration poverty policy. Critics note it obscured the rapid pre-1964 progress already underway and positioned government as the only agent capable of reaching those left behind.

×
Office of Economic Opportunity (OEO)

The administrative hub of the War on Poverty, created by the Economic Opportunity Act of 1964 and headed by Sargent Shriver. It coordinated Community Action Programs, Job Corps, VISTA, Head Start, and other programs. By the late 1960s it had over 2,000 employees and a budget exceeding $1 billion annually. Nixon dismantled the OEO in the early 1970s, though most programs it administered were transferred to other agencies and continued.

×
Community Action Programs (CAPs)

Local anti-poverty organizations funded by the OEO under the mandate of "maximum feasible participation" of the poor in designing their own uplift. In practice, many CAPs became vehicles for political organizing by activist organizations with little connection to the communities they claimed to represent. Their conflicts with elected local governments generated political backlash and eventually contributed to the OEO's dismantlement. Hayek's critique of central planning applies with particular force to CAPs: Washington-designed programs that presumed to empower local communities ended up substituting outside organizers for genuine community institutions.

×
AFDC — Aid to Families with Dependent Children

The primary federal cash welfare program, originally part of the Social Security Act of 1935 but expanded dramatically under the Great Society. AFDC provided cash assistance to low-income families with children. Many states administered "man-in-the-house" rules requiring that no able-bodied male be present for a family to qualify for benefits. This created a financial incentive for fathers to leave or remain absent from households — precisely the family structure consequence Moynihan warned about in 1965. AFDC was replaced by TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families) in the 1996 welfare reform.

×
Friedrich Hayek (1899–1992)

Austrian-British economist and philosopher, Nobel laureate in Economics (1974). His 1945 essay "The Use of Knowledge in Society" argued that the central problem of economic organization is not the allocation of resources by a rational planner but the utilization of knowledge that exists only in dispersed, particular, tacit forms — knowledge about local conditions, individual preferences, community norms, and specific circumstances that no central authority can aggregate. The price system, Hayek argued, solves this problem through signals that transmit information without requiring anyone to possess it completely. His analysis was directly applicable to Great Society planning, though most of the programs' architects had not seriously engaged with his work.
