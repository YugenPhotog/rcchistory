Destructive Generation

The Sixties Counterculture and the Assault on the American Creed
HIST 102: U.S. History Since 1877  |  Chapter 29, Lecture 2

Section I

The World the Counterculture Rejected

What did midcentury America actually look like — in measurable terms?

The 1950s: What the Data Show

  • Church attendance at highest recorded levels — ~49% weekly by 1958
  • Violent crime rates low and declining
  • Divorce rates stabilized after postwar spike, then falling
  • SAT scores reached their post-WWII peak in the early 1960s
These are the baseline indicators — measure the counterculture's legacy against them
📸 Search: "1950s American suburban life photograph"

Midcentury American social fabric · Public Domain

The 1950s: Real Failures

Genuine Injustices
  • Legal segregation — a constitutional atrocity
  • McCarthyism produced injustice against falsely accused individuals
  • Women's professional aspirations systematically constrained
The Analytical Task
  • Civil Rights Movement: appealed to the American Creed to fix specific violations
  • Counterculture: moved toward rejecting foundational principles altogether
  • These are categorically different political projects
Critique and rejection are not the same act

Two Ways to Challenge a Society

Civil Rights Movement
  • Invoked the Declaration and Constitution
  • Held America to its own stated principles
  • Operated through constitutional processes
  • Produced Voting Rights Act, legal transformation
Counterculture
  • Framed American principles as themselves oppressive
  • Rejected the foundations — not specific violations
  • Moved toward coercive disruption
  • Produced social disintegration alongside reform
The first produced constitutional progress; the second produced the social indicators crisis

⏸ Pause & Reflect

The Civil Rights Movement and the counterculture both challenged aspects of 1950s American society. What is the fundamental difference in how they made their arguments — and which approach was more likely to produce lasting constitutional progress?

Section II

The New Left's Origins

Port Huron, participatory democracy, and the critique of bureaucracy

Students for a Democratic Society

  • SDS founded 1960; Port Huron Statement adopted June 1962
  • Drafted primarily by Tom Hayden
  • Called for participatory democracy — politics as meaningful engagement, not institutional routine
  • Challenged the Old Left's entrenchment in labor unions and the Democratic Party
📸 Search: "Port Huron Statement 1962 SDS pamphlet cover"

Port Huron Statement · 1962 · Public Domain

Port Huron Statement (1962)

"We are people of this generation, bred in at least modest comfort, housed now in universities, looking uncomfortably to the world we inherit... the permeating and victimizing fact of human degradation, symbolized by the Southern struggle against racial bigotry, compelled most of us from silence to activism."
— Tom Hayden et al., Port Huron Statement, Students for a Democratic Society, June 1962
Note: the authors were bred in modest comfort — alienation here is cultural, not economic

Port Huron: Legitimate Critique & Hidden Flaws

Genuine Insights
  • Bureaucratic alienation in universities was real
  • Students felt excluded from meaningful civic participation
  • Critique of Cold War complacency
Corrosive Assumptions
  • Anti-anti-communism: U.S. and USSR treated as morally equivalent
  • Prosperity framed as spiritual poverty rather than genuine achievement
  • Elite students cast as the natural vanguard of radical change
Horowitz: the framework made America's achievements invisible and its imperfections absolute

The Vacuum Bell Didn't See

  • Daniel Bell, The End of Ideology (1960): grand ideological conflict exhausted in prosperous democracies
  • Argued pragmatic reform, not revolution, was the appropriate mode
  • Bell was right about the Old Left — but missed the vacuum the New Left would fill
  • Students in the most affluent generation in history treated prosperity as alienation
Glazer: a revolt against successful bourgeois management, not genuine deprivation
📸 Search: "Daniel Bell sociologist portrait"

Daniel Bell · Sociologist · Columbia University

Elite Alienation vs. Economic Grievance

New Left leadership: students from affluent families at elite universities

People with the most to gain from the existing order

Their radicalism: cultural, not economic

Workers moderate demands over time; culturally alienated students do not

Cultural radicalism follows different logic than labor movements — no economic stake in stability

Section III

Berkeley and Beyond

From free speech to coercive disruption — where did the line get crossed?

The Free Speech Movement (1964)

  • Trigger: UC Berkeley banned political tables on campus sidewalk
  • Initial demand: defensible — free speech rights shouldn't end at the university gate
  • Both left-wing civil rights groups and Young Republicans objected
  • Nathan Glazer (Berkeley faculty): sympathized with the free-speech claim — at first
📸 Search: "Mario Savio Free Speech Movement Berkeley 1964"

Mario Savio · Sproul Hall · Berkeley, 1964 · Public Domain

From Claim to Veto

"There is a time when the operation of the machine becomes so odious, makes you so sick at heart, that you can't take part; you can't even passively take part, and you've got to put your bodies upon the gears and upon the wheels, upon the levers, upon all the apparatus, and you've got to make it stop."
— Mario Savio, Sproul Hall Steps, UC Berkeley, December 2, 1964
This is not an argument — it is an announcement of a veto right over institutions one finds morally intolerable

The Moment Glazer Drew the Line

Legitimate Protest
  • Appeals to constitutional principles
  • Invokes shared values
  • Makes an argument
  • Tolerates disagreement
Coercive Disruption
  • Sit-ins, building occupations, shutdowns
  • Physical interference with institution's operation
  • Moral theater — intensity of indignation replaces argument
  • Overrides procedural norms that protect everyone
Podhoretz: this replaced the liberal tradition of institutional engagement with a politics of moral theater

Radical Tactics Spread, 1965–1968

  • Columbia (1968): SDS occupied president's office; faculty files rifled — goal was delegitimization, not reform
  • Cornell: Black student militants occupied student union armed with rifles
  • Mark Rudd's slogan: "Bring the war home"
  • Horowitz: moral narcissism — certainty of one's indignation as sufficient warrant for any action

Section IV

The Moral Inversion

Family, faith, violence, and the collapse of social norms

The Counterculture at Its Peak

  • Summer of Love (1967), Woodstock (1969)
  • Rejection of monogamy, family structure, and Judeo-Christian moral framework
  • Psychedelics, Eastern spiritualism as replacement for religious practice
  • Core logic: individual desire for liberation is the highest moral standard
Podhoretz: "If it feels good, do it" is not just an attitude — it is a comprehensive moral philosophy
📸 Search: "Haight-Ashbury 1967 Summer of Love San Francisco"

Haight-Ashbury district, San Francisco · 1967

The Principle of Constraint

Judeo-Christian Tradition
  • Authentic selfhood achieved through discipline, commitment, fidelity
  • Subordination of desire to duty
  • Institutions — marriage, family, church — as vehicles of flourishing
Counterculture Inversion
  • Authentic selfhood = what one feels before external norms
  • Institutions are instruments of repression
  • Constraint itself — not specific unjust rules — is the enemy
This philosophy is incompatible with the sustained commitment that families, communities, and democracy require

The Social Indicators Respond

  • 1963–1973: Violent crime rate roughly doubles — falling trend in 1950s reversed
  • After 1965: Divorce rates climb steeply — had stabilized through 1950s
  • 1963–1978: SAT scores decline for fifteen consecutive years
  • 1960s–70s: Illegitimacy rates begin long upward trajectory; drug use expands from marginal to mass
Magnitude and timing of shifts make it analytically untenable to treat these as entirely unrelated to the simultaneous cultural revolution

From SDS to the Weather Underground

  • By 1968 SDS: ~100,000 members, fractured by internal competition over who was most radical
  • Logic of escalation: if America is fundamentally corrupt, reformists are part of the problem
  • The Weathermen (Bill Ayers, Bernardine Dohrn, Mark Rudd): embraced revolutionary violence
  • Horowitz: moral certainty operated as epistemological closure — evidence that didn't fit was discarded
📸 Search: "Vietnam War protest march Washington DC 1967"

Anti-war march · Washington, D.C. · 1967 · Public Domain

The Logical Endpoint

"Dig it. First they killed those pigs, then they ate dinner in the same room with them, then they even shoved a fork into a victim's stomach. Wild!"
— Bernardine Dohrn, 1969 SDS National Council, praising the Manson Family murders
From "love, reflectiveness, reason" (Port Huron, 1962) to celebrating civilian murder (1969) — not an aberration but a trajectory

Television as Amplifier

The Reach
  • 93% of U.S. households owned a TV by 1965
  • Three networks commanded audiences dwarfing today's fragmented media
  • Protest imagery entered living rooms of millions with no direct contact with the movement
The Irony
  • "Silent majority" was real — many viewers alienated, not converted
  • Commercial media separated counterculture style from political content
  • The anti-capitalist movement became one of the most successfully marketed cultural products in history

⏸ Pause & Reflect

David Horowitz argues that Bernardine Dohrn's 1969 statement praising the Manson murders was the logical endpoint of the New Left's moral framework — not an aberration. At what point in the movement's development did the moral inversion become irreversible?

Closing Synthesis

Cultural Capital, Its Destruction, and Its Partial Recovery

Who actually bore the costs of the counterculture's disruption?

Elite Rebellion, Working-Class Cost

Who Led the Revolt
  • Students from affluent families at elite universities
  • The demographic with most to gain from the existing order
  • Alienation was cultural, not economic
Who Bore the Costs
  • Urban and rural poor
  • Working-class families
  • Minority communities still working toward economic stability
  • Lacked resources to insulate themselves from cultural disorder
Magnet: the counterculture's assault on norms most damaged those at the bottom — who had no buffer

The Insider Verdict

David Horowitz
  • Active radical at Berkeley; edited Ramparts
  • Left the movement after Black Panther Party violence
  • Diagnosis: moral certainty produced epistemological closure
  • American wickedness was the prior commitment — evidence was filtered through it
Ronald Radosh
  • Knew many SDS founders personally
  • Anti-anti-communism was ideological, not naïve
  • New Left replicated Old Left's selective epistemology about Stalin
  • Learning was impossible because it required confronting the framework itself

Damage, Recovery, Open Questions

  • 1980: Reagan election — significant popular repudiation of the counterculture's political expression
  • 1990s: Crime rates decline; welfare reform (1996) reduces dependency rolls
  • Still open: Cultural capital — shared norms, institutional trust, family structures — is not rebuilt quickly
  • The question: Is the damage primarily contained — or still compounding?
The historical record alone cannot answer that question — but it can clarify what was at stake

What This Lecture Teaches

A political movement can begin with defensible grievances and escalate toward comprehensive social indictment that the original grievances do not justify. Understanding that transition — and what enables it — is one of the central analytical problems in modern American political history.

Critique holds a society to its own principles  |  Rejection discards those principles as oppressive

The first produced the Voting Rights Act — the second produced the social disintegration data

Social Indicators Crisis

Between the early 1960s and mid-1970s: violent crime roughly doubled, divorce rates climbed ~100%, illegitimacy rates began a long upward trajectory, SAT scores fell for 15 consecutive years, and drug use expanded from a marginal to a mass phenomenon. The timing coincides precisely with the counterculture's peak years of influence.

×
Tom Hayden (1939–2016)

Principal author of the Port Huron Statement. Later a California state assemblyman and senator. His trajectory — from radical organizer to mainstream Democratic politician — illustrates how the New Left's energy was eventually absorbed by institutional politics, though not without lasting cultural consequences.

×
Anti-Anti-Communism

A stance that refused to criticize the Soviet Union and its communist allies while subjecting American institutions to comprehensive moral indictment. Identified by Norman Podhoretz as the intellectual signature of the New Left. The result: evidence of communist atrocities (the Cultural Revolution, Stalin's purges) was minimized or explained away, while evidence of American imperfection was treated as confirming the fundamental indictment of American society.

×
Daniel Bell (1919–2011)

Harvard sociologist and co-founder of The Public Interest. His 1960 book The End of Ideology argued that grand ideological conflicts had been exhausted in prosperous postwar democracies. Within two years, the Port Huron Statement showed he had identified the Old Left's decline without anticipating the New Left's emergence. Bell later described the 1960s as producing "cultural contradictions of capitalism."

×
Nathan Glazer (1923–2019)

Sociologist at Berkeley during the Free Speech Movement and later at Harvard. Co-author of The Lonely Crowd and Beyond the Melting Pot. His contemporaneous account of the FSM in The Public Interest (1968) is among the most analytically rigorous firsthand accounts. He was initially sympathetic to the free-speech claim but drew the line at the tactics that followed, making him one of the early voices of neoconservative critique of the New Left.

×
The Weathermen / Weather Underground

A domestic terrorist faction that emerged from SDS's 1969 collapse. Led by Bill Ayers, Bernardine Dohrn, and Mark Rudd. Conducted bombings of the U.S. Capitol, Pentagon, and New York City Police headquarters in the early 1970s. The group's "Days of Rage" in Chicago (October 1969) ended with violent clashes with police. Ayers later became a Chicago education professor; his 2001 memoir's publication on September 11, 2001 created significant controversy.

×
David Horowitz (b. 1939)

Son of communist parents; edited Ramparts magazine in the 1960s, a leading New Left publication. His break with the movement came after a bookkeeper he had recommended to the Black Panther Party was murdered — and the movement's response made clear that ideological loyalty outweighed truth-telling. His memoir Radical Son (1997) is the most analytically penetrating insider account of the New Left's moral structure.

×
Ronald Radosh (b. 1937)

Historian and former communist who spent decades documenting the Old and New Left from the inside. His scholarly work on the Rosenberg case (The Rosenberg File) demonstrated their guilt — a finding that estranged him from many former allies. His memoir Commies (2001) traces the continuities between the Old Left's Stalin apologetics and the New Left's anti-anti-communism, showing how selective epistemology operated across generations.
