Introduction

Today's Big Question: How did "democracy" for white men coexist with exclusion of others?

Three Lenses for Understanding This Era:

  1. Economic Change: Market Revolution transforms daily life
  2. Social Divisions: Class, race, immigration create new tensions
  3. Cultural Expression: Popular entertainment reveals anxieties
Key Paradox: Era of "common man" democracy is also era of increasing inequality and racial violence

What Was Jacksonian Democracy?

The Era: ~1828–1850

Key Political Changes:

  • Elimination of property requirements for voting (white men only)
  • Rise of mass political parties (Democrats vs. Whigs)
  • Popular political participation: parades, rallies, campaigns

The Jacksonian Ideal:

  • "Common man" can succeed through hard work
  • Hostility to privilege and "aristocracy"
  • Suspicion of elites, banks, and federal power

The Central Contradiction:

This "democracy" was built on:

  • Exclusion of women (no voting rights)
  • Exclusion of free Blacks (even in North)
  • Violent removal of Native Americans
  • Expansion of slavery in South

Democracy for some required domination of others

The Market Revolution Transforms Daily Life

New Transportation & Communication:

  • Erie Canal (1825), railroads, telegraph connect distant markets
  • Result: Local economies become part of national system

From Self-Sufficiency to Wage Labor:

  • Before: Families produce what they consume (farm, artisan shop)
  • After: Work for wages, buy goods in market
  • Effect: More consumer goods BUT less economic independence

"Time Discipline" Replaces Natural Rhythms:

  • Factory system: Work by the clock, not by the task
  • Before: Farmer works by daylight and weather
  • After: Factory worker punches clock, fixed hours
  • New anxiety: "Time is money"
The Paradox: More opportunity AND more insecurity

The "Self-Made Man" Ideal

New Definition of Success:

  • Old idea (aristocratic): Success = inherited wealth, family name
  • New idea (democratic): Success = hard work, ambition, talent
  • The ideal: Poor boy becomes rich through effort

Why This Matters:

  • Opens doors for white men without family connections
  • Celebrates mobility and opportunity
  • Creates intense competition

The Dark Side:

  • If success = personal effort, then failure = personal failing
  • Poverty seen as individual moral failure, not structural problem
  • Blames the victim for economic hardship

Reality Check: Most people did NOT become rich, but the myth was powerful

Working-Class Life and Labor Protest

Urban wage laborers form a distinct class identity

Workingmen's Parties demand:

  • Shorter hours (10-hour workday instead of 12-14)
  • Free public schools
  • Fair wages

Early Labor Actions:

  • Strikes and early unions among printers and carpenters
  • Philadelphia shoemakers strike (1806)
  • Workingmen's Party of New York (1829)
The Paradox: America celebrates "equality" but develops sharp class divisions
Class tension in a culture that denied the existence of classes

Race and Free Black Communities

Free Black populations grew rapidly in northern cities during the 1820s–1840s

Black churches, schools, and mutual aid societies:

  • The African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church became center of faith, education, activism
  • Founded by Richard Allen (1816)

Writers and orators challenged white supremacy:

  • David Walker: Appeal to the Coloured Citizens of the World (1829)
  • Maria Stewart: First American woman to speak publicly about race and gender (1830s)

Reality of "Freedom":

  • Racism, segregation, job discrimination limited opportunity
  • Most northern states restricted voting, jury service, public access

These communities became foundation for later abolitionist and civil rights activism

Immigration, Nativism, and Urban Life

Rising Immigration Reshapes Cities:

  • The Numbers: Irish & German immigration (1820s-40s): ~1 million each
  • By 1850: Foreign-born = 10% of U.S. population

Immigrant Experiences:

  • Irish: Urban laborers, concentrated in cities
  • Germans: Artisans and farmers, more dispersed

Urban Problems:

  • Poverty, overcrowding, poor sanitation
  • Cholera outbreaks devastate immigrant neighborhoods
Nativist Backlash:
Anti-Catholic riots and violence
Philadelphia riots (1844): 20+ killed
Shows how "democracy" can unleash mob violence

Popular Culture in the Democratic Age

Mass Media Emerges:

  • Penny press (1830s): Cheap newspapers for working class
  • Campaign songs, broadsides: Politics becomes entertainment
  • Literacy rates rising (white population)

Mass Entertainment:

  • Theaters, circuses, taverns become public gathering spaces
  • Entertainment crosses class lines
  • Minstrel shows become most popular entertainment
What This Reveals:
Democracy creates mass culture (not just elite culture)
Entertainment reflects anxieties about race, class, gender
We'll focus on minstrelsy because it shaped American race relations for generations

Origins and Rise of Blackface Minstrelsy

What Was Blackface Minstrelsy?

  • White performers in blackface makeup
  • Caricatured Black speech, dance, and music
  • Most popular entertainment form in 19th-century America

How It Started:

  • Thomas D. Rice's "Jim Crow" character (1820s-30s)
  • Claimed to "borrow" from Black performers
  • Virginia Minstrels (1843): First full minstrel troupe

The Show Structure:

  • Act I: Comic songs and banter in semicircle
  • Olio: Variety acts and specialty numbers
  • Finale: One-act farce or skit

Stock Characters: "Jim Crow" (rural buffoon), "Zip Coon" (urban dandy), "Mammy"

Why Minstrelsy Mattered

Cultural Function:

1. United White Audiences Across Class Lines

  • Working-class AND elite venues
  • North AND South enjoyed shows
  • Created shared "American" culture through racism

2. Appropriated Black Culture While Dehumanizing

  • White performers stole Black music, dance, humor
  • But portrayed Blacks as inferior, childlike, comic
  • "Safe" way to engage Black culture without acknowledging humanity

3. Justified Slavery and Racial Hierarchy

  • Shows depicted enslaved people as happy, content
  • Reinforced idea that Blacks were naturally inferior
  • Made racism entertaining and "normal"

The Central Contradiction of Minstrelsy

Simultaneous Fascination and Dehumanization:

  • White audiences LOVED Black music and dance
  • But could only enjoy it through racist caricature
  • Appropriation masked as appreciation

What This Reveals About American Culture:

  • White Americans drawn to Black cultural expression
  • But unwilling to treat Black people as equals
  • This pattern continues: jazz, blues, rock, hip-hop—all appropriated

Historical Significance:

  • First truly national mass entertainment (North and South)
  • Created template for American variety entertainment (→ vaudeville → TV)
  • Shaped American racial imagination for generations

Mass entertainment and racism emerge together in the U.S.

Conclusion: The Paradoxes of Jacksonian America

  • Democracy's Contradictions: Expanded participation for white men, built on exclusion of women, Blacks, Native Americans
  • Market Revolution's Double Edge: New opportunities and mobility BUT growing inequality and insecurity
  • Class Tensions: Ideology says "all (white) men equal" but reality shows sharp class divisions
  • Race and Immigration: Growing diversity meets rising racism and nativism
  • Cultural Expressions: Mass entertainment reveals anxieties about race, class, identity
The Big Question: Can democracy survive its own contradictions?
