The Trail of Tears

A Case Study in Ethnic Cleansing (1830–1839)

Good morning. Our focus today is not merely on a historical event but on a deliberate state action designed to redraw the demographic map of the United States: the forced displacement known as the Trail of Tears. The goal is to analyze the operational phase of this policy, highlighting the sheer logistics, the timeline of suffering, and the catastrophic human cost, which many scholars, drawing on the definition presented in our sources, classify as ethnic cleansing. Ethnic cleansing is defined as the deliberate, organized removal of an ethnic or religious group from a territory to create a homogeneous population. The Indian Removal Act of 1830 provided the legal framework for this process, driven by American settlers' demand for 25 million acres of fertile southeastern land, particularly for cotton cultivation and following the discovery of gold in Cherokee territory. The Trail of Tears was the brutal, operational execution of this policy, compelling tens of thousands of Native Americans westward.

Ethnic Cleansing in the Yugoslav Wars

Nationalism, Violence, and the Remaking of Territory

Today we begin by looking at the Yugoslav wars of the 1990s as a modern example of ethnic cleansing. This will help us understand the concept before we apply the same analytical framework to early U.S. history and the Trail of Tears.

Collapse of Yugoslavia

  • Communist federation collapses in early 1990s
  • Six republics + mixed ethnic communities
  • Nationalist leaders seek ethnic homogeneity
  • Shared state replaced by competing identities
When Yugoslavia’s communist system collapsed in the early 1990s, the institutions holding six republics and many ethnic communities together began to crumble. Into that vacuum stepped nationalist leaders promising security through separation — defining political belonging not by citizenship, but by ethnicity.

A Deeply Intermixed Society

  • Serbs, Croats, Bosniaks, Slovenes, Macedonians, Albanians
  • Centuries of shared towns, marriages, markets
  • Borders are often arbitrary; they don't reflect lived reality.
  • Diversity seen as “threat” by nationalists
Yugoslavia’s peoples lived side-by-side for centuries: Serbs, Croats, Bosniaks, Slovenes, Macedonians, Albanians, and others. Nationalists tried to turn administrative borders into ethnic borders, but centuries of social mixing resisted that project.

The Turn to Ethnic Cleansing

  • Violence used to “purify” territory
  • Civilians targeted for identity, not actions
  • Homes, schools, sacred sites destroyed
  • Mass expulsions, terror, sexual violence
When persuasion failed, force began. Civilians were terrorized to induce flight. Homes and cultural sites were burned to erase presence. Families were separated. Sexual violence was used to humiliate and destroy communal bonds. This was not accidental chaos; it was removal by design — territory scrubbed of people whose only “crime” was belonging to the “wrong” identity.

Srebrenica, July 1995

  • UN “safe area” overrun by Bosnian Serb forces
  • Men and boys separated from families
  • 8,000+ executed; mass graves concealed
  • Ruled genocide by international courts
Srebrenica was the starkest expression of this logic. In July 1995, Bosnian Serb forces overran a UN-declared safe area. They separated men and boys from families, executions followed, bodies dumped in mass graves, then moved again to hide evidence. Over 8,000 Bosniak males were killed. Courts later ruled Srebrenica a genocide — an attempt not just to remove a population, but to destroy its future.

What Is Ethnic Cleansing?

  • Deliberate removal of a group from territory
  • Motivated by identity, not individual actions
  • Achieved through force, terror, and coercion
  • Goal: create ethnic homogeneity
Ethnic cleansing is the organized removal of a particular ethnic or religious group from a territory so another group may claim it. It is removal as policy — carried out through fear, coercion, and organized brutality.

Analytical Framework: Ethnic Cleansing

  • Targeted group identified
  • Intent to remove from territory
  • Violence, terror, and destruction used
  • Organized by state, military, or police
  • Large-scale displacement
  • Detention, massacres, sexual violence
  • Seizure of property & land
  • Preventing return
Scholars look for patterns. One: Is a specific ethnic group targeted? Two: Is the goal to expel them from a territory? Three: Are methods violent — terror, rape, destruction of homes, starvation? Four: Is it systematic — coordinated by political or military authorities? We also look for scale — entire communities displaced — and accompanying atrocities: massacres, detention camps, sexual violence, disappearances, property seizure. When these converge, we are watching a strategy: violence used to redraw the human map.
“Ethnic cleansing does not arise because neighbors cannot coexist; it arises when power decides they must not.”
The takeaway: ethnic cleansing isn’t ancient hatred. It’s political imagination armed with force. Leaders decide the future must be pure — and violence becomes the tool to make reality match ideology.

The Targeted Population and Intent

  • Five Civilized Tribes: Cherokee, Choctaw, Creek, Chickasaw, and Seminole
  • Targeted based on ethnic and racial identity, not individual actions
  • Cherokee assimilation efforts provided no protection
The Indian Removal Act of 1830 targeted specific Native American nations in the Southeast—the Five Civilized Tribes: the Cherokee, Choctaw, Creek, Chickasaw, and Seminole. This was not a policy directed at individuals based on their actions or choices, but rather at entire populations based solely on their ethnic and racial identity as Native Americans. The Cherokee had adopted many aspects of Euro-American society: they had a written constitution, published a newspaper in both Cherokee and English, operated farms and schools, and had converted to Christianity in significant numbers. Despite this assimilation, they remained targeted for removal. The Supreme Court recognized Cherokee sovereignty in Worcester v. Georgia (1832), affirming their right to their land—yet this legal protection was deliberately ignored by President Andrew Jackson and the state of Georgia.

Worcester v. Georgia (1832)

  • Supreme Court recognized Cherokee sovereignty
  • Affirmed Cherokee right to their land
  • Deliberately ignored by President Jackson and Georgia
The Supreme Court recognized Cherokee sovereignty in Worcester v. Georgia (1832), affirming their right to their land—yet this legal protection was deliberately ignored by President Andrew Jackson and the state of Georgia. This demonstrates that legal protections meant nothing when they conflicted with the policy goal of removal.

The Goal: Demographic Transformation

  • 25 million acres of Native land targeted
  • Desired for cotton cultivation and gold mining
  • Create white-controlled Southeast, free from Native presence
  • ~60,000 individuals to be displaced
The policy's intent was explicit: to clear approximately 25 million acres of Native American-occupied land to make it available exclusively for white settlement and economic exploitation. The land was coveted for cotton cultivation using enslaved labor, and gold had been discovered in Cherokee territory in Georgia in 1828. The goal was demographic transformation—to create a white-controlled Southeast, free from Native American presence. This required the displacement of approximately 60,000 individuals from their ancestral homelands. The justifications offered for removal were paternalistic claims that it would "protect" Native Americans from white encroachment and assertions about civilizational incompatibility. Georgia claimed states' rights and sovereignty over Cherokee land. Yet the implementation of the policy—as we will see—reveals the true nature of these stated motivations.

The Systematic State Apparatus

Legal Framework

  • 1830: Indian Removal Act passes
  • 1835: Treaty of New Echota signed by unauthorized minority
  • 1836: Senate ratifies illegitimate treaty
  • 1838: Van Buren authorizes military enforcement
The Trail of Tears was not a spontaneous event but a highly systematic operation involving coordinated federal legislation, executive action, and military enforcement spanning nearly two decades. The removal process followed a deliberate institutional pathway: 1830 - The Indian Removal Act passed Congress by a narrow margin, providing the legal framework for forced relocation. 1835 - The Treaty of New Echota was signed by an unauthorized minority faction of approximately 100 Cherokee, directly contradicting Principal Chief John Ross and the overwhelming majority of the Cherokee Nation who opposed removal. 1836 - The U.S. Senate ratified this illegitimate treaty, despite its rejection by the Cherokee leadership and people. 1838 - When the May deadline for voluntary departure passed and the vast majority of Cherokee refused to leave, President Martin Van Buren authorized General Winfield Scott to enforce removal militarily.

Military Organization

  • General Winfield Scott commanded removal operations
  • 7,000 U.S. troops and state militia deployed
  • Massive military operation against civilian population
  • Organized state power deployed systematically across multiple states
General Scott commanded an armed force of approximately 7,000 U.S. troops and state militia—a massive military operation designed to compel the removal of a civilian population. This was not ad hoc violence but organized state power deployed systematically across multiple states simultaneously. The removal affected all Five Tribes between 1830 and 1850, with each nation enduring its own forced march. The Choctaw were first, signing a treaty in 1830 and beginning removal in 1831 during a harsh winter—a journey their chief called a "trail of tears and death." The Creeks were forcibly removed between 1834 and 1837 after violent encroachment and land fraud sparked resistance. The Cherokee forced relocation of 1838–1839 serves as the most detailed case study of military-enforced ethnic cleansing in this policy.

Methods of Forced Removal

Military Roundups (May 1838)

  • Armed soldiers removed families at gunpoint
  • Virtually no time to gather possessions
  • Men arrested in fields; women dragged from homes
  • Elderly refused even moments to pray with family
The operational mechanics of ethnic cleansing involve severing people from their property, culture, and security through immediate, overwhelming force. Starting in May 1838, federal troops and militia conducted coordinated military roundups across Georgia, Tennessee, North Carolina, and Alabama. The operations were marked by extreme coercion and brutality. Armed soldiers removed families from their homes at gunpoint. Individuals were given virtually no time to gather possessions, often leaving "with only the clothes which they had on." Men working in fields were arrested on the spot. Women were dragged from their homes. One elderly Cherokee man was refused even a moment to pray with his family before being forced out, receiving instead a "brutal oath" and a push toward the door.

Complete Property Confiscation

  • Everything left behind "belonged to the state"
  • $15–20 million in Cherokee property seized/destroyed
  • White settlers described as "vultures" by federal officer
  • Generations of labor and wealth obliterated
Everything not carried on the person instantly "belonged to the state." Cherokee land, farms, homes, and all improvements were confiscated. The total value of seized or destroyed Cherokee property is estimated at $15–20 million—a staggering sum representing generations of labor and accumulated wealth. A federal officer described white settlers as "vultures" who immediately closed in to strip the Cherokees of everything left behind.

Detention Camps

  • Over 15,000 Cherokee confined in ~33 stockades
  • Described as crude wooden pens—"cattle pens"
  • Confinement up to five months in unsanitary, overcrowded conditions
  • Rampant disease: measles, dysentery, fevers
Following the initial roundups, over 15,000 Cherokee were confined in approximately 33 temporary stockades and forts, including Fort Cass. These were not humane holding facilities but instruments of suffering. The enclosures were crude wooden pens—witnesses described them as "cattle pens." People were confined for up to five months in unsanitary and severely overcrowded conditions. Disease spread rapidly: measles, dysentery, and fevers ravaged the imprisoned population. The camps functioned as death traps even before the westward journey began.

Detention Camps Death Toll

~2,000 deaths

More than one in eight died in detention camps before the march even began

It is estimated that around 2,000 Cherokee—more than one in eight of those detained—died in these detention camps during the summer of 1838 before the forced march had even started. This demonstrates that lethal conditions were not merely a product of the difficult journey but were built into the removal process from its inception.

The Forced March

Scale and Organization

  • October 1838 – March 1839: Main removal period
  • ~1,000 miles from Southeast to Indian Territory (Oklahoma)
  • Multiple detachments via overland and water routes
The journey itself transformed policy into mass death, creating a literal trail of graves across the American landscape. The main forced march began in October 1838 and concluded in March 1839. The Cherokee removal covered approximately 1,000 miles from homelands in Georgia, Alabama, North Carolina, and Tennessee to Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma). The routes collectively spanned approximately 5,043 miles across nine states (administered today as the Trail of Tears National Historic Trail). Cherokee detachments followed various routes, including overland paths like the Northern Route and Benge Route, as well as water-based routes using the Tennessee, Ohio, Mississippi, and Arkansas rivers. The last detachment arrived on March 26, 1839.

Winter Conditions

  • Unusually severe winter: sleet, snow storms, freezing temperatures
  • Many slept on ground without fire or adequate blankets
  • Rivers frozen 8–12 inches thick
  • Thousands stranded for weeks in deep snow at river crossings
The timing of the march created catastrophic conditions. Although it began in October, an unusually severe winter struck. Groups endured sleet and snow storms with freezing temperatures. Many slept either in wagons or on the ground without fire or adequate blankets. In southern Illinois in December 1838, streams and rivers were frozen 8 to 12 inches thick, compelling groups to cut through ice for water. Thousands were stranded for weeks in deep snow when they reached the frozen Ohio and Mississippi Rivers. At locations like Mantle Rock in Kentucky, many Cherokee died while waiting to cross the rivers—and were charged wildly inflated prices by ferry operators for the privilege. The terrain was punishing throughout, but the winter conditions transformed difficulty into lethality. The decision to proceed with the march during these months, rather than waiting for spring, represents a critical policy choice with foreseeable deadly consequences.

Logistical Failures

  • Supplies often inadequate or ran short
  • Contractors failed to provide sufficient food and medical care
  • Malnutrition exacerbated disease vulnerability
  • Policy choices functioned as coercive force
Beyond environmental exposure, systemic failures in provisioning multiplied the suffering. Supplies often ran short or were inadequate. When contractors failed to provide sufficient food and medical care, malnutrition exacerbated disease vulnerability. The logistical choices made by the state—including the winter timing and restricting access to towns where Cherokee might seek help—functioned as additional coercive force.

Disease and Death

Primary Causes of Death

  • Infectious disease as principal killer
  • Dysentery from contaminated water
  • Pneumonia from cold exposure
  • Measles spreading through weakened populations
The combination of detention camp conditions, winter exposure, inadequate provisions, and forced marching through difficult terrain created a mortality crisis of staggering proportions. Infectious disease was the primary cause of death throughout the removal process. Overcrowding in camps and on the trail facilitated disease transmission. Contaminated water and inadequate sanitation led to widespread dysentery. Exposure to cold caused pneumonia. Measles spread rapidly through the weakened population. Malnutrition compromised immune systems, making people vulnerable to infections that might otherwise have been survivable.

Eyewitness: Private John G. Burnett

"the most brutal order in the History of American Warfare"
  • Served as interpreter during Cherokee removal
  • Documented burying "fourteen or fifteen at every stopping place"
  • Chief John Ross's wife died giving her blanket to sick child
Private John G. Burnett, a soldier who served as an interpreter during the Cherokee removal, provided crucial eyewitness testimony. Writing years later, he called the operation "the most brutal order in the History of American Warfare." Burnett documented the systematic nature of death on the trail, noting that they "buried fourteen or fifteen at every stopping place." He recorded specific cases that revealed the human cost. Chief John Ross's wife died of pneumonia after giving her only blanket to a sick child. The deaths resulted directly from exposure and inadequate care—conditions created by policy choices. Burnett's later reflection was damning: "Murder is murder, whether committed by the villain skulking in the dark or by uniformed men stepping to the strains of martial music."

The death toll across the removed nations confirms the catastrophic nature of forced displacement. Out of approximately 16,000 Cherokee forcibly removed, around 4,000 people died during the internment period and the march itself. This represents roughly one-quarter—25%—of the entire relocated population. The Creeks suffered similar devastation, with approximately 3,500 to 4,500 deaths out of 14,000–15,000 removed, placing their mortality rate at 20–25%. The cumulative number of deaths resulting from the forced relocation of the Five Civilized Tribes is estimated at between 12,000 and 17,000 people. These are not merely statistics but represent thousands of individual human beings—elders, children, mothers, fathers—who died from preventable causes during a government-enforced displacement.

Outcomes: Demographic Transformation

  • Southeast transformed from multi-ethnic to white-dominated region
  • 25 million acres transferred to white control
  • Indigenous presence nearly eliminated east of Mississippi
  • Displacement was permanent—no return to ancestral lands
The removal achieved its stated demographic goal…

Destruction Beyond Physical Removal

  • Cultural: Loss of connection to sacred sites and homelands
  • Social: Community networks and institutions shattered
  • Economic: Farmland, businesses, resources obliterated
  • Psychological: Generational trauma affecting survivors
The harm extended to cultural, social, and spiritual dimensions. The forced removal caused "the loss of ancient connections to homelands and sacred sites"—places that held religious, cultural, and historical significance spanning centuries or millennia. Community networks built over generations were shattered. Political institutions had to be rebuilt from scratch in unfamiliar territory. Economic systems were demolished—improved farmland, businesses, and communal resources all lost. The survivors carried the psychological and cultural wounds of the removal, creating trauma that affected subsequent generations.

Conclusion: State Power and Ethnic Cleansing

  • Full institutional mobilization: legislative, executive, military
  • Systematic targeting of ethnic groups for removal
  • Comprehensive coercive methods employed
  • Mass death as foreseeable consequence of policy choices
The Trail of Tears demonstrates how ethnic cleansing operates when legislative, executive, and military power align in service of racial and economic goals. The U.S. government mobilized its full institutional capacity—passing laws, negotiating fraudulent treaties, ignoring Supreme Court rulings, deploying thousands of troops, constructing detention facilities, and organizing mass deportations—to achieve a singular objective: the demographic transformation of the Southeast through the forced removal of Native American populations. The policy was systematic and organized. It targeted specific ethnic groups for removal based solely on their identity. It employed comprehensive coercive methods: military force, property confiscation, detention camps, and forced marches under lethal conditions. It affected tens of thousands of people and succeeded in its goal of creating ethnically homogeneous territory for white expansion. It resulted in mass death on a catastrophic scale—with mortality rates of 20–25% representing not unfortunate accidents but foreseeable and accepted consequences of deliberate policy choices.

The Legacy

Law can become a weapon for violent dispossession when political will, racial ideology, and economic greed align.

Ethnic cleansing does not require explicit orders to kill—it can be accomplished through "legal" frameworks, bureaucratic procedures, and systemic indifference to suffering.

The timing of the march through winter, the inadequacy of provisions, the brutality of the roundups, the conditions in detention camps, and the government's willingness to ignore legal protections all reveal a pattern of decisions that treated Native American lives as expendable in service of white economic and territorial ambitions.
