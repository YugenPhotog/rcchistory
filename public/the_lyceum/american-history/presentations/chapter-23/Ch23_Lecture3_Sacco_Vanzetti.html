The Argument of This Lecture

The postwar Left faced two simultaneous crises: a foreign model (Bolshevism) that distorted its understanding of American conditions, and a domestic terror (the Red Scare) that destroyed the organizations it had built over two decades.

Sacco and Vanzetti became the symbol of both crises at once ‚Äî and the Left's response to their case reveals exactly why American radicalism failed to take root.

Section I

Before Bolshevism: American Socialism

What did the Left look like before the Russian Revolution changed everything?

The Socialist Party of America, 1901

  • Founded 1901 ‚Äî a coalition of labor organizers, reform movements, and immigrant radicals
  • Peak influence 1912: Eugene V. Debs wins 900,000 votes ‚Äî ~6% of the popular vote
  • Won municipal offices in cities from Milwaukee to Schenectady
  • Debs represented a distinctly American socialism rooted in republican producerism ‚Äî broader than European Marxist orthodoxy

Why Socialism Grew ‚Äî Social Conditions

  • 1880‚Äì1920: millions of immigrants from southern and eastern Europe ‚Äî factory floors, tenement neighborhoods
  • Real wages rose in aggregate ‚Äî but masked cyclical unemployment, dangerous conditions, no safety net
  • Triangle Shirtwaist fire (1911): 146 garment workers killed ‚Äî dramatized the lethal consequences of unregulated industrial capitalism
  • Many radicals interpreted these crises as evidence of capitalism's inherent tendency toward exploitation, not temporary failures

Why Revolution Did Not Come

  • Ford's moving assembly line (1913) + five-dollar workday (1914) ‚Üí foundations for mass consumption
  • Rising living standards, middle-class expansion, regulatory reform undermined the revolutionary scenario
  • AFL under Samuel Gompers pursued collective bargaining, not revolution ‚Äî and won real gains
  • Interlocking developments stabilized capitalism rather than undermining it ‚Äî frustrating the expectations of those who predicted inevitable crisis

The Industrial Workers of the World (IWW)

  • Founded Chicago, 1905 ‚Äî the Wobblies
  • Rejected AFL's craft-union model; advocated organizing all workers ‚Äî unskilled, migrant, immigrant ‚Äî in "One Big Union"
  • Openly called for destruction of the capitalist system ‚Äî contempt for the state and the capitalist class
  • Organized: lumber camps (Pacific Northwest), copper mines (Southwest), textile mills (Paterson, NJ) ‚Äî before WWI faced surprisingly limited government interference

Its openly revolutionary rhetoric would make it the primary target of wartime and postwar suppression

Section II

World War I and the Destruction of the Old Radical Left

How the government used the war to dismantle two decades of radical organizing

The Legal Infrastructure of Repression

  • Espionage Act (1917): criminalized obstruction of military recruitment ‚Äî up to 20 years, $10,000 fine
  • Sedition Act (1918): criminalized any speech deemed disloyal, profane, or abusive toward the flag, military, or government
  • September 1917: federal agents raided IWW offices in 24+ cities simultaneously ‚Äî 165 leaders charged
  • Radical newspapers denied mailing privileges by Postmaster General Burleson ‚Äî effectively silenced publications dependent on the mail

Debs and the Socialist Party

  • SPA officially opposed U.S. entry ‚Äî denounced the war as a capitalist conflict waged at the expense of working people
  • June 1918: Debs delivers antiwar speech in Canton, Ohio ‚Äî convicted under Espionage Act, sentenced to 10 years
  • Ran for president from prison in 1920 ‚Äî received nearly 1 million votes
  • President Harding released him on Christmas Day 1921 ‚Äî but never restored his citizenship

Section III

Red Dawn: The Impact of the Russian Revolution

How Bolshevism changed ‚Äî and distorted ‚Äî American radical politics

The Bolshevik Revolution, November 1917

  • Bolshevik seizure of power in Petrograd electrified radical movements worldwide
  • Apparent proof that revolution was not merely theoretical ‚Äî a disciplined party had actually seized power in a major industrial nation
  • For American radicals battered by wartime repression: simultaneously an inspiration and a puzzle
  • Created an entirely new reference point ‚Äî shifting analysis from indigenous American traditions to a foreign model

As Theodore Draper argued: the Russian Revolution would distort American radical understanding of American conditions for a generation

Lenin's Vanguard Party Model

What Lenin Argued
  • Workers develop only trade union consciousness on their own ‚Äî awareness of immediate grievances, not political revolution
  • A tightly organized party of professional revolutionaries must provide ideological leadership from without
  • Revolution = organized conscious action at a crisis moment, not economic inevitability
What It Meant for Americans
  • Elevated the revolutionary organization to decisive importance
  • Implied the SPA's loose, democratic structures were inadequate
  • Native-born socialists resisted ‚Äî saw it as imposing Russian models on American circumstances

The Revolutionary Moment ‚Äî 1919

  • Many radicals genuinely believed a worldwide revolutionary wave was beginning ‚Äî Germany, Hungary, Bavaria all saw upheaval
  • SPA membership rose to 108,000 in 1919 ‚Äî concentrated in foreign-language federations (Finnish, Russian, Polish, Hungarian) with direct ties to European events
  • Expectations were wildly inflated: German revolution suppressed, Hungarian Soviet crushed, European capitalism stabilized
  • But in the electric atmosphere of 1919, these outcomes were not yet apparent ‚Äî and the sense of turning point shaped thousands of choices

The Communist International (Comintern)

  • Founded Moscow, March 1919 ‚Äî conceived as a centralized world party with Moscow at its head
  • Unlike the old Socialist International (loose federation), the Comintern demanded strict discipline and ideological uniformity
  • The Twenty-One Conditions (1920) mandated organizational restructuring along Leninist lines and submission to Comintern directives
  • For American communists: joining meant accepting Soviet leadership ‚Äî tying political fortunes to decisions made in Moscow

The Comintern would increasingly function as an instrument of Soviet foreign policy rather than autonomous international revolution

Section IV

Founding American Communism

How the Bolshevik Revolution split the American left ‚Äî and what that split produced

Confusion and Fierce Internal Conflict

  • Enthusiasm for the Russian Revolution quickly translated into factional warfare within the SPA
  • Key disputes: work within existing unions or build revolutionary industrial organizations? Electoral politics or reject the bourgeois state entirely?
  • Foreign-language federations (with direct European connections) pushed toward rigid Leninism
  • Native-born socialists resisted what they saw as inappropriate imposition of Russian models on American conditions

The Communist Party USA ‚Äî Founded 1919

  • Chicago, September 1, 1919 ‚Äî emerged from a split in the SPA as left-wing factions broke away to align with Comintern demands
  • Initial membership: ~24,000 ‚Äî predominantly foreign-born
  • Organizational and financial support from Moscow helped stabilize the chaotic early years
  • The CPUSA was from its inception not merely a party with friendly Soviet relations ‚Äî it accepted Soviet organizational and ideological direction as a matter of principle

The Revolutionary Mindset ‚Äî and Its Chasm

  • Founding generation genuinely believed the American working class stood on the verge of revolutionary action
  • Internal party documents spoke matter-of-factly of "Soviet America" as an imminent prospect
  • Every strike wave = evidence of growing class consciousness; every act of repression = proof of capitalism's desperation
  • The chasm between these expectations and American social reality ‚Äî rising wages, consumer culture actively integrating the working class ‚Äî would not become fully apparent until the 1920s ran their course

Section V

Anarchism

The tradition within which Sacco and Vanzetti lived their political lives

What Is Anarchism?

  • Rejects all forms of coercive government authority as incompatible with human freedom and dignity
  • Unlike socialists ‚Äî who seek to capture or transform the state ‚Äî anarchists argue the state itself is the problem
  • Found its most fervent adherents among immigrant workers from Italy, Russia, and Spain ‚Äî where the state had historically functioned primarily as an instrument of elite oppression
  • Sacco and Vanzetti's deepest political commitments lay within this tradition ‚Äî impossible to understand the case without grasping its intensity

Section VI

Propaganda of the Deed

Why some anarchists believed that violence was the only language power would hear

Propaganda of the Deed

  • Logic: the capitalist press would never fairly represent the anarchist cause; working-class people exhausted by toil could not be reached through pamphlets alone
  • A spectacular act of violence against a hated oppressor would demonstrate that the powerful were vulnerable ‚Äî and provoke repression that would radicalize broader populations
  • 1886: Haymarket bombing ‚Äî 7 police officers killed; 4 anarchists executed in what was widely condemned as a judicial travesty
  • 1892: Alexander Berkman attempts to assassinate Carnegie Steel executive Henry Clay Frick after the Homestead Strike

McKinley's Assassination and the Image That Stuck

  • September 1901: President William McKinley shot by Leon Czolgosz, who identified himself as an anarchist
  • The image of the violent foreign anarchist became a fixed and terrifying fixture in American public consciousness
  • By the 1920s, defense attorneys would find this image almost impossible to dislodge regardless of the specific evidence presented
The critical point for the Sacco-Vanzetti case: in the jury room and in the press, what was on trial was not just two men ‚Äî it was the entire image of the violent foreign anarchist that had been building in American culture for thirty years.

"Propaganda of the deed" assumed that spectacular violence would radicalize bystanders. In almost every case, it produced the opposite effect ‚Äî mass repression and public sympathy for the state. What does this tell us about the relationship between political violence and political change?

Section VII

Wall Street Bombing, 1920

The act that made conviction of Sacco and Vanzetti almost inevitable

September 16, 1920 ‚Äî Noon

  • A horse-drawn wagon laden with iron sash weights and 100 pounds of dynamite exploded on Wall Street at noon ‚Äî peak of the lunch-hour rush
  • 38 killed, 200+ injured ‚Äî the deadliest mass killing on American soil to that point
  • 5 days after the formal indictment of Sacco and Vanzetti
  • Evidence pointed to followers of Luigi Galleani ‚Äî whose network included associates of Sacco and Vanzetti

No one was ever conclusively identified as the perpetrator

What the Bombing Did

The bombing hardened public opinion against foreign radicals with a force that no amount of political argument could easily overcome.

In many minds it cemented the equation: immigrant + anarchist = lethal violence. Sacco and Vanzetti's trial began in this atmosphere ‚Äî and could not escape it.

Section VIII

The Red Scare, 1919‚Äì1921

Fear, repression, and the destruction of institutional distinction

Foundations Already in Place

  • The Red Scare did not emerge from a vacuum ‚Äî built on wartime legal infrastructure already constructed
  • Federal prosecutors had secured hundreds of convictions under Espionage and Sedition Acts; agencies had built extensive files on radical organizations
  • The transition from wartime to postwar repression was a continuous process ‚Äî targets shifted from antiwar dissent to postwar radicalism; frameworks remained intact
  • The machinery was already running. It just needed new targets.

Sources of Fear: Labor Unrest

  • 1919: 4 million workers ‚Äî 1 in 5 of the nonagricultural workforce ‚Äî participated in strikes
  • Seattle General Strike (Feb. 1919): city-wide work stoppage ‚Äî widely interpreted as a Bolshevik-inspired revolution attempt despite the conservative aims of AFL organizers
  • Great Steel Strike (Sept. 1919): 365,000 workers ‚Äî collapsed after 4 months under employer resistance, government pressure, racial division
  • Boston Police Strike: Coolidge's remark ‚Äî "no right to strike against the public safety by anybody, anywhere, anytime" ‚Äî catapults him to national prominence
üì∏ Seattle General Strike ¬∑ February 1919

Seattle General Strike ¬∑ February 1919 ¬∑ Public Domain ¬∑ Wikimedia Commons

Sources of Fear: Political Violence

  • April‚ÄìMay 1919: anarchist mail bombs sent to Rockefeller, Justice Holmes, Seattle Mayor ‚Äî one bomb maimed Senator Hardwick's housekeeper
  • June 2, 1919: bombs explode nearly simultaneously in 8 American cities, including one on the front steps of Attorney General Palmer's home in Washington
  • Bombing campaign 1919‚Äì1920: ~35 killed, 200+ injured ‚Äî mostly linked to Galleanist network, not the Communist Party
  • Crucial point: distinctions between anarchist, socialist, and communist were largely invisible to a frightened public that lumped all foreign radicals together

Public Reaction: The Failure of Distinction

  • In popular perception and much of the mainstream press, anarchists, socialists, and communists merged into a single undifferentiated threat ‚Äî foreign, violent, un-American
  • This conflation served employers who wanted to discredit legitimate labor organizing by associating it with bomb-throwing revolutionaries
  • It aligned with nativist anxieties about immigrants from eastern and southern Europe
  • Fear created the political conditions for a crackdown that would have been difficult to sustain in more settled times

Section IX

The Palmer Raids and Their Aftermath

How the government weaponized immigration law against political dissent

The Architects of the Raids

  • A. Mitchell Palmer ‚Äî Attorney General, Pennsylvania Democrat with presidential ambitions; survived the June 1919 bomb attack and transformed himself into the most aggressive advocate for radical suppression in the Wilson administration
  • J. Edgar Hoover ‚Äî appointed head of the Bureau's new General Intelligence Division at age 24; compiled index cards on 150,000 alleged radicals
  • Orchestrated the largest mass arrest campaign in American history
üì∏ A. Mitchell Palmer ¬∑ Attorney General 1919‚Äì1921

A. Mitchell Palmer ¬∑ Attorney General ¬∑ Public Domain ¬∑ Wikimedia Commons

The Legal Framework: Schenck v. United States (1919)

  • Justice Holmes: the "clear and present danger" standard ‚Äî speech can be restricted when it presents a clear and present danger of substantive evils
  • Holmes's metaphor of falsely shouting fire in a crowded theater became one of the most quoted passages in American constitutional history
  • Holmes and Brandeis later dissented from convictions they believed crossed the line ‚Äî but their dissents did not command majorities
  • The legal architecture of repression remained largely intact through the Red Scare years

The Raids ‚Äî Scope and Method

  • November 1919: raids on Union of Russian Workers ‚Äî hundreds arrested
  • January 2, 1920: simultaneous strikes against communist organizations in 30+ cities ‚Äî 5,000 to 10,000 arrested in a single night
  • Conducted largely without individual arrest warrants ‚Äî general warrants for organizations
  • Suspects held in crowded conditions, denied access to legal counsel, subjected to interrogations ‚Äî in Boston, prisoners marched through streets in chains for newspaper photographers

The Ultimate Weapon: Deportation

  • Immigration Act (1918): authorized expulsion of alien anarchists ‚Äî required only an administrative hearing, not a criminal trial with constitutional protections
  • ~600 formally deported; hundreds more expelled in proceedings that lacked even minimal procedural protections
  • December 1919: 249 alien radicals ‚Äî including Emma Goldman and Alexander Berkman ‚Äî transported to Soviet Russia on the ship the press nicknamed the "Soviet Ark"
  • Goldman had been the most famous radical voice in America for three decades ‚Äî spent the rest of her life in exile

State and Local Repression

  • Many states enacted criminal syndicalism laws ‚Äî criminalized advocacy of violent political change
  • California's statute used aggressively against IWW and, later, Communist Party members; Illinois imprisoned suspected communists under similar statutes
  • New York's Lusk Committee raided the Russian Soviet Bureau and the Rand School of Social Science; published voluminous reports on the radical threat
  • Overlapping jurisdiction of federal, state, and local law enforcement ‚Äî plus private employer associations funding vigilante action ‚Äî created a pervasive apparatus of repression at every level

The Backlash: "Report Upon Illegal Practices"

  • May 1920: 12 eminent lawyers ‚Äî including Roscoe Pound (Harvard Law School dean) and Felix Frankfurter (later Supreme Court Justice) ‚Äî published a documented indictment of the raids
  • Catalogued constitutional violations: warrantless searches, denial of counsel, detention without due process, use of agents provocateurs
  • Argued: the primary danger to American institutions did not come from the radical left ‚Äî it came from the government's own systematic violation of constitutional rights
  • Struck a chord with segments of the press and public grown uneasy with the scale and methods of the federal crackdown

The Collapse ‚Äî and What Remained

  • Palmer predicted a massive radical uprising on May Day 1920 ‚Äî when the day passed without incident, his credibility collapsed
  • Harding released many political prisoners, including Debs (Dec. 1921) ‚Äî but never apologized for the raids
  • Communist Party membership fell from ~24,000 to ~5,000 ‚Äî a loss of 80% ‚Äî driven underground, organizationally dependent on Moscow, nearly bereft of its claimed working-class base
  • The institutional infrastructure of surveillance and repression did not disappear ‚Äî incorporated into the permanent federal apparatus under Hoover's direction, available for future mobilization

Sacco and Vanzetti

The Facts, the Trial, the Symbol

Where criminal justice, political culture, and radical politics collide

Just the Facts

  • April 15, 1920: Paymaster Frederick Parmenter and guard Alessandro Berardelli robbed and shot at South Braintree, MA shoe factory payroll
  • May 5, 1920: Nicola Sacco (shoe worker) and Bartolomeo Vanzetti (fish peddler) arrested ‚Äî both armed; both initially lied to police about movements and associations
  • Prosecutors characterized their lies as "consciousness of guilt"
  • Convicted July 1921; executed August 23, 1927 ‚Äî after 6 years of appeals, protests, and worldwide controversy

The Evidence Question

  • Disputed ballistic testimony ‚Äî later forensic evidence suggests Sacco's .32 Colt may have fired the bullet that killed Berardelli
  • Vanzetti's role was more ambiguous ‚Äî he was not placed at the scene with the same physical evidence
  • Trial judge Webster Thayer's conduct was widely criticized even by contemporaries ‚Äî his private remarks about "those anarchist bastards" were well documented
  • In 1977, Massachusetts Governor Michael Dukakis proclaimed that Sacco and Vanzetti had been unfairly tried and convicted

Sacco and Vanzetti and the Communist Left

  • The Comintern organized protest demonstrations in dozens of countries ‚Äî framing the case as evidence of American capitalism's fundamental violence and injustice
  • Sacco and Vanzetti portrayed as victims of class bias and ethnic prejudice ‚Äî both framings had genuine force

A Single Vision of Reality

  • The case forged an alliance between communists and liberal fellow-travelers based on a shared narrative: attacks on radicals are necessarily attacks on civil liberties
  • Support for Sacco and Vanzetti persisted ‚Äî and intensified ‚Äî despite growing forensic evidence of Sacco's probable guilt
  • For many on the American left, the two men had become symbols whose factual innocence had become almost beside the point
  • This cast a long shadow: it created a presumption among left-wing opinion that accusations against immigrant dissidents were inherently suspect ‚Äî a disposition Soviet intelligence would later find very useful

Historical Significance

Why American Radicalism Failed to Take Root

The Gap Between Expectation and Reality

  • Founding communists entered the 1920s convinced they were in a pre-revolutionary situation analogous to Russia in 1917
  • Instead: the U.S. moved in the opposite direction ‚Äî stabilizing capitalism through rising living standards, consumer culture, middle-class expansion
  • The AFL, pursuing business unionism within the capitalist framework, represented far more American workers than the revolutionary unions communists hoped to build
  • The movement's expectations were shaped far more by the Russian example than by any serious analysis of American conditions

Draper's Conclusion

The Sacco-Vanzetti case served less as a catalyst for revolution than as a rallying point for a left that was losing its struggle to gain a serious foothold in American working-class life.
  • When American conditions failed to conform to the Russian template, the movement was intellectually ill-equipped to understand why
  • A movement organizationally dependent on Moscow, internally unstable, and nearly bereft of the working-class base it claimed to represent

Looking Forward

  • The surveillance infrastructure built during the Red Scare would remain inside the federal government ‚Äî available for future use
  • The coalition between the communist left and liberal civil libertarians forged around Sacco-Vanzetti would define American left politics through the 1930s and 1940s
  • The questions raised ‚Äî about immigration, radical speech, political violence, and due process ‚Äî were not settled in 1927. They are still being debated.

Eugene V. Debs (1855‚Äì1926)

Railroad union leader radicalized during his imprisonment following the Pullman Strike of 1894. Five-time Socialist Party presidential candidate; his 1912 campaign received nearly 900,000 votes. AFL ‚Äî American Federation of Labor

Founded 1886 under Samuel Gompers, the AFL organized skilled craft workers by trade rather than by industry. Its strategy was "pure-and-simple" business unionism: winning concrete gains (higher wages, shorter hours, better conditions) through collective bargaining rather than seeking to overthrow the capitalist system. The AFL explicitly excluded unskilled workers, immigrants, and Black workers from many affiliates ‚Äî a decision that made it more acceptable to employers and courts but left the majority of the industrial workforce unorganized. Its conservative approach frustrated IWW and socialist organizers who saw it as complicit in capitalist exploitation.

Industrial Workers of the World (IWW)

Founded Chicago, 1905. Popularly known as the "Wobblies," the IWW rejected the AFL's strategy of organizing skilled workers by craft. Instead, it advocated industrial unionism ‚Äî organizing all workers in a given industry, regardless of skill level, into a single union ‚Äî and ultimately aimed to unite all workers in "One Big Union" that would seize control of the means of production. The IWW openly embraced revolutionary industrial unionism and made no secret of its contempt for the capitalist state. Philip Foner's comprehensive history documents its diverse membership of workers for whom mainstream unionism offered nothing. Its openly revolutionary rhetoric made it the primary target of wartime and postwar repression.

Vanguard Party Theory

Lenin's argument, developed in What Is To Be Done? (1902), that workers left to their own devices would develop only "trade union consciousness" ‚Äî awareness of immediate economic grievances, not political revolutionary consciousness. What was required was a tightly organized party of professional revolutionaries who would provide ideological leadership to the working class from without. This theory distinguished Bolshevik communism from the democratic, reform-minded socialism of the Second International, and it implied that revolutionary success depended on organizational discipline and ideological unity rather than mass spontaneous uprising.

Communist International (Comintern)

Founded Moscow, March 1919 by Lenin to coordinate world revolution. Unlike the old Socialist International ‚Äî a loose federation of autonomous national parties ‚Äî the Comintern was conceived as a centralized world party with Moscow at its head, operating on strict discipline and ideological uniformity. Affiliated parties were required to accept the Twenty-One Conditions (1920): organizational restructuring along Leninist lines, expulsion of reformist elements, and submission to Comintern directives. For American communists, this meant accepting Soviet organizational and ideological direction as a matter of principle. The Comintern would increasingly function as an instrument of Soviet foreign policy rather than an autonomous revolutionary body.

Anarchism

The political philosophy that rejects all forms of coercive government authority as fundamentally incompatible with human freedom and dignity. Unlike socialists, who generally seek to capture or transform the state, anarchists argue that the state itself is the problem ‚Äî no matter who controls it, state power will inevitably serve the interests of domination. Anarchists envisioned a society organized on principles of voluntary cooperation and free association, governed through decentralized, non-hierarchical institutions. Anarchism encompassed a wide range of tendencies, from the peaceful philosophical anarchism of Tolstoy to the revolutionary syndicalism associated with Bakunin and Proudhon. It found its most fervent adherents among immigrant workers from Italy, Russia, and Spain ‚Äî where the state had historically functioned primarily as an instrument of elite oppression.

Propaganda of the Deed

A tactical doctrine that emerged within the anarchist movement in the 1870s‚Äì1880s. The logic: the capitalist press would never fairly represent the anarchist cause; working-class people exhausted by toil could not be reached through pamphlets and speeches alone. A spectacular act of violence against a hated oppressor ‚Äî an assassination, a bombing ‚Äî would demonstrate that the powerful were vulnerable, inspire the oppressed with the knowledge that resistance was possible, and provoke the state into repressive overreaction that would radicalize broader populations. The intended targets were political and economic leaders: kings, presidents, industrialists, judges. Between 1890 and 1920, anarchists carried out a remarkable series of assassinations and bombings across Europe and the Americas.

Haymarket Affair, 1886

On May 4, 1886, a bomb was thrown at police officers who were dispersing a labor rally in Haymarket Square, Chicago, killing seven officers and four workers. Eight anarchists were arrested and charged with conspiracy; four were executed and one committed suicide in prison. The trial was widely condemned as a travesty of justice ‚Äî the defendants were convicted on the basis of their radical beliefs rather than evidence directly linking them to the bomb. The Haymarket affair became a founding symbol of both the American labor movement and the persecution of radical politics, commemorated internationally on May Day (May 1).

Luigi Galleani and Galleanismo

Luigi Galleani (1861‚Äì1931) was an Italian anarchist theorist who emigrated to the United States and published the influential anarchist newspaper Cronaca Sovversiva from 1903 to 1919. Galleani was one of the most prominent advocates of "propaganda of the deed" ‚Äî political violence as a revolutionary tactic ‚Äî and his network of followers, known as Galleanisti, included many Italian immigrant workers in industrial New England. The Galleanisti were responsible for or linked to several of the most dramatic acts of political violence in the United States between 1914 and 1920, including the 1919 bombing campaign and the 1920 Wall Street bombing. Sacco and Vanzetti were members of the Galleanist circle, and this connection was central to the government's case against them.

A. Mitchell Palmer (1872‚Äì1936)

Pennsylvania Democrat who served as Attorney General of the United States from 1919 to 1921. A progressive reformer before the war, Palmer survived the June 1919 bombing of his Washington home and transformed himself into the most aggressive advocate for radical suppression in the Wilson administration. He directed the Bureau of Investigation's mass arrest campaign against suspected alien radicals and harbored presidential ambitions that were destroyed when his predicted May Day 1920 radical uprising failed to materialize. He left office in 1921 without apology or acknowledgment of excess. His name became permanently attached to the raids as a symbol of political repression.

Appointed head of the Bureau of Investigation's new General Intelligence Division in August 1919 at the age of 24. Hoover compiled index cards on approximately 150,000 alleged radicals and orchestrated the logistics of the Palmer Raids. He would go on to serve as Director of the FBI from 1935 until his death in 1972, building the Bureau into a permanent surveillance and intelligence apparatus that conducted campaigns against civil rights leaders, antiwar activists, and political dissidents throughout the 20th century. Schenck v. United States (1919)

The Supreme Court's first major free speech case under the Espionage Act. Charles Schenck, general secretary of the Socialist Party, was convicted for distributing leaflets urging men to resist the draft. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes, writing for a unanimous Court, articulated the "clear and present danger" test: speech could be restricted when it presented a clear and present danger of producing substantive evils that Congress had the power to prevent. Holmes's metaphor ‚Äî "The most stringent protection of free speech would not protect a man in falsely shouting fire in a crowded theatre" ‚Äî became one of the most quoted passages in American constitutional history. Holmes himself later grew uncomfortable as the standard he articulated was used to justify prosecutions he found untenable, and he and Brandeis began dissenting in subsequent cases.

Criminal Syndicalism Laws

State statutes enacted primarily between 1917 and 1920 that criminalized advocacy of, or membership in organizations that advocated, the use of violence or other unlawful means to accomplish industrial or political change. More than 20 states enacted such laws during this period, modeled initially on Idaho's 1917 statute. California's criminal syndicalism law was used to prosecute hundreds of IWW members and later Communist Party members. In Whitney v. California (1927), the Supreme Court upheld California's law ‚Äî though Justices Brandeis and Holmes wrote a famous concurring opinion arguing for a broader interpretation of free speech that would influence later jurisprudence.
