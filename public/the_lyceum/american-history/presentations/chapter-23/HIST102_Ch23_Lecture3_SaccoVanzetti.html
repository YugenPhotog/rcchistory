Sacco & Vanzetti

What actually happened — before the politics begin

📖 Understanding This Slide This slide gives you the raw facts—no interpretation yet. It's the foundation for everything else. The lecture wants you to know what the jury actually heard before anyone turned the case into a symbol. Think of this like reading the police report before watching the movie version. Breaking It Down • April 15, 1920 – The Crime: Armed robbery at Slater & Morrill shoe factory payroll delivery in South Braintree, MA. Paymaster Frederick Parmenter and guard Alessandro Berardelli shot and killed. $15,776.51 stolen (exact trial figure). • May 5, 1920 – Arrests: Nicola Sacco and Bartolomeo Vanzetti arrested in Brockton, MA. Both Italian immigrants, both active in the Galleanist anarchist network (violent faction led by Luigi Galleani). • Armed at Arrest: Sacco carrying loaded .32-caliber Colt automatic + extra cartridges; Vanzetti carrying .38-caliber revolver and shotgun shells. • False Statements: Both lied to police about activities, connections, and alibis. Prosecution called this "consciousness of guilt"—behavior suggesting they knew they were in trouble. • Ballistics Evidence: One fatal bullet (Bullet III) matched Sacco's pistol (confirmed in trial and later tests: 1927 Goddard microscope, 1961 state lab, 1983 review). Vanzetti's link was circumstantial (revolver possibly stolen from guard). • Outcome: Convicted of first-degree murder July 14, 1921; executed August 23, 1927 after six years of appeals and worldwide protests. Why This Matters These are the building blocks of the controversy. The evidence was strong enough for conviction, especially against Sacco, but the trial atmosphere was poisoned by prejudice. The radicals later focused almost entirely on the flaws and symbolism—ignoring or downplaying the ballistics. Knowing these facts first helps you see how much of the story depends on selective emphasis. 🎓 Historians Weigh In Francis Russell – Sacco & Vanzetti: The Case Resolved (1986) Russell began as a believer in their innocence but changed his mind after examining the ballistics and trial record. He concluded Sacco was almost certainly guilty (bullet match was convincing) and Vanzetti likely complicit by association. Russell's shift shows how close attention to evidence can override initial sympathy.

Section II

World War I & Radical Suppression

When dissent became a federal crime

📖 Understanding This Slide

World War I gave the U.S. government powerful tools to silence dissent — tools that were used broadly against radicals. This slide shows how the state cracked down on the Left during wartime, setting the stage for the even harsher Red Scare afterward.

Breaking It Down

Espionage Act (1917) & Sedition Act (1918): Made it a crime to obstruct military recruitment or speak disloyally about the government, flag, or military. Thousands prosecuted.

IWW Raids (September 1917): Federal agents raided IWW offices in dozens of cities simultaneously; 165 leaders charged with conspiracy.

Postmaster General Burleson: Revoked second-class mailing privileges for radical newspapers — effectively killed their distribution.

Eugene V. Debs: Convicted for anti-war speech in Canton, Ohio (1918); sentenced to 10 years. Ran for president from prison in 1920 and received nearly 1 million votes. Sentence commuted by President Harding on Christmas 1921, but citizenship never restored.

Why This Matters

World War I showed how quickly the government could shut down dissent when national security was at stake. The SPA and IWW were decimated — creating the conditions for the Red Scare and the communists' desperate need for a new rallying cry like Sacco-Vanzetti.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Geoffrey R. Stone – Perilous Times: Free Speech in Wartime (2004)

Stone calls the Espionage and Sedition Acts some of the most severe restrictions on speech in U.S. history. They effectively silenced much of the pre-war Left and created the atmosphere of fear that made the Red Scare possible.

Wartime Legal Framework

  • Espionage Act (1917) criminalized anti-war speech and interference with military recruitment
  • Sedition Act (1918) extended criminalization to any "disloyal" language about the government, the flag, or the military
  • Massive raids on IWW offices in 1917; 165 leaders charged under the Espionage Act
  • Postmaster General Burleson revoked mailing privileges for radical newspapers — effectively silencing the radical press

Section V

Anarchism in America

The ideology behind Sacco & Vanzetti

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide introduces anarchism and connects it directly to Sacco and Vanzetti. It helps you understand the radical worldview they came from—one that rejected all government and embraced violence as a revolutionary tool. Knowing this context is essential because it explains why many people at the time saw them as dangerous, even before the trial began.

Breaking It Down

Anarchism Defined: Political philosophy that sees all forms of government as unnecessary and harmful; advocates voluntary cooperation and free association instead of state authority.

19th-Century Growth: Became a mass movement, especially among immigrants from countries with oppressive governments (Italy, Russia, Spain).

Reaction to Industrialization: Workers felt alienated and exploited; anarchists blamed both capitalism and the state that protected it.

Difference from Socialism: Socialists usually want to capture or transform the state; anarchists want to abolish it entirely—no "workers' state."

Sacco & Vanzetti's Affiliation: Both were Galleanists—followers of Luigi Galleani, who openly advocated violent "propaganda of the deed" (bombings, assassinations) to inspire revolution.

Why This Matters

Understanding anarchism—and especially its violent Galleanist wing—helps you see why the public and prosecution viewed these men as threats, not just ordinary workers. This context made it easier for radicals to later flip the narrative: instead of "dangerous bombers," they became "persecuted innocents." The lecture argues this symbolic shift was deliberate and very effective.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Paul Avrich – Sacco and Vanzetti: The Anarchist Background (1991)

Avrich's definitive work shows that Sacco and Vanzetti were not passive victims—they were active in a network that carried out bombings and robberies to fund revolution. This background explains why many Americans feared them, even though the trial itself was marred by prejudice.

What Was Anarchism?

  • Political theory rejecting all coercive government; seeking voluntary cooperation instead of state authority
  • By the 19th century, a mass movement with genuine mass followings in parts of Europe and among immigrant communities in the U.S.
  • Differed from socialism by opposing state control entirely — even socialist state power was the enemy
  • Attracted immigrants fleeing oppressive regimes in Italy, Russia, and Spain

📖 Understanding This Slide

Breaking It Down

Propaganda of the Deed: Belief that dramatic acts of violence (assassinations, bombings) against leaders would show the powerful were vulnerable and inspire the masses to revolt.

Goal: Make the state look weak; provoke harsh repression that would radicalize more people.

Historical Examples: Haymarket bombing (1886), Alexander Berkman's attempt on Henry Clay Frick (1892), assassination of President William McKinley by Leon Czolgosz (1901).

Robberies for Funding: Galleanists and other anarchists committed "expropriations" (armed robberies) to finance their activities—contrary to later claims that anarchists didn't commit "common crimes."

Public Impact by 1920: The image of the "violent, bomb-throwing anarchist" was deeply ingrained in American culture—making it easy to see Sacco and Vanzetti as threats.

Why This Matters

This tactic created real fear—some justified, some exaggerated. That fear shaped how people viewed the trial. Radicals later flipped the script: instead of dangerous terrorists, Sacco and Vanzetti became victims of the same repressive state they had attacked. The lecture argues this rebranding was a deliberate propaganda victory.

Galleanist Movement

Followers of Italian anarchist Luigi Galleani (1861–1931), who published the newspaper Cronaca Sovversiva ("Subversive Chronicle") in the United States. Galleani advocated "propaganda of the deed" — violent acts including assassination and bombing as tools of revolutionary agitation. His movement was responsible for a series of bombings in the United States between 1914 and 1932, including the 1919 mail bombs and the 1920 Wall Street bombing. The FBI considered Galleanism the most dangerous anarchist network in the United States.

Cause Célèbre

(French: "celebrated case") A legal case or incident that excites widespread public interest and becomes a rallying point for political or social controversy. A cause célèbre typically transcends the specific facts of the case to become a symbol of a broader struggle — justice vs. injustice, the powerful vs. the powerless. The term implies that the symbolic stakes of the case have become more important to participants than its narrow legal outcome.

Producerist Socialism

A distinctly American variant of socialist thought emphasizing the dignity and rights of "producers" — farmers, craftsmen, and industrial workers — against parasitic "non-producers" such as financiers, speculators, and monopolists. Unlike European Marxist orthodoxy, which emphasized class conflict and the seizure of state power, producerist socialism drew on older American traditions of republican virtue and saw exploitation in moral as well as economic terms. Eugene V. Debs was the movement's most eloquent spokesperson.

×
Anarcho-Syndicalism

A form of anarchism that envisioned trade unions — not a revolutionary party — as the vehicle for social transformation. Anarcho-syndicalists sought to build "One Big Union" of all workers that would eventually call a general strike, seize the means of production, and abolish both the state and capitalist ownership simultaneously. The IWW (Industrial Workers of the World) drew heavily on anarcho-syndicalist ideas. Unlike Bolshevism, anarcho-syndicalism rejected the "revolutionary vanguard party" model and any form of state power, even a workers' state.

×
Communist International (Comintern)

Founded in Moscow in March 1919 by Lenin to coordinate communist and revolutionary socialist parties worldwide. Unlike the earlier Socialist International, which was a loose federation of autonomous parties, the Comintern required member parties to adopt Leninist organizational structures, purge reformist elements, and submit to binding directives from Moscow. The "Twenty-One Conditions" (1920) made these requirements explicit. Member parties were thus understood as national sections of a single world party under Soviet leadership — raising persistent questions about whose interests they actually represented.

Fellow Traveler

A person who sympathizes with the goals or narrative of a political movement — particularly communism — without being a formal member. Fellow travelers were crucial to the CPUSA's influence in the 1920s–1940s: they amplified communist messaging in journalism, academia, literature, and the arts without being subject to party discipline or ideological tests. The Sacco-Vanzetti case was particularly effective at attracting fellow travelers because its civil libertarian framing allowed liberals to support the campaign without explicitly endorsing revolutionary communism.
