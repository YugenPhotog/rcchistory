Section I

The Exodusters, 1870s–1880s

The first mass movement — before the Great Migration had a name

Why Black Southerners Fled Reconstruction

The Broken Promise
  • Reconstruction (1865–1877) briefly extended political rights to Black men
  • Redeemer Democrats systematically dismantled those gains after 1877
  • Sharecropping replaced slavery in economic function: debt cycles, no land ownership
  • Racial terror — lynching, night riders, Klan violence — enforced subordination
The Rational Response
  • Migration as resistance — voting with feet when voting booths were closed
  • Kansas represented genuine homestead opportunity under federal land grants
  • Freedom from Black Codes and vagrancy laws
  • Self-determination over the terms of one's own labor
📌 Study Buddy Note

The "rational response" framing is key — historians like Thomas Sowell emphasize that migration was an economic calculation, not merely a flight from terror. Keep both columns in mind when asked why people moved.

"Pap" Singleton and the Kansas Exodus

  • Benjamin "Pap" Singleton — formerly enslaved Tennessee carpenter; organized the first deliberate colonization campaigns to Kansas
  • Distributed circulars and organized wagon trains; called himself "the Moses of the Colored Exodus"
  • 1879–1880: Approximately 40,000–60,000 Black Southerners moved to Kansas — the "Exodusters"
  • Founded all-Black townships: Nicodemus, Kansas (est. 1877) survives as a National Historic Site today
The Exodus demonstrated that organized, self-directed migration was possible — a model the later Great Migration would follow at far larger scale
📸 Benjamin "Pap" Singleton

The Exodusters Arrive

  • Many arrived destitute — few had resources for land or supplies
  • Kansas communities, initially welcoming, strained under the sudden influx
  • Some settled successfully; others returned South or moved to Kansas City and other towns
  • The movement alarmed Southern planters who depended on Black labor — state legislatures passed laws restricting emigration agents
The Exoduster movement proved both the will to migrate and the obstacles waiting — a preview of the Great Migration's dynamics

Section II

The First Great Migration, 1910–1940

War, industry, and the opening of the urban North

Push and Pull, 1910–1940

Pushed Out of the South
  • Boll weevil devastated Southern cotton agriculture after 1915 — sharecroppers lost their livelihoods
  • Jim Crow entrenched: lynching peaked 1890–1920; legal apartheid total
  • Flood of 1927 destroyed Delta farms and displaced hundreds of thousands
Pulled to the North
  • World War I (1917–1918): European immigration stopped; Northern factories desperately needed workers
  • Steel mills, meatpacking plants, auto factories — wages 3–4× Southern agricultural work
  • The Chicago Defender actively recruited Black Southerners with job listings and migration advice
📌 Study Buddy Note

Push = reasons to leave; Pull = reasons to go somewhere specific. On exams, be ready to give at least two of each. The boll weevil (push) and WWI labor demand (pull) are the most commonly tested pair.

Scale and Destinations

  • Approximately 1.6 million Black Americans relocated North, 1910–1940
  • Primary destinations: Chicago, New York, Detroit, Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, Cleveland
  • Chicago's Black population: 44,000 (1910) to 278,000 (1940)
  • New York's Harlem became the cultural capital of Black America
Migration routes followed the railroads — Illinois Central from Mississippi to Chicago; Atlantic Coast Line from the Carolinas to New York

Cultural Consequences: The Harlem Renaissance

A New Black Culture
  • Migration concentrated Black intellectual and artistic talent in Northern cities
  • The Harlem Renaissance (1920s–1930s) produced a flowering of literature, music, and visual art
  • Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Duke Ellington, Louis Armstrong
  • Jazz and blues moved from the rural South to become America's defining popular music
Political Awakening
  • Northern Black men could vote — political leverage unavailable in the South
  • Marcus Garvey's UNIA reached its peak influence in Northern cities
  • NAACP membership surged; legal challenge to Jim Crow began in earnest
  • The "New Negro" asserting dignity and demanding rights
📌 Study Buddy Note

The Harlem Renaissance was made possible by the migration — it wasn't an accident of geography. Without concentrated Black population in Northern cities, the cultural explosion wouldn't have had the critical mass to happen.

The North Was Not the Promised Land

  • Residential segregation — restrictive covenants and redlining confined Black migrants to overcrowded urban neighborhoods
  • Race riots — East St. Louis (1917), Chicago (1919), Tulsa (1921): white mobs attacked Black communities during economic competition
  • Union exclusion — AFL craft unions largely barred Black workers; some industries segregated their workforces
  • Wage gains were real despite barriers — even discriminatory Northern wages exceeded Southern sharecropping income substantially
The North offered better wages, political rights, and freedom from legal apartheid — at the cost of new forms of discrimination

The Second Great Migration, 1940–1970

Five million Americans — the wave that transformed the 1950s

World War II as Catalyst

  • Defense mobilization from 1940 opened industrial jobs at unprecedented scale — shipyards, aircraft plants, steel mills, munitions factories
  • Executive Order 8802 (1941) — FDR banned racial discrimination in federal defense contracts
  • New destinations beyond the Midwest: Los Angeles, Oakland, San Francisco, Seattle, Portland
  • Black women entered factory work in large numbers for the first time
The war did not end discrimination — but it pried open industrial labor markets that had been largely closed

The Wage Differential — Why Millions Moved

Staying in the South
  • Sharecropping: tenant farmer gave 50% of crop to landlord
  • Debt bondage to plantation store — cash rarely seen
  • Mississippi Delta cotton picker: approximately $500/year in 1950
  • Legal apartheid, terror, no political rights
Moving North or West
  • Chicago steel mill laborer: approximately $3,000/year in 1950
  • Detroit auto assembly: $4,000–5,000/year
  • California defense industry: comparable to Detroit
  • Wage differential of 6:1 or higher — rational choice even with discrimination
📌 Study Buddy Note

This is the mechanism behind Sowell's poverty data. The 40-point drop in Black poverty (87% → 47%, 1940–1960) happened largely through migration and market wages — before the Great Society programs of the mid-1960s.

The West Coast: A New Destination

  • The Second Migration extended far beyond the traditional Northeast corridor — California alone received over 350,000 Black migrants, 1940–1960
  • Black population of Los Angeles: 63,000 (1940) to 334,000 (1960) — more than fivefold
  • Oakland and Richmond: Kaiser shipyards employed Black workers at scale; community built around industrial employment
  • The West offered somewhat less entrenched residential segregation than the Northeast — though discrimination was real
The Second Migration created Black urban communities in cities that had barely existed as such before 1940

The Migration and the 1950s Prosperity

The Second Great Migration was not a consequence of the 1950s prosperity — it was a cause of it, for Black Americans. Millions of families exchanged Southern agricultural poverty for Northern and Western industrial wages. The result: Black poverty fell from 87% in 1940 to 47% in 1960 — before the Civil Rights Act, before the Great Society, driven by individual family decisions and a labor market that, imperfectly, rewarded productive workers.
The migration was an act of self-determination on a massive scale — not a government program, not a court order

Black poverty fell 40 points between 1940 and 1960 — largely through migration and market wages, before major civil rights legislation. What does this suggest about the relative power of market mechanisms versus government programs in reducing poverty? And what limits does it reveal?

Section IV

Legacy and Significance

What a century of movement built — and left behind

What a Century of Migration Built

For Black Americans
  • An urban Black middle class built on industrial wages and homeownership
  • Political power in Northern cities — voting blocs that neither party could ignore
  • Educational access: Northern schools far superior to segregated Southern institutions
  • The institutional base — churches, newspapers, civic organizations — that made the civil rights movement possible
For American Culture
  • Jazz, blues, gospel, R&B — Southern Black musical forms that became American popular music
  • The Harlem Renaissance and Chicago Renaissance — defining moments in American literary and visual culture
  • The demographic transformation of Northern cities: Chicago, Detroit, New York, Philadelphia became majority-minority cities by the late 20th century

The Migration's Unfinished Business

  • Urban deindustrialization (1970s–1980s): The industrial jobs that had justified migration began disappearing — auto plants closed, steel mills shuttered, the economic foundation of Northern Black communities eroded
  • Residential segregation hardened: Redlining and restrictive covenants had concentrated Black families in specific neighborhoods; white flight and disinvestment followed
  • The reverse migration: Since the 1970s, Black Americans have been moving back to the South — drawn by economic opportunity, lower costs, and family ties. Atlanta, Charlotte, Houston now lead in Black population growth.
Migration is not a finished story — it is a continuing response to changing economic and political conditions

The Takeaway: Migration as Self-Determination

From the Exodusters of the 1870s to the five million migrants of the Second Great Migration, Black Americans responded to oppression not only through protest and litigation but through the most fundamental act available to a free person: moving to where life could be better. Every wave of migration was a rational calculation — wages, rights, safety, opportunity — made by families who understood their options and chose the best one available.
  • The migrations created the institutional base that made the civil rights movement possible
  • The Second Migration drove the poverty reduction that preceded the Great Society
  • The story is one of agency and achievement — not only of victimization and suffering
📌 Study Buddy Note

This is the thesis to remember: migration was an act of agency. On the exam, frame your answer around rational choice and self-determination — not just oppression and escape.

Redeemer Democrats

The term applied to Southern white Democrats who "redeemed" their states from Republican Reconstruction governments between 1869 and 1877. Once in power, Redeemers systematically rolled back Black political and civil rights through a combination of legal disenfranchisement (poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses), racial terror (Klan violence, white leagues), and economic coercion (sharecropping contracts, debt peonage). By 1877, when federal troops were withdrawn from the South as part of the Compromise of 1877, Reconstruction's achievements had been largely dismantled.

×
Black Codes

Laws passed by Southern states in 1865-1866, immediately after the Civil War, designed to restrict the freedom of formerly enslaved people and maintain a supply of cheap agricultural labor. Black Codes typically required Black people to sign annual labor contracts, criminalized "vagrancy" (being without employment), restricted movement between counties, and prohibited Black people from owning certain types of property. Congress responded by passing the Fourteenth Amendment and the Reconstruction Acts, but similar restrictions returned under Jim Crow after Reconstruction ended.

×
The Boll Weevil

A beetle that entered the United States from Mexico around 1892 and spread across the entire Southern cotton belt by 1922. The boll weevil devastated cotton crops by feeding on cotton bolls and flowers. The economic destruction was catastrophic for Southern agriculture, which had been overwhelmingly dependent on cotton. For Black sharecroppers whose livelihoods depended on cotton, the weevil's spread made an already precarious economic situation unsustainable and accelerated the decision to seek industrial employment in the North.

×
The Chicago Defender

Founded in 1905 by Robert S. Abbott, the Chicago Defender became one of the most influential Black newspapers in America and a primary instrument of the First Great Migration. The Defender published job listings in Northern cities, printed letters from migrants describing their improved conditions, and explicitly encouraged Southern Black readers to move North, famously declaring "The Great Northern Drive" in 1917. Southern whites tried to suppress its distribution; Black Pullman car porters secretly distributed copies throughout the South. By 1920 the Defender had a national circulation of approximately 230,000.

×
The Harlem Renaissance

A flourishing of African American intellectual, literary, and artistic culture centered in the Harlem neighborhood of New York City during the 1920s and into the 1930s. Made possible by the concentration of Black talent that the First Great Migration had produced in Northern cities, the Harlem Renaissance produced landmark works by Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Claude McKay, Countee Cullen, and Jean Toomer in literature; Aaron Douglas in visual art; and Duke Ellington, Louis Armstrong, Bessie Smith, and Fats Waller in music. It represented the first large-scale assertion of a distinctly African American cultural identity on American and world stages.

×
Marcus Garvey and the UNIA

Marcus Garvey (1887-1940) was a Jamaican-born Black nationalist leader who founded the Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA) and built it into the largest Black mass movement in American history during the early 1920s. Garvey promoted Black pride, economic self-sufficiency through Black-owned businesses, and the "Back to Africa" movement — the idea that Black people in the diaspora should return to Africa to build a powerful independent nation. His movement attracted hundreds of thousands of Northern Black migrants who had grown disillusioned with the pace of racial progress in America. Garvey was convicted of mail fraud in 1925, imprisoned, and later deported.

×
Redlining

The practice by which banks, insurance companies, and the federal Home Owners' Loan Corporation (created 1933) systematically denied mortgages and insurance to residents of neighborhoods designated as high-risk — which in practice meant neighborhoods with significant Black populations. Maps literally colored these areas red. Redlining prevented Black families from building home equity during the postwar housing boom — the primary mechanism of middle-class wealth accumulation in the 1950s — concentrating them in rental housing in deteriorating urban cores while white families built equity in the expanding suburbs.

×
Executive Order 8802 (1941)

Signed by President Franklin D. Roosevelt on June 25, 1941, Executive Order 8802 prohibited racial discrimination by federal agencies and defense contractors, and established the Fair Employment Practice Committee (FEPC) to investigate complaints. It was issued after A. Philip Randolph, president of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, threatened a 100,000-person march on Washington if the federal government did not act on employment discrimination. The order was imperfectly enforced but opened significant defense industry employment to Black workers — accelerating the Second Great Migration's westward expansion into shipyards and aircraft plants.
