Children’s Rights Movement: A History of Advocacy and Protection

From the Moral Family to Civil Society

Introduction: Why Children’s Reform Matters

  • Antebellum moral reform expanded civic virtue beyond politics to moral life.
  • Child reform extended this moral project to the next generation.
  • Voluntary associations bridged private virtue and public welfare.
  • Scope: From Puritan discipline to Progressive child advocacy.
If virtue sustains democracy, childhood formation becomes a civic project.

Children in the Colonial World: Family as Moral Commonwealth

  • Puritan Ideal: Family as “Little Commonwealth,” linking household order to civic virtue.
  • Body of Liberties (1641): Early legal protection for children.
  • Stubborn Child Law (1646): Salvation through discipline.
Household order was imagined as the seedbed of civic order.

The Colonial & Early Republic Era: Orphans and Dependency

  • New England “placing out” children to other families.
  • Chesapeake courts appoint guardians—early state oversight.
  • Vendue and early orphanages: from auctions to faith-based care.
Care shifted from kinship to organized benevolence.
Birth of child welfare as a civic obligation.

Enlightenment, Romanticism, and the Moral Psychology of Childhood

  • Locke’s tabula rasa: environment shapes virtue.
  • Rousseau’s romantic innocence: childhood as sacred and pure.
  • Moral psychology: discipline and affection form character.
  • Institution building: moral engineering through education.
Ideas reframed childhood as improvable through environment and care.

Industrialization, Urbanization, and the Crisis of Childhood

  • Industrial labor and child poverty (mills, mines, glassworks).
  • Immigration and urban overcrowding concentrate poverty.
  • “Street Arabs” / “Street Rats”; ≈30,000 homeless children in NYC by 1850s.
  • Reformers warned of a “terrible social crisis.”
  • Civil society awakens: churches & charities mobilize.

Voluntary action steps in where family and state fail.

Public Schools: From Moral Laboratory to Children’s Right

  • We already met the Common School crusade in Lecture 2 (Slides 21–32): Horace Mann’s tax-funded classrooms, the hidden curriculum of punctuality & obedience, and the dream of education as “the great equalizer.”
  • New lens today: By the 1850s reformers re-branded schools as a children’s right — a legal duty of the state to rescue poor, immigrant, and working kids from factories and streets.

Compulsory Education Laws, 1852–1918

  • 1852 Massachusetts passes the world’s first compulsory-school law: every child 8–14 must attend school 12 weeks/year.
  • 1867 Massachusetts hires the first truancy officers—badge-carrying “attendance men.”
  • By 1900 every Northern state (and most border states) mandates school attendance.
  • 1918 Mississippi becomes the last state; America now has universal compulsory education.
  • Legal hinge: education shifts from voluntary civic duty to enforceable state obligation.
The schoolhouse door became the first arm of the child-welfare state.

“I was taken by the truant officer and carried to school.”

Institutional Reform: Houses of Refuge and the Moral State

  • Juvenile crime & the “street rat” problem.
  • New York House of Refuge (1825): first juvenile reformatory.
  • Discipline, education, and work: reform through moral training.
  • Parens patriae: the state as moral guardian.
Labor and discipline were seen as pathways to redemption.

Women-led organizations pioneered rescue but enforced Victorian purity.

Raising the Age of Consent, 1886–1905

  • 1885 Most states set statutory-rape age at 10 (Delaware still 7).
  • 1886 WCTU launches “White Life for Two” purity crusade.
  • 1886–1905 WCTU women flood 40 state legislatures with petitions, sermons, and pink-ribbon parades.
  • Outcome 38 states raise the age to 16 or higher; Delaware jumps from 7 to 18.
  • Key weapon “purity literature” mailed to every pastor and sheriff.
WCTU “White Life for Two” campaign → 40 state age-of-consent laws.
One ribbon, one Bible, one ballot at a time.

“No girl is safe at ten.”
—Frances Willard, WCTU convention, 1886

The Children’s Aid Society and the Orphan Trains

  • Charles Loring Brace (1853) founded the Children’s Aid Society.
  • Industrial schools and lodging houses for poor children.
  • Orphan Trains (1854–1929): ≈250,000 children sent west.
  • Reform motto: “Good Christian Homes for Street Children.”
  • Civil society at national scale: philanthropy bridges city & nation.
National networked benevolence links urban orphans or street children and rural homes.
[Insert image: Orphan Train photo / placement notice]

Orphan Trains: The Shadow Side

  • 1865–1870 Southern “apprentice” laws bind 40,000+ Black orphans to former slave-owners — legal re-enslavement.
  • 1879–1934 Federal Indian Boarding Schools: 100,000 Native children forcibly removed; motto “Kill the Indian, Save the Man.”
  • 1854–1929 Orphan Trains refuse Catholic & Jewish children in Protestant towns; Irish kids re-labeled “incorrigible.”
  • Result: the same reformers who “saved” white street arabs built pipelines to plantation, reservation, and reformatory for children of color.
Shadow Truth
Child “protection” was never color-blind.
The train west for white orphans was the chain south or the wagon north for Black and Native children.

The Mary Ellen Moment, 1874–1875

  • 9 April 1874 — Etta Wheeler carries a scarred, chained 10-year-old out of a Hell's Kitchen tenement.
  • 21 April 1874 — Judge rules: a living child can be removed by habeas corpus.
  • 15 Dec 1874 — New York Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children chartered ("The Cruelty").
  • First agency on earth with police powers to enter homes and seize abused children.
  • By 1910 → 250 sister societies nationwide.
One whip-scarred girl + one courtroom photograph = the legal invention of child protective services.
Private charity became public authority in eight months.
Moral outrage → statute → badge → CPS.

The Illinois Juvenile Court Act of 1899

  • April 21, 1899 — Illinois signs the world’s first juvenile-court statute.
  • July 1, 1899 — Cook County opens the first children’s court in a rented room above a Chicago saloon.
  • Core inventions:
    1. Separate docket for under-17s
    2. Closed hearings, sealed records
    3. Probation officers instead of jailers
    4. Chancery (civil) not criminal procedure
  • New legal person: the “juvenile delinquent” — neither criminal nor adult.
  • By 1925 every state but two had copied it; by 1945 every state.
1899 → 1967
Chicago’s experiment → In re Gault (due-process rights) → Tinker (student speech) → UN Convention on the Rights of the Child.
One rented courtroom invented the legal category “child” that still governs every school suspension and foster-care plan in America.
[Insert image: First juvenile courtroom, 219 S. Clark St. / Judge Richard Tuthill on the bench / 1899 probation badge]
“The child is not prosecuted for a crime; the child is protected from a condition.”
—Judge Richard Tuthill, opening day, July 3, 1899

Child-Labor Reform Milestones, 1904–1938

  • 1904 National Child Labor Committee (NCLC) founded in NYC; Lewis Hine hired to photograph “the shame of the states.”
  • 1910 Hine’s photos of 8-year-olds in coal breakers and cotton mills printed in every major magazine.
  • 1916 Keating-Owen Act bans interstate sale of goods made by children under 14 (or 16 in mines).
  • June 3, 1918 Supreme Court strikes it down in Hammer v. Dagenhart (“Congress cannot regulate local labor”).
  • 1938 Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) survives court challenge; sets first federal floor: 16 for regular work, 18 for hazardous.
1904 → 1916 → 1918 → 1938
One Lewis Hine photograph → one federal law → one permanent childhood.
Child labor did NOT vanish in 1850. It took 34 years of lobbying, photography, and two Supreme Court defeats to outlaw it nationwide.

Gilded-Age Nationalization: The WCTU “Do Everything” Revolution

  • Frances Willard (WCTU president 1879–1898) turned temperance into a total social gospel.
  • “Do Everything” platform: child labor laws, kindergartens, age-of-consent reform, juvenile courts, suffrage.
  • 1886–1905: WCTU raises statutory rape age from 10 to 16 in forty states.
  • 1899 Illinois Juvenile Court Act—first children’s court—drafted with WCTU lobbyists.
  • 1904 National Child Labor Committee founded in WCTU offices.
Willard’s motto: “Organize the motherhood of the world.”
Women’s moral networks became the scaffolding of the 20th-century welfare state.

WCTU & Frances Willard

  • Founded 1874, Cleveland. World’s largest women’s organization by 1890.
  • Willard’s alliances: Prohibition Party, Knights of Labor, NAWSA.
  • Laid groundwork for 18th Amendment (1919) and 19th Amendment (1920).
