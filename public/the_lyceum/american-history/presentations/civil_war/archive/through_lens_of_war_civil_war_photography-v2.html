Before Photography: War as Art

War existed through paintings, sketches, and words‚Äîall filtered through artistic interpretation

An artist chose what to include, what to omit, how to pose the figures, what expressions to give them.

üìö Study Buddy Notes

Before 1839, if you wanted to know what a battle looked like, you had to rely on someone's painting or sketch. Even "realistic" battle paintings were composed for dramatic effect‚Äîheroic poses, clear sight lines, moral clarity.

The next three slides show examples of how pre-photography war imagery worked. Pay attention to the staging, the drama, the way death is made noble.

Washington Crossing the Delaware
Emanuel Leutze, 1851

üìö Study Buddy Notes

This is probably the most famous American war painting. It's also historically inaccurate in almost every detail:

  • Wrong flag (the Stars and Stripes didn't exist yet in 1776)
  • Wrong boat (too small for the Delaware crossing)
  • Impossible ice conditions (not chunks like this)
  • Washington standing heroically (he would have sat to avoid capsizing)

Leutze painted this in 1851 to inspire German revolutionaries‚Äîit's about drama and heroism, not historical accuracy. That's how pre-photography war imagery worked.

The Death of General Warren at the Battle of Bunker's Hill, June 17, 1775
John Trumbull, 1786

üìö Study Buddy Notes

John Trumbull was an eyewitness to the Revolutionary War, but his paintings are still carefully staged for dramatic effect. Look at how Warren dies:

  • Noble pose, reaching toward the sky
  • Officers arranged in classical composition
  • Clean uniform, dignified expression
  • The battle becomes a theatrical scene

Compare this to Gardner's photographs you saw earlier‚Äîbloated faces, distorted features, bodies sprawled in awkward positions. Death in reality vs. death as art.

The Death of General Wolfe

Benjamin West's painting of British General Wolfe dying at the Battle of Quebec (1759) established the template for heroic military death in art. Notice:

Then Came the Camera

  • Photography promised mechanical reproduction: the camera recorded what was there
  • Radical idea: seeing could be separated from human judgment
  • No artist to idealize, no painter to make death noble

Or could it?

üìö Study Buddy Notes

After seeing those three paintings, you understand what photography seemed to replace: artistic interpretation, dramatic staging, idealized death.

The camera just recorded light hitting a plate. No interpretation, no idealization. The truth, mechanically captured.

But is that really true? Someone still has to point the camera, choose the moment, decide what to include in the frame. Keep that tension in mind‚Äîit's central to the whole lecture. We'll come back to it when we talk about Gardner's staged sharpshooter.

Photography was an industry, not just documentation

üìö Study Buddy Notes

Mathew Brady was already the most famous photographer in America‚Äîhe'd made portraits of presidents and celebrities. When war came, he saw opportunity: create a comprehensive visual record and sell it.

Gardner and O'Sullivan hauled heavy equipment to battlefields, worked near combat, and developed images in portable darkrooms. Brady put his name on their work. Gardner eventually broke with Brady over this and published his own book, crediting individual photographers.

üí° Why This Matters

Understanding that these were commercial enterprises‚Äînot neutral documentation‚Äîhelps explain the choices they made about what to photograph and how to present it.

What the Camera Could‚Äîand Couldn't‚ÄîShow

  • Wet-plate collodion required 5-15 second exposures
  • You could photograph corpses‚Äîthey held still
  • You could not photograph combat‚Äîeverything blurred

Civil War photography is a record of stillness‚Äîwhich is why it feels so haunted.

×

Wet-Plate Collodion Process

The dominant photographic process of the 1850s-1880s. Here's how it worked:

  • Coat a glass plate with collodion (a syrupy solution)
  • Sensitize it with silver nitrate
  • Expose while still wet (it lost sensitivity as it dried)
  • Develop immediately in a portable darkroom

Imagine doing this in July heat, under artillery fire, surrounded by decomposing bodies. The physical demands were extraordinary.

üìö Study Buddy Notes

This is crucial: the technology determined what could be seen. A 5-15 second exposure meant anything moving would blur. So Civil War photographs show: the dead, troops posing before or after battle, landscapes, fortifications. They don't show: charges, artillery fire, hand-to-hand combat, the chaos of battle.

The absence of motion is the presence of death. That's why these images feel so eerie‚Äîthey're records of absolute stillness in a war that was defined by violence and movement.

"The Dead of Antietam"

üìö Study Buddy Notes

This video covers the October 1862 exhibition at Mathew Brady's New York gallery‚Äîthe first time Americans saw photographs of their war dead.

Pay attention to: the public's reaction, what the New York Times said about it, and why this moment was so significant in American history.

What Made Antietam Different

  • Scale: September 17, 1862‚Äîbloodiest single day in American history (~23,000 casualties)
  • Intimacy: Close-up views showed buttons, belt buckles, facial expressions
  • Civilian confrontation: Horror invaded domestic space‚Äîa gallery on Broadway

üìö Study Buddy Notes

Scale: 23,000 casualties in one day is almost incomprehensible. The photographs gave that number bodies‚Äîyou couldn't think of it as an abstraction anymore.

Intimacy: Gardner got close. These weren't distant panoramas. You could see individual details that made the dead specific‚Äîthis man, with these shoes, in this position.

Civilian confrontation: Before this, war's visual horror stayed at the front. Now it came to Manhattan, surrounded by shops and theaters. The distance between battlefield and home collapsed.

Contemporary Reaction

"Mr. Brady has done something to bring home to us the terrible reality and earnestness of war. If he has not brought bodies and laid them in our dooryards and along the streets, he has done something very like it."
‚Äî New York Times, October 1862

üìö Study Buddy Notes

This quote captures the shock of the moment. The reviewer understood that something new had happened: war's dead had come home. Not literally, but visually‚Äîwhich was almost as powerful.

Notice the language: "bring home," "dooryards," "streets." The photographs invaded domestic space. You couldn't keep the war at a comfortable distance anymore.

ü§î Think About It

The review credits Brady, but Gardner took the photographs. This was common‚ÄîBrady put his name on his employees' work. It's a reminder that even attributing these images is complicated.

What Viewers Didn't See

  • Most Antietam dead were already buried‚ÄîGardner arrived two days later
  • His "harvest" was selective: mainly Confederate dead (Union burial details prioritized their own)
  • No photographs of field hospitals, surgery, the wounded‚Äîtoo much motion, not commercially viable

The camera forced a moral choice: to look was to become complicit in knowing

üìö Study Buddy Notes

The photographs were powerful but partial. What you didn't see matters as much as what you did.

Gardner showed a skewed sample‚Äîmore rebel dead than Yankee. And he couldn't show the wounded, the surgery, the screaming. The technology couldn't capture it, and audiences might not have bought it.

So the "truth" of Antietam was a selected truth. Still powerful, still important‚Äîbut not the whole story.

üí° Key Insight

Once you saw these images, you couldn't unsee them. You had to decide whether to look, and once you looked, you couldn't claim ignorance of what war cost. That's a new kind of civilian responsibility.

The Intimate War: Cartes-de-Visite

×

Cartes-de-Visite (kart-duh-vee-ZEET)

French for "visiting cards." Small albumen prints (2.5 x 4 inches) mounted on cardstock.

  • Format: A multi-lens camera made 8 exposures on one plate, then cut apart‚Äîthat's why they were cheap
  • Cost: About 25 cents (down from several dollars for earlier processes)
  • Scale: Millions produced during the war
  • Function: Soldiers sent them home; families kept them in albums, lockets, Bibles. If a soldier died, the carte became a sacred relic.

üìö Study Buddy Notes

Click the term "Cartes-de-Visite" for the full definition. Key points:

  • Cheap enough for ordinary families (25 cents)
  • Portable and easy to mail
  • Millions made during the war
  • If a soldier died, the photograph became a sacred object

This is photography entering the home‚Äînot public spectacle like Brady's gallery, but private grief. Families mourned a specific face they could hold in their hands.

üîç Notice the Images

These six cartes show the range: a Union soldier, Lincoln, a general, plus international celebrities like Charles Dickens, Queen Victoria, and Tennyson. People collected and traded these like baseball cards‚Äîthe birth of modern celebrity culture.

Photography Enters the Home

  • Brady's gallery = public spectacle
  • Cartes-de-visite = private intimacy
  • Grief became visual and personal‚Äîmourning a face, not an abstraction

But whose grief was recorded? Who controlled these images?

üìö Study Buddy Notes

This is the shift: from public to private. The Antietam exhibition shocked New York crowds. Cartes-de-visite transformed how families mourned in their own homes.

Women bore most of the labor here‚Äîcurating albums, arranging photographs, maintaining the visual archives of memory. This was gendered work: men went back to business while women tended the altars of remembrance.

This became one of the most iconic images from the war. A Confederate sniper lies dead in his rocky nest at Devil's Den, his rifle propped beside him. The caption tells a story: he died alone at his post, a soldier to the end.

It's powerful and moving. It's also staged.

üîç The Evidence

Scholars have studied Gardner's Gettysburg images closely. The evidence is clear: he moved bodies, arranged limbs, added props. This wasn't unusual‚Äîit's what photographers did to make compelling images.

A random corpse in an open field was less commercially interesting than a sniper in his "home" with his weapon beside him. Gardner was running a business. He needed images that sold.

ü§î The Big Question

So was this a lie? Or was Gardner using staging to tell a larger truth about war's tragedy‚Äîthe young man who would never go home? When does artistic shaping become deception?

All Photographs Are Framed

"To photograph is to frame, and to frame is to exclude."
‚Äî Susan Sontag, Regarding the Pain of Others (2003)
  • Someone chose what to include and exclude
  • Someone chose the angle, the moment, the caption
  • The mechanical process conceals human judgment‚Äîit doesn't eliminate it

üìö Study Buddy Notes

Susan Sontag (1933-2004) was a major American critic who wrote extensively about photography's power and ethics. Her book Regarding the Pain of Others is essential reading on war photography.

Her argument: we look at photographs and think we're seeing "reality." But every photograph is a selection. What's outside the frame? What moment came before or after? What did the caption tell you to see?

Gardner's staging is just an extreme version of what all photographs do. Even "authentic" images make choices about what to show and how to show it.

üí° Key Insight

The question isn't "Did he lie?" It's "What truth was he trying to tell?" and "Whose interests did that truth serve?"

Controlling the Frame

Sojourner Truth (c. 1797-1883)

  • Sold cartes-de-visite to fund her activism
  • Caption: "I Sell the Shadow to Support the Substance"
  • She controlled: pose, caption, distribution

Most Black Americans didn't get this control.

üìö Study Buddy Notes

Sojourner Truth is the crucial counterexample. She understood photography's political power and used it strategically.

She controlled everything: how she was photographed (dignified, composed, well-dressed), how she was captioned (her own words), and how her image circulated (sold by her, for her cause). She refused to let white photographers or publishers define her.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Point

Most Black Americans didn't have this power. When freedpeople were photographed in contraband camps, they were captioned as types‚Äî"contraband," "freedwoman and children"‚Äînot as named individuals. Their images were taken and used by others.

The "democratization of memory" recorded some lives and erased others. The camera told some people's truths, not everyone's.

Whose Truth?

  • USCT regiments (180,000 soldiers) photographed far less than white units
  • Freedpeople captioned as types: "contraband," "freedman"‚Äînot named individuals
  • The archive itself is a form of selection

The ethical question isn't "Is this photograph true?" It's "Whose truth does this photograph serve?"

×

USCT: United States Colored Troops

African American soldiers who served in the Union Army during the Civil War. About 180,000 Black men served in the USCT, making up roughly 10% of the Union Army by war's end.

They fought in major engagements including the assault on Fort Wagner (1863), the Battle of the Crater (1864), and the capture of Richmond (1865). Despite their service, they faced discrimination in pay, equipment, and recognition‚Äîincluding in how often they were photographed.

üìö Study Buddy Notes

This is the most important point about "democratization": it had sharp limits based on race, class, and geography.

Black soldiers served in huge numbers but were photographed far less. When enslaved or formerly enslaved people appeared in photographs, they were often presented as specimens or types, not individuals with names and stories.

The photographs that survive shape our memory of the war. We see the faces that were recorded. We don't see the faces that weren't. That absence is itself a kind of framing.

üéØ Key Takeaway

Ask of any photograph: whose truth does it serve? Who benefits from this image? Whose story is being told‚Äîand whose is being left out?

Democratic Monuments

  • Before photography: only generals and statesmen got monuments
  • Civil War photographs created memorials for ordinary soldiers‚Äîprivates, corporals, farmboys
  • The common dead could now be seen, not just counted

This was radical‚Äîeven if incomplete.

üìö Study Buddy Notes

Monuments are selective: they tell us which deaths matter. Before photography, ordinary soldiers were buried in mass graves and forgotten. Only the famous got statues.

Civil War photography changed that‚Äîimperfectly, incompletely, but genuinely. A carte-de-visite in a family album was a monument. Gardner's photograph of an unknown soldier was a monument. The dead became visible in a new way.

ü§î Think About It

We've already discussed the limits of this democratization (race, class). But within those limits, something real happened. Ordinary people's faces survived. That mattered.

Preserving the Horror

  • After the war, memory would be contested‚ÄîLost Cause, reconciliation narratives
  • But the photographs remained: bloated corpses couldn't be rewritten into noble sacrifice
  • The camera fixed the raw emotional register before ideology could sanitize it

üìö Study Buddy Notes

We'll talk in Lecture 3 about the Lost Cause‚Äîhow Southerners transformed military defeat into a story of noble sacrifice and erased slavery from the narrative. Reconciliationist memories emphasized shared valor and downplayed causes.

But you can't make Gardner's "Harvest of Death" into a noble sacrifice. Those swollen faces resist easy mythology. The photographs preserved the shock of 1862 before anyone could explain it away.

üí° Key Insight

The photographs are evidence against easy stories. They're not the whole truth, but they're a check on comfortable lies.

The Long Legacy

  • Civil War photography established a principle: war should be seen
  • This shaped every subsequent conflict‚Äîmost dramatically Vietnam
  • But it also taught governments to manage visual access

The camera promised democratic witness‚Äîbut seeing is never neutral

(See Part 2: Independent Study for extended discussion of Vietnam photography)

üìö Study Buddy Notes

Civil War photography established an ideal: citizens have the right to see what is done in their name. That ideal would be tested a century later in Vietnam, where television and photography influenced public opinion and arguably policy.

But the counter-tradition also began here: once governments learned that images could shape opinion, they learned to control visual access. Embedded journalism, restricted zones, Pentagon media strategy‚Äîall responses to what happened when Gardner's photos reached New York.

What We've Seen

  • Photography created a new relationship between citizens and warfare
  • It promised truth but delivered framed selections
  • It democratized memory but not equally
  • It preserved horror but also sold it

These tensions remain unresolved.

üìö Study Buddy Notes

Here's the synthesis. Photography changed how Americans witnessed war‚Äîbut that change was complicated:

  • Truth vs. framing: mechanical objectivity, but always selected and shaped
  • Democratization vs. exclusion: more people remembered, but not everyone equally
  • Horror vs. commerce: confronting reality, but also packaging it for sale

These aren't problems that got solved. They're the permanent condition of war photography, from 1862 to today.
