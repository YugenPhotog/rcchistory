Section I

The Strategic Revolution

Why did controlling a canal matter more than winning battles?

Transition: From opening hook (French failure) into the strategic logic. Students need to understand Mahan before Panama 1903 makes sense.

The Two-Ocean Problem

  • Two coastlines = strategic weakness without a canal
  • Atlantic & Pacific fleets: effectively separate
  • Moving a battleship east ‚Üí west: 8,000 extra miles
  • Only option: sail around Cape Horn (~2 months)
üì∏ USS Oregon Battleship
Search: "USS Oregon battleship 1898 Wikimedia"

USS Oregon, c. 1898 ¬∑ Public Domain ¬∑ Wikimedia Commons

üìö What You Need to Know: The two-ocean problem was not abstract. The U.S. had acquired colonies in the Pacific (Philippines, Guam) and the Caribbean (Cuba, Puerto Rico) after 1898. Defending both required moving warships between oceans rapidly ‚Äî which was impossible without a canal.

üí≠ Context: Two months is two months of vulnerability. Ask students: what could change in two months during a military crisis? This makes the problem visceral.

ü§î Discussion Prompt: Is having two coastlines inherently an advantage or disadvantage for a naval power?

The Oregon's Race

April 1898: War declared. USS Oregon in San Francisco ‚Äî needs Cuba.

Problem: 14,000 miles of ocean between them.

68 days around Cape Horn. Arrived barely in time for the Battle of Santiago.

The whole country watched the clock. Roosevelt watched too.

His conclusion: A genuine two-ocean power requires a canal. Full stop.

üìö What You Need to Know: The Oregon's voyage was front-page news for weeks. Newspapers ran daily updates on her position. The public anxiety generated by her slow progress became a major argument for canal construction. Roosevelt, as Assistant Secretary of the Navy in 1898, was deeply attentive to the strategic lesson.

üí≠ Context: "Barely in time" is not hyperbole ‚Äî the Oregon arrived just two days before the decisive Battle of Santiago, July 3, 1898. She fired 17 thirteen-inch shells in the engagement. Her presence mattered. But the 68-day delay demonstrated the vulnerability clearly.

ü§î Discussion Prompt: What would it have cost if the Oregon had arrived after the battle instead of before?

The Commercial Twin

The Economic Argument
  • American exports booming in 1890s‚Äì1900s
  • Pacific trade with Asia: rapidly growing
  • Canal = dramatic reduction in shipping distances
  • Cuts costs for American exporters ¬∑ integrates U.S. into global markets
Who Understood This
  • J.P. Morgan ‚Äî saw the commercial opportunity
  • Railroad interests ‚Äî saw it with dread
  • Canal shipping would undercut cross-continental freight rates
  • Strategic logic and commercial logic aligned perfectly
The canal was not built for idealism. It was built because military power and economic power both required it.

üìö What You Need to Know: The military and commercial arguments for the canal reinforced each other completely. This is why the project attracted such broad elite support ‚Äî it was simultaneously a strategic necessity and a commercial opportunity. The railroads were the only major interest opposed, and their opposition was itself evidence of how significant the commercial stakes were.

üí≠ Connect forward: The alignment of military and commercial interest is a recurring pattern in American imperial expansion. Cuba (sugar), Philippines (Pacific trade routes), Panama (canal tolls and shipping cost reduction). This is not a coincidence; it is the structure of how 19th-century American empire operated.

ü§î Discussion Prompt: When military necessity and commercial interest point in the same direction, what happens to the moral arguments that might complicate the decision?

  • Routes run through chokepoints

üìö What You Need to Know: Alfred Thayer Mahan published The Influence of Sea Power Upon History in 1890. Section II

Getting the Land

Panama 1903 ‚Äî the mechanics of imperial acquisition

Transition: Now we know why the canal was strategically necessary. This section explains how the U.S. obtained the right to build it ‚Äî and what that story reveals about how empire actually operates.

The Colombia Problem

  • 1903: Panama was a province of Colombia ‚Äî not an independent country
  • Jan 1903: Hay-Herr√°n Treaty ‚Äî 99-year lease, $10M + $250K/yr
  • Aug 1903: Colombian senate rejected it unanimously
  • Colombia wanted more money, more sovereignty protection
  • This was a reasonable position for a sovereign government

üìö What You Need to Know: The Colombian senate's rejection was not irrational. They wanted either more money or more sovereignty protections ‚Äî both legitimate negotiating positions. The U.S. offered the same terms to Colombia that it would later offer to Panama. The difference was that Colombia had the institutional capacity to say no. Panama, as a newly independent nation three weeks old, did not.

üí≠ Key contrast: Roosevelt's private language vs. his public "reluctant" Corollary language. The gap between these two registers is analytically important ‚Äî deliver the private quotes plainly, without theatricality.

ü§î Discussion Prompt: If Colombia's position was reasonable, what options did the U.S. government have other than the one Roosevelt chose?

  • Colombian troops arrive the same day
  • Nashville's presence: Colombia cannot move troops across the isthmus

üì∏ Philippe Bunau-Varilla

üìö What You Need to Know: Philippe Bunau-Varilla was a French engineer who had invested heavily in the old French canal company. He stood to profit enormously if the U.S. purchased the French canal assets. He coordinated with Panamanian independence leaders AND with Roosevelt administration officials. üí≠ The mule: A stray Colombian artillery shell killed one mule. That was the total violence of the "revolution." The detail lands well because it makes the manufactured quality of the event concrete and slightly absurd. Deliver it with a straight face.

ü§î Discussion Prompt: If a revolution succeeds without a single battle, what actually produced the outcome?

The Hay-Bunau-Varilla Treaty

Bunau-Varilla ‚Äî the French engineer, not a Panamanian ‚Äî was appointed Panama's negotiator.

Panamanian diplomats raced to Washington. They didn't make it in time.

He signed before they arrived:

  • U.S. gets sovereign authority over a 10-mile strip ‚Äî in perpetuity
  • Price: $10M + $250K/yr ‚Äî same terms Colombia had rejected

The three-week-old nation's territory was signed away by a Frenchman.

üìö What You Need to Know: The Hay-Bunau-Varilla Treaty is remarkable for what it reveals about the power dynamics at work. The Panamanian government objected strenuously ‚Äî they had expected better terms. But they were a new nation of three weeks, dependent on U.S. military protection for their survival. They ratified the treaty despite their objections.

üí≠ Key analytical point: "In perpetuity" becomes the pivot for the closing section ‚Äî the canal zone ran 1903‚Äì1999, 96 years. Plant that word now.

Engineering Empire

Labor, disease, and the racial architecture of construction

Transition: Most students know the canal exists. Almost none know who built it, under what conditions, or how the workforce was organized. This section connects directly to Lecture 2's analysis of Jim Crow applied to the Philippines ‚Äî the same framework, exported again.

Solving the French Problem

Why the French died
  • Engineers, not doctors, ran the effort
  • Hospital flower pots = mosquito nurseries
  • Better patient care = faster disease spread
  • Death toll suppressed to protect investors
Dr. William Gorgas, 1904

üì∏ Dr. William Crawford Gorgas
Search: "William Crawford Gorgas Wikimedia Commons"

Dr. William Crawford Gorgas, U.S. Army Surgeon ¬∑ Public Domain

üìö What You Need to Know: Gorgas had previously demonstrated in Havana (1901) that systematic mosquito control could eliminate yellow fever entirely. His Panama campaign was even more ambitious: draining swamps, oiling standing water, screening buildings, fumigating houses systematically. By 1906, yellow fever had been virtually eliminated. Malaria was never fully eliminated but rates dropped dramatically.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical analytical point coming on the next slide: The sanitation success is real, but it was operational, not humanitarian. Set up the analytical reversal.

Sanitation as a Tool of Empire

"The public health campaign was not humanitarian.
It was operational. The goal was not to improve Panamanian health broadly.
The goal was to keep the workforce alive long enough to finish the canal."
  • Medical resources allocated along the same hierarchical lines as everything else
  • American engineers/supervisors: highest standard of care
  • West Indian laborers: progressively less
  • Empire organizes human life according to its own priorities

üìö What You Need to Know: This analytical reversal is the most important move in Segment 3. Students tend to receive the Gorgas story as a straightforward triumph ‚Äî smart American doctors saved lives. The reversal makes visible that whose lives were saved, how much care they received, and what the campaign was actually designed to accomplish were all structured by the same racial hierarchy that organized everything else in the Zone. Don't soften this. The sanitation was brilliant AND it was imperial. Both are true simultaneously.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner: Julie Greene, The Canal Builders (2009), documents that in some years the official death statistics for West Indian workers were so undercounted as to be nearly meaningless ‚Äî deaths were recorded differently depending on racial classification.

üìö What You Need to Know: Approximately 20,000 West Indian workers, predominantly Barbadian, built the canal. They did the most dangerous work: drilling, dynamiting, removing spoil from the Culebra Cut. Landslides were constant and lethal ‚Äî workers had seconds, not minutes, when a slide came.

Make this connection explicit.

Section IV

Improvisation Becomes Doctrine

The Roosevelt Corollary, 1904

Transition: By 1904, Roosevelt has spent six years improvising empire. Cuba, the Philippines, Panama ‚Äî each justified after the fact with the same vocabulary. Here he names it, formalizes it, and gives it hemispheric scope.

Six Years of Improvisation

  • Cuba, 1898: "Liberate the oppressed"
  • Philippines, 1899‚Äì1902: "Temporary stewardship" ¬∑ "Order before liberty"
  • Panama, 1903: "Civilization requires it" ¬∑ "However reluctantly"
Each intervention produced the same argument:
This nation cannot govern itself. We must step in. This will be temporary.
December 1904: Roosevelt writes it into doctrine.

üìö What You Need to Know: Roosevelt is not inventing new ideas in 1904. He is naming what the United States has already been doing and extending it to hemispheric scale. This is the analytical point students must feel: the Corollary is descriptive before it becomes prescriptive ‚Äî it describes the logic that produced Cuba, the Philippines, and Panama.

üí≠ Framing: Ask students to identify the recurring phrases across all three interventions before you show the Corollary text. They should be able to supply "order," "civilization," "temporary," "reluctantly" themselves.

The Roosevelt Corollary (1904)

"Chronic wrongdoing, or an impotence which results in a general loosening of the ties of civilized society, may in America, as elsewhere, ultimately require intervention by some civilized nation, and in the Western Hemisphere the adherence of the United States to the Monroe Doctrine may force the United States, however reluctantly, to the exercise of an international police power."
‚Äî Theodore Roosevelt, Annual Message to Congress, December 6, 1904

üìö READ THIS SLOWLY. Do not rush. After reading, pause. Then ask students to identify the specific words:

"Chronic wrongdoing." "Impotence." "General loosening of the ties of civilized society." "Civilized nation." "International police power." "However reluctantly."

Then ask: Where have you heard this language before? They should recognize it immediately ‚Äî it's the language from Lecture 2: "pacification," "order," "civilization," "they are not ready for self-rule."

üéì The analytical point: Roosevelt didn't invent new ideas in 1904. He named what the United States had already been doing and gave it hemispheric scope.

Section V

How Long Does "Temporary" Last?

The Canal Zone from 1903 to 1999

Transition: We've seen how the Canal Zone was acquired and how it operated. Now: how long did "in perpetuity" actually last? And why did it end when it did?

96 Years of "Perpetuity"

  • 1903: Hay-Bunau-Varilla Treaty ‚Äî U.S. control "in perpetuity"
  • 1964: Panamanian riots ‚Äî 20+ killed; Panama breaks diplomatic relations
  • 1977: Torrijos-Carter Treaties ‚Äî signed under sustained pressure; transfer timeline established
  • 1979: Canal Zone abolished; joint administration begins
  • 1999: Full control transferred to Panama ‚Äî December 31
A student born when the treaty was signed might not have seen the transfer completed in their lifetime.

üìö What You Need to Know: The 1964 riots are the pivot. American students raised the U.S. flag in the Canal Zone in defiance of an agreement to fly both flags. Panamanian students tried to raise their flag. American forces ‚Äî and civilian crowds ‚Äî attacked them. More than 20 Panamanians were killed. Panama broke diplomatic relations with the U.S. The riots made the Zone's political arrangement untenable ‚Äî but it still took 13 more years to sign a treaty.

üí≠ Key analytical point: The 1977 negotiations happened because the 1964 riots happened. Empire was not dismantled from the top. It was pressured from below, at cost. The transfer of the canal is not a story of American magnanimity. It is a story of sustained resistance producing a negotiated result over half a century.

A systemic logic reproduces itself because the infrastructure makes certain actions feel rational and certain alternatives feel costly. The canal doesn't make intervention inevitable ‚Äî but it makes intervention available in ways that shape what counts as a "reasonable" response to crisis. Once the infrastructure is there, the burden of proof shifts.

American naval officer and historian whose 1890 work The Influence of Sea Power Upon History became one of the most influential strategic texts of the era. Gold Roll and Silver Roll

The official two-tier pay and status system of the Panama Canal Zone, written into U.S. government administrative policy from the project's beginning. Gold Roll workers ‚Äî almost exclusively white Americans and some European skilled workers ‚Äî were paid in U.S. gold currency and received better housing, food, medical care, and facilities. Silver Roll workers ‚Äî almost exclusively Black Caribbean laborers (primarily from Barbados) and some Latin American workers ‚Äî were paid in Panamanian silver currency at lower rates and received separate, inferior accommodations and medical attention. The system was not informal workplace custom; it was federal administrative policy that exported Jim Crow racial classification into an imperial context. Historian Julie Greene documents the system in detail in The Canal Builders (2009).

Roosevelt Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine (1904)

An addition to the Monroe Doctrine announced by President Theodore Roosevelt in his Annual Message to Congress, December 6, 1904. The original Monroe Doctrine (1823) had declared that European powers should not colonize or intervene in the Americas. The Roosevelt Corollary added: if Latin American nations could not maintain order or meet their international obligations, the United States reserved the right to intervene as an "international police power." It was first applied to the Dominican Republic in 1905 and subsequently invoked to justify interventions in Nicaragua (1909, 1912, 1926), Haiti (1915‚Äì1934), and the Dominican Republic again (1916‚Äì1924). The Corollary was officially repudiated by the Franklin Roosevelt administration in 1934 as part of the "Good Neighbor Policy."

Panamanian Flag Riots (January 9, 1964)

A pivotal confrontation in the Canal Zone. American high school students raised a U.S. flag in the Zone in defiance of an agreement to fly both the American and Panamanian flags at civilian sites. Panamanian students attempted to raise their flag; fighting broke out. American forces and civilian crowds attacked Panamanian students and the violence spread. More than 20 Panamanians and 4 Americans were killed. Panama temporarily broke diplomatic relations with the United States and brought charges before the Organization of American States. The riots made the Zone's political arrangements untenable and began the process that eventually led to the 1977 Torrijos-Carter Treaties. January 9 is now commemorated in Panama as "Martyrs' Day."

Torrijos-Carter Treaties (1977)

Two treaties signed September 7, 1977 between the United States and Panama, negotiated by President Jimmy Carter and Panamanian leader General Omar Torrijos. The first treaty ended U.S. claims to permanent sovereignty over the Canal Zone and established a joint administration with a transfer timeline. The second guaranteed permanent neutrality of the canal. The treaties were deeply controversial in the United States and required a difficult Senate ratification fight. They were the result of more than a decade of Panamanian pressure ‚Äî including the 1964 riots ‚Äî and significant diplomatic pressure from other Latin American nations. Full control was transferred to Panama on December 31, 1999.
