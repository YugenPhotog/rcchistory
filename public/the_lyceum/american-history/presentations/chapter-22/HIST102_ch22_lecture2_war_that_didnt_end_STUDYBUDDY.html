The War That Didn't End

Roosevelt and the Moral Mechanics of Occupation

üìö What This Lecture Is About

Lecture 1 showed you how 1898 was made into a spectacle ‚Äî how media, emotion, and Roosevelt's celebrity turned a war into a political performance. This lecture asks the harder question: what happened after the curtain came down? The Treaty of Paris gave the United States the Philippines, Puerto Rico, and Guam. What followed was not a peaceful transition to American benevolence. It was a brutal, grinding counterinsurgency war that lasted years, killed hundreds of thousands, and forced Americans to confront a contradiction they had never fully resolved: can a republic built on consent govern millions of people who never consented?

‚ö†Ô∏è A Note on the Material

This lecture covers documented atrocities ‚Äî torture, civilian massacres, mass starvation as deliberate military strategy. The evidence is in Senate testimony, Army reports, and soldier letters. Treating this history seriously means not softening it. The goal isn't to make you feel bad about America ‚Äî it's to give you the analytical tools to understand how democracies can do terrible things while genuinely believing they're doing good.

üí≠ The Gap Between What America Expected and What It Got

Americans in December 1898 expected the Treaty of Paris to mean victory, peace, and a quick return home. What they got instead was an unplanned empire ‚Äî 8 million people in the Philippines alone, none of whom had been consulted about the transfer of sovereignty from Spain to the United States. The Filipinos weren't part of the negotiation. They weren't in the room. The treaty was signed in Paris by two colonial powers, and the Philippines changed hands like a property deed.

üí° The Sentence That Frames the Whole Lecture

"Wars do not end when treaties are signed. They end when questions of sovereignty, legitimacy, and consent are resolved." Hold onto that. The Treaty of Paris ended the Spanish-American War legally. It did not resolve who had the right to govern the Philippines ‚Äî a question Filipino fighters and the new Philippine Republic had a very clear answer to. The shooting started again by February 1899, six weeks after the treaty was signed.

Section I

The Philippines Before the United States

The Philippines did not enter history in 1898.

üìñ Why This Section Comes First

Before the lecture can tell you what the U.S. did in the Philippines, it needs to establish who was already there. The standard American narrative of 1898 tends to treat the Philippines as a prize won from Spain ‚Äî passive, waiting, undefined. This section corrects that. By the time American soldiers arrived, the Philippines had an organized independence movement, military forces, political leadership, and a functioning revolutionary government. Understanding that changes everything about what followed.

üáµüá≠ The Philippines Before the U.S. Arrived

Two years before any American soldier set foot in the Philippines, Filipinos had already launched a revolution. The Katipunan ‚Äî a secret revolutionary society ‚Äî began armed revolt against Spain in 1896. This was not a spontaneous uprising. It had newspapers, ideology, a military structure, and mass participation. By June 1898, Emilio Aguinaldo had declared Philippine independence. By January 1899, the Philippines had ratified a constitution and established the Malolos Republic ‚Äî with a president, a cabinet, and a legislature.

‚ö†Ô∏è What This Means

The people the United States was about to govern had not been consulted ‚Äî and they had not been sitting still waiting to be governed. They had spent two years fighting for their independence from one colonial power. When the United States arrived presenting itself as a liberator, Aguinaldo had reasonable grounds to expect that liberation meant independence. What he got instead was a new colonial master ‚Äî and he chose to fight.

Aguinaldo's Argument

"My government cannot remain indifferent in view of such a violent and aggressive seizure of a portion of its territory by a nation which has arrogated to itself the title of champion of oppressed nations... We are not savages ignorant of how to govern ourselves."
‚Äî Emilio Aguinaldo, Proclamation, January 1899
  • This is a precise political argument ‚Äî not a confused response
  • He was right that the U.S. was replacing one colonial power with another
  • He was right that Filipinos had the capacity for self-governance
  • He lost the war anyway

üìñ Read This Carefully ‚Äî It's a Precise Political Argument

Aguinaldo's January 1899 proclamation is not the confused protest of someone who doesn't understand what's happening. It is a specific, accurate, and sophisticated political argument. He identifies exactly what the U.S. is doing ‚Äî replacing one colonial power with another ‚Äî and he calls it what it is: seizure of territory by a nation that claims to champion freedom. The phrase "we are not savages ignorant of how to govern ourselves" is a direct rebuttal to the civilizational justifications that American politicians were already deploying in Congress.

üí° He Was Right

Aguinaldo's claim that the U.S. was arrogating to itself the title of "champion of oppressed nations" while oppressing a nation was not propaganda ‚Äî it was an accurate description of the contradiction at the heart of American imperial policy. Senator Albert Beveridge would stand on the Senate floor that same month and argue that Filipinos were racially unfit for self-government. Aguinaldo's proclamation was a direct answer to that argument ‚Äî made before Beveridge even delivered it.

‚ö†Ô∏è This Was Not a Misunderstanding ‚Äî It Was a Power Struggle

The slide lists two sets of assumptions ‚Äî American and Filipino ‚Äî and it would be easy to read this as a tragic miscommunication, two sides talking past each other. That reading is too comfortable. Both sides understood the disagreement perfectly. The Americans knew Filipinos wanted independence. The Filipinos knew the Americans intended to stay. The conflict wasn't caused by confusion ‚Äî it was caused by one side having the military and economic power to impose its assumptions on the other.

üìå Notice the Word "Ingratitude"

The American framing of Filipino resistance as ingratitude is worth sitting with. It assumes that American presence was inherently a gift ‚Äî that the Filipinos should have been thankful for occupation. That assumption required believing that Filipinos were incapable of governing themselves, that American rule was genuinely benevolent, and that independence was something to be earned rather than a right. Aguinaldo's proclamation directly challenged all three. The "ingratitude" framing was a way of refusing to engage with his argument.

ü§î A Question Worth Sitting With Honestly

By January 1899, the Philippines had a constitution, a president, a legislature, and an army. On what grounds did the United States claim authority to govern them? The lecture will surface three common answers students arrive at:

  • Purchase from Spain: The U.S. paid Spain $20 million for the Philippines ‚Äî but can sovereignty be bought and sold like property without the consent of the people involved?
  • Military victory: American forces defeated the Spanish fleet at Manila Bay ‚Äî but defeating one colonial power doesn't automatically grant you the right to replace it
  • "Civilizational" arguments: Filipinos weren't "ready" for self-rule ‚Äî but who decides readiness, and what are the criteria?

None of these answers is satisfying on its own ‚Äî and the lecture's job is to show what those justifications actually produced in practice. The question isn't resolved here. Hold it.

Section II

From Ally to Enemy

The Philippine-American War begins with incompatible visions of freedom.

üìñ The War That Wasn't Called a War

Pay attention to the language the U.S. government used: not "war," but "pacification campaign." Not "soldiers," but "insurgents." Not "enemy combatants," but "criminals." That language wasn't accidental ‚Äî it was doing political work, and this section will show you exactly what work it was doing and why it mattered for what the U.S. military was permitted to do.

February 4, 1899

  • American sentries shoot two Filipino soldiers near Manila ‚Äî fighting erupts within hours
  • U.S. government refuses to call it a war ‚Äî officially: a "pacification campaign" against "insurgents"
  • Initial Filipino strategy: conventional warfare ‚Äî set-piece battles
  • Result: decisive American victories ‚Äî industrial firepower overwhelms conventional resistance by mid-1899
  • Filipino response: shift to guerrilla warfare
The language matters. "Insurgents" are criminals. Soldiers are combatants. The word choice determined how violence was permitted to proceed.

üí• How the War Started

On the night of February 4, 1899, American sentries shot two Filipino soldiers near Manila. Within hours, fighting had spread across the city. The Philippine-American War had begun ‚Äî though the U.S. government refused to call it that for over a century. Officially, it was a "pacification campaign" against "insurgents." That word choice had real legal and moral consequences: soldiers are covered by the laws of war; insurgents and criminals are not.

üìå Why the Language Mattered

Calling Filipino fighters "insurgents" rather than soldiers accomplished several things at once. It delegitimized the Philippine Republic and its constitution. It framed armed resistance to occupation as criminality rather than self-defense. It removed Filipino fighters from the protections of the laws of war. And it made the conflict sound like a policing problem rather than a war ‚Äî smaller, more manageable, and less morally fraught than it actually was. The language was doing the work that justification couldn't do directly.

üí° The Military Logic That Followed

Filipino conventional forces lost quickly ‚Äî American industrial firepower overwhelmed set-piece resistance by mid-1899. The Filipino response was to shift to guerrilla warfare, which was militarily rational given the power imbalance. That shift then triggered a specific American counterinsurgency logic ‚Äî one whose conclusions the next slides document in full.

The Logic of Counterinsurgency

The problem: When conventional resistance fails, guerrilla fighters rely on civilian support ‚Äî food, shelter, intelligence.
  • You cannot defeat guerrillas without separating them from the civilian population
  • That separation requires controlling civilians
  • Controlling civilians at scale requires coercion
  • This logic is militarily rational
  • It is also humanly catastrophic
The tactics that follow are not aberrations. They are the conclusions of this logic.

‚öôÔ∏è Follow the Logic ‚Äî It Leads Somewhere Specific

This slide asks you to follow a chain of military reasoning that is, in its own terms, coherent. Guerrilla fighters depend on civilian support ‚Äî food, shelter, intelligence. You cannot defeat them by killing fighters alone, because fighters blend back into the civilian population. Therefore, to defeat the guerrillas, you must separate them from civilians. Separating fighters from civilians at the scale of an entire archipelago requires controlling where civilians live, what they eat, and who they talk to. Controlling civilians at scale requires coercion. This logic is militarily rational. It is also humanly catastrophic.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Crucial Point

The tactics that follow in this lecture ‚Äî reconcentration camps, food destruction, village burning ‚Äî are not aberrations by rogue soldiers. They are the conclusions of this logic, applied systematically. Understanding that is not the same as excusing it. It means that moral responsibility doesn't rest only with the soldiers who carried out the tactics. It rests with the commanders who ordered them, the officials who approved them, and the politicians who funded a war whose logic made them inevitable.

Before the lecture shows you what the U.S. actually did, it's asking you to reason toward it yourself. That's not an accident. That makes them more disturbing, not less. Evil that follows a logic is harder to prevent than evil that comes from individual wickedness.

This section presents what actually happened ‚Äî not editorial, not interpretation, but the documented record: Senate testimony, court-martial proceedings, Army reports, soldier letters. The evidence is uncomfortable. It is also unambiguous. The goal is not to make you angry at America in the abstract, but to give you specific, verifiable knowledge of what was done ‚Äî so you can think clearly about how it was justified and why it was tolerated.

üìä Numbers That Require Attention

200,000 to 700,000 Filipino civilian deaths. That range is wide because the historical record is incomplete ‚Äî the U.S. military did not keep careful count of civilian deaths, which is itself a form of evidence about how the war was being conducted. For comparison: approximately 4,200 American military personnel died, mostly in combat. The asymmetry is not incidental. It reflects the difference between an organized army with industrial firepower and a civilian population caught between guerrilla fighters and counterinsurgency operations.

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Most Deaths Were Civilian

The majority of Filipino deaths came not from battlefield combat but from famine and disease deliberately caused by U.S. military strategy ‚Äî the destruction of food supplies, the burning of crops and livestock, the forced relocation of civilians into reconcentration zones where disease spread rapidly. This was not collateral damage in the ordinary sense. It was a predictable and predicted consequence of tactics that military commanders knew would cause mass civilian suffering. The numbers are the evidence that the strategy worked as designed.

üéì Historians Weigh In

Reynaldo Ileto and Paul Kramer, The Blood of Government (2006): the racial ideology that governed American counterinsurgency in the Philippines was not incidental to the violence ‚Äî it was structural. The thresholds for acceptable violence were set differently for Filipino civilians than they would have been for white European civilians, and that difference was explicit in military communications.

üìå The Tactics ‚Äî Listed Without Euphemism

  • Village burning: Destruction of civilian homes and structures as collective punishment for harboring or supplying guerrillas
  • Population relocation: Forcing civilians into reconcentration zones ‚Äî controlled areas where food, movement, and contact were monitored
  • Destruction of food supplies: Burning crops, killing livestock, destroying stores ‚Äî deliberate famine as military strategy
  • Water cure torture: Interrogation technique (detailed on the next slide)

‚ö†Ô∏è The Cuba Comparison ‚Äî Read This Carefully

In 1897‚Äì98, American newspapers ran front-page outrage about Spanish reconcentration camps in Cuba ‚Äî General Weyler's policy of forcing Cuban civilians into controlled zones, where they died of disease and starvation by the tens of thousands. That outrage was one of the primary drivers of American intervention in Cuba. By 1901, the U.S. Army was running reconcentration camps in the Philippines using the same logic and producing the same results. Same word. Same practice. Same consequences. Three years later. The hypocrisy is not subtle, and it was documented in real time by American critics ‚Äî who are covered in Section V.

The Water Cure ‚Äî What It Actually Was

Interrogators forced water ‚Äî sometimes contaminated ‚Äî into a prisoner's mouth and stomach through repeated pouring until the abdomen visibly distended. Then they beat or compressed the abdomen to expel it. Then repeated ‚Äî until the prisoner talked or lost consciousness.
  • Not a rogue practice ‚Äî widespread enough to generate Senate hearings, press coverage, official investigations
  • Soldiers testified matter-of-factly ‚Äî some defended it as effective
  • Officers who ordered it: minimal consequences

‚ö†Ô∏è What It Was ‚Äî Described Plainly

The water cure was a torture technique used systematically in the Philippines. Interrogators forced water ‚Äî sometimes contaminated ‚Äî into a prisoner's mouth and stomach through repeated pouring until the abdomen visibly distended. Then they compressed or beat the abdomen to expel it. Then repeated. Until the prisoner talked, lost consciousness, or died. This is not a disputed description ‚Äî it comes from the soldiers who administered it, testifying under oath before the United States Senate.

üìå Why "Not a Rogue Practice" Matters

The water cure was widespread enough to generate Senate hearings, press coverage, and official military investigations. Soldiers who testified described it matter-of-factly ‚Äî some defended it as effective. Officers who knew about it and failed to stop it received mild or no punishment. This is the evidence that the water cure was not the aberration of a few bad actors. It was a practice the institution knew about, tolerated, and in many cases implicitly authorized. When a practice is widespread, documented, and produces no serious institutional consequences, it is institutionally sanctioned ‚Äî whatever the official policy says.

In the Public Record

"Senator: 'Describe the water cure as it was administered.' Witness: 'The men would be laid on their backs and water would be poured down their throats until their stomachs were full. Then pressure would be applied...'"
‚Äî U.S. Senate Hearings on the Philippines, 1902
That testimony is in the public record. It was heard by United States senators. The war continued.

üìñ Read What the Slide Is Doing

The Senate testimony quoted here was heard by United States senators in 1902. They heard soldiers describe, under oath, in plain language, exactly what the water cure involved. The war continued. That three-word sentence at the bottom of the slide is doing a lot of work. It tells you that the decision to continue the war was made with full knowledge of what was happening. Not ignorance. Not a failure of information. Knowledge ‚Äî followed by a choice to continue.

üí° What This Means for Moral Responsibility

This is the slide that makes the "few bad apples" explanation impossible. Bad apple arguments require that the institution didn't know, couldn't have stopped it, or moved swiftly to correct it when it did know. The Senate hearings are evidence that the institution knew. The continuation of the war is evidence that knowledge did not produce correction. The gap between what was known and what was done is itself a moral and historical fact ‚Äî one that this lecture asks you to sit with rather than resolve too quickly.

üí• A Specific Anchor in the Record

On September 28, 1901, Filipino fighters carried out a surprise attack on American soldiers eating breakfast at Balangiga ‚Äî killing 48, the deadliest Filipino attack of the war. The U.S. military response came from General Jacob Smith, whose orders are in the historical record verbatim: "I want no prisoners. I wish you to kill and burn. ‚ö†Ô∏è What Those Orders Mean

Smith's orders were not a threat or a figure of speech. They were operational instructions that were carried out. The interior of Samar was systematically devastated ‚Äî villages burned, population killed or displaced, the island described in military reports as a "howling wilderness." Smith was subsequently court-martialed ‚Äî but his punishment was a mild reprimand and forced retirement with full honors. This is where the analysis gets harder. Smith is easy to condemn as an individual. The more important question is what institutional conditions made his orders possible ‚Äî and what the mild punishment reveals about those conditions. An institution that court-martials an officer for saying "kill everyone over ten" but then retires him with full honors has told you something very specific about what it actually tolerates. The punishment is not the end of the analysis. It's the beginning of it.

üí° Understanding Context Is Not Excusing

Explaining why atrocities occurred on both sides ‚Äî the desperation of a resistance movement facing industrial firepower and collective punishment, the rage that follows watching your village burned ‚Äî is not the same as excusing them. It is, in fact, the only way to understand them honestly. A history that condemns only one side's atrocities and ignores the other's is propaganda, not scholarship. A history that treats all atrocities as equivalent regardless of scale and context is also dishonest. The analytical goal is precision: what happened, why, at what scale, and with what institutional consequences.

‚è∏ Pause & Reflect

The U.S. condemned Spain for reconcentration in Cuba in 1898 ‚Äî then used it in the Philippines in 1901.

What does this tell us about how imperial powers understand their own actions?

ü§î The Hypocrisy Is Exact ‚Äî That's What Makes It Worth Analyzing

The United States condemned Spain for reconcentration in Cuba in 1897‚Äì98. American newspapers ran front-page outrage. That outrage helped drive the country into war. Three years later, the U.S. Army was running reconcentration camps in the Philippines using the same tactic, producing the same mass civilian deaths. Same word. Same practice. Same consequences.

The question isn't just "isn't that hypocritical?" ‚Äî of course it is. The interesting question is: what mechanism allows people to see the same practice as an atrocity when the enemy does it and a military necessity when we do it? The answer the lecture is building toward involves racial ideology ‚Äî the dehumanizing logic that sets different thresholds for acceptable violence against different people. When Filipinos are "insurgents" rather than soldiers, when they are "not yet ready for self-government," the same tactics that were outrageous against Cubans become administratively normal. That's the mechanism worth articulating.

Section IV

The Language of Empire

Making violence sound reasonable ‚Äî and believing it.

üìñ The Language That Made Violence Invisible

This section moves from what was done to how it was described ‚Äî and how those descriptions made it possible for Americans to participate in and fund a colonial war while genuinely believing they were doing good. Language isn't just cover for actions. It shapes how people perceive actions, what they're willing to do, and what they're able to see. "Pacification" and "insurgency" and "civilizing mission" are not neutral descriptions. They are arguments ‚Äî arguments that the lecture is about to unpack.

The Same Event, Two Descriptions

Senate Testimony
"Senator: 'What happened to the village?' Soldier: 'We burned it.'"
Official Army Report
"Pacification operations completed in the district of Batangas."
When a government can describe the destruction of a civilian population as "pacification," language itself has become a tool of power.

üìå Two Ways of Saying the Same Thing

A soldier testifies under oath: "We burned it." An official Army report reads: "Pacification operations completed in the district of Batangas." The same event. The same actions. Two completely different moral worlds. "Pacification" is a word that makes violence sound administrative ‚Äî a technical process with a benevolent goal. It removes the fire, the people running, the village that no longer exists. It places the burden of causation on the people being "pacified" ‚Äî implying that the violence was made necessary by their failure to cooperate.

üí° Language as a Tool of Power

When a government can describe the destruction of a civilian population as "pacification," language has become a tool of power. This is not unique to the Philippines or to 1901. Official language in every imperial context performs this function ‚Äî making violence invisible, making coercion sound benevolent, and making resistance sound like the real problem. Learning to read official language critically ‚Äî to ask what it's concealing and what work it's doing ‚Äî is one of the most durable skills this lecture can give you.

üìñ Beveridge Believed What He Said

Senator Albert Beveridge's 1900 Senate speech is worth reading in full ‚Äî it's one of the most articulate expressions of American imperial ideology ever put into the congressional record. He believed in both democracy and civilizational hierarchy simultaneously. He was not lying about his convictions. He genuinely thought that American expansion was a divine mission, that Filipinos were not yet capable of self-government, and that American rule ‚Äî however coercive ‚Äî was ultimately in Filipino interests. Empire was not cynical cover. It was genuine moral conviction.

‚ö†Ô∏è Why Genuine Belief Is Not a Defense

The sincerity of Beveridge's belief doesn't make it less dangerous ‚Äî it makes it more so. Cynical operators who know they're doing wrong can be shamed into stopping. True believers cannot. And Beveridge was not alone: many American Progressives ‚Äî people who supported labor rights, regulation of corporations, conservation ‚Äî also supported the Philippine occupation. The same moral framework that told them government should improve people's lives also told them that some people weren't yet capable of improving their own. Progressive imperialism is not a contradiction. It is a coherent worldview with horrifying consequences.

üñºÔ∏è Look at the "School Begins" Cartoon

The 1899 Puck cartoon "School Begins" depicts Uncle Sam as a schoolteacher with new pupils ‚Äî the Philippines, Cuba, Puerto Rico, and Hawaii ‚Äî represented as children of color, while white children (representing existing states) sit at their desks looking on. Native Americans and Chinese students sit in the back. The cartoon was published approvingly. It captures the civilizational hierarchy that animated American imperial ideology ‚Äî the assumption that some peoples were children who needed to be taught, and that the United States had both the right and the duty to teach them.

The Connected Color Lines

The racial language used to describe Filipinos ‚Äî "children," "not ready for self-government," requiring firm guidance ‚Äî was borrowed directly from the domestic language used to justify Jim Crow.
  • The overseas color line and the domestic color line were not separate systems
  • They were the same system, deployed in two different places
  • Racial hierarchy lowered thresholds for acceptable violence ‚Äî different rules for "not fully civilized" peoples

üîó One System, Two Theaters

This is one of the most important analytical points in the lecture: the racial language used to justify governing Filipinos ‚Äî "children," "not ready for self-government," requiring firm guidance ‚Äî was not invented for the Philippines. It was borrowed directly from the domestic language used to justify Jim Crow. The same framework that told white Americans that Black citizens needed to be supervised, disenfranchised, and denied full civil participation also told them that Filipinos needed to be governed rather than liberated. The overseas color line and the domestic color line were not separate systems. They were the same system deployed in two different places.

üí° The Practical Consequence

Racial hierarchy did something specific and measurable in the Philippines: it lowered the threshold for acceptable violence. Different rules applied to people constructed as "not fully civilized." This is why Jacob Smith's orders could be issued, carried out, and met with a mild reprimand. And it's why Black American critics of the war ‚Äî covered in Section V ‚Äî saw the connection so clearly: they were living under one version of the same logic that was being applied to Filipinos. The overseas and domestic systems were not analogous. They were continuous.

üìñ The Argument ‚Äî Stated Precisely

Roosevelt's position on the Philippines had a coherent internal logic: liberty requires order; order requires force; force in service of freedom is regrettable but necessary; Filipino resistance to American rule is therefore not political self-determination but criminality ‚Äî an obstacle to the very freedom it claims to pursue. This is the argument of "liberal imperialism" ‚Äî the claim that coercion in the short term serves freedom in the long term, and that the occupying power is the legitimate judge of when the short term ends.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Problem With the Logic

Roosevelt's framework has no limiting principle. "Not yet ready for self-government" can always be extended. The timeline for when coercion becomes liberty can always be pushed forward. And the occupying power ‚Äî which benefits from the occupation ‚Äî is simultaneously the judge of when the occupation has achieved its stated purpose. The Canal Zone, acquired in 1903 under Roosevelt, ran until 1999. "Temporary" in imperial logic tends to mean "as long as we find it useful." The anti-imperialists understood this. That's why they called it what it was.

Individual bad actors ‚Äî cynics and true believers alike ‚Äî can be identified, removed, prosecuted. Institutional normalization doesn't require individual bad intent. It just requires that the institution's incentives, language, and procedures make certain questions hard to ask. And citizens who don't ask ‚Äî who consume the official language without interrogating it ‚Äî are the condition under which institutional normalization persists. That's the question this lecture is really asking: what allows democratic citizens to fund and support a colonial war while believing they're doing good?

Section V

The Anti-Imperialist Challenge

Empire was contested at home ‚Äî seriously, publicly, by people whose names you know.

üìñ Empire Was Contested ‚Äî Seriously

It would be easy to read the history of the Philippine-American War as a story with only perpetrators and victims. This section corrects that. There were Americans who said no ‚Äî publicly, loudly, with sophisticated arguments ‚Äî and some of their names are ones you know. The anti-imperialist challenge wasn't a fringe position. It was a serious political movement that included former presidents, major industrialists, prominent writers, and Black journalists who understood the connection between overseas imperialism and domestic racial hierarchy better than almost anyone.

üìã Who Said No

The Anti-Imperialist League, founded in 1898, was ideologically diverse in ways that are worth noting. Mark Twain was a satirist who attacked imperial ideology with devastating precision. Andrew Carnegie was one of the richest men in America and offered to personally buy the Philippines its independence. Grover Cleveland was a former president who found empire incompatible with republican principles. Booker T. Washington and Ida B. üí° The Core Argument

The anti-imperialists' central claim was not simply that the war was brutal ‚Äî though it was. Their argument was constitutional and philosophical: governing without consent corrupts the democracy doing the governing, not just the people being governed. Empire requires practices ‚Äî arbitrary detention, military tribunals, denial of civil liberties ‚Äî that are incompatible with republican self-government. You cannot maintain those practices abroad indefinitely without them migrating home. That argument was not resolved in 1902. It is still being made.

Black American critics of the Philippine War had a specific and uniquely powerful argument: they were living under the same racial logic being applied to Filipinos. David Fagen ‚Äî a Black corporal in the 24th Infantry ‚Äî crossed over to fight with Aguinaldo's forces in 1899 and continued fighting for nearly two years. His decision was not random. Black soldiers in the Philippines had been ordered to enforce on Filipinos the same racial subordination they were subjected to at home. Some refused. Fagen's defection was the most dramatic expression of a recognition many Black soldiers shared: that the color line being drawn in the Philippines and the color line being drawn in the American South were the same line, drawn by the same hand, for the same reasons.

Twain's Satire: Taking It at Its Word

"We have pacified some thousands of the islanders and buried them; destroyed their fields; burned their villages, and turned their widows and orphans out of doors; furnished heartbreak by exile to some dozens of disagreeable patriots; subjugated the remaining ten millions by Benevolent Assimilation..."
‚Äî Mark Twain, "To the Person Sitting in Darkness," 1901

‚úçÔ∏è How Satire Works as Historical Evidence

Mark Twain's "To the Person Sitting in Darkness" (1901) is one of the most effective pieces of anti-imperial writing in American history ‚Äî and understanding why it works helps you understand what made imperial ideology so vulnerable to this kind of attack. Twain doesn't argue against empire in the abstract. He takes the official language of "Benevolent Assimilation" completely seriously and applies it directly to what was actually happening. The result is devastating because Twain is not exaggerating. He is summarizing the documented record.

üí° What Makes It Devastating

The passage on the slide ‚Äî "we have pacified some thousands of the islanders and buried them" ‚Äî works because every word in it is defensible. The islands were being "pacified." People were being buried. The word "benevolent assimilation" was Roosevelt's own phrase. Twain's technique is to put the official language next to what it was describing, and let the gap do the work. When "Benevolent Assimilation" means burning villages and killing people who bear arms ‚Äî and "capable of bearing arms" means males over ten ‚Äî the language has become its own indictment. Section VI

The "Order vs. Liberty" Stress Test

This is synthesis ‚Äî not optional.

üìñ This Is the Synthesis ‚Äî Don't Skip It

The final section is where the lecture's argument comes together. Everything you've seen ‚Äî Filipino political agency, counterinsurgency logic, documented atrocities, the language of empire, the anti-imperialist challenge ‚Äî converges on four questions that the United States faced in 1899 and never fully resolved. These questions don't have clean answers. That's not a failure of the lecture ‚Äî it's the point. Some of the most important political questions are ones that endure precisely because they resist easy resolution.

Four Questions ‚Äî No Easy Answers

  • Can liberty be postponed? If so, who has the authority to postpone it?
  • Who decides when order is sufficient for freedom to begin?
  • How long does "temporary" rule usually last?
  • When soldiers commit atrocities within a system that institutionally tolerates them ‚Äî where does individual moral responsibility end and structural responsibility begin?
These are the actual political and moral questions the United States faced in 1899 and never fully resolved.

‚ùì Four Questions Worth Taking Seriously

These are not rhetorical questions designed to produce predetermined answers. They are the actual political and moral questions the United States confronted in 1899 ‚Äî and they are still live questions today.

  • Can liberty be postponed? If so, who has the authority to postpone it ‚Äî and on what grounds? Note that the people whose liberty is being postponed are never the ones deciding.
  • Who decides when order is sufficient for freedom to begin? In every case of liberal imperialism, the answer has been: the occupying power. That is a structural problem, not an individual one.
  • How long does "temporary" rule usually last? The Canal Zone: 1903‚Äì1999. The Philippines: formal independence not until 1946. "Temporary" in imperial logic is an elastic concept.
  • Where does individual moral responsibility end and structural responsibility begin? This is the hardest question. Individual soldiers carried out torture. Individual commanders issued orders. Individual senators heard testimony and continued funding the war. At what point does the structure ‚Äî the institution, the ideology, the democratic citizenry ‚Äî become the responsible party?

What Occupation Reveals About Empire

  • Empire requires sustained coercion at scale
  • Moral language does not eliminate violence ‚Äî it makes violence administratively normal
  • Democratic states can practice colonial rule while genuinely believing they are doing good
  • Colonial wars brutalize all participants ‚Äî because brutalization is structural, not merely individual
  • Wars for liberation can become wars for control without anyone formally deciding to change course

üî≠ Five Things This War Teaches

  • Empire requires sustained coercion at scale. There is no version of governing without consent that doesn't eventually require force ‚Äî and force at the scale of an archipelago of 8 million people is not a brief intervention. It is a commitment.
  • Moral language does not eliminate violence ‚Äî it makes violence administratively normal. "Pacification" doesn't reduce the burning. It makes the burning easier to fund and authorize.
  • Democratic states can practice colonial rule while genuinely believing they are doing good. Beveridge was sincere. Roosevelt was sincere. That sincerity was not a defense ‚Äî it was a mechanism.
  • Colonial wars brutalize all participants. Not equally ‚Äî the occupied suffer far more than the occupiers ‚Äî but the dehumanization is structural and spreads through the conflict in both directions.
  • Wars for liberation can become wars for control without anyone formally deciding to change course. The slide from "freeing Cuba" to "governing the Philippines" happened through a series of decisions that each seemed locally reasonable but added up to something none of their architects had explicitly chosen.

Lecture 3 turns to Roosevelt as president ‚Äî and the question it's going to ask is whether the same man who ordered the Philippines "pacified" and presided over the documented record of this lecture is also the man who busted the trusts, created the national parks, and pushed the regulatory state into existence. The Philippines was the laboratory. The regulatory state was the experiment running simultaneously at home. Roosevelt's presidency is where those two projects met ‚Äî and the friction between them reveals something important about what Progressivism was and what it could not fully see about itself.

The First Philippine Republic, established under the Malolos Constitution ratified in January 1899. The constitution provided for a unicameral legislature, separation of church and state, and civil liberties. Emilio Aguinaldo served as president. The republic was fighting for survival, not aspiring toward some future capacity.

Reconcentration Zones (Philippines, 1901‚Äì1902)

A counterinsurgency tactic used by U.S. forces in the Philippines ‚Äî particularly in Batangas and Samar ‚Äî in which the civilian population of a region was forcibly relocated into controlled zones, leaving the surrounding area subject to systematic destruction. Food supplies, crops, and livestock outside the zones were destroyed to deny resources to guerrilla fighters. The tactic caused massive civilian deaths from disease and starvation in the camps. The United States had used Spanish General Weyler's identical reconcentration policy in Cuba as a major justification for the Spanish-American War in 1898 ‚Äî branding it the work of a "butcher." The American use of reconcentration in the Philippines just three years later is one of the clearest documented examples of the gap between imperial rhetoric and imperial practice.

Water Cure

A torture technique used by U.S. forces during the Philippine-American War to extract information from prisoners. The procedure involved forcing large quantities of water ‚Äî sometimes contaminated ‚Äî into a restrained prisoner's mouth and throat until the abdomen distended, then applying compression or beating to expel the water, and repeating the process. It was not a rogue practice: it was widespread enough to generate Senate hearings in 1902, extensive press coverage, and official military investigations. Soldiers who testified about it in Senate hearings described it matter-of-factly, and some defended it as effective. The officers who ordered it generally received minimal or no punishment. The Senate hearings produced a public record but resulted in no systematic accountability.

Balangiga Massacre / The Samar Campaign (1901)

On September 28, 1901, Filipino fighters launched a coordinated surprise attack on Company C of the U.S. 9th Infantry at Balangiga on the island of Samar, killing 48 American soldiers ‚Äî the deadliest Filipino attack of the war. The American response, ordered by General Jacob Smith, was the systematic devastation of Samar's interior. Smith's documented orders specified killing all males over age ten, leaving no prisoners, and turning Samar into a "howling wilderness." The campaign killed an undetermined number of civilians through direct violence, displacement, and starvation. Smith was court-martialed, received a mild reprimand, and was allowed to retire with full honors. The three church bells from Balangiga were taken as war trophies and held by the U.S. Army for over a century; they were returned to the Philippines in 2018 after decades of Filipino requests.

Liberal Imperialism

A framework for justifying colonial rule that argues coercion in the short term serves freedom in the long term ‚Äî with the imperial power alone deciding when conditions are sufficient for self-governance to begin. Associated with Roosevelt's public defense of Philippine occupation: liberty requires order; order requires force; force in service of eventual freedom is regrettable but necessary. Critics identified the framework's fatal flaw immediately: it has no internal limiting principle. The timeline for "eventual freedom" can always be extended; the threshold for "readiness" can always be raised. In practice, "temporary" imperial rule routinely became permanent. The U.S. administered the Panama Canal Zone from 1903 to 1999 ‚Äî 96 years ‚Äî under frameworks descended from this logic.
