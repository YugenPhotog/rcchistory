The answer runs through photography. Keep that question in mind as you work through each section.

Three Questions for This Lecture

  • Why did Prohibition fail so visibly — not merely why it failed, but why the failure was so public and so thoroughly documented?
  • How did photography reshape public attitudes toward law and authority?
  • What did Prohibition reveal about class, power, and selective enforcement — and what did ordinary Americans learn from watching it?

📖 Understanding This Slide

These three questions are the spine of the entire lecture. They're worth reading slowly, because they're not asking what you probably expect. Most textbook treatments of Prohibition ask why it failed. This lecture is asking something harder: why it failed so visibly, so photographically, and so unequally. Each question opens a different dimension of the same story.

Breaking It Down

Question 1 — Why so visibly? Plenty of laws fail quietly. Prohibition failed on the front page of every major newspaper in the country, every week, for thirteen years. The question is: what made that visibility possible, and what did it produce? The answer is photography — but not photography as passive documentation. Photography as an active participant.

Question 2 — Photography and authority. This is the lecture's central intellectual move. Before mass photography, a corrupt or unenforceable law could be experienced locally but not distributed. The photograph changed that. A staged raid in Chicago became visible in Kansas City, Memphis, and Portland on the same morning. What did that do to public trust in the law?

Question 3 — Class, power, and selective enforcement. This is the most politically charged question. Prohibition was not enforced equally. The people who got raided were not the same people who drank at private clubs. Photography made that gap visible in a way that statistical reports never could. This question is also the one most directly connected to Category 4 of your assignment.

Section I

The Moral Logic Behind Prohibition

Before continuing, review the background lecture on the temperance movement and the origins of Prohibition.

📖 Ch. 12, Lecture 3 — Temperance & the Origins of Prohibition →

Section I asks you to take the moral case for Prohibition seriously before the lecture starts dismantling the law's enforcement. That sequence is deliberate: you cannot understand what Prohibition's failure meant to Americans unless you first understand what it meant to the people who fought for it.

Before You Continue

The link on this slide connects to the Chapter 12 Lecture 3 study guide on the temperance movement — the deep backstory for everything that follows here. If you're fuzzy on the WCTU, the Anti-Saloon League, or the long arc from antebellum moral suasion to the Eighteenth Amendment, review that material first. This lecture picks up where Lecture 12.3 leaves off and assumes you have that foundation. The slides ahead will review the essentials, but the fuller context lives in Chapter 12.

Why This Matters

A common mistake in studying Prohibition is to treat the temperance reformers as cranks or fanatics whose moral hysteria briefly overwhelmed American common sense. This lecture will not let you do that. The reformers had genuine evidence, sophisticated organizations, and a coherent moral framework that made sense within their cultural world. Understanding that framework is what makes Prohibition's failure analytically interesting rather than just predictable.

Why the 18th Amendment Passed

  • Culmination of nearly a century of Protestant temperance organizing — not a sudden invention
  • WCTU (1874) and Anti-Saloon League (1893) built the political machine
  • By 1919, 33 states already had some form of prohibition legislation
  • Reformers genuinely believed moral legislation could change behavior at scale

📖 Understanding This Slide

Prohibition didn't come from nowhere. This slide establishes the organizational and political machinery that made a constitutional amendment banning alcohol possible — and it asks you to respect that machinery rather than dismiss it. The woman in the photograph, Frances Willard, built one of the most effective political organizations in nineteenth-century American history.

Breaking It Down

Nearly a century of organizing. The Eighteenth Amendment was ratified in January 1919, but the movement that produced it had been building since at least the 1820s — through the American Temperance Society, the Maine Law of 1851 (the first statewide prohibition), and successive waves of organizing that deepened after the Civil War. Prohibition was not an aberration. It was the culmination of an unusually long and disciplined political campaign.

The Woman's Christian Temperance Union (WCTU, 1874) was the organizational engine of that campaign. Under Frances Willard's leadership (1879–1898), the WCTU grew to between 150,000 and 500,000 members and became one of the largest women's organizations in American history. Willard's genius was connecting temperance to women's broader civic rights — arguing that women, who bore the domestic consequences of male drinking most directly, had both the authority and the obligation to demand legal prohibition. The WCTU trained a generation of women in the tactics of legislative lobbying, petition campaigns, and public moral argument — tactics that fed directly into the suffrage movement.

The Anti-Saloon League (ASL, 1893) brought explicit political machine logic to the cause. Where the WCTU mobilized moral sentiment, the ASL translated that sentiment into legislative majorities — electing dry candidates, defeating wet ones, building state-level victories into federal momentum. By 1919, 33 states had already enacted some form of prohibition legislation. The ASL didn't need to convert a skeptical Congress; it needed to finish a job that was mostly done.

Reformers genuinely believed moral legislation could work. This is the crucial point. The Progressive Era had demonstrated that legislation could regulate food safety, break up monopolies, and protect workers. Prohibition was the high-water mark of that confidence — the belief that law could engineer social improvement at scale. The reformers were not naive; they had watched state-level prohibition reduce drinking. They did not anticipate how urban political culture, ethnic diversity, and organized crime would respond to a federal mandate.

Why This Matters

The photograph of Frances Willard is doing work here. She is composed, respectable, authoritative — exactly the visual opposite of the chaotic enforcement imagery that will fill the later sections of this lecture. The contrast is the point: the movement that produced Prohibition presented itself with moral dignity. What happened to that dignity when the enforcement photographs started arriving on the front pages is the lecture's central question.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Daniel Okrent – Last Call: The Rise and Fall of Prohibition

Okrent describes the Anti-Saloon League as arguably the most effective single-issue pressure group in American history to that point. What he means is that the ASL succeeded not by converting its opponents but by making the political cost of opposing prohibition higher than the cost of supporting it — a strategy that worked at the state level for thirty years before it reached Congress. Understanding the ASL as a political machine rather than a moral crusade helps explain both why Prohibition passed and why its enforcement was so immediately compromised by the same political dynamics that had produced it.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Reform photographs were designed to produce an emotional argument. In what ways is an enforcement photograph designed to do the same thing — just for a different purpose and a different audience?

📖 Thinking Through This Question

This question is asking you to make a comparison — and the comparison has a slightly surprising answer. The reform photograph and the enforcement photograph look completely different on the surface: one shows suffering families, the other shows agents smashing barrels. But at the level of intent, they are doing the same thing. Both are making a case. Both are selecting what to show and what to leave out. Both are trying to produce a specific emotional response in a specific audience.

Working Toward an Answer

A reform photograph says: look at the damage alcohol causes — the law is necessary. An enforcement photograph says: look at the action being taken — the law is working. Both are arguments about the law's legitimacy. The reform image argues for the law's existence; the enforcement image argues for the law's effectiveness. Different purposes, different audiences (temperance activists vs. newspaper readers), but the same underlying logic: use a carefully chosen image to make a claim about law and authority.

The crucial difference — which the lecture will develop in Section III — is that the enforcement photographs started to fail. Reform photographs could be emotionally powerful even if you weren't sure they were accurate representations. Enforcement photographs were supposed to demonstrate something verifiable: that the law was being enforced. When the gap between the staged performance and the actual reality became too obvious to ignore, the enforcement photographs began to backfire.

Section II

The Reality of Prohibition

A law passed by rural Protestant America — enforced in cities that had no intention of complying

📖 Understanding This Slide

Section II turns from the reformers' ideal to the reality they produced. The tagline on this slide contains the lecture's structural tension in a single sentence: a law passed by rural Protestant America — enforced in cities that had no intention of complying. Prohibition was a legal imposition of one cultural community's values on another. What happens when enforcement runs into that reality is the subject of everything that follows.

Why This Matters

The phrase "had no intention of complying" is doing a lot of work. It's not that city dwellers forgot about the law or found compliance inconvenient. Many of them — especially immigrant communities — regarded the law as a direct assault on their cultural identity, imposed by a Protestant rural majority that didn't drink the same way they did and didn't understand why they did. That perception of illegitimacy is part of what made non-compliance so open, so defiant, and so thoroughly photographed.

The Gap Between Law and Reality

The Law's Ambition
  • 18th Amendment + Volstead Act banned manufacture, sale, and transport of alcohol
  • Prohibition Bureau created within Treasury — chronically underfunded
  • Tens of thousands of agents; tens of millions of drinkers
The Urban Reality
  • Speakeasies multiplied — New York had an estimated 30,000+ by the mid-1920s
  • Bootlegging became an organized industry almost overnight
  • Enforcement was underfunded and systematically corrupted
The gap between ambition and reality was enormous — and the camera was there to document every inch of it.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide establishes the basic structural failure of Prohibition through numbers. The two columns work together to show why comprehensive enforcement was never possible: not because the Bureau was incompetent (though corruption was rampant), but because the math was impossible. You cannot enforce a law that a substantial majority of the urban population is actively violating when you have a fraction of the agents needed to do so.

Breaking It Down

The Volstead Act was the implementing legislation for the Eighteenth Amendment — it defined what counted as an "intoxicating liquor" (anything over 0.5% alcohol) and created the enforcement apparatus. The Prohibition Bureau was established within the Treasury Department, not the Justice Department, which created jurisdictional complications. More importantly, Congress never funded it adequately. Bureau agents numbered in the low thousands for a country with tens of millions of drinkers.

30,000+ speakeasies in New York City alone is not a typo. Estimates vary, but most historians accept that the number of illegal drinking establishments in New York during the 1920s substantially exceeded the number of legal saloons before Prohibition. The law had not reduced access to alcohol — it had reorganized it, driven it underground, and connected it to criminal distribution networks. The Prohibition Bureau's entire national force could not have raided 30,000 establishments in a year, let alone shut them down permanently.

Systematic corruption compounded the structural problem. Bureau agents were poorly paid and worked in an environment where bribery was pervasive and politically protected. The historian Michael Lerner documents that New York's enforcement apparatus functioned as a patronage system — raids were tools of political pressure, not law enforcement. The corruption wasn't incidental to the system; it was the system.

Why This Matters

The bottom-line statement on this slide is the key: "the camera was there to document every inch of it." The gap between the law's ambition and the urban reality was not hidden. It was photographed, published, and distributed to newspaper readers across the country. The camera did not create the gap — but it made the gap a public, shared experience rather than a local, private one. That transformation is what makes Prohibition significant for this lecture's argument.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Michael Lerner – Dry Manhattan: Prohibition in New York City

Lerner's focus on New York reveals something that national accounts of Prohibition tend to miss: the enforcement apparatus was not simply failing — it was being deliberately managed to appear to function while actually serving political and financial interests that had nothing to do with sobriety. Commissioner Grover Whalen understood that the political viability of Prohibition depended less on actual compliance than on the maintenance of a credible performance of official effort. Lerner's argument is that the performance was the policy.

Two Americas, One Law

Wet Cities
  • Large immigrant populations for whom wine and beer were cultural staples
  • Democratic political machines resistant to federal enforcement
  • Speakeasy culture openly visible; enforcement openly negotiable
Dry Rural America
  • Protestant evangelical communities where the law reflected genuine moral consensus
  • Compliance considerably higher — but not absolute
  • Moonshining persistent in rural South and Appalachia
Prohibition imposed one community's moral framework on another. Photography made that imposition's failures visible — nationally, daily.

Why would an immigrant community's newspaper cover a Prohibition-era raid differently than a mainstream metropolitan paper?

  1. It would use fewer photographs
  2. It would frame the raid as enforcement against its own community rather than evidence that the law was working
  3. It had no interest in Prohibition politics
  4. It would praise the Bureau agents more warmly

📖 Thinking Through This Question

The correct answer is B. This question is testing whether you can apply the cultural analysis from slides 8 and 9 to a specific scenario. Let's work through why each option is right or wrong.

Working Through the Options

A — "It would use fewer photographs." Wrong. There is no reason to think an ethnic newspaper would use fewer photographs than a mainstream paper. Photographs were essential to newspaper sales across all publications. This answer mistakes a content difference for a technical one.

B — "It would frame the raid as enforcement against its own community." Correct. An Italian-language newspaper covering a raid on an Italian neighborhood is writing for readers who live in that neighborhood, may know the people arrested, and regard the raid as an act of cultural aggression rather than legitimate law enforcement. The same event — the same agents, the same arrests, the same photographs — tells a completely different story depending on whose community is in the frame and who is reading the account. This is what the lecture means when it says photography's meaning depends on publication context.

C — "It had no interest in Prohibition politics." Wrong and the opposite of true. Ethnic communities were intensely interested in Prohibition because they were its primary targets. The Chicago Defender, the Italian Tribune, and other ethnic papers covered enforcement extensively precisely because it affected their readers most directly.

D — "It would praise the Bureau agents more warmly." Exactly backwards. An ethnic newspaper covering a raid on its own community would be far more critical of agents than a mainstream paper — often characterizing enforcement as discriminatory targeting rather than neutral law application.

The Bigger Point

This question is actually a preview of Category 4 in your assignment — Selective Enforcement. If you find a photograph published in an ethnic or community newspaper, the publication context is part of the primary source. It changes what the image means. The lecture will develop this argument fully in Section V.

Section III

Enforcement Theater

How a law that could not be enforced learned to perform enforcement for the camera

📖 Understanding This Slide

Section III is where the lecture's central argument comes into focus. Section I established that reformers had genuine moral logic. Section II established that enforcement was structurally impossible. Section III asks: so what did enforcement actually do, if it couldn't actually enforce the law? The answer is that it performed enforcement — for the camera, for the newspapers, for a public that needed to believe the law was being upheld. This is the most directly relevant section for your assignment's Paragraph 3.

A Concept to Hold Onto

The tagline on this slide — "a law that could not be enforced learned to perform enforcement" — is the lecture's sharpest formulation of its core claim. Keep it in mind as you work through this section. The key word is perform: a theatrical term, not a legal one. The Bureau wasn't enforcing the law. It was staging a performance for an audience, and the camera was the instrument through which the performance reached that audience.

The Staged Raid

  • Prohibition Bureau agents routinely tipped off reporters and photographers before raids
  • Confiscated barrels and bottles arranged for maximum visual effect; agents posed with axes raised
  • Front-page photographs communicated official seriousness — without significantly disrupting the flow of illegal alcohol
  • Historian Michael Lerner: New York enforcement organized around managing appearances, not achieving compliance
A raid without photographers was a private act. A raid with photographers was a public claim about the law's authority.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide lays out the mechanics of how Prohibition enforcement actually worked — not in theory, but in practice. The staged raid was the primary instrument of enforcement theater, and its mechanics were understood by everyone involved: the agents, the photographers, the newspaper editors, and, eventually, the readers. What is remarkable is not that the staging happened, but how completely it was organized around producing a photographic result.

Breaking It Down

Tipping off reporters before raids. This was standard operating procedure, not an occasional accommodation. Reporters and photographers received advance notice of significant raids so that press coverage could be coordinated with the enforcement action. In some cases, photographers arrived before the agents; in others, they arrived together. The press presence was built into the operational plan because the press coverage was the point.

Arranging confiscated materials for visual effect. Barrels were stacked to look impressive; bottles were arranged in rows; agents posed with raised axes at the moment of maximum photogenic drama. None of this was candid documentation. It was staging — identical in its logic to the composition of a formal portrait or an advertisement. The agents understood they were creating an image, not documenting an action.

Front pages without actual disruption. Here is the key irony: the raids that generated the most dramatic front-page photographs were often the ones that had the least effect on actual alcohol availability. The establishments raided were often back in operation within days, having paid the necessary fines and bribes. The photograph, once published, had served its purpose regardless of what happened afterward.

Michael Lerner's argument — that New York enforcement was organized around "managing appearances, not achieving compliance" — is the historical foundation for everything on this slide. His research in Dry Manhattan (2007) shows that Commissioner Grover Whalen ran the enforcement apparatus as a public relations operation, selecting targets based on their photogenic and political value rather than the severity of their violations.

Why This Matters

The bottom line on this slide is the lecture's most quotable formulation: "A raid without photographers was a private act. A raid with photographers was a public claim about the law's authority." This sentence draws the sharpest possible line between enforcement as action and enforcement as performance. For your assignment: when you look at an enforcement photograph, you are not looking at documentation of a law being enforced. You are looking at the performance itself.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Michael Lerner – Dry Manhattan: Prohibition in New York City

Lerner's central contribution is showing that the corruption and theatricality of Prohibition enforcement were not bugs in the system — they were features. The political economy of Prohibition enforcement in New York created incentives for managed appearance rather than actual compliance. Officials who achieved compliance would have eliminated the bribery revenue that sustained the entire arrangement. The performance of enforcement was what kept the system profitable for everyone in it — agents, politicians, bootleggers, and newspapers alike.

  • Newspapers weren't reporting on events — they were participants in a collaborative performance
  • The audience: the general public, being asked to believe the fiction that Prohibition was being enforced
  • The staging was understood by everyone involved — the fiction was collaborative

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide takes the argument one step further than the previous one. The staged raid slide described what happened. This slide asks why it worked — and the answer is more interesting than simple deception. The staging was not a secret. The agents knew they were posing. The photographers knew they were shooting a performance. The newspapers knew they were publishing staged images. And yet the performance worked, for years, because all of the participants had reasons to sustain it. The fiction was collaborative.

Breaking It Down

Agents producing evidence of their own legitimacy. This is the most analytically important point on the slide. The agents who arranged barrels and posed with axes were not primarily trying to deceive newspaper readers. They were producing the documentary evidence that their superiors, their political patrons, and their own institutional records required. The photograph was not incidental to the enforcement action — it was the enforcement action, in the sense that it was the product that the system needed to generate.

Newspapers as participants, not reporters. A newspaper that publishes a staged enforcement photograph is not simply reporting on an event. It is choosing to distribute a claim — the claim that the law is being vigorously enforced — to its readership. Newspapers had their own reasons to sustain that claim: it was good copy, it served their relationships with official sources, and it gave readers the drama they expected. The newspaper's decision to publish without questioning the staging made it a participant in the performance rather than a neutral observer.

The collaborative fiction. The most sophisticated point on this slide is the one that sounds almost paradoxical: the fiction worked precisely because it was collaborative and because everyone knew it. There was no exposé waiting to be written, because the staging was an open secret. What eventually undermined the performance was not a revelation of its artificiality — everyone already knew — but the accumulation of enough evidence that the gap between the performance and reality had become too large to sustain even symbolically.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

If both the photographers and the agents knew the raid was staged for the camera — and if the newspapers knew it too — what kept the performance working for as long as it did?

📖 Thinking Through This Question

This is one of the most interesting questions in the lecture, because it has no single clean answer. It is asking you to think about how a shared fiction sustains itself — which is a genuinely difficult problem in social analysis. A few different threads are worth considering.

Working Toward an Answer

Everyone benefited from the performance continuing. Bureau agents got their salaries and their public legitimacy. Politicians got cover — they could point to enforcement activity without disturbing the patronage networks that actually governed who got raided. Bootleggers got a predictable and manageable enforcement environment in which the rules (pay the right people, avoid the wrong attention) were stable. Newspapers got compelling front-page photographs. Readers got the drama of the law at work. The collaborative fiction persisted because dismantling it was in no one's immediate interest.

Audiences wanted to believe it. A segment of the American public — particularly in rural Protestant communities — genuinely wanted Prohibition to succeed and were willing to accept the performance as evidence that it was working. They were not deceived so much as they brought confirming expectations to the images. A staged raid photograph, to a reader who believed in the law, looked like vigorous enforcement. Skepticism requires a prior reason to doubt.

What finally broke the performance. By the late 1920s, the accumulation of evidence — the open operation of thousands of speakeasies, the celebrity of gangsters, the obvious corruption of enforcement — had made the gap between the performance and reality too large to sustain even for willing audiences. The Wickersham Commission report of 1931, which formally documented the failure of enforcement, marked the moment when the official acknowledgment caught up with what ordinary Americans had been reading between the lines of the raid photographs for a decade.

Section IV

Crime, Celebrity, and the Camera

How Prohibition turned bootleggers into public figures — and what that required from photography

📖 Understanding This Slide

Section IV moves from the enforcement side of the photographic record to the criminal side — but the argument is continuous. Section III showed how law enforcement used photography to perform legitimacy. Section IV shows how criminals used the same logic to perform power and status. The gangster celebrity photograph is the enforcement theater photograph's mirror image: both use the camera to construct a public identity that serves the subject's interests.

A Concept to Hold Onto

The tagline — "what Prohibition turning bootleggers into public figures required from photography" — is pointing at something specific. It required that the press treat criminals as celebrities rather than simply as criminals. It required a visual grammar borrowed from entertainment culture rather than law enforcement. And it required an audience willing to find that grammar legible and even appealing. This section explains how all three of those conditions came to exist simultaneously.

The Gangster as Public Figure

  • Bootleggers understood that public visibility was a form of power
  • Al Capone gave interviews, held press conferences, attended sporting events openly
  • His photographs borrowed visual grammar from entertainment celebrity — full face to camera, expensive suit, confident expression
  • The press obliged: found him useful, quotable, visually compelling
📸 Al Capone press photograph · 1930

Al Capone · Press photograph · 1930 · Public Domain · Wikimedia Commons

📖 Understanding This Slide

Look at the photograph of Al Capone on the right side of this slide before you read the bullets. Notice what the image is doing: expensive suit, direct gaze, confident posture, slight smile. This is not a mugshot. It is not a candid. It is a posed portrait that borrows its visual grammar from entertainment celebrity photography — from the movie star portraits and publicity stills that Jazz Age audiences were consuming in fan magazines and newspapers. Capone understood exactly what kind of image he was producing, and he produced it deliberately.

Breaking It Down

Public visibility as a form of power. This is counterintuitive — most criminals try to stay out of the newspapers. Capone had the opposite instinct, and it was strategically sophisticated. As long as his power was publicly acknowledged, it was real power. As long as his fame was intact, he had leverage over politicians, police, and rivals who all knew that acting against him would generate unfavorable publicity. Visibility was protection.

Capone's media management. Al Capone ran the Chicago Outfit's operations from the mid-1920s until his imprisonment for tax evasion in 1931. During this period he gave interviews to major newspapers, attended sporting events in public, held press conferences, and made himself consistently available to photographers. He presented himself as a successful businessman — a provider of services the public wanted, merely caught in an unfortunate conflict with an unenforceable law. Large segments of the press accepted this framing and reproduced it.

Borrowing the visual grammar of entertainment celebrity. The photograph on this slide is doing the same visual work as a publicity still from a Hollywood studio. Full face to the camera, composed expression, expensive clothing foregrounded — these are the conventions of celebrity portraiture. Capone didn't invent this grammar; he recognized it from the entertainment coverage that filled the same newspapers he wanted to appear in, and he adopted it deliberately. The press found him endlessly useful as a source of compelling imagery and quotable copy, and largely obliged by publishing the images he offered.

Why This Matters

Capone's press photographs were a direct commentary on the social order. His wealth was visible. His power over the officials who were supposed to suppress him was openly discussed. He was evidence — in full public view — that the rules applying to ordinary Americans did not apply equally to those with sufficient money and ruthlessness to buy exemption. This made him simultaneously threatening and fascinating, which is exactly the combination that sells newspapers.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Daniel Okrent – Last Call: The Rise and Fall of Prohibition

Okrent traces the cultural logic that made Capone's celebrity possible: Prohibition had made criminals into the primary providers of a service that millions of Americans wanted. In that context, treating a bootlegger as a public enemy was incoherent — he was providing what the law refused to allow and what the public refused to stop demanding. The press celebrity that Capone cultivated reflected a genuine ambivalence in public attitudes. He was criminal and capitalist simultaneously, and the photographs let audiences hold both readings at once.

  • Press portrait: confident, well-dressed, the posture of a successful man
  • Booking photograph: institutional framing — identity, not persona
  • The gap between them is not accidental — it is the work of deliberate visual management

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide asks you to look at two photographs of the same person and notice how radically the visual treatment differs. The press portrait and the booking photograph of Al Capone depict the same human being, but they produce entirely different figures. One produces a celebrity; the other produces a criminal record. The gap between them is not incidental — it is the analytical core of Category 2 in your assignment.

Breaking It Down

The press portrait (previous slide) gives the subject control: he chooses his posture, his expression, his clothing, his relationship to the camera. The result is a persona — a public identity constructed for an audience. Capone's press photographs present a man who is powerful, comfortable with his power, and unafraid of public scrutiny. The visual conventions borrowed from entertainment celebrity tell you how to read him: as a figure of significance, not merely as a criminal.

The booking photograph (CPD, right side of this slide) removes all of that control. The subject's position is dictated by the institution. The lighting is flat and functional. The purpose is identification, not impression. A booking photograph is not trying to produce an appealing image — it is producing a record. The subject's identity is reduced to a set of measurements and reference numbers. The same man who looked powerful and confident in the press portrait looks like a processing number in the booking photograph.

Deliberate visual management. The gap between these two images was not accidental. It was the product of two different institutions with two different purposes making two different choices about what to put in front of a lens. Capone managed his press image as carefully as any Hollywood star. The CPD managed its booking photographs as institutional records with no interest in producing a flattering result. The contrast reveals how completely photographic meaning is constructed by context and intent.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The two film posters on this slide mark the moment when Prohibition-era gangster celebrity crossed from journalism into mass entertainment. Little Caesar and The Public Enemy, both released in 1931 — the same year Capone went to prison — took the visual grammar that press photography had developed for covering real bootleggers and transformed it into genre fiction. The transaction ran in one direction: from the newspaper front page to the movie screen. Hollywood did not invent the gangster. It borrowed him, suit and all, from the press photographs that had been circulating for a decade.

Breaking It Down

Little Caesar (1931) starred Edward G. Robinson as Rico Bandello, a small-time criminal who claws his way to the top of a Chicago gang — and then falls. Robinson's performance borrowed directly from the visual grammar of real gangster press photographs: the controlled expression, the expensive clothing, the projection of power and menace simultaneously. The film was a massive commercial success and established the template for the Hollywood gangster genre.

The Public Enemy (1931) starred James Cagney in a role modeled closely on real Chicago bootleggers. The film's title is itself telling: it uses the law enforcement language of threat and danger while the film's visual treatment of Cagney is unmistakably sympathetic and glamorizing. The audience is invited simultaneously to condemn the character and to find him exciting — which is exactly the ambivalence that Capone's press photographs had been producing for years.

The amplification. Newspaper circulation was large, but film reached audiences of a different scale and with a different emotional intensity. By translating the gangster celebrity image from newspaper photograph to dramatic narrative, Hollywood took a regional and episodic phenomenon and made it a national cultural archetype. The gangster as figure — ambitious, ruthless, stylish, doomed — became one of the defining characters of American popular culture, and his origins are directly traceable to Prohibition-era press photography.

Why This Matters

The bottom line connects the local to the national: "The gangster celebrity that press photography created, Hollywood immediately borrowed — and amplified to a national audience." This is worth pausing on. Prohibition was supposed to create a more orderly, sober, morally elevated public culture. Instead it created the American gangster as a cultural hero — a figure whose wealth, power, and style were products of the law's failure. That figure is still with us.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide introduces the racial dimension of gangster celebrity — a crucial complication of what might otherwise seem like a straightforward story about colorful criminals and an indulgent press. The celebrity treatment that white ethnic gangsters received was not available to Black criminal entrepreneurs operating in the same economy. This was not because those figures were less significant. Breaking It Down

The parallel economy. Prohibition supercharged existing illegal gambling economies — particularly the numbers game, a form of daily lottery that was a major economic institution in Black urban neighborhoods. The numbers bankers of Harlem were as wealthy and organizationally sophisticated as many of their white bootlegging counterparts, and they were genuine celebrities within their communities.

Stephanie St. Clair — known as "Madam Queen" — is the most historically significant example. A Harlem numbers banker who ran a multi-million dollar policy operation throughout the 1920s, St. Clair was feared, respected, and politically connected within her community. She openly fought the white organized crime figures who tried to muscle into Harlem's numbers business and was a genuine power in the neighborhood. But in the mainstream white press, when she appeared at all, she appeared in arrest records and crime reports — never in the celebrity profile treatment that Capone received for running a comparable organization. The contrast is not incidental. It is the argument.

The press treatment differential. The mainstream white press, when it covered Black criminal entrepreneurs, did so through the institutional lens of law enforcement: booking photographs, arrest records, crime stories. The sympathetic or admiring profile treatment extended to Capone was not extended across racial lines. A Black numbers banker who posed like a celebrity for a press photographer would not have found that image published as a celebrity portrait in the Chicago Tribune or the New York Times.

Why This Matters

The bottom line names this clearly: "The camera's decision about whose face belonged in a press portrait was itself a political act." Editors, publishers, and photographers made that decision embedded in a racial hierarchy that determined which subjects were legible as celebrities and which were legible only as criminals. This slide is a bridge directly into Section V's argument about who got raided and who got left alone.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Capone's press photographs borrowed the visual grammar of entertainment celebrity on purpose. What was he actually communicating to his audience — and why was the press willing to carry that message?

📖 Thinking Through This Question

This question has two parts — what Capone was communicating, and why the press cooperated — and both are worth thinking through carefully. They are related but distinct.

What Capone Was Communicating

On the surface, Capone's press photographs communicated success — that he was a powerful, prosperous, confident man. But the deeper message was more pointed: that he operated with impunity. The suit, the cigar, the direct gaze at the camera were all signals that he had nothing to fear from official scrutiny. A man who posed for press photographs while running a criminal organization worth millions of dollars was not hiding. He was broadcasting the message that the law could not touch him — and for several years, he was right. The celebrity performance was also a demonstration of power.

Why the Press Was Willing

The press had its own interests in the transaction. Capone gave interviews, returned calls, provided access, and generated reliable dramatic copy. In the competitive newspaper market of the 1920s, a source who delivered compelling material consistently was a source worth cultivating. More broadly, the story of a spectacularly successful criminal operating openly in a major American city was more interesting to readers than a story about an obscure criminal who had been quietly arrested. The press was not deceived — it made a rational calculation that Capone's celebrity was good for circulation.

There was also a structural dimension: the press and the bootlegging economy were not entirely separate. Many newspapers were themselves clients of the bootlegging supply chain — their parties, their owners' clubs, their social events were supplied by the same networks Capone oversaw. The lines of complicity were messier than a simple narrative of press credulity suggests.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The two-column layout here is doing the lecture's sharpest analytical work. Put the two lists side by side and read them as a single argument: the people being arrested for making wine in their basements were Italian, Irish, Polish, and Jewish working-class families. The people drinking bootleg Scotch openly at country clubs were wealthy white Protestants. The law was identical for both groups. The enforcement was not.

Breaking It Down

Who was targeted. The Prohibition Bureau and local police concentrated their enforcement activity in working-class immigrant neighborhoods for reasons that were entirely practical from the perspective of officials who needed to appear active while not disrupting the political and financial arrangements that sustained them. Working-class immigrant neighborhoods were politically safe to raid: their residents had less political protection, less access to legal defense, less ability to generate backlash. Their establishments were also physically concentrated and economically vulnerable. A tenement basement wine operation could not afford the same protection as a Fifth Avenue club.

Black-owned establishments in northern cities faced an additional layer of vulnerability. The Great Migration had brought hundreds of thousands of Black Americans to cities like Chicago, Detroit, and New York during and after World War I, and the nightlife economies of neighborhoods like Harlem and Chicago's South Side were economically significant and culturally vibrant. They were also systematically targeted by enforcement in ways that reflected both class vulnerability and racial hierarchy in the enforcement apparatus.

Who was left alone. The Racquet Club, the Union League, the private homes of Park Avenue — these were not raided because raiding them was politically dangerous, financially irrational, and socially impossible within the class structure of the enforcement apparatus. The agents and officials who would have had to conduct those raids moved in overlapping social worlds with their targets, or were directly dependent on them for political support and protection money.

Izzy Einstein and Moe Smith are worth knowing because they illustrate the selective enforcement pattern through comedy — and because their celebrity almost conceals the pattern's significance. Einstein and Smith were Prohibition Bureau agents who became nationally famous in the early 1920s for their elaborate undercover disguises: posing as gravediggers, football players, fishermen, and dozens of other characters to infiltrate illegal establishments. They made hundreds of arrests and generated enormous press coverage. But look at where they operated: their targets were working-class saloons, immigrant neighborhood speakeasies, and small ethnic establishments — not the Manhattan clubs where the wealthy drank openly. Their celebrated career was built entirely on operations in the communities that were politically safe to target. The disguises and the comedy were good copy. The systematic avoidance of elite targets was the operating logic underneath the entertainment.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "this was not occasional or accidental, it was the operating logic of the system" — is the lecture's most direct statement of the selective enforcement argument. This is not a claim that individual agents were consciously racist or class-biased in every individual decision (though many were). It is a structural argument: the incentives built into the enforcement system reliably produced discriminatory outcomes, and the photographic record made those outcomes visible.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Michael Lerner – Dry Manhattan: Prohibition in New York City

Lerner's granular account of New York enforcement shows that the targeting pattern was not the result of individual prejudice so much as institutional rationality within a corrupt system. Officials who raided the wrong targets — politically connected establishments, wealthy patrons — lost their positions. Officials who concentrated raids in immigrant neighborhoods, where political protection was weakest and photographic drama was easiest to stage, advanced. The discrimination was structural, self-reinforcing, and entirely rational from within the system's own logic.

A — "It created the illusion that enforcement was fair." This is close but backwards. The photographic record was itself class-inflected: working-class and immigrant arrests generated the dramatic front-page imagery the press wanted, while elite violations were either not photographed at all or appeared in the society pages without enforcement context.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Middletown (1929) is one of the foundational texts of American sociology, and its value for this lecture is specific: it provides systematic, contemporaneous evidence of what Prohibition actually did to a real American community in real time — not in retrospect, not from a coastal elite perspective, but from within an ordinary midwestern city while Prohibition was still in effect.

Breaking It Down

Robert and Helen Lynd were sociologists at Columbia University who spent eighteen months in Muncie, Indiana in 1924–25, conducting systematic interviews and observations about how ordinary Americans lived. They chose Muncie precisely because it was unremarkable — an industrial city of about 35,000 people with no distinctive features that would make it an outlier. Their goal was to produce a portrait of typical American community life, and what they found about Prohibition is therefore evidence about typical American attitudes, not exceptional ones.

The image on this slide shows Muncie as the Lynds would have found it — an ordinary midwestern streetscape, neither metropolitan sophistication nor rural isolation. Keep this image in mind as you read the next two slides: the political education the Lynds documented was happening in communities that looked like this, not in New York or Chicago.

Prohibition did not eliminate drinking — it glamorized transgression. The Lynds found that drinking in Muncie had gone underground but had not disappeared. More significantly, it had acquired a social valence it had not previously possessed: drinking had become associated with a certain defiance of official authority that gave it a glamour it had lacked when it was legal and ordinary. The law had unintentionally made alcohol more culturally interesting by making it forbidden.

Why This Matters

Muncie was not New York or Chicago. It was not an immigrant city with pre-existing reasons to regard Prohibition as cultural imposition. It was the kind of community the temperance reformers were trying to protect. The fact that Prohibition's political corrosion was visible even here — documented by systematic social science observation — is the lecture's strongest evidence for the broad scope of the damage.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Robert S. Lynd and Helen Merrell Lynd – Middletown: A Study in Modern American Culture (1929)

The Lynds were not writing about Prohibition specifically — they were writing about how all aspects of American life were changing in the 1920s. But their observations on drinking and law enforcement are among the most revealing passages in the book. They documented a community that knew enforcement was selective, that knew officials could be bought, and that was drawing quiet but durable conclusions about the integrity of public institutions. The sequel, Middletown in Transition (1937), followed up on the same community a decade later and found those conclusions had deepened rather than faded.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The title of this slide is doing important analytical work: "Common Knowledge, Not Secret Knowledge." The point is that what the Lynds documented in Muncie was not hidden information that investigators uncovered — it was the openly circulating knowledge of an ordinary community. People knew. They talked about it. They read about it in their newspapers. The political corrosion that Prohibition produced was not a slow leak from a concealed source; it was a publicly visible, widely acknowledged reality that accumulated in plain sight.

Breaking It Down

Muncie residents knew enforcement was selective. This is the Lynds' key finding. It was not that Muncie residents suspected that enforcement might be uneven — they knew it, with specificity. They knew which establishments operated openly while others were raided. They knew that the decisions about who to target reflected political protection and bribery rather than the law's actual provisions. This was not investigative knowledge; it was common neighborhood knowledge.

Common knowledge through newspapers. The crucial mechanism was the same daily newspaper that published the staged raid photographs. Those photographs communicated official enforcement activity. The society columns on other pages communicated continued elite access to alcohol. The combination — visible in the same newspaper, week after week — created a publicly available archive of evidence that enforcement was selective. Readers did not need to investigate; they only needed to read.

What did law actually mean? The Lynds documented that this knowledge was generating a specific political question in Muncie. Not "should Prohibition be repealed?" — though many residents thought it should — but something more fundamental: if the law is applied selectively depending on your wealth and political connections, if the people sworn to enforce it are openly violating it at private parties, then what is the law's claim on anyone's obedience? This is a question about the legitimacy of law itself, not just about one specific law.

Why This Matters

The erosion the Lynds documented was not confined to attitudes about drinking. The cynicism that Prohibition produced toward the honesty of law enforcement officials, the reliability of political promises, and the equal application of the law extended well beyond alcohol. It was a generalized political education in the gap between official claims and operational reality — and that education was being conducted through the photographic record of Prohibition's failure.

Working Toward an Answer

What the Middletown evidence shows. The Lynds documented that ordinary Americans in an unremarkable midwestern city were drawing political conclusions from a photographic record they encountered in their daily newspapers. Before Prohibition. The reform photography that preceded Prohibition demonstrates the same dynamic running in the opposite direction: carefully constructed images of alcohol's destruction helped build the political consensus that made the Eighteenth Amendment possible. After Prohibition. The lecture's central claim is that the visual vocabulary Prohibition photography created — for reading the gap between institutional claims and institutional practice — outlasted Prohibition itself. Once ordinary Americans had learned to look at an enforcement photograph and ask "is this staged?", "who is being targeted and who is being protected?", "what is absent from this frame?", they were equipped to ask those questions about any subsequent institutional image. The political education that Prohibition photography provided was not specific to alcohol. It was a general training in visual skepticism toward official claims.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the lecture's closing argument, and it is worth reading slowly. The central claim is stated in full: "The camera did not cause Prohibition to fail. It made the failure visible, specific, and nationally distributed — and in doing so, gave ordinary Americans a visual vocabulary for reading the gap between institutional claims and institutional practice that would outlast Prohibition by a century."

Unpacking the Claim

"The camera did not cause Prohibition to fail." This is the lecture's most important qualification. Photography is not the agent of Prohibition's failure. The failure was produced by a combination of factors: an unenforceable law, a structurally corrupt enforcement apparatus, a culturally divided population, and organized criminal networks that were more adaptable than the Bureau. Photography did not cause any of these things.

"It made the failure visible, specific, and nationally distributed." This is photography's specific contribution. Without the camera, Prohibition's failure would have been local, personal, and episodic — experienced by individuals and communities in isolation. With the camera, the failure was published on front pages across the country, accumulating into a national shared record of institutional dysfunction. The scale of that distribution is what gave photography its political power.

"A visual vocabulary for reading the gap between institutional claims and institutional practice." This is the legacy claim — the one that reaches beyond 1933. What Prohibition photography taught ordinary Americans was a way of looking at official images that asked: is this staged? Who is being protected and who is being targeted? What is absent from this frame? These questions did not disappear when Prohibition ended. They became part of how Americans looked at official photography — of law enforcement, of political events, of institutional claims — for the rest of the twentieth century and into the twenty-first.

The closing sentence connects you to the 1920s reader: "you are doing exactly what millions of 1920s newspaper readers were doing every morning." Woman's Christian Temperance Union (WCTU)

Founded in 1874, led from 1879–1898 by Frances Willard. Membership reached 150,000–500,000 by the 1890s, making it one of the largest women's organizations in American history. The WCTU's political mobilization was among the most important antecedents of the suffrage movement, training a generation of women in legislative lobbying, petition campaigns, and public moral argument. Historian Ruth Bordin, Woman and Temperance (1981).

Anti-Saloon League (ASL)

Founded in 1893. Historian Daniel Okrent (Last Call, 2010) describes it as arguably the most effective single-issue pressure group in American history to that point — a machine for translating Protestant moral consensus into legislative majorities state by state, then federally. By the time the 18th Amendment passed, 33 states had already enacted some form of prohibition legislation.

Volstead Act (1919)

The National Prohibition Act, passed October 1919, effective January 1920. Named for its sponsor, Rep. Andrew Volstead of Minnesota. The Act defined what counted as an "intoxicating liquor" (anything over 0.5% alcohol), established enforcement procedures, and created the Prohibition Bureau within the Treasury Department. It was systematically underfunded — Congress appropriated far too little to staff meaningful enforcement against a population of tens of millions of drinkers.

The Ethnic Press and Prohibition

Italian, Polish, Yiddish, and other immigrant-language newspapers covered Prohibition enforcement in ways that looked radically different from mainstream metropolitan coverage. Where the Chicago Tribune might frame a tenement-district raid as evidence of vigorous enforcement, the Italian Tribune or a Polish-language paper would frame the same raid as an assault on its own community. The same image, in a different newspaper context, makes a different argument. Chronicling America (chroniclingamerica.loc.gov) provides access to many ethnic papers from this period — including the Chicago Defender and other Black newspapers that covered the racial dimensions of enforcement in the urban North.
