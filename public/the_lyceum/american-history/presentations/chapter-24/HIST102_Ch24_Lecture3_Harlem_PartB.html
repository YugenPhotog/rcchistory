Section I

The New Visual Economy

The tradition they inherited — and the battlefield they walked into

📖 Understanding This Slide

"The New Visual Economy" — this section establishes why the 1920s were a turning point in the politics of visual representation. The Harlem Renaissance photographers and visual artists were not operating in a vacuum. They inherited a deliberate counter-tradition going back to Frederick Douglass, and they were working against a visual field that had been actively weaponized against Black Americans by Hollywood and mainstream illustrated media. Understanding both sides of that context is essential before the specific figures make full sense.

Frederick Douglass — The Tradition They Inherited

  • Douglass sat for formal portraits more than any other American of the 19th century — including Lincoln and Grant
  • He was not vain — he was conducting a long-running argument
  • The camera recorded reality without the distortions of minstrel caricature or racist illustration
  • Each portrait: a refutation addressed to any Black American who might see it
The Harlem Renaissance photographers knew this tradition. They were continuing it.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Frederick Douglass sat for more formal portraits than any other American of the 19th century — more than Lincoln, more than Grant. That fact is almost always surprising to students who know Douglass primarily as an abolitionist orator and memoirist. The portrait-sitting was a deliberate, decades-long political strategy — one that Douglass himself articulated explicitly in his essay "Pictures and Progress" (1861). He understood that the camera was an argument.

Breaking It Down

"A long-running argument" — Douglass's portraits countered the dominant visual representation of Black Americans in the 19th century, which was defined by minstrel caricature: exaggerated features, comic poses, figures stripped of individual dignity and inner life. A formal photographic portrait of Douglass — in a suit, with a direct gaze, composed and self-possessed — was a factual refutation of that caricature. The camera recorded what was actually there. And what was actually there was a man of obvious intelligence, dignity, and moral seriousness.

"A refutation addressed to any Black American who might see it" — This is the key interpretive point. Douglass's portraits were not primarily addressed to white audiences as proof of Black humanity. They were addressed to Black Americans as models of what was possible — what you could look like, how you could present yourself, what the camera could record about you. That inward-facing audience is as important as any outward one.

Why This Matters

Van Der Zee, sixty years later, was doing exactly what Douglass had done — in a visual economy that had grown enormously more powerful and enormously more hostile. Douglass fought minstrel illustrations in penny papers. Van Der Zee fought Birth of a Nation-era Hollywood distributed to 80 million Americans a week. The tradition is continuous; the stakes had escalated dramatically.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Deborah Willis — Reflections in Black: A History of Black Photographers

Willis's comprehensive history of Black photography places Douglass at the origin of a deliberate counter-visual tradition. She argues that Douglass's self-conscious use of photography as political argument was not an isolated individual choice but the founding act of a tradition that ran continuously through the Harlem Renaissance and beyond. Van Der Zee, in Willis's analysis, is Douglass's direct heir — not by influence necessarily, but by the logic of the same political situation demanding the same kind of response.

The Visual Field They Were Fighting

  • D.W. Griffith's The Birth of a Nation (1915) — technically innovative, enormously popular, and virulently racist
  • Depicted the Ku Klux Klan as heroic defenders of civilization; Black men as predators
  • Woodrow Wilson screened it at the White House; the revived Klan used it as a recruitment tool
  • The NAACP organized protests in cities across the country — and lost
Black photographers and artists were not making aesthetic choices in a neutral space. They were contesting a visual field that had been systematically weaponized against them.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The Birth of a Nation (1915) is essential context for everything that follows in this deck. It was the most technically sophisticated film made to that date — and one of the most consequential acts of racist propaganda in American cultural history. Understanding it clearly, without either minimizing its technical achievement or minimizing its political harm, is the kind of both/and thinking this course asks of you.

Breaking It Down

Technically innovative, enormously popular, and virulently racist — D.W. Griffith pioneered film techniques that became standard: cross-cutting, close-ups, iris shots, tracking shots. The film ran for three hours at a time when most films were under twenty minutes. Its box-office success was staggering. None of that technical achievement is separable from its content: a film that depicted Reconstruction as a catastrophe, Black men as predators, and the Ku Klux Klan as civilization's saviors.

Woodrow Wilson screened it at the White House — Wilson, a Democrat and Southerner who had re-segregated the federal civil service, reportedly said the film was "like writing history with lightning." Whether he said it or not, the story captured something real: the federal government's tacit endorsement of a film that the revived Klan used as a literal recruitment tool. Klan membership went from approximately 2,000 in 1915 to 4–6 million by the mid-1920s. The film did measurable political work.

"The NAACP organized protests and lost" — The NAACP mounted the first major organized campaign against a film in American history, lobbying for bans in city after city. They succeeded in some places, failed in most. The lesson was not lost: legal protest against distributed visual propaganda was slow and often ineffective. The more durable counter-strategy was to produce competing images — which is precisely what the Renaissance did.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "they were contesting a visual field that had been systematically weaponized against them" — is the interpretive frame for this entire section. Van Der Zee's portraits, Aaron Douglas's murals, the photographs in The Crisis — none of these were made in a context where Black visual representation was neutral or absent. They were made in active response to a hostile visual environment. That context transforms what might otherwise look like aesthetic choices into political acts.

80 Million Americans a Week

  • 1920 U.S. population: approximately 106 million
  • Weekly movie attendance: approximately 80 million
  • Same films, same faces, same visual codes — across all regional divides
  • Silent film trained audiences to read a face as a language
For the first time: a genuinely national visual culture

📖 Understanding This Slide

These numbers reframe everything that follows. In 1920, 80 million Americans — out of a total population of 106 million — attended movies weekly. This was not entertainment at the margins of American life; it was the dominant shared cultural experience of the decade. And it was saturated with representations of race.

Breaking It Down

"For the first time: a genuinely national visual culture" — Before film, visual culture was local and regional. The faces you recognized were faces you had met or seen in your town's newspaper. Film changed that completely. By 1920, a film released in New York was seen in Kansas City, in Atlanta, in San Francisco within weeks. The same actors, the same visual codes, the same representations of race — distributed simultaneously across every regional divide. This was unprecedented in human history.

"Silent film trained audiences to read a face as a language" — This is the technical mechanism that made film's racial representations so potent. Without sound, actors communicated entirely through facial expression, gesture, and appearance. Audiences became extraordinarily skilled at reading visual cues. When those cues were systematically coded along racial lines — Black characters telegraphing threat, laziness, or comedy through exaggerated performance — audiences absorbed those codes as fluently as they absorbed language. The racist representation wasn't accidental set decoration; it was the primary communication channel.

Why This Matters

The scale of film's reach puts Van Der Zee's portrait studio in sharp relief. Van Der Zee produced approximately 10,000 photographs over his career. Hollywood was distributing images to 80 million people every week. The asymmetry was enormous. The significance of the Harlem Renaissance's visual program was not that it matched Hollywood's reach — it couldn't. Its significance was that it established a counter-archive, a set of images that persisted in the community's own hands, on its own terms, that Hollywood's machinery could not erase.

Appearance as Argument

  • If appearance was a language — controlling how you appeared was speech
  • The studio portrait: a deliberate statement of social worth
  • "We are people whose lives have occasions worth commemorating"
  • Identity increasingly constituted through how you were seen, not only reputation
This is what the Harlem Renaissance was fighting over

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide makes the theoretical argument that the deck's case studies will illustrate: in a visual culture where appearance functions as language, controlling how you appear is a form of speech. That equation — appearance = speech — is not self-evident. It is a specific historical claim about what the 1920s visual economy had made true. Understanding it is the prerequisite for understanding why Van Der Zee's portraits were political rather than merely decorative.

Breaking It Down

"Controlling how you appeared was speech" — In an era when film had made visual literacy a mass phenomenon, the way a person appeared in an image carried meaning that audiences were trained to read. Clothing communicated class and aspiration. Posture communicated confidence or submission. The direct gaze communicated self-possession or threat, depending on who was looking. All of these visual codes were politically loaded when applied to Black subjects — because the dominant visual culture had spent decades coding Black appearance in specific, demeaning ways.

"We are people whose lives have occasions worth commemorating" — This is the implicit argument of the studio portrait. The very act of hiring a professional photographer, dressing carefully, and posing for a formal image asserted that this person's life — this marriage, this graduation, this family — mattered enough to be commemorated. In a culture that systematically denied the significance of Black lives, that assertion was not trivial.

Why This Matters

The final line — "Identity increasingly constituted through how you were seen, not only reputation" — points toward something characteristic of modern culture: the shift from reputation (what others say about you, built over time through acts) to image (how you appear at a given moment, distributable at scale). This shift, which accelerated through the 20th century and defines social media today, began in the 1920s. The Harlem Renaissance was the first major American movement to grapple with its implications.

The New Power of Visibility

The struggle over representation — who appeared in images, how they appeared, and who controlled those images — acquired a political weight in the 1920s it had not previously carried.
The visual economy of the 1920s made the Harlem Renaissance's artistic program a political program.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide is the section's synthesis statement. The argument it makes is precise: the Harlem Renaissance's artistic program and its political program were the same program, because the visual economy of the 1920s made representation itself a site of political contest. This is not a general claim that all art is political. It is a specific historical claim about a specific moment in which the scale and reach of visual media transformed the stakes of image-making.

Breaking It Down

"The struggle over representation acquired a political weight it had not previously carried" — Before mass visual media, controlling Black representation was primarily a local matter — the illustrations in a local newspaper, the performances in a local theater. Those images mattered, but their reach was limited. By the 1920s, a racist caricature embedded in a film could reach 80 million people in a week. The scale made the stakes qualitatively different, not just quantitatively larger.

Who appeared, how they appeared, who controlled those images — These three questions frame the entire visual section of this deck. Van Der Zee answers the first two directly: his subjects appeared in his portraits as specific, named, dignified individuals. The third question — who controlled the images — is more complicated, and Section III on Aaron Douglas and the Black press addresses it head-on.

Why This Matters

One common objection to framing art as political is that it reduces aesthetic achievement to mere instrumentalism. This course's argument is more nuanced: the Harlem Renaissance's art was genuinely excellent — not despite its political context but often because of it. The conditions that made the work politically urgent also generated the creative pressure that produced extraordinary work. Douglass's portraits are not merely interesting as historical documents; they are visually compelling objects. Van Der Zee's photographs are not merely interesting as political counter-arguments; they are beautiful images. The aesthetic and the political are fused, not in tension.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Douglass understood in the 1860s that controlling your image was a form of power. By the 1920s, Hollywood was distributing racist caricatures to 80 million people a week. What does that gap tell us about the difference between access to a medium and control of one — and why did the Harlem Renaissance's visual program constitute an act of heroic resistance?

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is one of the most conceptually rich questions in the deck. The distinction between access to a medium and control of one cuts right to the heart of how cultural power actually works — and it's a distinction that is highly relevant outside the 1920s. Think about it carefully before moving on.

Breaking It Down

Access vs. control — Douglass had access to photography in the 1860s. So did his enemies. The question of access is almost never the decisive one. The decisive question is: who controls the means of production and distribution? Who decides what gets made, what gets circulated, who sees it, and in what context? Douglass could produce portraits in portrait studios. Hollywood could produce films for 80 million viewers a week. Those are not comparable forms of access.

What the gap tells us — The 60 years between Douglass and the Harlem Renaissance saw the invention of mass visual media and its capture by interests that were indifferent or hostile to Black representation. Black Americans in the 1920s had more access to photography than Douglass did — but they had less control over the visual field as a whole, because that field had grown enormously and was dominated by institutions they did not own or run.

Why the Renaissance's visual program was heroic resistance — Because it built what it could control: Van Der Zee's studio, the images in The Crisis, Douglas's murals in a public library. These were not attempts to match Hollywood's reach. They were attempts to establish a counter-archive — images on Black terms, in Black hands, serving Black audiences — that Hollywood's machinery could not touch. That is heroic resistance: not matching the opponent's power, but building an alternative that the opponent cannot destroy.

Section II

The Portrait as Argument

James Van Der Zee — heroic resistance one portrait at a time

📖 Understanding This Slide

"Heroic resistance one portrait at a time" — the subtitle encodes the scale argument before the section begins. Hollywood's resistance came at industrial scale: 80 million viewers a week. Van Der Zee's resistance came one sitter at a time, over decades. The cumulative archive of approximately 10,000 images that resulted was not the product of an institution. It was the product of one photographer and the community that came to him. That granular, one-by-one quality is part of what makes Van Der Zee's archive distinctive.

The Guarantee Photo Studio

  • Opened 1916, West 125th Street, Harlem
  • Documented weddings, funerals, veterans, families, celebrities
  • Clients came prepared — finest clothes, symbols of achievement
  • Van Der Zee composed, lit, and retouched deliberately
📸 Search: "James Van Der Zee photographer portrait Harlem"
Source: Library of Congress / NGA

James Van Der Zee · Harlem photographer · 1886–1983

📖 Understanding This Slide

James Van Der Zee opened the Guarantee Photo Studio at 109 West 135th Street in 1916 and operated it for decades, producing an archive of Harlem life that has no parallel. He photographed virtually every stratum of Black Harlem: working families, church congregations, veterans in uniform, UNIA parades, jazz musicians, socialites. His studio was not a boutique for the elite — it was a community institution.

Breaking It Down

"Clients came prepared — finest clothes, symbols of achievement" — This is a crucial detail. Van Der Zee's subjects were not passive recipients of his camera. They came to him having thought carefully about how they wanted to appear. The Cadillac brought to the curb for a portrait, the military medal worn prominently, the fur coat carefully arranged — these choices belonged to the subjects. Van Der Zee's role was to honor those choices through his compositional and lighting decisions.

"Van Der Zee composed, lit, and retouched deliberately" — Retouching was standard studio practice, but Van Der Zee used it with specific intent: to maximize luminosity, to smooth surfaces, to create images of beauty that countered the ugliness of racist caricature. He understood that the photograph's formal properties — light, composition, clarity — were themselves arguments. A beautifully lit portrait of a Black subject was, in itself, a refutation of the visual tradition that denied Black beauty.

Why This Matters

Van Der Zee worked for 60 years and his archive was nearly lost. His work came to wide public attention largely through the 1969 Metropolitan Museum of Art exhibition "Harlem on My Mind" — when he was in his 80s. The belated recognition is itself historically significant: an archive built over sixty years, serving a community with genuine dedication, nearly disappeared because it existed outside the mainstream institutions of art history. Its recovery is part of the story.

What the Portraits Were Fighting

  • A century of racist visual culture: minstrel caricatures, Sambo figures, threatening stereotypes
  • These images were not merely offensive — they were arguments
  • Embedded in advertising, entertainment, and political cartoons since the antebellum era
  • They said: Black people are types, not individuals with inner lives
Van Der Zee's counter-argument: specific, named, durable evidence

📖 Understanding This Slide

Understanding what Van Der Zee's portraits were fighting requires understanding the visual tradition they opposed. That tradition — rooted in minstrelsy and extending through film — was not simply offensive imagery. It was a systematic argument, distributed at scale, that Black people were not individuals with interior lives but types: interchangeable figures defined by their racial characteristics rather than their personhood. Van Der Zee's counter-argument was formal and evidential: specific people, named, photographed with dignity, producing a durable record that said: here is a person, not a type.

Breaking It Down

Minstrel caricatures, Sambo figures, threatening stereotypes — The Sambo figure represented Black men as childlike, lazy, and comically submissive. Its counterpart represented Black men as sexually threatening predators — the figure that Birth of a Nation deployed most viciously. These were not incidental images; they were arguments about the nature and capacity of Black people, embedded in the most widely distributed entertainment of the era. They appeared in advertising, on packaging, in films, in illustrated newspapers.

"These images were arguments" — This is worth sitting with. A caricature is not just an offensive drawing; it is a claim. It claims that the caricatured group really is like this — that the exaggeration is revealing, not distorting. When those claims are embedded in entertainment consumed by tens of millions of people and reinforced across decades, they produce a visual common sense that is extremely difficult to challenge directly. Van Der Zee's method was not to challenge the caricatures directly but to flood the zone with counter-evidence.

Why This Matters

"Specific, named, durable evidence" — this three-part formulation captures what made Van Der Zee's archive effective as counter-argument. Specific: not an abstraction about Black humanity but a photograph of this person, in this moment, looking like this. Named: not an anonymous type but an individual with a name and a life. Durable: not a claim that could be dismissed in the morning but a physical object — a photograph — that persisted in the family's possession across decades and generations.

Deliberateness as Strategy

  • Historian Deborah Willis: respectability in these portraits was not assimilation
  • It was a strategic deployment of the visual language of dignity
  • The straight back, the direct gaze, the fine clothes: each a deliberate choice
  • Every print leaving his studio inserted a named individual into the visual record
"We are here, we are composed, we claim the right to be seen as we actually are."

📖 Understanding This Slide

Historian Deborah Willis's argument here is one of the most important interpretive moves in this entire unit — and one that will appear on the MC check. She is making a distinction that is easy to miss: the respectability in Van Der Zee's portraits was not assimilation. Assimilation would mean adopting white standards of appearance and behavior in order to gain white acceptance. What Van Der Zee's subjects were doing was something different — and understanding the difference is the key to understanding the politics of the portrait tradition.

Breaking It Down

"Respectability... was a strategic deployment of the visual language of dignity" — Willis's argument: the straight back, the direct gaze, the fine clothes were not borrowed from white culture as a way of proving Black worthiness by white standards. They were weapons in a counter-argument made in the visual language that white culture had made politically legible. If the 1920s visual economy read "dignity" through certain visual cues — formal dress, composed posture, direct gaze — then deploying those cues strategically was a way of inserting your claim to dignity into a system already structured to read it.

"Every print leaving his studio inserted a named individual into the visual record" — The word named is crucial. Anonymous images can be dismissed as representatives of a type. Named images anchor claims about specific individuals. Van Der Zee's clients were not "a Harlem couple" or "a Black family" — they were the Johnsons, the Smiths, specific people with specific lives. The naming transforms the portrait from illustration into testimony.

Why This Matters

The bottom-line quote — "We are here, we are composed, we claim the right to be seen as we actually are" — echoes the New Negro's posture from Part A almost exactly. Not petition. Assertion. The portrait does not ask to be recognized as dignified; it presents dignity as a fact and dares the viewer to deny it. That assertive quality — the refusal to explain, translate, or apologize — is the connective thread between Van Der Zee's photographs and Hughes's poetry and Garvey's UNIA parades.

📸 Search: "Van Der Zee Couple Raccoon Coats 1932"

James Van Der Zee · Couple in Raccoon Coats · 1932 · © Van Der Zee Archive, The Met

📖 Understanding This Slide

Couple in Raccoon Coats (1932) is Van Der Zee's most famous image and one of the defining photographs of the Harlem Renaissance. Look at it carefully before reading these notes. Notice the luminosity — the gleam of the coats, the shine of the Cadillac, the way light falls across the couple's faces. Van Der Zee composed and retouched specifically to achieve this quality. The light is not documentary; it is an argument.

Breaking It Down

"Taken during the Great Depression, third year" — The image is dated 1932, three years into the economic collapse that had devastated Black Americans first and hardest. Black workers were first fired, last hired; federal relief programs often excluded Black applicants through discriminatory local administration; Harlem's economy was contracting sharply. Into this context Van Der Zee placed a couple in full-length raccoon coats beside a Cadillac V-16. The extravagance was impossible to read as documentation. It was a statement.

"The display of prosperity is not naive — it is argument" — A naive reading of this image would call it aspirational: people dressing above their economic station. The more accurate reading is that the display of prosperity in 1932 was a refusal to accept diminishment — a visual assertion that Black life retained its beauty and dignity even under conditions of economic assault. The raccoon coats are not evidence of actual wealth; they are evidence of the will to project something other than defeat.

"Light as a claim about beauty" — Van Der Zee was a technically sophisticated photographer who understood lighting as a compositional element. His consistent choice to maximize luminosity — to make his subjects glow — was not a neutral stylistic preference. In a visual culture that consistently depicted Black figures in darkness, shadow, and visual subordination, Van Der Zee's light was a counter-claim about whose beauty the camera was capable of recording.

Why This Matters

This image sits in the permanent collection of the Metropolitan Museum of Art. That institutional recognition came decades after Van Der Zee made the image — long after his studio closed and his archive nearly vanished. The photograph's survival and eventual canonization illustrates the argument this deck makes about the Harlem Renaissance's legacy: the work outlasted the conditions that produced it. A photograph made as an act of resistance in 1932 is now one of the defining images of American cultural history.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Van Der Zee's portraits inserted named individuals into the visual record. Why does it matter that the subjects were named — not anonymous — and that the images were durable?

📖 Understanding This Slide

This reflection question asks you to think about two specific properties of Van Der Zee's archive — named and durable — and explain why each matters as a form of counter-argument. These are not decorative distinctions. Each property does specific work against a specific feature of racist visual culture.

Breaking It Down

Why named? The racist visual tradition operated by reducing Black people to types — interchangeable representatives of a category rather than specific individuals. The Sambo figure, the threatening brute, the happy servant — these were types, not persons. A named individual in a photograph resists that reduction at the most basic level. You cannot substitute another person for this specific person without lying. The name anchors the image to an irreducible individuality that the type-logic of racist caricature cannot absorb.

Why durable? Caricatures are ephemeral — a newspaper is read and discarded; a film runs for a season. A photographic print kept in a family album persists across generations. The family of the man photographed by Van Der Zee in 1918 still has that image in 1960, in 1990, in 2026. It continues to do its work — as testimony, as family memory, as historical evidence — long after the caricatures that surrounded its production have been forgotten. Durability is a form of power.

Why This Matters

Together, named and durable produce something that the racist visual tradition could not generate: an archive. Not just individual images but an accumulated body of evidence, stored in community hands, that builds over decades into a counter-record of Black life. That archive is what Van Der Zee built — not through institutional support or critical recognition, but through the simple accumulation of one portrait at a time over sixty years.

Aaron Douglas · Illustrator of the Harlem Renaissance · 1899–1979

📖 Understanding This Slide

Aaron Douglas arrived in Harlem in 1925 and almost immediately became the movement's foremost visual artist. His significance is not simply that he was talented — the Renaissance had many talented artists. His significance is that he developed a distinctive visual language that made a specific argument about Black identity, one that had not existed in American art before him.

Breaking It Down

Silhouette figures — bold, flat, angular, unmistakably Black, unmistakably heroic — Douglas's stylistic choices were not arbitrary. The flat silhouette, borrowed partly from Egyptian visual tradition and partly from the geometric abstraction of European modernism, produces figures that are simultaneously monumental and anonymous in a specific way. They are recognizably Black — the profiles, the body types, the cultural contexts — but they transcend individual identity toward the archetypal. They represent not this person but a people.

West African forms, Egyptian visual traditions, European modernism fused — This synthesis was Douglas's great formal achievement. European modernism in the 1920s was already borrowing heavily from African art — Picasso's engagement with African masks is the most famous example. Douglas reversed that transaction: he was a Black American artist who claimed African visual traditions as his own heritage, not as exotic material borrowed for avant-garde experimentation, and fused them with modernist formal innovations on his own terms.

"Rooted in Africa, oriented toward modernity" — This dual orientation is the visual argument. Douglas's figures are not trapped in a romanticized African past, nor are they stripped of African heritage in pursuit of American modernity. They hold both simultaneously — heritage and futurity, tradition and transformation. That synthesis was itself a political statement about what Black American identity could be.

Why This Matters

When you look at Douglas's work on the NYPL digital collections (Link Slide coming shortly), look specifically for the interplay between geometric abstraction and figurative representation. Notice how the silhouetted figures seem to move despite their flatness — Douglas created a sense of kinetic energy through overlapping forms and diagonal compositions. This visual dynamism is not decorative; it enacts the theme of a people in motion, in transformation, claiming their history and their future simultaneously.

What made them political was the context — a visual economy saturated with racist caricature — and the intention — to insert named individuals into the visual record as counter-evidence to that caricature. The politics lived in the gap between what the dominant visual culture said Black people looked like and what Van Der Zee's camera recorded that they actually looked like.

What made Douglas's murals political: Not simply the use of African imagery (that was also done by European modernists as exotic appropriation). The common thread: Both artists were making claims in a context where those claims were contested. Art becomes political not when it has political content in the abstract but when it argues against something — when the argument it makes has stakes, when someone with power would prefer the argument not be made.

Why This Matters

This question has a contemporary application worth noting. The same debate about when art is "political" continues today. When a film casts a Black actress as a historically white character, is that political? When a museum acquires work by previously excluded artists, is that political? The Harlem Renaissance case suggests: art is political when it contests the existing distribution of visibility and worth in a context where that distribution is maintained by power. Whether any specific instance meets that threshold is an empirical question, not a formal one.

The bottom line — "The music that traveled outward was the surplus product of an internal economy built for Black Harlem first" — inverts the conventional narrative. Savoy Ballroom · 596 Lenox Avenue, Harlem · Opened 1926

📖 Understanding This Slide

The Savoy Ballroom represents the Renaissance's vision of cultural self-determination in its purest institutional form: open to everyone, funded by the community it served, setting its own aesthetic standards through competition among the musicians who played there. It is the direct opposite of the Cotton Club — and understanding the contrast between them is the key analytical move of Section IV.

Breaking It Down

Black-Jewish partnership, open to everyone, 25-cent admission — The Savoy was founded by Moe Gale (Jewish) and Charles Buchanan (Black) in 1926 on Lenox Avenue. The 25-cent admission was deliberately affordable — this was not a venue for the wealthy but for Harlem's working community. "Open to everyone" meant, specifically, open to Black patrons — which the Cotton Club was not. The Savoy's interracial ownership and mixed-race clientele were unusual for the era.

Cutting contests — two bands on facing bandstands, crowd decides the winner — The cutting contest is one of jazz's most distinctive institutional innovations. Two bands played simultaneously or in alternation, each trying to outplay the other, with the crowd's response as the only judge. The aesthetic standard was set horizontally — by musicians competing with each other for the approval of their own community — rather than vertically, toward white commercial taste or critical approval. This is the structural mechanism of self-determination in cultural production.

Why This Matters

"The standard was set by the community it served" — this is the bottom line of the Harlem Renaissance's entire cultural argument, stated in institutional terms. Not: we will produce art that meets external standards and hope it earns recognition. But: we will produce art that serves our own community, judged by our own standards, for our own purposes. The art that results from that orientation turns out — as a matter of historical fact — to be some of the greatest music in American history. Self-determination and excellence turned out not to be in tension. They were, in this case, mutually reinforcing.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Duke Ellington's Cotton Club residency (1927–1931) is the most sophisticated case study in this deck because it demonstrates something counterintuitive: that genuine artistic excellence can convert a structure designed for exploitation into a platform for transcendence. That is not a universal truth — most artists in exploitative situations produce compromised work. Ellington's case is remarkable precisely because he was an exception. Understanding what he did and how he did it is the analytical task of this slide.

Breaking It Down

"Did not simplify his music to serve the 'jungle' aesthetic — used the Club's theatrical requirements as raw material" — This is the key move. Instead of producing simplified, caricatured music to fit the Club's primitivist framing, Ellington took the theatrical requirements — the need for music that evoked atmosphere, created dramatic effects, accompanied elaborate production numbers — and used them as compositional challenges. The "jungle" pieces he wrote were, formally, extraordinarily sophisticated compositions that happened to be titled with primitivist names.

Mood Indigo (1930) — unprecedented three-instrument voicing — Ellington scored this piece for muted trumpet, muted trombone, and clarinet in its lowest register — a combination that produced a sound no one had heard before. The emotional sophistication of Mood Indigo is incompatible with the "jungle" aesthetic the Club was selling. Ellington was simply producing better music than the venue's concept demanded or could contain.

CBS/NBC radio broadcasts — millions of listeners including Black households — The Cotton Club's radio broadcasts were, from management's perspective, a marketing tool for white audiences. From Ellington's perspective, they were a national distribution mechanism for serious jazz that reached Black listeners across the country who could hear him without confronting the Club's racial admission policy. The white institution's commercial instrument became, through Ellington's use of it, a tool of Black cultural dissemination.

Why This Matters

"The instrument of exploitation became the instrument of transcendence — because the artist was good enough and determined enough to make it so." This bottom line is carefully qualified: it was Ellington's specific excellence and determination that converted the exploitative structure. The implication is not that exploitation is acceptable because talented artists can transcend it. The implication is that excellence is a form of agency even inside structures that deny it — and that Ellington exercised that agency to a degree that transformed what was possible in American music. Whether that is sufficient, given the conditions, is what Pause & Reflect IV and the follow-up question will ask you to evaluate.

Section V

The Debate Inside the Movement

Du Bois, Hughes, and Hurston — whose art, for whom, on whose terms

📖 Understanding This Slide

Section V is the intellectual climax of the deck. Everything before it has been building toward this: the debate inside the Renaissance about what Black art should do, for whom, and on whose terms. Du Bois, Hughes, and Hurston gave three different answers to the same question — and all three answers have genuine merit. Your job is not to pick a winner but to understand each position well enough to evaluate it honestly.

The subtitle — "whose art, for whom, on whose terms" — is a three-part question that maps onto the three figures: Du Bois asked whose art (is it propaganda for the race, or something else?); Hughes asked for whom (for Black audiences who don't need translation, or for white gatekeepers who control distribution?); Hurston asked on whose terms (by what standards should Black art be judged, and who sets those standards?).

Du Bois: All Art Is Propaganda

"I do not care a damn for any art that is not used for propaganda. But I do care when propaganda is confined to one side while the other is stripped and silent."
— W.E.B. Du Bois, Criteria of Negro Art, The Crisis, October 1926
  • All art serves some interest, embodies some argument — there is no neutral art
  • The problem: white cultural institutions circulate pro-white arguments as common sense
  • Black counter-arguments have limited distribution — the field is unequal

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide contains one of the most important primary source quotes in the unit — and one that is routinely misread. Read Du Bois's statement carefully before accepting the conventional interpretation. He is not saying that art should be crude propaganda. He is making a sophisticated epistemological argument about what "neutral" art actually is.

Breaking It Down

"I do not care a damn for any art that is not used for propaganda" — The shock value of "propaganda" is intentional. Du Bois is deliberately using a word that sounds disreputable to make a point: all art encodes values, serves interests, and advances arguments — even art that presents itself as neutral or purely aesthetic. The European artistic tradition that American culture treated as the universal standard was itself propaganda for a particular vision of civilization, beauty, and human worth. Du Bois refused to pretend otherwise.

"But I do care when propaganda is confined to one side while the other is stripped and silent" — This is the actual argument. Du Bois is not calling for crude political art. He is calling for a level playing field. White cultural institutions circulate their values as common sense, as beauty, as universal truth. Black cultural production gets classified as "political" or "ethnic" the moment it asserts Black values or Black worth. Du Bois says: if that's the game, play it consciously and deliberately on our side.

"There is no neutral art" — This is the theoretical premise. Every aesthetic choice — what subjects are worthy of art, what forms are legitimate, what counts as beauty — embeds values. European academic painting embedded values about whose face, whose body, whose landscape was worth painting. The claim to neutrality was itself a political move, naturalizing a particular set of values as universal. Du Bois saw through it.

Why This Matters

Du Bois's argument anticipates debates that are very much alive today about representation, about whose stories get told, about whether cultural institutions have political responsibilities. His specific claim — that the problem is not propaganda itself but the unequal distribution of whose propaganda circulates as common sense — is a sharper and more defensible argument than it initially appears. Before deciding whether Hughes and Hurston were right to push back against it, make sure you understand what Du Bois actually said, not a caricature of it.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Ross Posnock — Color and Culture: Black Writers and the Making of the Modern Intellectual

Posnock reads Du Bois's "Criteria of Negro Art" not as a call for didactic political art but as a sophisticated argument about the politics of aesthetics — the way claims about beauty and artistic value always embed social power. He places Du Bois in the tradition of pragmatist philosophy, arguing that Du Bois's insistence on the inseparability of art and politics was a philosophical position, not a tactical one. Understanding Du Bois as a philosopher rather than merely a propagandist changes how you read the essay.

The Control Problem

  • Publishers, galleries, record labels distributing Renaissance work were overwhelmingly white-owned
  • Commercial success = something white gatekeepers chose to distribute — on whose terms?
  • The Cotton Club: Black performers, white audiences — the community's culture sold back at a distance
  • Du Bois: who controls the means of cultural distribution controls the meaning

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide develops the structural critique implicit in Du Bois's propaganda argument. Even if you produce excellent, uncompromising Black art — who distributes it, and on what terms? The control problem doesn't disappear once you decide to trust your own tradition. It reappears at the point of distribution.

Breaking It Down

"Publishers, galleries, record labels — overwhelmingly white-owned" — The Renaissance's artists who achieved mainstream success did so through white-owned institutions: Knopf published Hurston's novels, Columbia Records distributed jazz, mainstream galleries showed Douglas. Each of those transactions involved a white gatekeeper deciding what Black art was worth distributing to a broader audience. That decision-making power shaped what got widely circulated and what didn't.

"Commercial success = something white gatekeepers chose to distribute — on whose terms?" — This is Du Bois's sharpest question. When a white publisher accepts a Black novel, does that validate the novel — or does it mean the novel was legible and acceptable within the publisher's existing framework? Hughes would say the latter is exactly the wrong standard to aim for. Du Bois would say the question of who controls distribution is unavoidable regardless.

"Who controls the means of cultural distribution controls the meaning" — This is a structural argument, not a personal one. It doesn't accuse individual white publishers of bad faith. It argues that the structure of cultural distribution — who has the capital, the platforms, the networks to reach large audiences — shapes what gets produced, what gets recognized, and what gets remembered. Control of distribution is control of the cultural record.

Why This Matters

Du Bois's control problem is the structural context in which Hughes's and Hurston's aesthetic arguments have to be evaluated. Hughes says: trust your own culture, stop seeking white approval. But if the only way to reach an audience beyond your immediate community is through white-owned distribution channels, "stop seeking white approval" may also mean "accept permanent marginality." Du Bois saw that dilemma clearly. His answer — make the art explicitly political so it serves the struggle regardless of who distributes it — is not the only answer, but it is a serious one.

Hughes: Go to the Bottom

  • "The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain", The Nation, 1926
  • The greatest danger: the desire to please white audiences and meet white standards
  • Go to the folk tradition — jazz clubs, rent parties, blues, vernacular — and trust it
  • The racial mountain: wanting to be white rather than making art that serves Black life
Not petition. Not assimilation. Assertion from the inside out.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Hughes's essay "The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain" (1926) is the most direct statement of the aesthetic position that runs through his poetry. It was written partly in response to Du Bois's "Criteria of Negro Art" and partly in response to what Hughes saw as the middle-class Black intellectual's anxiety about Black vernacular culture. It is a declaration of independence — not from white standards, but from the desire to meet them.

Breaking It Down

"The greatest danger: the desire to please white audiences" — Hughes is not arguing against all engagement with white audiences. He is arguing against making that engagement the standard by which Black art judges itself. The moment you start writing toward white approval — softening the vernacular, elevating the subject matter, translating the culture — you have accepted white standards as the measure of Black artistic worth. That acceptance, Hughes argues, is the real racial mountain: the internalized conviction that to be excellent is to be white.

"Go to the folk tradition — jazz clubs, rent parties, blues, vernacular — and trust it" — Hughes's counter-proposal is specific: go down, not up. Not toward the European high-art tradition, but toward the most vernacular, most Black, most unrespectable parts of Black life. The blues musician playing to a rent-party crowd was not producing inferior art waiting to be elevated. He was producing excellent art on its own terms, and Hughes's job as a poet was to meet that standard, not to transcend it.

The racial mountain — The metaphor in the essay's title: a young Black poet who says "I want to be a poet, not a Negro poet" has confused excellence with whiteness. He wants to climb the mountain — to achieve recognition in white terms. Hughes says the mountain itself is the problem. The goal is not to climb it but to refuse to recognize it as the relevant summit.

Why This Matters

Hughes's position generates a specific tension with Du Bois's control problem. If you trust the folk tradition and produce art for the Black community on Black terms, you sidestep the question of white gatekeepers — but you also limit your reach to the circuits the Black community controls. Hughes accepted that tradeoff. The rent party circuit, the Black press, the church — these were sufficient audiences. Whether they were sufficient platforms for producing the cultural change Du Bois wanted is the question that Hurston complicates further.

Hurston: The Tradition Is the Standard

  • Went south to collect African American folk culture — stories, songs, speech, religious practice
  • Published Mules and Men (1935): folklore collected by a Black American, for the first time
  • Their Eyes Were Watching God (1937): written entirely in African American vernacular English
  • The vernacular is not a deficiency — it is a tradition of its own authority
This language is sufficient. It is, in its own terms, beautiful.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Zora Neale Hurston took Hughes's argument one step further — and in a direction that alienated both Du Bois and some of Hughes's own allies. Where Hughes said "trust the folk tradition," Hurston said "the folk tradition is the standard." Not a tradition that needs to be trusted or validated, but a tradition that is already complete, already authoritative, already sufficient as the measure of its own excellence.

Breaking It Down

Went south to collect African American folk culture — Hurston, trained as an anthropologist under Franz Boas at Columbia, returned to the rural South not as a journalist documenting poverty or oppression but as a scholar documenting a living intellectual tradition. The stories, songs, religious practices, and speech patterns she collected in Florida and Louisiana were not survivals of a broken culture. They were evidence of a sophisticated, continuously evolving tradition with its own aesthetic standards and its own forms of excellence.

Mules and Men (1935) — folklore collected by a Black American, for the first time — Before Hurston, African American folklore had been collected primarily by white scholars who treated it as an object of study, something to be preserved and explained to outside audiences. Hurston collected it as a participant, as a member of the communities she studied, and wrote about it from inside rather than outside. That shift in perspective — from observer to inheritor — changed both the methodology and the meaning of the collection.

Their Eyes Were Watching God (1937) — written entirely in African American vernacular English — This was Hurston's most radical formal choice. Not a novel with dialect passages surrounded by standard English narration, but a novel whose narrative voice itself was inflected with vernacular. The prose does not explain the vernacular to outside readers; it assumes it. That assumption — that the vernacular is sufficient, that it does not require translation — is the formal enactment of Hurston's entire argument.

Why This Matters

Hurston's position was the most politically isolated of the three. Du Bois's NAACP had institutional power. Hughes had critical recognition in both Black and white publications. Hurston was attacked from the left for writing work that wasn't explicitly political enough, and dismissed by mainstream critics who couldn't hear the vernacular as literature. Their Eyes Were Watching God went out of print after its initial publication and was not recovered until Alice Walker wrote about it in 1975. The belated recognition is itself evidence for Hurston's argument: the work was excellent on its own terms before anyone with institutional power acknowledged it.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Du Bois asked: who controls the means of cultural distribution? Hughes answered: stop seeking their approval and trust your own culture. Ellington answered: refuse to simplify, and turn their platform into yours. Are these different answers — or are they all expressions of the same act of heroic resistance through excellence?

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the question that the entire Section V has been building toward. It asks you to evaluate whether three apparently different positions — Du Bois's structural critique, Hughes's aesthetic autonomy, Ellington's institutional conversion — are actually versions of the same underlying commitment. Think carefully before deciding. The "are they all the same?" framing is an invitation to synthesis, but synthesis that papers over real differences is not honest analysis.

Breaking It Down

What they genuinely share: All three refused to accept the existing terms of the cultural field as fixed. Du Bois refused to pretend that white cultural standards were neutral. Hughes refused to produce work that deferred to white aesthetic preferences. Ellington refused to simplify his music to fit a racist venue's demands. In each case, the refusal was enacted through excellence — through work so good that the existing terms could not contain it. That shared structure is real.

Where they genuinely differ: Du Bois wanted art to serve the political struggle for equality. Hughes wanted art to serve the Black community's own self-knowledge and self-celebration, regardless of political utility. Ellington, by working inside a white-controlled institution, implicitly accepted some of Du Bois's structural critique while enacting Hughes's aesthetic autonomy. These are not the same position — and conflating them loses something important about the actual debate.

The synthesis question: "Heroic resistance through excellence" can encompass all three — but only if you define "heroic resistance" broadly enough to include Du Bois's explicitly political art, Hughes's explicitly apolitical aesthetic autonomy, and Ellington's conversion of exploitation into platform. Whether that broad definition is analytically useful or analytically empty is worth arguing about.

Synthesis

Heroic Resistance Through Excellence

They chose to build rather than beg

📖 Understanding This Slide

The synthesis section draws together every thread from both Part A and Part B into the lecture's final statement. "They chose to build rather than beg" — six words that compress the entire interpretive argument. Every figure, every institution, every artistic choice covered in these two decks was an expression of that choice. What the Evidence Shows

  • Van Der Zee — 10,000 portraits accumulated over decades constitute a counter-archive that minstrelsy and Hollywood could not erase
  • Douglas — African visual heritage was not a curiosity borrowed by Europeans but a living foundation for monumental public art, available to any artist confident enough to claim it
  • Ellington — a white-owned, Black-excluding club with a racist performance mandate can be converted, by a sufficiently determined artist, into a national dissemination mechanism for the most sophisticated jazz in America
  • Hughes & Hurston — art that trusts its own culture completely, without translation or apology, produces work that outlasts every argument made against it
Excellence produced for a community that genuinely needed it turned out to be the most durable kind

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide converts the theoretical argument into an evidentiary claim. Each bullet point is a specific, documentable demonstration of what the lecture has argued. Read each one as a conclusion supported by the evidence you have encountered across both decks.

Breaking It Down

Van Der Zee — 10,000 portraits constitute a counter-archive that minstrelsy could not erase — The scale matters. One portrait is an anecdote. Ten thousand portraits, accumulated over decades, serving a community across every stratum of its life, is a counter-record. It is the accumulated visual proof that every claim the racist visual tradition made about Black people's appearance and dignity was false. Hollywood produced the caricature. Van Der Zee produced the evidence.

Douglas — African visual heritage was not a curiosity but a living foundation — Douglas's murals in the Harlem public library are still there. Students still encounter them. The argument he made in 1934 — that African visual traditions are co-equal with European ones and can be synthesized into something new — is now uncontroversial in art history. He made it first, and he made it on the walls of a building where anyone could see it.

Ellington — a white-owned, Black-excluding club can be converted into a national platform — The Cotton Club closed in 1940. Ellington's recordings from that period are still listened to as some of the greatest music in American history. The venue's racial exclusion is a historical footnote. The music is permanent.

Hughes and Hurston — art that trusts its own culture produces work that outlasts every argument made against it — Their Eyes Were Watching God went out of print in 1937. It was recovered in 1975 and is now taught in virtually every American literature survey. Hughes's poems are among the most widely anthologized in American poetry. The arguments made against them — too vernacular, too political, not universal enough — are forgotten. The work remains.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "Excellence produced for a community that genuinely needed it turned out to be the most durable kind" — is a historical claim, not an inspirational slogan. The durability is documented. The work survives. The institutions that tried to marginalize it do not. That outcome does not vindicate every choice every Renaissance figure made, and it does not mean that excellence is sufficient protection against political violence (Tulsa demonstrated that). But it does mean that the bet — trust your own culture, build rather than beg, produce work of genuine excellence for the community that needs it — paid off in the historical record.

The Deepest Form of Resistance

The Harlem Renaissance was a deliberate, collective assertion that Black Americans would not wait for permission to create, to build, or to claim their full humanity. From the Great Migration's calculated departure, through Harlem and Greenwood's entrepreneurial vision, to the artists who trusted their own vernacular traditions without apology — this generation chose to build rather than beg.
The lasting irony: art that refused to announce itself as political argument did more damage to the ideology of white supremacy than most explicitly political work of the same era — precisely because it was undeniably excellent.

Breaking It Down

"A deliberate, collective assertion that Black Americans would not wait for permission to create, to build, or to claim their full humanity" — Three verbs again: create, build, claim. The same three verbs from Part A's closing slides. Not petition. Not demonstrate worthiness. Not ask. Create. Build. Claim. The deliberateness matters: this was not an accidental flowering. It was a series of choices, made by specific people, in specific conditions, about what to do with their talent, their rage, and their resources.

"From the Great Migration's calculated departure... to the artists who trusted their own vernacular traditions without apology" — The arc runs from the largest scale (1.6 million people choosing to leave) to the most intimate (a poet choosing to write in vernacular). At every scale, the same posture: we will not accept the current arrangement as permanent, and we will act accordingly.

"Art that refused to announce itself as political argument did more damage to the ideology of white supremacy than most explicitly political work of the same era — precisely because it was undeniably excellent" — This is the bottom line's core claim, and it is worth sitting with. Explicit political argument can be dismissed, debated, rejected. Excellence is harder to dismiss because it presents itself as fact rather than as argument. When Louis Armstrong plays the opening of "West End Blues," the question "are Black Americans capable of the highest artistic achievement?" is not answered by argument. It is answered by evidence. The ideology of white supremacy required, for its coherence, that the answer be no. The Harlem Renaissance produced so much evidence to the contrary that the ideology became increasingly difficult to maintain. That is a form of damage that political argument, by itself, could not have achieved.

Why This Matters

This bottom line also answers Du Bois's question about whether art should be propaganda. The irony Du Bois didn't fully anticipate: the art that most effectively served the political struggle was precisely the art that refused to subordinate itself to political utility. Hughes's poetry, Hurston's novel, Ellington's compositions — none of these announced themselves as arguments against white supremacy. All of them were. The excellence was the argument, and it was unanswerable in a way that explicitly political art, which can always be countered with another argument, was not.

Breaking It Down

Cultural excellence produced work that outlasted every political structure that tried to suppress it — Hughes's poems, Hurston's novel, Ellington's recordings, Van Der Zee's archive are all permanent. "Or are both required, in different measures, at different times?" — This is the most defensible position, and the one the deck implicitly endorses. Not culture over politics. Not politics over culture. Both, at different scales, through different mechanisms, producing different kinds of change on different timelines. The Renaissance changed what America understood itself to be. The Civil Rights Movement changed what America's laws required. Both changes were real. Neither was sufficient without the other.

Why This Matters — One Final Thought

The conventional framework students often bring to this material — that the Harlem Renaissance was a cultural achievement that demonstrated Black dignity and therefore advanced the cause of civil rights — is not wrong, but it is incomplete in a specific way: it treats cultural achievement as instrumental, valuable because it served political ends. This course has been asking you to consider a different possibility: that cultural achievement has its own form of permanence, separate from and not reducible to political utility. Hughes's poems matter not because they contributed to passing the Civil Rights Act but because they are excellent poems that tell true things about human experience. That excellence is its own argument, its own form of resistance, its own kind of victory — one that no political setback can permanently undo. Whether you find that claim sufficient or insufficient, convincing or unconvincing, is a question worth carrying with you out of this course.

Douglass and the Portrait as Argument

In a series of lectures delivered in the 1860s and early 1870s — "Pictures and Progress" was the most fully developed — Frederick Douglass articulated a philosophy of photographic representation. The camera, he argued, was a democratic instrument precisely because it recorded reality without the distortions of the painter's or illustrator's hand. An honest portrait could not lie about what a person looked like. In an era when minstrel caricature and pseudoscientific racial taxonomy were producing systematic visual dehumanization of Black Americans, Douglass recognized that photographic portraiture offered something unprecedented: the possibility of showing Black men and women as they actually were — composed, dignified, intelligent, and individual. He wore formal dress, set his jaw, and looked directly into the lens with an expression that combined dignity with visible challenge. He was not performing for white approval. He was insisting, in a visual idiom the entire country could read, that the person in the frame was a man of full moral and intellectual standing.

×
The Birth of a Nation (1915)

D.W. Griffith's three-hour epic was the most technically innovative American film made to that point and one of the most virulently racist cultural artifacts ever produced at industrial scale. Based on Thomas Dixon's novel The Clansman, it depicted the Ku Klux Klan as heroic defenders of white civilization and Black men — played by white actors in blackface — as sexual predators and political incompetents. Woodrow Wilson screened it at the White House and reportedly described it as "history written with lightning." The revived Klan of the 1920s, which at its peak claimed four million members, used it as a recruitment tool. The NAACP organized protests in cities across the country — and largely failed to stop its distribution. Understanding this film is essential for understanding what Black visual artists, photographers, and press editors were doing when they chose to produce and circulate dignified images of Black American life: they were contesting a visual field that had been systematically weaponized against them.

×
The Savoy Ballroom

Opened in 1926 at 596 Lenox Avenue, the Savoy was owned by a Black-Jewish business partnership and admitted anyone — Black, white, or mixed — for a twenty-five cent admission. Unlike the Cotton Club, which excluded Black customers from its audience, the Savoy was built for and by the Harlem community. It hosted "cutting contests" — competitive improvisational battles in which two bands performed simultaneously on facing bandstands, with the crowd determining the winner. These contests were the crucible in which the most technically demanding jazz innovations were forged. The competitive pressure was horizontal, between musicians performing for knowledgeable Black audiences, rather than vertical toward white commercial taste. The Savoy's dance floor, large enough for thousands, made it the defining social institution of Black Harlem's leisure culture through the 1930s and 1940s.

×
Race Records

The recording industry's condescending label for records marketed specifically to Black audiences — a term that revealed the industry's assumption that its primary market was white. From the community's perspective, race records were something quite different: a distribution system that allowed Black musicians to reach Black listeners in Chicago, Detroit, Atlanta, and Birmingham without dependence on white radio gatekeepers or white-owned concert venues. Bessie Smith's "Downhearted Blues" sold approximately 780,000 copies in its first year of release — not primarily to white households, but to Black ones across the country, carried by a parallel distribution network built on Black community institutions. The race records market was commercially exploitative — Black artists consistently received far less than white artists for equivalent sales — but it was also, from the community's vantage point, an infrastructure for reaching a mass Black audience on Black cultural terms, without white editorial filtering.

×
Owney Madden and the Cotton Club's Structure

Owen "Owney" Madden was an Irish-born bootlegger and organized crime figure who opened the Cotton Club at 142nd Street and Lenox Avenue in 1923 as a front for his illegal liquor business. Madden understood that Black jazz talent was the most marketable entertainment in the country and constructed the club's arrangement with precise exploitation in mind: Black musicians performed on stage, Black kitchen workers and waitstaff served the floor, and every customer paying for drinks was white. The arrangement was not accidental or incidental — it was the business model. Black Harlemites who attempted to enter as customers were turned away at the door. The neighborhood that produced the music was not permitted to hear it at its most commercially prominent venue. Madden's CBS and NBC broadcast arrangements were designed to attract white suburban listeners; they became, through Ellington's exploitation of them, the mechanism for a very different kind of distribution.

Visual Economy

A term historians use to describe the system through which images are produced, distributed, and consumed in a society, and the power relations that govern that system. In the 1920s, the visual economy was transformed by mass media — film, photography, advertising — which gave images unprecedented reach and standardized the visual codes through which Americans understood identity, aspiration, and social worth. Who controlled the production and distribution of images in this new economy effectively controlled a significant dimension of social reality.

×
Sambo / Minstrel Stereotypes

A set of dehumanizing caricatures of Black Americans that circulated in American popular culture from the antebellum period through the twentieth century. The Sambo figure depicted Black people as childlike, servile, lazy, and comic. The "threatening Buck" depicted Black men as violent and sexually predatory. The Mammy figure depicted Black women as loyal, asexual servants. These were not merely offensive images — they were systematic arguments, embedded in the visual culture of entertainment, advertising, and political cartoons, about the fundamental incapacity of Black people for full citizenship and self-determination.

Deborah Willis

Historian and photographer whose Reflections in Black: A History of Black Photographers, 1840 to the Present (2000) is the definitive account of African American photography as an artistic and political tradition. Willis argues that Black photographers — from the daguerreotype era through the twentieth century — understood the portrait as a political instrument: a way of inserting dignified, complex, fully human images of Black Americans into a visual record that had systematically denied their humanity. Her analysis of Van Der Zee is the scholarly foundation for this section of the lecture.

×
Douglas's Visual Synthesis

Aaron Douglas drew on three visual traditions simultaneously. From West African sculpture and Egyptian hieroglyphics he took flat, stylized human figures, angular silhouettes, and a visual vocabulary rooted in African civilization rather than European academic painting. From European modernism — particularly Cubism and Art Deco — he took geometric abstraction, bold flat planes of color, and the breaking of the figure into simplified, powerful shapes. The synthesis argued visually what Alain Locke was arguing intellectually: that the New Negro was heir to both African and modern traditions, not a derivative of European culture.

×
The Cotton Club

The most famous nightclub in Harlem, located at 142nd Street and Lenox Avenue, operating from 1923 to 1940. The Cotton Club featured the best Black performers in America — Duke Ellington, Cab Calloway, Lena Horne, Ethel Waters — and its radio broadcasts made Ellington's band nationally famous. But the Cotton Club admitted only white customers. Black Harlemites could not attend performances in their own neighborhood's most famous cultural institution. The club's arrangement — Black performers, white audiences, white owners — was a perfect illustration of Du Bois's concern about who controlled the means of cultural distribution.

×
The Racial Mountain

Hughes's metaphor from his 1926 essay "The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain." The mountain is the obstacle that stands between a Black artist and genuine creative freedom: the internalized desire to be white, or to produce art that will earn white approval. Hughes described a Black poet he had met who "wanted to write like a white poet" — not consciously, but structurally, in the sense that his standards of what constituted good poetry were derived entirely from European models and the judgment of white critics. Hughes argued that climbing over this mountain required going back to the folk tradition — to the blues, the vernacular, the specific textures of Black life — and trusting that material to carry the weight of serious art without translation.

×
African American Vernacular English

The variety of English spoken by many African Americans, with its own grammatical structures, phonological patterns, vocabulary, and rhetorical traditions that developed from the intersection of West African languages, English, and the specific conditions of enslaved and free Black communities in America. Linguists recognize it as a fully systematic and rule-governed language variety — not "broken" English but a distinct dialect with its own internal logic. Hurston's decision to write Their Eyes Were Watching God almost entirely in AAVE was a deliberate formal choice: it said that this language was not a deficiency to be corrected but a tradition with its own aesthetic authority, sufficient for serious literary art.
