📖 Understanding This Slide

The title "Fame, Harlem, and the Right to Be Seen" is doing real interpretive work before the lecture even begins. The Harlem Renaissance is often presented as a cultural flowering — interesting, creative, historically significant. This course frames it differently: as a political act, a deliberate assertion of visibility by people whose humanity had been systematically denied in law, in media, in public life, and in the history books themselves. Keep that framing in mind as you move through the slides.

Why This Matters

Most textbook treatments of the Harlem Renaissance celebrate it as a moment of Black artistic achievement — which it was — and stop there. That framing, while not wrong, misses the harder question: achievement in the face of what? And toward what end? This lecture insists on keeping the harder context visible. The art was extraordinary. The conditions that produced it were brutal. Both things are true, and holding them in tension is more honest than celebrating one while softening the other.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This isn't just atmosphere — it's an argument delivered before a word of lecture has been spoken. Duke Ellington and Louis Armstrong were not merely popular entertainers. They were artists whose work represented something that had never existed before: a distinctly Black American musical tradition that would go on to become one of the most influential art forms in world history. Starting class with their music is a way of saying: the lecture is about this, and this deserves to be heard on its own terms before we analyze it.

Breaking It Down

"It Don't Mean a Thing (If It Ain't Got That Swing)" (1931) — Ellington's declaration that swing, the driving rhythmic energy of jazz, was the non-negotiable core of the music. Not technical proficiency. Not complexity. Swing. That insistence on feel over formula was a cultural statement as much as a musical one. Ellington composed over 1,000 pieces and led his orchestra for nearly fifty years.

"West End Blues" (1928) — often cited as the single most important recording in jazz history. Louis Armstrong's opening trumpet cadenza, improvised without accompaniment, demonstrated that jazz could achieve the emotional and technical complexity of any European classical form. It was proof, issued in three minutes of recorded music, that Black American vernacular culture was not a lesser tradition waiting to be elevated — it was already elevated.

Why This Matters

One pattern you will see throughout this lecture is Black Americans building institutions, art forms, and communities that required no external validation to be genuinely excellent — and getting them anyway. Armstrong and Ellington didn't ask permission to be world-class artists. They simply were. That same posture — confident, self-generated, unapologetic — runs through everything from Langston Hughes's poetry to the Greenwood district's economy.

These three questions form the spine of the entire lecture. Breaking It Down

Question 1 — Where did Harlem come from, and why the 1920s? History doesn't produce cultural explosions at random. The Harlem Renaissance happened when and where it did because of specific, traceable causes: migration, war, broken promises, institutional density. Understanding those causes prevents you from treating the Renaissance as a fortunate accident.

Question 2 — What did the New Negro movement claim? The word "claim" is doing important work here. Not "what did it celebrate?" or "what did it produce?" A claim is an assertion of right. The New Negro movement was demanding something — full humanity, artistic legitimacy, citizenship — and demanding it on its own terms, not as a petition but as a statement of fact.

Question 3 — How did the art make that claim? This is the most sophisticated question. Art is not just decoration on top of a political argument — in the Harlem Renaissance, the art was the argument. Hughes's poems didn't just describe Black life; their form performed it. Douglas's paintings didn't just depict Black figures; they made them heroic through visual choices. Understanding how the art argues is the payoff of the whole lecture.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "The art is the argument" — is the key interpretive frame. Conventional treatments of this era often separate politics from culture, as if the novels and poems are illustrative examples of what Black people were going through politically. This course rejects that separation. The art was not illustrative of something else. It was the thing.

Section dividers are orientation markers. The World They Were Leaving

  • No practical right to vote; locked into sharecropping debt

  • Lynchings were public spectacles: photographed, attended, sold as postcards
The purpose was terror — and it was effective

Jim Crow America · ca. 1900–1920 · Public Domain

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide establishes the baseline conditions that the Harlem Renaissance was a response to. It matters that we look at this clearly and without softening. The conventional textbook tendency is to present Jim Crow as an unfortunate background condition and then move quickly to the cultural achievements. This course reverses that priority: the brutality of the baseline is the essential context without which the Renaissance cannot be understood.

Breaking It Down

Jim Crow as a "total system" — This is the most important phrase on the slide. Jim Crow was not primarily about seating arrangements on buses or entrances to restaurants. It was a comprehensive architecture of subordination: legal disenfranchisement, debt bondage through sharecropping, exclusion from meaningful economic participation, and constant physical threat. It was designed to be inescapable — and for most Black Southerners in this period, it was.

4,000+ lynchings, 1877–1950 — These were not random acts of racial hatred by fringe actors. They were community events, often advertised in advance, attended by hundreds or thousands, photographed and turned into postcards sold as souvenirs. Local law enforcement frequently participated or stood aside. The federal government consistently failed to pass anti-lynching legislation despite decades of NAACP lobbying. The purpose was, as the slide states, terror — the maintenance of racial hierarchy through the constant demonstration that Black life could be taken without legal consequence.

Why This Matters

The interpretive temptation — especially in a cultural history unit — is to pivot quickly from this darkness to the creativity that followed. Resist that pivot. The creativity that produced the Harlem Renaissance was generated by people living under these conditions. That is not incidental background — it is the engine. When Du Bois writes "We return fighting," and when Hughes writes "I, too, am America," the force of those words is incomprehensible without understanding the system those words were pushing against.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Leon Litwack — Trouble in Mind: Black Southerners in the Age of Jim Crow

Litwack's exhaustive study of the Jim Crow South emphasizes the psychological dimension of the system — the daily humiliations, the enforced deference, the requirement to perform submission in every public interaction. He argues that understanding Jim Crow requires not just counting laws or recording violence, but grasping the texture of daily life under a system designed to communicate, every day, that you did not fully belong to the society in which you lived. The Great Migration, in Litwack's framing, was not just an economic calculation — it was a rejection of that daily experience.

The Great Migration

  • 1910–1930: approximately 1.6 million Black Americans left the South
  • Destinations: Chicago, Detroit, Philadelphia, New York
  • Pull: wartime industrial labor demand; the Chicago Defender campaigns
  • Push: Jim Crow, sharecropping, the boll weevil, and violence
Not the Promised Land — but different. And different was enough.

The Great Migration · 1910s–1930s · Public Domain

📖 Understanding This Slide

The Great Migration is one of the most consequential demographic events in American history — and one of the most misunderstood. It tends to be presented as a story of victimhood: people fleeing oppression. That framing is not wrong, but it's incomplete. The Migration was also a story of agency — of people making rational calculations, gathering information, taking enormous risks, and building new lives. Understanding it as a deliberate act changes how you read everything that followed, including the Harlem Renaissance.

Breaking It Down

1.6 million people, 1910–1930 — This number understates the full scale: a second wave from the 1940s through the 1970s brought the total to roughly 6 million. The First Migration was specifically driven by World War I labor demand — Northern factories were desperate for workers after European immigration dried up, and Black Southerners were ready to come. The Chicago Defender ran active recruitment campaigns, publishing wage comparisons, letters from successful migrants, and sometimes train schedules.

Push and pull factors operating simultaneously — The boll weevil's destruction of cotton agriculture in the mid-1910s hit Black sharecroppers particularly hard, since sharecropping was already a debt trap. Combine economic collapse with Jim Crow's violence and the possibility of industrial wages in Chicago or Detroit, and the calculation became straightforward for many families. "Different was enough" — the North was not paradise; racial discrimination and segregated housing existed there too. But it was different, and different opened possibilities that simply did not exist in the South.

Why This Matters

The Great Migration was an act of self-determination. No government program organized it, no political movement directed it. Families made individual decisions, gathered information through Black newspapers and community networks, and moved. That pattern — collective action emerging from individual decisions made without central direction — is worth noting. It prefigures the self-organized character of the Renaissance itself.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Isabel Wilkerson — The Warmth of Other Suns

Wilkerson's landmark narrative history of the Great Migration reconstructs three individual migrants' journeys over decades, arguing that the Migration deserves to be understood alongside the great population movements of world history. She pushes back against framings that treat migrants as passive victims of push factors, emphasizing instead the deliberateness and courage of the decision to leave. Her central argument — that the Migration permanently transformed American culture, politics, and cities — provides essential context for why Harlem could become what it became.

Why Harlem?

  • Overbuilt by speculators — white tenants never materialized
  • Philip Payton convinced landlords to rent to Black tenants: door opened, and didn't close
  • By early 1920s: largest Black urban community in the United States
  • Newspapers, churches, theaters, nightclubs, colleges, political organizations — density of institutions
Harlem didn't just grow — it developed a self-consciousness about itself as something new

📖 Understanding This Slide

Harlem's emergence as the center of Black American cultural life was not inevitable — it was the result of a specific real estate accident and the entrepreneurial instincts of one man. That contingency matters. History produces its transformations through very specific mechanisms, and understanding those mechanisms is part of understanding the history itself.

Breaking It Down

The speculative collapse — Harlem had been developed in the early 1900s by real estate speculators who built apartment buildings anticipating a white middle-class demand that never materialized. Landlords were stuck with empty buildings and unpaid mortgages. Into this situation came Philip Payton Jr., a Black real estate entrepreneur who made a straightforward business proposition: he would fill those buildings with Black tenants who would pay premium rates. Desperate landlords agreed. The door opened.

Density of institutions — Once Black residents arrived in significant numbers, institutions followed: churches, newspapers, political clubs, fraternal organizations, literary societies, theaters, nightclubs. This institutional density was the condition of possibility for the Renaissance. You can't have a cultural movement without the organizations, publications, and venues that allow artists to find audiences, editors, and each other. Harlem had all of that in concentrated form.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "Harlem developed a self-consciousness about itself as something new" — points toward one of the Renaissance's most distinctive features. Harlem's intellectuals didn't just live there; they theorized it. Alain Locke, Du Bois, and others wrote explicitly about what Harlem meant, what it represented, what it was becoming. That self-aware quality transformed a neighborhood into a symbol — and a symbol into a movement.

The War's Broken Promise

  • 380,000 Black Americans served in World War One
  • Segregated units, dangerous assignments, officers who treated them as less than human
  • Du Bois argued: demonstrate patriotism — America will acknowledge citizenship
  • It did not. Black veterans were lynched in their uniforms
Red Summer 1919 — racial massacres in 25+ cities

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide is about a broken contract — and the rage that broken contract produced. W.E.B. Du Bois famously urged Black Americans to "close ranks" and support the war effort, arguing that demonstrating patriotism would earn recognition of citizenship rights. The United States government did not keep that implicit bargain. Understanding the depth of that betrayal is essential to understanding what the Harlem Renaissance was responding to.

Breaking It Down

380,000 Black Americans served — Many served in the segregated 92nd and 93rd Infantry Divisions. The 369th Infantry — the "Harlem Hellfighters" — spent 191 days in frontline combat, longer than any other American unit. French military command (which had no racial segregation policy) awarded the Croix de Guerre to the entire regiment. American military command, meanwhile, circulated a memo to French officers asking them not to treat Black soldiers with equality or respect, because it would undermine American racial customs. The contrast was not lost on the men who experienced it.

Black veterans were lynched in their uniforms — This is not a rhetorical flourish; it is documented. The white supremacist calculus was explicit: a Black man in a military uniform, having served his country and returned with expectations of recognition, represented an intolerable challenge to the racial hierarchy. The violence of Red Summer was in significant part a reaction to that challenge.

Why This Matters

The broken promise of World War I radicalized a generation of Black intellectuals who might otherwise have pursued gradualist strategies. Du Bois himself — who had urged cooperation with the war effort — emerged from 1919 permanently angrier and more confrontational. The Renaissance's refusal to seek white approval, its insistence on asserting rather than petitioning, was born in this specific betrayal. The war didn't create the Harlem Renaissance, but it removed the last arguments for patience.

We Return Fighting

"We return fighting. Make way for Democracy! We saved it in France, and by the Great Jehovah, we will save it in the United States of America, or know the reason why."
— W.E.B. Du Bois, Returning Soldiers, The Crisis, May 1919
Written in May 1919. The Red Summer began in July.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Primary sources are windows into the emotional reality of historical moments. Du Bois's "Returning Soldiers" editorial is one of the most important documents in American history — not because of its argument (which was simple) but because of what it represents: the point at which a leading Black intellectual who had urged patience and patriotism declared that the time for patience was over. The dating at the bottom of the slide is not a coincidence — it's a historical irony that the editorial must live with.

Breaking It Down

"We return fighting" — This is a declaration, not a request. Du Bois is not petitioning Congress, not arguing a legal case, not appealing to white conscience. He is announcing a posture. The word "fighting" is deliberate and double: fighting as the veterans fought in France, and fighting as they will now fight for their rights at home. The register of this language is entirely different from the cautious, qualified language Black leaders had typically used in public communications directed at white audiences.

"We saved it in France, and by the Great Jehovah, we will save it in the United States" — This is also a deliberate rhetorical strategy. Du Bois is invoking the logic of the war itself: if democracy is worth fighting for abroad, it is worth fighting for at home. The argument doesn't ask for special treatment; it asks for consistency. The invocation of "the Great Jehovah" places this in a tradition of Black religious rhetoric going back to abolitionism — the appeal to a higher authority than American law, which had repeatedly failed Black Americans.

Why This Matters

The timing — May 1919, two months before Red Summer — is devastating in retrospect. Du Bois announced that Black Americans would fight for democracy, and within weeks the United States demonstrated yet again how it intended to respond to that demand. The Red Summer didn't silence the movement that produced this editorial. It intensified it. The Harlem Renaissance is, in part, the answer to the question: what do you do with this rage?

🎓 Historians Weigh In

David Levering Lewis — W.E.B. Du Bois: Biography of a Race

Lewis's two-volume biography of Du Bois traces the evolution of his thought from gradualism to confrontation, arguing that the events of 1919 represent a genuine turning point. The Du Bois who wrote "Returning Soldiers" was a different intellectual from the Du Bois who had urged Black Americans to "close ranks" in 1918. Lewis shows how Du Bois's encounter with betrayal — personal and collective — sharpened his radicalism and made him the intellectual godfather of the New Negro movement.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is a comprehension check, not a trick question — the answer is C. But the value of the question is not in selecting the right letter; it's in recognizing why the other options were real historical possibilities, and why C is more remarkable than it sounds.

Breaking It Down

Option A — "Accepted the existing racial hierarchy as permanent" — This was not an irrational position in 1919. Booker T. Washington's accommodationist strategy had been the dominant Black leadership model for decades. The Red Summer had just demonstrated what militant resistance could cost. Acceptance was a choice many made, and historians should not judge it out of context.

Option B — "Emigrated to Africa under Marcus Garvey's program" — This was a genuine mass movement, not a fringe idea. Garvey's UNIA attracted millions of followers, and his Pan-African vision of building an independent Black nation in Africa was a serious intellectual and political program. The Garvey movement is covered in detail in the next section.

Option C — "Produced one of the most extraordinary explosions of cultural creativity in American history" — This is the correct answer, and it's genuinely surprising when you stop to think about it. People experiencing systematic oppression, betrayal, and racial terror produced poetry, novels, paintings, music, and institutions of lasting world-historical significance. That is not an inevitable response to those conditions. It is a remarkable one.

Option D — "Retreated from political engagement entirely" — Some individuals did. The Renaissance itself was not politically uniform — there were significant debates about whether art should serve political ends. But "retreated" as a description of the generation as a whole is simply wrong.

Why This Matters

The real lesson of this question is that rage and betrayal do not have a single predictable output. They can produce accommodation, emigration, cultural explosion, or withdrawal. The Harlem Renaissance represents a specific choice about what to do with those conditions — and understanding it as a choice, rather than an automatic response, helps you appreciate the agency of the people who made it.

Section II

The New Negro

A demand, not a description

📖 Understanding This Slide

The subtitle — "A demand, not a description" — is the interpretive key to Section II. The phrase "New Negro" could be read as a sociological description: a new demographic reality, a new generation with new characteristics. The slide insists on a more aggressive reading: this was a claim, an assertion of what Black Americans were and would be. It was not asking anyone to acknowledge that a new generation had arrived. It was announcing it.

Alain Locke — The Manifesto

  • The New Negro anthology, 1925 — poetry, fiction, essays, visual art
  • Older generation: sought white approval, proved worthiness by white standards
  • New generation: "shaking off the psychology of imitation and implied inferiority"
  • The New Negro is urban, educated, self-possessed, unapologetic
Not petition. Assertion.

Alain Locke · 1885–1954 · Howard University · Public Domain

📖 Understanding This Slide

Alain Locke is sometimes called the "Dean" of the Harlem Renaissance — the intellectual who gave the movement its most articulate theoretical framework. His 1925 anthology The New Negro was not just a collection of art and literature; it was a manifesto that argued Black cultural production should be understood as world-historically significant, not as a marginal subculture seeking admission to the mainstream.

Breaking It Down

"Shaking off the psychology of imitation and implied inferiority" — This phrase gets at what Locke thought was the deeper problem with the older generation's strategy. Booker T. Washington's accommodationism, Du Bois's earlier emphasis on demonstrating Black worthiness by white standards — both, in Locke's analysis, accepted the white evaluation of Black culture as the starting point and then tried to argue against it. The New Negro began from a different premise: Black culture needs no external validation. It is already excellent. The starting point is assertion, not defense.

"Urban, educated, self-possessed, unapologetic" — Note what this description leaves out. It doesn't say "approved by white gatekeepers," "integrated into mainstream institutions," or "translated for white audiences." The New Negro's legitimacy was self-generated. This was a genuine philosophical position, not just a rhetorical posture.

Why This Matters

There's a line here worth thinking about carefully: the older model of Black leadership sought white approval. Locke — and the Renaissance generally — rejected that frame. But this raises a question the Renaissance itself debated internally: if you produce art of unquestionable quality, does that naturally translate into political power? Or are cultural achievement and political power on separate tracks? That question sits at the center of this lecture and is taken up directly at the end.

📖 Understanding This Slide

One of the most underappreciated facts about the Harlem Renaissance is that it had its own publishing infrastructure. The movement didn't depend on white publishers, white magazine editors, or white-owned newspapers to reach its audience. It built parallel institutions — and those institutions had real scale. This slide maps the three most important ones.

Breaking It Down

The Crisis — Founded by Du Bois in 1910 as the NAACP's official magazine, The Crisis was not just a political journal. It published fiction, poetry, photographs, and art alongside its coverage of civil rights and racial violence. At 100,000 subscribers it was reaching an audience that rivaled major mainstream magazines. Du Bois used it to launch careers, frame debates, and maintain the visibility of Black intellectual life at the national level.

Opportunity — The Urban League's literary magazine, edited by sociologist Charles S. Johnson, was in some ways more focused on pure literary culture than The Crisis. Johnson ran essay contests that brought Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, and Countee Cullen to national attention. Opportunity dinner parties — where Black writers mingled with white publishers and editors — were a significant mechanism through which Renaissance figures gained access to mainstream publishing.

The Chicago Defender — Included here because its 250,000 readers represent a mass audience that dwarfed the literary journals. The Defender reached Black Americans who would never subscribe to The Crisis, which meant that Black cultural and political news could circulate at a genuinely popular scale.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "a parallel media infrastructure producing images on its own terms" — points toward something important about how cultural power actually works. Mainstream American media in the 1920s produced a steady stream of demeaning caricatures of Black Americans. These three publications provided an alternative visual and intellectual world, controlled by Black editors and writers, that told a completely different story. That self-sufficiency was not incidental to the Renaissance; it was structural.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is your "who's who" reference slide. Five figures, five distinct positions. The bottom line — "these people disagreed, sharply, publicly, in print" — is a direct pushback against one of the most common distortions in how the Harlem Renaissance gets taught: the tendency to treat it as a unified movement with a single voice. It was not. It was a vigorous, sometimes bitter argument about strategy, aesthetics, and the purpose of art conducted among people who had profoundly different answers to the same problems.

Breaking It Down

W.E.B. Du Bois — Harvard-trained sociologist, editor of The Crisis, founder of the NAACP. His position: full citizenship now, political rights as the foundation on which everything else rests. Art, in Du Bois's view, should serve the political struggle — a position he stated explicitly in "Criteria of Negro Art" (1926). He was in his 50s during the Renaissance and represented an older generation's priorities.

Langston Hughes — Born 1902, the quintessential New Negro voice. Hughes's position was that Black vernacular culture — the blues, jazz, the rent party, street life — was the proper subject matter for Black art, without apology or translation for white audiences. He was explicitly resistant to the pressure (from both Du Bois and some white patrons) to produce "respectable" art that would make middle-class audiences comfortable.

Zora Neale Hurston — Anthropologist and novelist, born in Eatonville, Florida — the first incorporated all-Black municipality in American history. Hurston's distinctive position was that Black folk culture in the rural South was not a wound or an embarrassment but a monument: a sophisticated, living tradition of storytelling, music, and communal life that deserved documentation and celebration, not translation into the idioms of urban modernity.

Aaron Douglas — The movement's foremost visual artist. Douglas fused West African visual traditions with the flat geometric forms of European modernism to create a heroic Black visual language unlike anything that had existed before. His murals and illustrations appeared in The Crisis, Opportunity, and Locke's The New Negro anthology.

Marcus Garvey — Covered in more depth on the next slide. His position was the most radical structural departure: rather than demanding inclusion in American society, Garvey questioned whether that society was worth being included in.

Why This Matters

The internal argument of the Harlem Renaissance — Du Bois vs. Hughes on whether art should serve politics, Hughes vs. Hurston on urban vs. rural Black culture, Du Bois vs. Garvey on integration vs. separatism — was not a weakness. It was evidence of a movement confident enough in its own significance to argue about what it meant. Movements that cannot afford internal debate are movements that feel too precarious to survive disagreement. The Renaissance argued because it had the institutional density and the intellectual ambition to sustain argument.

Marcus Garvey — A Different Answer

  • Arrived New York from Jamaica, 1916 — built the largest Black mass movement in American history within four years
  • UNIA claimed 2+ million members — parades, uniforms, flags through Harlem streets
  • Where Du Bois demanded inclusion in America: Garvey questioned whether that society was worth being included in
  • Black Star Line — a Black-owned international shipping company, funded by ordinary Black Americans' savings

Marcus Garvey · 1887–1940 · Founder, Universal Negro Improvement Association

📖 Understanding This Slide

Marcus Garvey tends to get one of two treatments in American history courses: either he is dismissed as a demagogue whose schemes collapsed, or he is romanticized as a visionary martyr. Both framings shortchange him. Garvey's actual historical significance is that he demonstrated — with evidence — that Black Americans could organize at mass scale around a vision of independent power that required no white approval, white investment, or white validation. The scale of the UNIA is the point.

Breaking It Down

Arrived 1916, built the largest Black mass movement in American history within four years — This is the most important fact on the slide. Garvey came to New York as an unknown Jamaican immigrant with no established network and no existing organization. Four years later he was leading a movement that dwarfed anything Du Bois's NAACP had achieved in membership. The organizational achievement was extraordinary regardless of what you think of his ideology.

UNIA parades through Harlem — These were not casual demonstrations. Uniformed marchers, brass bands, flags, tens of thousands of participants moving through the streets of Harlem — these were public assertions of Black dignity and Black institutional power in a space where Black public life was systematically humiliated nearly everywhere else in America. The theater mattered.

"Questioned whether that society was worth being included in" — This is Garvey's most fundamental challenge to Du Bois's integrationist strategy, and it deserves to be taken seriously rather than dismissed. Du Bois's argument assumed that the goal was full citizenship within the American democratic system. Garvey asked: what if that system is structurally incapable of delivering that citizenship? What if the goal should be building an independent Black nation rather than reforming a system designed by and for someone else?

Black Star Line — The shipping company Garvey founded in 1919 was a demonstration of Black economic ambition at a scale never attempted before. Ordinary working-class Black Americans bought shares. The company collapsed due to mismanagement and federal interference, and Garvey was convicted of mail fraud. But the attempt itself — thousands of Black families investing their savings in a Black-owned international shipping company — was an act of collective economic imagination that had no precedent.

Why This Matters

The federal government's treatment of Garvey is worth examining carefully. J. Edgar Hoover's Bureau of Investigation (later the FBI) had agents monitoring Garvey from 1919 onward. The mail fraud conviction — which many historians consider politically motivated — resulted in imprisonment and eventual deportation. What exactly alarmed federal authorities about Garvey? Not his business ventures, which were failing. It was his capacity to organize Black Americans around a vision of independent power. That is a useful data point about what the government found threatening.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Judith Stein — The World of Marcus Garvey

Stein's study places Garvey in the context of international Pan-Africanism and the postwar political moment, arguing that his program was a rational response to the failure of liberal integrationism — not a utopian fantasy but a coherent strategy given the evidence available in 1919. She resists both hagiography and dismissal, insisting that Garvey's failure cannot be separated from the active federal suppression his movement faced. Understanding Garvey requires holding both his genuine organizational genius and his genuine managerial failures simultaneously.

  • Political rights now — not after proving economic worthiness
  • Black Americans are already American; demand what belongs to you
  • Called Garvey "the most dangerous enemy of the Negro race"

  • Build Black institutions outside the white system entirely

  • Called Du Bois "a white man's Negro"

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is one of the most intellectually honest slides in the deck. The Du Bois–Garvey debate is one of the great strategic arguments in American history, and the temptation — especially in courses with a particular political orientation — is to declare a winner. This course resists that. Both positions had genuine strengths; both faced genuine limitations; history vindicated neither cleanly. That is a harder, more honest conclusion than declaring one man right and the other wrong.

Breaking It Down

Du Bois's position: full citizenship inside American democracy — Du Bois's strategy ultimately produced results. The NAACP's legal strategy, culminating in Brown v. Board of Education (1954) and the Civil Rights Act (1964), vindicated the integrationist approach in important ways. Political rights, won through litigation and legislation, did eventually come. Du Bois's insistence on demanding full citizenship — not proving worthiness, not asking for partial inclusion, but demanding the whole thing — was morally correct and strategically productive.

Garvey's position: build outside the white system — And yet Garvey's critique was not wrong either. Black economic self-sufficiency, Black institutional independence, Black pride that didn't require white validation — these ideas did not die with Garvey's deportation. They resurfaced in the Nation of Islam, in Malcolm X, in Black Power, in contemporary movements for Black economic autonomy. Every time the integrationist project has stalled — and it has stalled repeatedly — Garvey's alternative has re-emerged.

The insults they traded — Du Bois called Garvey "the most dangerous enemy of the Negro race." Garvey called Du Bois "a white man's Negro." These were not rhetorical flourishes; they were substantive accusations. Du Bois was arguing that Garvey's program would abandon the field to white supremacy. Garvey was arguing that Du Bois's program required Black Americans to perpetually audition for the approval of the people who had enslaved them. Both accusations contained truth.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "Both men were partly right. History did not give either a clean victory" — is the key. Students who've been taught history as a morality play where correct positions win and incorrect positions lose will find this uncomfortable. But history is not a morality play. Both strategies produced genuine achievements; both confronted genuine limits. Learning to hold that complexity without collapsing it into a simple verdict is exactly what historical thinking requires.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Du Bois and Garvey were both responding to Red Summer, Jim Crow, and the broken promise of the war. One said: demand inclusion. The other said: build your own. What experiences or assumptions might lead a person to each position?

📖 Understanding This Slide

This reflection question asks you to think causally and empathetically — not "which position is correct?" but "what would lead a person to each?" That is a more sophisticated historical question because it treats both positions as rational responses to real conditions rather than as one right answer and one wrong answer.

Breaking It Down

What might lead a person toward Du Bois's integrationism? Education and investment in American institutions. Belief that the founding ideals of the republic — equality, citizenship, rights — are genuine and achievable, if imperfectly realized. Experience with white allies who acted in good faith. A sense of belonging to American culture and history so deep that leaving or separating feels like conceding something that isn't the enemy's to take. Du Bois himself was deeply American — Harvard-educated, New England-rooted — and his strategy reflected that rootedness.

What might lead a person toward Garvey's separatism? The experience of repeated betrayal — of watching integration fail, promises broken, legal victories nullified. An analysis that says American racism is not an aberration to be corrected but a structural feature of a society built on slavery, not a bug but a design element. A Pan-African sense of identity that sees Black Americans as part of a global African diaspora rather than primarily as Americans. Garvey was Jamaican-born, and his internationalism reflected an experience of Blackness that wasn't routed through American national identity.

Why This Matters

Notice that this question doesn't ask you to decide which position is right. It asks what would lead you to each. That framing treats both as legitimate responses to real conditions — which they were. The habit of asking "what experiences or assumptions lead to this position?" is one of the most valuable tools in historical thinking. It replaces judgment with understanding without abandoning the capacity for judgment entirely.

The Literature

Langston Hughes — the blues as serious art

📖 Understanding This Slide

The subtitle "the blues as serious art" is doing real interpretive work. It encodes Hughes's core aesthetic argument in five words: that a vernacular form associated with working-class Black life deserved the same critical seriousness as any European literary tradition. That claim was controversial in 1926 — not just among white critics but within the Black middle class, some of whom viewed the blues as an embarrassment to be transcended rather than a tradition to be celebrated. Hughes disagreed, loudly, in print.

Langston Hughes

  • Harlem-born, prolific, politically engaged
  • Deliberate choice: took Black vernacular culture seriously as subject matter
  • The blues, the jazz club, the rent party — not embarrassments to translate, but the material itself

Langston Hughes · 1902–1967 · Poet of the Harlem Renaissance

📖 Understanding This Slide

Langston Hughes is the central literary figure of the Harlem Renaissance, but his significance is easily misread. He was not primarily a poet who wrote about hardship and discrimination — though he did that too. He was a theorist of Black cultural autonomy who used poetry as his medium. His most important argument was made through choices about form and subject matter, not just through the content of individual poems.

Breaking It Down

"Deliberate choice: took Black vernacular culture seriously as subject matter" — The word "deliberate" matters here. Hughes was not writing about the blues because it was all he knew. He was formally educated, widely traveled, had access to multiple cultural traditions. He chose the blues. He chose the rent party. He chose Lenox Avenue. That choice was a statement: these subjects are worthy of the full weight of poetic attention. No apology, no translation, no uplifting framing to make white readers comfortable.

"Made the poem perform the music it described" — This is the technical achievement that distinguishes Hughes from poets who merely wrote about jazz and blues. His poems don't just mention music; their rhythms, repetitions, and silences enact it. When you read "The Weary Blues" aloud, you can hear the piano player. That is a formal accomplishment, not just a thematic one — and it's the reason Hughes's work has held up for a century while more "respectable" Harlem Renaissance poetry has largely faded.

Why This Matters

Hughes's aesthetic position had a political dimension that is easy to miss. In choosing vernacular culture over "respectable" high art, Hughes was implicitly arguing against the Du Bois position that Black art should demonstrate Black worthiness by meeting white standards of excellence. Hughes was saying: Black vernacular culture does not need to be elevated to be excellent. It is already excellent, on its own terms, and the standards by which it should be judged are the standards it generates from within itself. That is a more radical claim than it might initially appear.

The Rent Party — What Hughes Was Elevating

  • Harlem rents were high — landlords charged Black tenants premium rates in a segregated market
  • Solution: throw a Saturday night party, charge 25–50 cents admission, hire a pianist, cover the rent
  • Entirely Black spaces — invisible to white Harlem; incubators for the rawest, most authentic blues and jazz
  • A micro-economy of mutual aid operating below the radar of the formal economy
Hughes saw rent parties as perfect symbols of Black self-reliance — and made them serious art

📖 Understanding This Slide

The rent party is easy to sentimentalize and easy to miss. Don't do either. The rent party was a practical response to a structural problem — discriminatory housing markets that extracted premium rents from Black tenants who had no alternatives — and it produced both an economic solution and a cultural institution simultaneously. Hughes recognized its significance because he understood that the conditions Black Americans were forced to improvise within had produced something genuinely valuable, not despite the constraints but through them.

Breaking It Down

The economic mechanism — Black tenants in Harlem paid 40–60% more than white tenants in comparable apartments in other neighborhoods. This was not market pricing; it was extraction made possible by segregation. The rent party was a community response: mutual aid, self-organized, operating outside any formal institution. One family's emergency became the neighborhood's Saturday night. That is not a trivial achievement.

"Entirely Black spaces — incubators for the rawest, most authentic blues and jazz" — This matters culturally because the music that emerged from rent parties was not shaped by white audience expectations, white venue owners' commercial calculations, or the desire to be "respectable." It was made by Black musicians for Black audiences in Black homes. The Cotton Club — Harlem's most famous jazz venue — famously excluded Black patrons. Rent parties were the counter-institution, invisible to white Harlem, where the music developed on its own terms.

Why This Matters

The rent party is a small-scale version of the argument that runs through the entire lecture. A system designed to extract and exclude produced, in response, a set of self-organized institutions — the rent party, the Black press, the UNIA, eventually the Greenwood district — that were more vital, more creative, and more durable than the system's architects intended. The capacity to build within and despite constraints is one of the Renaissance's defining features. Hughes saw it clearly in the rent party and said so in verse.

"The Weary Blues" (1926)

Droning a drowsy syncopated tune,
Rocking back and forth to a mellow croon,
     I heard a Negro play.
Down on Lenox Avenue the other night
By the pale dull pallor of an old gas light
     He did a lazy sway. . . .
     He did a lazy sway. . . .
To the tune o' those Weary Blues.
With his ebony hands on each ivory key
He made that poor piano moan with melody.
     O Blues!

📖 Understanding This Slide

Read this poem slowly. Better yet, read it aloud. The analysis comes on the next slide; this slide is for experiencing the formal achievement before you dissect it. Notice the repetition ("He did a lazy sway . . . / He did a lazy sway . . ."), the visual imagery ("ebony hands on each ivory key"), the musical reference embedded in the syntax itself. Hughes is not describing a blues performance. He is staging one.

Breaking It Down

Lenox Avenue — Harlem's central boulevard, the spine of its nightlife and street culture. Setting the poem here is a specific geographic assertion: this is Black urban space, and it is the appropriate setting for art of the highest order. Not a concert hall. Not a university auditorium. Lenox Avenue, by the light of an old gas lamp, where a man plays blues for no audience in particular.

"With his ebony hands on each ivory key" — This image is doing double work. On the surface it describes a pianist. Beneath the surface, it puts Black hands on white keys and makes them moan with melody — a racial dynamic encoded in a physical image. Hughes was not naive about the imagery he chose.

The sound of the poem — "Droning a drowsy syncopated tune" — the alliteration slows the line down, makes it feel languid, makes it perform the "drowsy" quality it describes. "Rocking back and forth to a mellow croon" — the rhythm rocks. This is not an accident. Hughes was a formally sophisticated poet who made every word and every sound choice count.

Why This Matters

The Weary Blues won first prize in Opportunity's 1925 literary contest and launched Hughes's career. Its significance lies precisely in what it refused to do: it refused to explain the blues to an outside audience, refused to apologize for its subject matter, refused to frame Black street life as a problem to be solved. It treated a Harlem musician with the same seriousness that European poets treated their subjects. That was, in 1926, a genuinely radical act.

  • The blues was not, in 1926, considered "high art" by mainstream white culture
  • Hughes said: it is

The form is the argument

📖 Understanding This Slide

"The form is the argument" — this is one of the most important analytical statements in the entire deck. It means that Hughes didn't just write a poem about the blues and then make a separate argument that the blues was serious art. The poem's own formal properties — its rhythm, its sound, its structure — enacted the blues in literary language. The form and the argument are the same thing. Understanding this is the difference between reading Hughes as a chronicler of Black life and reading him as a theorist of Black culture.

Breaking It Down

"The blues was not, in 1926, considered 'high art' by mainstream white culture" — This historical context is essential. The blues was associated with the rural South, with poverty, with a tradition that the white cultural mainstream viewed as primitive at best. Jazz had somewhat more status, but only in venues like the Cotton Club, which commodified Black performance for white audiences. The idea that a blues musician on Lenox Avenue was a legitimate subject for serious literary treatment was not a consensus view in 1926 — it was a claim.

"Hughes said: it is" — Three words. The confident brevity of that slide point mirrors the confident brevity of Hughes's own position. He didn't argue at length that the blues deserved respect. He wrote a poem that treated it as worthy of the full apparatus of literary craft, and let that treatment make the case.

Why This Matters

The principle "the form is the argument" extends beyond Hughes. Aaron Douglas made the same argument visually — African design traditions embedded in modernist painting didn't just depict Black heritage; the fusion of African and European form asserted that both traditions were legitimate and could be synthesized as equals. Van Der Zee's portrait photography didn't just show Black Harlemites; the formal grammar of the dignified portrait — borrowed from mainstream studio photography — asserted Black dignity through the form's own conventions. Across every medium in the Harlem Renaissance, the form carries the argument.

"I, Too" (1926)

I, too, sing America.

I am the darker brother.
They send me to eat in the kitchen
When company comes,
But I laugh,
And eat well,
And grow strong.

Tomorrow,
I'll be at the table
When company comes.
Nobody'll dare
Say to me,
"Eat in the kitchen,"
Then.

Besides,
They'll see how beautiful I am
And be ashamed —

I, too, am America.
— Langston Hughes, I, Too, 1926

📖 Understanding This Slide

Read this poem carefully — and notice what it is not doing. It is not asking. It is not appealing to conscience. It is not arguing that Black Americans deserve a place at the table. It is announcing that they will be there. The poem's grammar and tense structure are doing as much work as its images. Before you look at the analysis slide, try to identify yourself: what is the poem's emotional register? What does the speaker feel? What is the speaker's posture toward the people who "send him to eat in the kitchen"?

Breaking It Down

"I, too, sing America" — The opening line positions the poem in direct conversation with Walt Whitman's "I Hear America Singing" (1860). Whitman catalogued American workers — carpenters, masons, mechanics — as the voice of the nation. Hughes inserts the word "too": I also sing America. I am also part of this. The "also" is the argument; the poem claims inclusion in the American tradition by simply asserting it.

"They send me to eat in the kitchen / When company comes" — The domestic setting is precise. This is not abstract oppression; it is the specific, daily humiliation of being made invisible in the house where you live and work. The "company" are white guests whose comfort requires that the "darker brother" disappear. Hughes chooses a domestic scene rather than a legal or political one, which makes the exclusion feel intimate and immediate rather than systemic and abstract.

"Tomorrow, / I'll be at the table" — Future tense. Not "I should be at the table." Not "I deserve to be at the table." I will be. The confidence is the point. The poem doesn't argue for inclusion; it predicts it as a certainty.

Why This Matters

"I, Too" became one of the defining texts of the Harlem Renaissance because it crystallized the New Negro's emotional posture in twenty lines. It ends with the speaker asserting not that he will be allowed at the table but that "they'll see how beautiful I am / and be ashamed." The shame belongs to the people who excluded him — not to the excluded person. That reversal is the poem's deepest move, and it is entirely characteristic of the Renaissance's refusal to accept the evaluative framework of the people who were doing the excluding.

"I, Too, Am America"

  • Twelve lines. The New Negro claim in a single sentence.
  • Not: please include me. Not: I have proven my worthiness.
  • "I, too, am America" — already, inherently, without your permission
  • The tense: tomorrow — not describing the present; announcing the future
That confidence — the refusal to accept the current arrangement as permanent — is the emotional core of the Renaissance

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide unpacks what "I, Too" is doing grammatically and philosophically. The analysis is clean and precise: the poem's argument lives in its tense structure, not primarily in its imagery. Understanding that makes you a more sophisticated reader of any text — not just this poem.

Breaking It Down

"Not: please include me. Not: I have proven my worthiness." — This is the critical contrast. The petition model of civil rights rhetoric — "we have demonstrated our fitness, please grant us rights" — runs from Frederick Douglass through Booker T. Washington in various forms. Hughes refuses it entirely. The poem does not make a case. It makes a declaration. The difference is not merely rhetorical; it encodes a fundamentally different relationship to the people being addressed.

"Already, inherently, without your permission" — This is the New Negro position in three phrases. The claim to American identity and humanity does not require external validation. It is not contingent on performance, approval, or demonstration. It exists prior to any other party's acknowledgment of it. "I, too, am America" — not "I have earned the right to be called American" or "I would like to be included in America" but simply: I am.

The tense: tomorrow — announcing the future, not describing the present — The poem lives in future tense deliberately. The present is the kitchen. The future is the table. Hughes is not confused about which is which. He is asserting that the kitchen is temporary and the table is inevitable. That confidence — stated as fact rather than argued as possibility — is the poem's most subversive move.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "the refusal to accept the current arrangement as permanent" — is the emotional core of not just this poem but the entire Harlem Renaissance. Every major figure — Hughes, Hurston, Douglas, Du Bois, even Garvey — shared this conviction: the current arrangement is not the final arrangement. What divided them was the question of how to get from here to there. But the refusal to accept permanence is the common ground beneath all their disagreements, and it is what made the Renaissance a movement rather than just a collection of talented individuals.

What is "The Weary Blues" saying? The skill this question develops — asking "how does the form of this thing support its argument?" — is transferable to every medium in this unit. Form is never accidental. It is always chosen. And the choices always mean something.

Tulsa 1921

The cost of visibility — and the depth of resilience

📖 Understanding This Slide

The subtitle holds two ideas in tension: "the cost of visibility" and "the depth of resilience." The Tulsa section will not let you celebrate the Renaissance's achievement without confronting what that achievement cost — and what it could not protect against. But neither will it leave you only with the destruction. Both halves of the subtitle matter equally, and neither cancels the other out.

Black Wall Street — What Was Built

  • Greenwood district, Tulsa, Oklahoma — the most concentrated demonstration of Black entrepreneurial achievement in American history
  • Banks, hotels, law offices, medical practices, two high schools, a library, a newspaper — the Tulsa Star
  • Property values exceeded $1.5 million in 1921 dollars — built in one generation by people whose parents had been enslaved

Greenwood — Black Wall Street · Tulsa, Oklahoma · Documentary

📖 Understanding This Slide

Greenwood is the Harlem Renaissance's economic equivalent — and in some ways its most striking achievement, because it was built in a smaller city, in the South, in a state with its own violently enforced racial hierarchy. What Harlem was to culture, Greenwood was to economics: a demonstration, built in one generation, that the capacities Jim Crow claimed Black Americans lacked were real and fully operational when the system stepped back far enough to allow them expression.

Breaking It Down

"Most concentrated demonstration of Black entrepreneurial achievement in American history" — This is a precise claim, not rhetorical inflation. Greenwood contained banks, medical and dental practices, law offices, hotels, two high schools, a public library, theaters, grocery stores, churches, and its own newspaper — the Tulsa Star, edited by A.J. Smitherman, which covered both local affairs and national civil rights news. All of this in a single district of a mid-sized Oklahoma city, built between roughly 1905 and 1921.

$1.5 million in 1921 dollars — Adjust for inflation and you are looking at roughly $25–30 million in today's terms, accumulated in property alone by people whose parents and grandparents had been enslaved. The economic achievement was not incidental to Greenwood's significance; it was the argument. Greenwood was proof that the claims White supremacy made about Black incapacity — economic, intellectual, institutional — were false.

"Black dollars circulated multiple times before leaving" — Community leaders in Greenwood actively promoted this multiplier effect, encouraging residents to spend within the district before taking money outside it. This is a sophisticated economic strategy — local recirculation of capital — that community organizations promoted explicitly. It was not accidental; it was designed.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "A proof of concept. Not an exception — a demonstration" — is the interpretive key. The conventional textbook framing of Greenwood treats it as a remarkable exception: look how much Black Americans achieved despite everything. This course frames it differently: Greenwood is evidence about capacity, not a lucky anomaly. It was replicable — was being replicated in Durham, North Carolina ("Black Wall Street of the East"), in Richmond, in other cities. What destroyed Greenwood was not Black incapacity. It was white violence.

🎓 Historians Weigh In

Scott Ellsworth — Death in a Promised Land: The Tulsa Race Riot of 1921

Ellsworth's 1982 study was the first major academic history of the Tulsa massacre, written when most Tulsans still refused to acknowledge it had happened. He documented both the scale of Greenwood's achievement and the deliberateness of its destruction, arguing that the massacre was not a spontaneous outbreak of racial hatred but a targeted attack on a specific, visible demonstration of Black economic success. His work is the foundation on which all subsequent Tulsa scholarship rests.

May 31 – June 1, 1921

  • Dick Rowland — a disputed incident set in motion by a newspaper headline and years of white resentment
  • White mob — eventually thousands, some deputized by local police — moved on Greenwood with firearms and torches
  • 35 city blocks burned: homes, banks, churches, schools, the hospital, the Tulsa Star
  • Estimated 100–300 Black Tulsans killed · 10,000 left homeless · 6,000 arrested — the victims, not the perpetrators

📖 Understanding This Slide

The Tulsa Massacre of 1921 is one of the worst episodes of racial violence in American history — and one of the most deliberately suppressed. The numbers on this slide need to sit with you for a moment. 35 city blocks. 100–300 dead. 10,000 left homeless. 6,000 arrested — the victims, not the perpetrators. And the final line: not a single prosecution. This is not a story about how justice moved slowly. It is a story about how government actively participated in and protected the destruction.

Breaking It Down

Dick Rowland and the trigger — A nineteen-year-old Black shoeshiner entered an elevator operated by a white woman. What happened was never established — she declined to press charges. The Tulsa Tribune published a sensationalized headline. By nightfall a white mob had gathered at the courthouse. A group of Black veterans, determined not to allow a lynching, went armed to the courthouse. A confrontation broke out. What followed was not a "riot" in the usual sense. It was an organized assault on a neighborhood — with firearms, with torches, and with aircraft that some eyewitness accounts describe as firing on Greenwood from above.

Local government participation — The slide notes that some members of the white mob were deputized by local police during the attack. The National Guard was eventually called in — not to protect Greenwood but to intern Black residents. Local officials subsequently worked to prevent Greenwood's rebuilding by changing zoning laws. The massacre was not tolerated by authorities; in significant ways it was organized and protected by them.

"Not a single white rioter was ever prosecuted" — This is the culminating fact. The legal system — which had already demonstrated its hostility to Black rights through its failure to prosecute lynchings — failed again, completely, in the face of documented mass murder. The 6,000 Black residents arrested in the aftermath were the victims of the attack. This inversion of justice was the system functioning exactly as designed.

Why This Matters

Tulsa sits in direct dialogue with Garvey's critique of Du Bois's integrationist strategy. Du Bois argued that excellence, citizenship, and institutional achievement would earn recognition and protection. Greenwood was excellence and institutional achievement in concentrated form. It was destroyed in forty-eight hours with government complicity, and its destruction went unpunished. What conclusion does that evidence support? The lecture does not answer that question for you — but Pause & Reflect IV will ask you to wrestle with it directly.

The Suppression of Memory

  • Oklahoma textbooks omitted the massacre for decades — survivors pressured into silence
  • City of Tulsa refused to compensate residents; actively blocked insurance claims
  • One of the most deliberate acts of historical erasure in American history
  • Many Americans first encountered it through the TV series Watchmen in 2019
The decision to suppress it was itself a political act — and it failed. Oral history kept the truth alive inside the community.

📖 Understanding This Slide

Historical erasure is not a passive process — it requires active effort. The suppression of the Tulsa Massacre is a case study in how official history gets constructed, and in the limits of that construction. The story survived because the Black community kept it alive through oral tradition, even when the textbooks, the courts, and the city government all pretended it hadn't happened.

Breaking It Down

Suppression mechanisms — Oklahoma textbooks omitted the massacre for decades. The city of Tulsa refused to compensate survivors and actively obstructed insurance claims. Newspapers stopped covering it. Social pressure discouraged survivors from speaking publicly. These were not passive omissions; they were sustained, organized efforts to prevent the event from entering official memory.

The failure of suppression — And yet it failed. Survivors talked to their children and grandchildren. Community memory survived. Historians like Scott Ellsworth began documenting it in the 1970s and '80s. A formal Oklahoma state investigation in 2001 produced a detailed report. The HBO series Watchmen (2019) depicted the massacre in its opening sequence and introduced it to a national audience that had never encountered it in school. The centennial commemorations in 2021 brought national media coverage.

"Oral history kept the truth alive inside the community" — This is worth pausing on. Oral history is often treated as a lesser form of historical evidence — less reliable than documents, less authoritative than archives. The Tulsa case demonstrates the opposite: when official institutions systematically suppress a historical truth, oral tradition within affected communities may be the only mechanism keeping that truth alive. The survivors and their descendants were the archive.

Why This Matters

The suppression of the Tulsa Massacre raises a question about historical knowledge itself: how many similar events have we not recovered? Tulsa survived in memory partly because it was so large that total suppression was impossible. Smaller events — individual lynchings, local acts of racial terror, community destructions in smaller towns — may have been suppressed more completely. The history we know is shaped by what was allowed to survive. That is a challenge to anyone who assumes official history tells the whole story.

Visibility as Target

Greenwood's visible prosperity made it a target, not a protection. The massacre was, at least in part, a response to Black success — to the undeniable evidence that white supremacy's claims about Black incapacity were false.
The same excellence the Renaissance celebrated could, under conditions of racial terror, become exposure rather than power.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the hardest slide in the deck — and one of the most important. It asks you to hold two things simultaneously that feel like they should resolve into one: excellence is genuinely empowering, and excellence under conditions of racial terror can make you a target. Both things are true. The massacre did not prove that Greenwood's achievement was worthless. It proved that achievement without political protection is vulnerable. Those are different conclusions.

Breaking It Down

"At least in part a response to Black success" — Historians of Tulsa have documented that white resentment of Greenwood's visible prosperity was a significant factor in the intensity of the attack. The district was called "Little Africa" dismissively by white Tulsans, and there is evidence that its economic success generated the kind of resentment that sees someone else's achievement as a grievance. This pattern is not unique to Tulsa; it appears in other episodes of anti-Black violence in American history, including the destruction of Rosewood, Florida (1923) and the Colfax Massacre (1873).

"Exposure rather than power" — This is the tension the slide insists you sit with. The Harlem Renaissance celebrated visibility — the right to be seen, the assertion of full humanity through cultural production. Van Der Zee's portraits, Hughes's poems, Aaron Douglas's murals all make Black Americans visible in ways the dominant culture refused to. But Greenwood was also visible — visibly successful, visibly thriving — and that visibility made it a target. Visibility is not neutrally empowering. It depends on the conditions in which it operates.

Why This Matters

Students trained in a framework where cultural representation is straightforwardly empowering will find this slide uncomfortable. Good. The discomfort is the point. Representation matters. Visibility matters. Cultural assertion matters. And none of those things is sufficient protection against organized political violence backed by state power. Holding both of those truths simultaneously, without letting either cancel the other, is what the lecture asks of you.

  • Within years, Greenwood had recovered enough commercial infrastructure to continue functioning as a Black economic hub

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide provides the counterweight to "Visibility as Target." If the previous slide complicated the celebration of excellence, this one reclaims what can be reclaimed — without pretending that the destruction didn't happen and without romanticizing survival as an adequate substitute for justice. The rebuilding of Greenwood was a genuine statement. It was also not sufficient. The lecture holds both.

Breaking It Down

"Many Black Tulsans returned and began rebuilding within months" — This is historically documented and genuinely remarkable. The people who returned had just watched their homes and businesses burned, watched members of their community killed, been interned by the National Guard, received no compensation, and faced active obstruction of insurance claims. They came back anyway. That decision was not passive or inevitable — it was a choice, and it carried meaning.

"The decision to rebuild was itself a statement" — This is the interpretive point. The perpetrators of the massacre expected — and some explicitly intended — that Greenwood would not recover, that the destruction would be permanent, that the community would scatter. Rebuilding was a refusal to let that intention stand. It said: you can destroy what we built, but you cannot destroy the capacity that built it.

"Build your own, even when what you built has been destroyed" — This connects Greenwood's rebuilding to the Great Migration, to the Harlem Renaissance, to the entire arc of the lecture. The pattern is consistent: when the existing system excludes you, you build your own. When what you built is destroyed, you build again. That pattern is the deepest argument the Renaissance makes.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "What no act of destruction can ultimately reach is the capacity and the will to build again" — is close to this course's deepest interpretive claim about the Harlem Renaissance. The art, the poetry, the institutions, the music all outlasted the violence directed against them. Not because violence failed — it succeeded at destroying specific things. But the generative capacity that produced those things survived. Hughes's poems survived. Hurston's novel survived. The proof of concept that was Greenwood survived in the historical record and in the imagination of subsequent generations. That is a form of permanence that violence cannot reach.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Greenwood was built through exactly the self-reliance and excellence the New Negro movement celebrated. It was destroyed in two days by a white mob, with the cooperation of local government. What does Tulsa tell us about the relationship between cultural and economic achievement and political power?

  1. Excellence is sufficient — Greenwood proved Black capacity regardless of the outcome
  2. Excellence without political power is permanently vulnerable
  3. Rebuilding after the massacre was the more important achievement than building before it
  4. Both A and B are true simultaneously, and that tension has never been resolved

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the most analytically demanding Pause & Reflect in the deck because it does not have a clean correct answer — and it says so explicitly. Option D is the most intellectually honest response, and the question is designed to bring you to it. But notice that Option D is not a comfortable resolution; it is a statement that two contradictory things are simultaneously true and that the contradiction has never been resolved. That is the actual state of the historical question.

Breaking It Down

Option A — "Excellence is sufficient — Greenwood proved Black capacity regardless of the outcome" — This position has real intellectual content. Greenwood demonstrated, beyond any serious dispute, that the white supremacist claim about Black incapacity was false. That demonstration entered the historical record permanently. The massacre did not erase the proof — it confirmed it by showing that the proof had to be violently suppressed. In that sense, Greenwood's achievement was not negated by its destruction.

Option B — "Excellence without political power is permanently vulnerable" — This position also has real intellectual content, and the evidence of Tulsa supports it directly. Greenwood had no meaningful political protection. Oklahoma's Black residents could not vote in any practical sense; federal anti-lynching legislation had failed repeatedly; local government actively participated in the attack. The excellence was real and the vulnerability was real simultaneously.

Option C — "Rebuilding after the massacre was the more important achievement" — This is the most sentimental option and the least analytically satisfying, though it points toward something real. Resilience matters. But "more important" is a false hierarchy — both the building and the rebuilding mattered, and neither was more important than the other.

Option D — "Both A and B are true simultaneously, and that tension has never been resolved" — This is the honest answer, and the one the course endorses. Excellence without political power is vulnerable. And excellence that has been destroyed retains its evidentiary value regardless. Both are true. The tension between them is not a puzzle to be solved but a condition to be understood.

Why This Matters

Students who have been trained to expect historical questions to have correct answers will find Option D unsatisfying. That discomfort is productive. The relationship between cultural achievement and political power is one of the most contested questions in the history of social movements — not just in the Harlem Renaissance but in virtually every context where marginalized communities have confronted structural exclusion. The Renaissance does not answer this question. It poses it with extraordinary clarity.

📖 Understanding This Slide

"They chose to build rather than beg" — this is the lecture's thesis in six words. Every major figure and movement covered in Part A — the Great Migration, Harlem's institutional density, the New Negro manifesto, Hughes's aesthetic choices, Garvey's economic ambitions, Greenwood's commercial infrastructure, the rebuilding after Tulsa — all of them share this orientation. Not petition. Not appeal to conscience. Construction. The closing slides will now draw that thread together.

Heroic Resistance Through Excellence

The Harlem Renaissance was never merely a cultural flowering in one neighborhood. It was a deliberate, collective assertion that Black Americans would not wait for permission to create, to build, or to claim their full humanity.
  • The Great Migration — a calculated departure from a system designed to be inescapable
  • Harlem and Greenwood — entrepreneurial vision that built cultural capitals and entire economies in one generation
  • Hughes and Hurston — trusted their own vernacular traditions without apology, without translation, without permission
The art, the music, the poetry, the institutions outlasted the violence. They chose to build rather than beg.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This is the lecture's synthesis statement — its thesis made explicit after the evidence has been laid out. Read it carefully. The phrase "heroic resistance through excellence" is a deliberate reframe of how the Harlem Renaissance is typically presented. The conventional framing emphasizes the cultural achievement. This framing insists on its political character — it was a form of resistance — while also insisting on its specifically cultural method: excellence itself as the instrument.

Breaking It Down

"Never merely a cultural flowering in one neighborhood" — The word "merely" is doing important work. A "cultural flowering" sounds beautiful and historically interesting but politically passive — something that happened, not something that argued. The lecture rejects that framing. The Renaissance was a deliberate, collective assertion with political content and political stakes. Calling it a "cultural flowering" is not wrong; it's incomplete.

"Would not wait for permission to create, to build, or to claim their full humanity" — Three parallel verbs: create, build, claim. All three are active. None of them are verbs of supplication. The Renaissance didn't ask for permission to be excellent; it was excellent. It didn't ask for permission to build institutions; it built them. It didn't ask for recognition of its humanity; it claimed it. That posture of non-petitionary assertion is what "heroic" means in this context.

Great Migration, Harlem and Greenwood, Hughes and Hurston — These three examples represent three scales of the same argument: population-level self-determination (the Migration), community-level institutional construction (Harlem, Greenwood), and individual-level aesthetic assertion (Hughes, Hurston). The argument operates at all three scales simultaneously, and that breadth is what makes it a movement rather than a collection of individual achievements.

Why This Matters

The interpretive frame of "heroic resistance through excellence" pushes against two conventional distortions. One is the victimhood narrative — the story of Black Americans as people things happened to, acted upon by history rather than acting within it. The other is the liberal triumphalism narrative — the story of steady progress toward inclusion, with the Renaissance as one chapter in an arc bending toward justice. This lecture insists on a third frame: people under conditions of systematic oppression choosing, deliberately, to build rather than beg, and producing work that outlasted both the oppression and the era. That is a more demanding and more honest frame than either alternative.

What Excellence Could and Couldn't Do

The art, the poetry, and the institutions they left behind permanently changed what America understood itself to be. The Harlem Renaissance did not end Jim Crow — but it changed the terms on which the fight would be waged.
  • Violence, segregation, and exclusion remained brutal realities — Tulsa made that painfully clear
  • Yet the work outlasted those realities. Hughes's poetry. Hurston's novel. Greenwood's proof of concept.
  • Jim Crow fell to the NAACP's legal strategy and the Montgomery Bus Boycott — built on a foundation this generation laid
Self-reliance, cultural confidence, and institutional creativity produced work no act of suppression could permanently destroy.

📖 Understanding This Slide

This slide completes the honest accounting that the lecture has been building toward. It does not resolve the tension between Option A and Option B from Pause & Reflect IV — it holds both. The art changed American culture permanently. Jim Crow persisted for decades. The Civil Rights Movement eventually succeeded through legal and political strategy. The foundation for that strategy was laid by this generation. All of these things are true simultaneously, and no single one of them is the whole story.

Breaking It Down

"The art, the poetry, and the institutions permanently changed what America understood itself to be" — This is a strong but defensible claim. Before the Harlem Renaissance, mainstream American culture largely agreed with the white supremacist evaluation of Black cultural production as primitive, derivative, or marginal. After it, that claim became increasingly difficult to sustain — not because white attitudes changed quickly (they didn't) but because the evidence was now in the permanent record. You cannot unknow that Louis Armstrong existed, that Langston Hughes wrote those poems, that Aaron Douglas painted those murals.

"Did not end Jim Crow — but changed the terms on which the fight would be waged" — This is the most precise statement of the Renaissance's political legacy. It did not win the Civil Rights struggle. It created the cultural and intellectual foundation without which that struggle would have been fought from a weaker position. The NAACP's legal strategy, the language of dignity and equality that Martin Luther King deployed, the self-confidence of the students who sat at lunch counters — all of it was shaped by the generation that produced the Renaissance.

"Jim Crow fell to the NAACP's legal strategy and the Montgomery Bus Boycott — built on a foundation this generation laid" — This is a historically specific causal claim. The NAACP was founded in 1909 by figures including Du Bois. Its legal strategy, executed by Thurgood Marshall, culminated in Brown v. Board in 1954. The Montgomery Bus Boycott of 1955–56 drew on organizational infrastructure and intellectual frameworks that the Renaissance generation had built. The connection is direct and documentable.

Why This Matters

The bottom line — "Self-reliance, cultural confidence, and institutional creativity produced work no act of suppression could permanently destroy" — is this lecture's counter to a particular kind of pessimism. Tulsa demonstrates that excellence without political power is vulnerable. This slide demonstrates that what excellence produces can survive its own destruction. The poems didn't burn when Greenwood burned. The music didn't disappear when Garvey was deported. The arguments didn't die when Du Bois was harassed by the FBI in his old age. The creative output outlasted every attempt to suppress it. That is worth calling a victory, even while acknowledging what it was not.

The Question You Leave With

Is cultural excellence the foundation on which political change becomes possible?

Or does it operate on a separate track — producing its own permanent transformation in the human record while political systems change through other means?

The Harlem Renaissance raises this question with extraordinary clarity. It does not answer it. That's why it's still worth arguing about.

📖 Understanding This Slide

The lecture ends not with an answer but with a question — deliberately. This is not intellectual evasion; it is intellectual honesty. The relationship between cultural achievement and political change is genuinely unsettled, and any course that pretends otherwise is doing its students a disservice. The Harlem Renaissance is historically valuable precisely because it poses this question so clearly that you cannot avoid it.

Breaking It Down

"Is cultural excellence the foundation on which political change becomes possible?" — This is the Du Bois-adjacent position, and there is historical evidence for it. The Renaissance generated cultural authority that the Civil Rights Movement drew on. Dignity, self-confidence, the established fact of Black artistic genius — all of these made certain arguments easier to make in 1955 than they would have been in 1905. In this reading, the Renaissance was a precondition for later political victories.

"Or does it operate on a separate track — producing its own permanent transformation in the human record while political systems change through other means?" — This is the more radical reading, and in some ways the more honest one. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 was won by lawyers, organizers, boycotters, marchers, and legislators — not by poets. Hughes's poems did not desegregate schools. The Montgomery Bus Boycott was organized through Black churches, not through Harlem literary salons. Maybe cultural achievement and political change operate through different mechanisms, and the error is to conflate them.

The New Negro

A term crystallized by philosopher Alain Locke in his 1925 anthology The New Negro, though it had circulated in Black intellectual culture since the 1890s. The New Negro rejected the older model of Black leadership that sought white approval and tried to prove worthiness by meeting white standards. Instead, the New Negro claimed the full complexity of Black experience as legitimate subject matter for art and demanded recognition — not as a favor, but as a right. Urban, educated, self-possessed, and unapologetic, the New Negro did not explain or justify Black humanity to white audiences. The New Negro assumed it.

Jim Crow

The system of racial segregation and disenfranchisement that governed the American South from the end of Reconstruction through the Civil Rights era. Jim Crow was not merely a set of laws about who sat where on a bus. It was a total system — economic, legal, social, and violent — designed to ensure that the gains of Reconstruction never translated into actual equality. Black Americans in the South could not vote in any practical sense, could not own land on equal terms, were locked into sharecropping arrangements that kept them perpetually in debt, and lived under the constant threat of racial violence that was tolerated, and in some cases celebrated, by the surrounding white society.

The Chicago Defender

The most widely read Black newspaper in the country during the early 20th century, founded in Chicago in 1905 by Robert Abbott. The Defender ran active campaigns urging Black Southerners to migrate north, publishing stories of economic opportunity, higher wages, and relative freedom from racial violence. It was sometimes banned in Southern states, and copies were smuggled in on Pullman railroad cars. At its peak, the Defender had a national circulation of approximately 230,000 and was read by an estimated three times that many people. It was one of the primary engines of the Great Migration.

The Boll Weevil

A beetle that devastated Southern cotton crops beginning in the early 1910s, spreading from Texas eastward across the Cotton Belt. The boll weevil destroyed the economic foundation of sharecropping in many Southern counties, displacing hundreds of thousands of Black sharecroppers who had already been living in debt bondage. The weevil's destruction of the cotton economy was one of the most important economic push factors driving the Great Migration — combined with the pull of wartime industrial wages in the North, it accelerated Black movement out of the South dramatically in the mid-1910s.

Philip Payton Jr.

A Black real estate entrepreneur in New York who is credited with opening Harlem to Black residents. When Harlem's real estate market collapsed around 1904–1905 due to overbuilding, Payton convinced white landlords — who were desperate for tenants — to rent to Black families at premium rates. His company, the Afro-American Realty Company, managed and brokered dozens of Harlem properties. Once the door opened, it didn't close: Black residents moved in large numbers, building institutions, churches, and community organizations that made Harlem the cultural capital it became in the 1920s. Payton's intervention was, in retrospect, one of the most consequential real estate transactions in American cultural history.

Red Summer (1919)

The period from late spring through early fall of 1919 in which white mobs attacked Black communities in more than 25 cities across the United States. The violence was triggered by a combination of factors: Black veterans returning home with heightened expectations, competition for industrial jobs, and white anxieties about racial hierarchy in the postwar period. Major attacks occurred in Chicago (38 dead, 537 injured), Washington D.C., Elaine Arkansas (an estimated 100–240 Black Americans killed), and Knoxville, Tennessee. The term "Red Summer" was coined by writer and NAACP official James Weldon Johnson to describe the blood spilled in the violence.

Marcus Garvey (1887–1940)

Born in Jamaica, Garvey arrived in New York in 1916 and within four years had built the Universal Negro Improvement Association into the largest Black mass movement in American history. His message was a direct challenge to Du Bois's integrationism: rather than demanding inclusion in American society, Garvey argued that Black people should build their own institutions, achieve economic self-sufficiency, and ultimately return to Africa to establish a free Black nation. Federal authorities monitored him from 1919, alarmed by his capacity to mobilize Black Americans around a vision of independent power. He was convicted of mail fraud in 1923, imprisoned, and deported to Jamaica in 1927. His Pan-African vision prefigured movements from the Nation of Islam to Black Power that followed in subsequent decades.

Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA)

Founded by Marcus Garvey in Jamaica in 1914 and brought to New York in 1916, the UNIA at its peak claimed two to four million members worldwide — a figure historians debate, but even skeptical estimates place in the hundreds of thousands. The UNIA's newspaper, The Negro World, reached an international audience. Its Harlem parades — uniformed marchers, flags, brass bands moving through the streets — were public assertions of Black dignity at a moment when Black public life was systematically humiliated nearly everywhere else. The UNIA demonstrated that Black people could organize at mass scale, pool capital, and build institutions that required no white approval or white investment.

The Black Star Line

Incorporated by Marcus Garvey in 1919 through a stock offering aimed specifically at ordinary Black Americans, the Black Star Line was a shipping company designed to demonstrate that Black people could compete in global commerce. Thousands of Black Americans invested — many of them working-class families who could ill afford to risk their savings — because the company represented something worth more than a sound business investment: proof of possibility. The ships Garvey purchased were overpriced and mechanically unreliable; the company's management was chaotic. The Black Star Line collapsed in debt by 1924. Federal authorities who had been targeting Garvey prosecuted him for mail fraud in connection with the stock offering. The failure as a business does not exhaust its historical meaning — the attempt itself, and the mass investment it inspired, demonstrated a scale of Black economic ambition that had never been seen before.

The Rent Party

In 1920s Harlem, landlords charged Black tenants premium rates in a segregated housing market while Black workers earned substantially less than white workers for the same labor. Many families couldn't reliably cover monthly rent on a single paycheck. The solution was communal and inventive: the rent party. A family in need would throw a Saturday night apartment party, charge a small admission, serve home-cooked food, hire a piano player or small band, and collect enough to make the rent. Rent parties were entirely Black spaces, invisible to white Harlem, and they functioned simultaneously as fundraisers, neighborhood social institutions, and incubators for the blues and jazz that Hughes celebrated. Langston Hughes saw rent parties as perfect symbols of Black self-reliance — people systematically excluded from mainstream venues had built their own vibrant world, not as a consolation prize but as a genuine alternative.

Greenwood and the Tulsa Star

The Greenwood district of Tulsa, Oklahoma was a self-contained Black urban economy built over two decades through mutual aid, reinvestment, and deliberate community building. At its peak in 1921 it contained over 300 businesses including banks, hotels, law offices, medical and dental practices, grocery stores, theaters, two high schools, a public library, multiple churches, and the Tulsa Star — Greenwood's own newspaper, edited by A.J. Smitherman, which covered local news and national civil rights issues. Black dollars were estimated to circulate through Greenwood businesses an average of thirty-six to one hundred times before leaving the community, a multiplier effect that community leaders consciously promoted. Property values in the district exceeded $1.5 million in 1921 dollars — an enormous accumulation of wealth built from effectively nothing by people whose parents had been enslaved.

Dick Rowland and the Trigger

Dick Rowland was a nineteen-year-old Black shoeshiner who on May 30, 1921, entered an elevator in a downtown Tulsa office building operated by a white woman named Sarah Page. What happened in that elevator was never established — Page herself declined to press charges, and the accusation of assault was almost certainly false or based on a misunderstanding. The Tulsa Tribune published a sensationalized account the following day under the headline "Nab Negro for Attacking Girl in Elevator." Within hours a white mob had gathered at the courthouse demanding Rowland be handed over. A group of Black Tulsans — many of them World War I veterans who had resolved not to allow another lynching — went to the courthouse armed. A confrontation broke out, shots were fired, and what followed was not a riot in any meaningful sense of the word. It was a massacre — the trigger was minor, but the destruction that followed reflected years of white resentment of Greenwood's visible success.

Historical Erasure

The deliberate suppression of a historical event from the public record — through omission from textbooks, absence from official commemoration, social pressure on survivors not to speak, and the simple passage of time. The Tulsa Race Massacre was almost entirely absent from Oklahoma history education for decades. Survivors were alive and living in Tulsa, but the community's grief was treated as a private matter, and local newspapers ceased to report on it. The suppression was not passive — it was active and sustained. Many survivors died without acknowledgment. The massacre began to re-enter public consciousness in the 1990s through the work of historians like Scott Ellsworth, was the subject of a formal Oklahoma state investigation in 2001, and became widely known nationally after the centennial commemorations in 2021 and — notably — after the HBO series Watchmen depicted it in its opening sequence in 2019.
