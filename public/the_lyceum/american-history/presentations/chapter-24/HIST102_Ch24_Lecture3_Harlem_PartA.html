Section I

The World That Made Harlem

Migration, war, and the breaking point

The World They Were Leaving

  • Jim Crow — a total system, not merely segregated seating
  • No practical right to vote; locked into sharecropping debt
  • Constant threat of racial violence — 4,000+ lynchings, 1877–1950
  • Lynchings were public spectacles: photographed, attended, sold as postcards
The purpose was terror — and it was effective

The Great Migration

  • 1910–1930: approximately 1.6 million Black Americans left the South
  • Destinations: Chicago, Detroit, Philadelphia, New York
  • Pull: wartime industrial labor demand; the Chicago Defender campaigns
  • Push: Jim Crow, sharecropping, the boll weevil, and violence
Not the Promised Land — but different. And different was enough.
📸 Search: "Great Migration African Americans 1917 photograph"
Source: Library of Congress

The Great Migration · 1910s–1930s · Public Domain

Why Harlem?

  • Overbuilt by speculators — white tenants never materialized
  • Philip Payton convinced landlords to rent to Black tenants: door opened, and didn't close
  • By early 1920s: largest Black urban community in the United States
  • Newspapers, churches, theaters, nightclubs, colleges, political organizations — density of institutions
Harlem didn't just grow — it developed a self-consciousness about itself as something new

The War's Broken Promise

  • 380,000 Black Americans served in World War One
  • Segregated units, dangerous assignments, officers who treated them as less than human
  • Du Bois argued: demonstrate patriotism — America will acknowledge citizenship
  • It did not. Black veterans were lynched in their uniforms
Red Summer 1919 — racial massacres in 25+ cities

We Return Fighting

"We return fighting. Make way for Democracy! We saved it in France, and by the Great Jehovah, we will save it in the United States of America, or know the reason why."
— W.E.B. Du Bois, Returning Soldiers, The Crisis, May 1919
Written in May 1919. The Red Summer began in July.

Du Bois's essay was a direct response to the broken promise of the war. What did his generation of Black Americans do with that rage?

  1. Accepted the existing racial hierarchy as permanent
  2. Emigrated to Africa under Marcus Garvey's program
  3. Produced one of the most extraordinary explosions of cultural creativity in American history
  4. Retreated from political engagement entirely

Section II

The New Negro

A demand, not a description

Alain Locke — The Manifesto

  • The New Negro anthology, 1925 — poetry, fiction, essays, visual art
  • Older generation: sought white approval, proved worthiness by white standards
  • New generation: "shaking off the psychology of imitation and implied inferiority"
  • The New Negro is urban, educated, self-possessed, unapologetic
Not petition. Assertion.

Who You Need to Know

  • Langston Hughes — the poet; blues as serious art

Section III

The Literature

Langston Hughes — the blues as serious art

Langston Hughes

  • Harlem-born, prolific, politically engaged
  • Deliberate choice: took Black vernacular culture seriously as subject matter
  • The blues, the jazz club, the rent party — not embarrassments to translate, but the material itself
  • Made the poem perform the music it described
📸 Search: "Langston Hughes poet portrait photograph 1920s"
Source: Library of Congress / Carl Van Vechten Collection

Langston Hughes · 1902–1967 · Poet of the Harlem Renaissance

"The Weary Blues" (1926)

Droning a drowsy syncopated tune,
Rocking back and forth to a mellow croon,
     I heard a Negro play.
Down on Lenox Avenue the other night
By the pale dull pallor of an old gas light
     He did a lazy sway. . . .
     He did a lazy sway. . . .
To the tune o' those Weary Blues.
With his ebony hands on each ivory key
He made that poor piano moan with melody.
     O Blues!
— Langston Hughes, The Weary Blues, 1926

"The Weary Blues" — What Hughes Did

  • A blues musician on Lenox Avenue — treated with the seriousness of classical poetry
  • The blues was not, in 1926, considered "high art" by mainstream white culture
  • Hughes said: it is
  • The form of the poem performs the music — it doesn't just describe it
The form is the argument

"I, Too" (1926)

I, too, sing America.

I am the darker brother.
They send me to eat in the kitchen
When company comes,
But I laugh,
And eat well,
And grow strong.

Tomorrow,
I'll be at the table
When company comes.
Nobody'll dare
Say to me,
"Eat in the kitchen,"
Then.

Besides,
They'll see how beautiful I am
And be ashamed —

I, too, am America.
— Langston Hughes, I, Too, 1926

"I, Too, Am America"

  • Twelve lines. The New Negro claim in a single sentence.
  • Not: please include me. Not: I have proven my worthiness.
  • "I, too, am America" — already, inherently, without your permission
  • The tense: tomorrow — not describing the present; announcing the future
That confidence — the refusal to accept the current arrangement as permanent — is the emotional core of the Renaissance

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Both poems make a claim about Black identity and Black culture. The Weary Blues makes its argument through form — the poem performs the music. I, Too makes its argument through tense and grammar. What is each poem saying, and how does its form support that argument?

Section IV

The Music

Jazz as the voice of the New Negro

Jazz Was Not Background Music

  • Emerged from African American musical traditions — spirituals, work songs, field hollers
  • By the 1920s: the most vital and innovative musical form in the world
  • Improvisation — spontaneous composition in real time — was itself a statement
  • When white critics called jazz "primitive", they were making a political argument
When Black musicians played jazz at the highest level of mastery, the music was the answer

The Voices of the Decade

Duke Ellington
  • Cotton Club residency, 1927
  • Radio broadcasts reached millions of white listeners
  • Composer, bandleader, architect of modern jazz
Louis Armstrong
  • West End Blues, 1928
  • Redefined what was possible on a trumpet
  • Improvisation as perfect inevitability
Bessie Smith
  • Empress of the Blues
  • Record sales proved Black audiences were a commercial force
  • Voice of the Great Migration

The Visual Art

Aaron Douglas, James Van Der Zee, and the visual language of the Renaissance

Aaron Douglas — Look Before You Read

  • Arrived Harlem 1925 — met Locke through Renaissance circles; contributed illustrations to The New Negro anthology that year
  • Silhouette figures — bold, flat, angular — unmistakably Black, unmistakably heroic

  • His figures: connected to a deep past, oriented toward the future

Section VI

The Debate Inside the Movement

Zora Neale Hurston and the question the Renaissance never resolved

Zora Neale Hurston

  • From Eatonville, Florida — one of the first all-Black incorporated towns in the U.S.
  • Studied anthropology under Franz Boas at Columbia
  • Went south to collect African American folk culture — stories, songs, speech
  • Mules and Men (1935): first African American folklore collected by a Black American

Whose Art Is It For?

Hughes & Hurston
  • Go to the folk tradition
  • Trust the vernacular as serious art
  • Don't translate for white audiences
  • Black culture has its own standards — it doesn't need white validation
Du Bois
  • All art is propaganda
  • Black artists performing for white audiences — on whose terms?
  • Who controls the means of distribution?
  • Cultural achievement ≠ political equality
This debate was never resolved. It is still alive today.

Du Bois, 1926

"I do not care a damn for any art that is not used for propaganda. But I do care when propaganda is confined to one side while the other is stripped and silent."
— W.E.B. Du Bois, Criteria of Negro Art, The Crisis, October 1926

Hughes said go to the folk tradition and stop seeking white approval. Du Bois said cultural achievement without political equality is insufficient. Can both be right at the same time?

  1. No — they are fundamentally incompatible positions
  2. Yes — they are addressing different problems within the same struggle
  3. The question itself is a false choice

Closing

The Right to Be Seen

What the Renaissance accomplished — and the question it couldn't answer

What the Renaissance Made

  • Hughes — made the blues into poetry; made Black vernacular life serious art
  • Ellington & Armstrong — demonstrated jazz mastery that refuted every claim of Black inferiority
  • Hurston — preserved folk traditions and made vernacular English a literary medium
  • Douglas & Van Der Zee — inserted fully human Black subjects into the permanent visual record
You cannot understand American culture in the 20th century without understanding what happened in Harlem in the 1920s

The Question That Remains

Can you change a society's politics by changing its culture? Or can a community produce extraordinary art — brilliant, world-altering art — and still be denied the vote, the mortgage, the job, the physical safety?
In Part B, you will read about what happened in Tulsa in 1921 — to a community so prosperous it was called Black Wall Street.

The New Negro

A term crystallized by philosopher Alain Locke in his 1925 anthology The New Negro, though it had circulated in Black intellectual culture since the 1890s. The New Negro rejected the older model of Black leadership that sought white approval and tried to prove worthiness by meeting white standards. Instead, the New Negro claimed the full complexity of Black experience as legitimate subject matter for art and demanded recognition — not as a favor, but as a right. Urban, educated, self-possessed, and unapologetic, the New Negro did not explain or justify Black humanity to white audiences. The New Negro assumed it.

×
Jim Crow

The system of racial segregation and disenfranchisement that governed the American South from the end of Reconstruction through the Civil Rights era. Jim Crow was not merely a set of laws about who sat where on a bus. It was a total system — economic, legal, social, and violent — designed to ensure that the gains of Reconstruction never translated into actual equality. Black Americans in the South could not vote in any practical sense, could not own land on equal terms, were locked into sharecropping arrangements that kept them perpetually in debt, and lived under the constant threat of racial violence that was tolerated, and in some cases celebrated, by the surrounding white society.

×
The Chicago Defender

The most widely read Black newspaper in the country during the early 20th century, founded in Chicago in 1905 by Robert Abbott. The Defender ran active campaigns urging Black Southerners to migrate north, publishing stories of economic opportunity, higher wages, and relative freedom from racial violence. It was sometimes banned in Southern states, and copies were smuggled in on Pullman railroad cars. At its peak, the Defender had a national circulation of approximately 230,000 and was read by an estimated three times that many people. It was one of the primary engines of the Great Migration.

×
The Boll Weevil

A beetle that devastated Southern cotton crops beginning in the early 1910s, spreading from Texas eastward across the Cotton Belt. The boll weevil destroyed the economic foundation of sharecropping in many Southern counties, displacing hundreds of thousands of Black sharecroppers who had already been living in debt bondage. The weevil's destruction of the cotton economy was one of the most important economic push factors driving the Great Migration — combined with the pull of wartime industrial wages in the North, it accelerated Black movement out of the South dramatically in the mid-1910s.

×
Philip Payton Jr.

A Black real estate entrepreneur in New York who is credited with opening Harlem to Black residents. When Harlem's real estate market collapsed around 1904–1905 due to overbuilding, Payton convinced white landlords — who were desperate for tenants — to rent to Black families at premium rates. His company, the Afro-American Realty Company, managed and brokered dozens of Harlem properties. Once the door opened, it didn't close: Black residents moved in large numbers, building institutions, churches, and community organizations that made Harlem the cultural capital it became in the 1920s. Payton's intervention was, in retrospect, one of the most consequential real estate transactions in American cultural history.

×
Red Summer (1919)

The period from late spring through early fall of 1919 in which white mobs attacked Black communities in more than 25 cities across the United States. The violence was triggered by a combination of factors: Black veterans returning home with heightened expectations, competition for industrial jobs, and white anxieties about racial hierarchy in the postwar period. Major attacks occurred in Chicago (38 dead, 537 injured), Washington D.C., Elaine Arkansas (an estimated 100–240 Black Americans killed), and Knoxville, Tennessee. The term "Red Summer" was coined by writer and NAACP official James Weldon Johnson to describe the blood spilled in the violence.

Marcus Garvey and Black Nationalism

Marcus Garvey (1887–1940) was a Jamaican-born Black nationalist leader who founded the Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA) in 1914. At its peak in the early 1920s, the UNIA claimed millions of members worldwide. Garvey's message was radical in its rejection of integration: rather than demanding inclusion in white American society, he argued that Black people should build their own institutions, achieve economic self-sufficiency, and ultimately return to Africa to build a Black nation. His Black Star Line shipping company and other enterprises were meant to demonstrate Black economic capability. He and Du Bois were bitter opponents — Du Bois called Garvey "the most dangerous enemy of the Negro race," while Garvey called Du Bois a "white man's Negro." Their disagreement reflected a genuine divide about strategy and goals that has never been fully resolved in Black political thought.

Improvisation in Jazz

Jazz improvisation is the creation of new musical material in real time during a performance — not reading from a written score but composing spontaneously within a structure (chord changes, rhythm, key) that all the musicians share. This requires extraordinary musical intelligence: the ability to hear what other musicians are playing, anticipate where they are going, and respond in the moment with melodic and rhythmic ideas that are both original and coherent. When critics in the 1920s called jazz "primitive," they were implying that improvisation was evidence of an inability to compose or execute written music — the opposite of what improvisation actually requires. The mastery of jazz improvisation by Armstrong, Ellington, and their contemporaries was, for those who understood it, a direct and decisive refutation of those claims.

Duke Ellington (1899–1974)

Edward Kennedy "Duke" Ellington was a pianist, composer, and bandleader who is widely considered one of the most important figures in the history of American music. Born in Washington D.C., he moved to New York in 1923 and began his long residency at the Cotton Club in 1927. His radio broadcasts from the Cotton Club introduced his music to millions of white Americans who had never visited Harlem. Ellington's compositions ranged from three-minute pop songs to extended orchestral suites, and his band featured virtuoso soloists whose individual voices he wove into sophisticated ensemble arrangements. He composed over 1,000 pieces, including "Sophisticated Lady," "Mood Indigo," and "Take the A Train."

×
Louis Armstrong (1901–1971)

Louis Armstrong was a trumpeter, vocalist, and bandleader from New Orleans who is widely regarded as the most important single figure in the history of jazz. His recording of "West End Blues" in 1928 — which opens with an unaccompanied trumpet cadenza of extraordinary invention and authority — is considered by many historians of jazz to be the moment when jazz announced itself as a serious art form. Armstrong's improvisations had a quality that his contemporaries and successors found almost inexplicable: spontaneous invention that sounded inevitable, as if the music could not have been any other way. His influence on every instrumentalist and vocalist who followed him is incalculable.

×
Bessie Smith (1894–1937)

Bessie Smith was a blues singer from Chattanooga, Tennessee, who became the highest-paid Black performer in the United States during the 1920s. Known as the "Empress of the Blues," she recorded for Columbia Records beginning in 1923, and her records sold in numbers that forced the recording industry to take Black audiences seriously as a commercial market. Her voice — powerful, expressive, technically masterful, and capable of conveying enormous emotional depth — was the defining sound of the classic blues era. Her recordings documented the experience of the Great Migration: leaving the South, surviving in Northern cities, navigating poverty and violence and love and loss. She died in 1937 in a car accident in Mississippi.

×
Eatonville, Florida

Incorporated in 1887, Eatonville is one of the first all-Black self-governing municipalities in the United States. Located near Orlando, it was founded by Black residents who purchased land and established their own town government, institutions, and community life. Zora Neale Hurston grew up in Eatonville, and its existence shaped her understanding of Black culture as a complete, self-sufficient world — not a deficient version of white culture but a tradition with its own standards, stories, and ways of being. This background distinguishes Hurston from many of her Harlem contemporaries, who had grown up in white-dominated environments and had a more complicated relationship with Black folk culture.

Du Bois on Propaganda and Art

When Du Bois said "all art is propaganda," he did not mean that art should be crude or didactic. He meant that art always serves some interest, embodies some argument, operates in some political context — and that pretending otherwise is itself a political act, usually one that serves the status quo. In the context of 1926, his claim was pointed: white American popular culture was producing and distributing images, stories, and music that argued for white supremacy — as entertainment, as common sense, as the default visual language of the society. Black artists who claimed to be making "pure art" were still operating in that context. The question, for Du Bois, was not whether your art had a political dimension, but whose interests it served.
