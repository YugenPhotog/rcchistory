Chapter 20: Urbanization

Race, Exclusion, and the Architecture of Belonging

How race, law, and culture structured who could belong in American cities

üìö Lecture Overview

This is lecture 2 in the Chapter 20 series on urbanization. Today we're exploring how American cities became sites of both diversity AND exclusion‚Äîspecifically focusing on Asian migration and the legal/cultural systems that racialized belonging.

üí≠ The Big Picture

When cities grew rapidly in the late 1800s, Americans had to decide: who gets to be part of this new urban society? The answer was determined by race more than anything else. While European immigrants eventually became "white" and gained access to citizenship, Asian migrants faced systematic exclusion through law, labor discrimination, and popular culture.

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Understand Asian migration as part of global labor circulation
  • Analyze how "coolie labor" became racialized
  • Trace the legal construction of racial exclusion (1875-1924)
  • Examine "Yellow Peril" discourse and its cultural impact
  • Recognize Asian American resistance and survival strategies

The Central Paradox

üîë Key Concept

Urbanization did not produce openness by default.

It produced boundaries.

As cities filled with newcomers, the United States increasingly defined itself not just by citizenship, but by race.

üìö What You Need to Know

It's tempting to think that when lots of different people move to cities, society becomes more open and accepting. But that's NOT what happened in late 1800s America. Instead, diversity created anxiety, and Americans responded by drawing harder lines about who belonged.

üí≠ Why This Matters

This lecture challenges the "melting pot" myth. Cities didn't automatically blend everyone together. Instead, immigration law, labor systems, and popular culture worked together to decide who could enter, stay, work, and belong. And those decisions were based on race.

üîë The Thesis

Pluralism expanded‚Äîbut selectively. Race set hard limits on inclusion.

Some immigrant groups (European) could eventually become "American." Others (Asian) were legally barred from citizenship and socially marked as permanent foreigners.

Part I

Asian Migration in Global Context

Understanding the transnational flow of labor

üìñ Section Overview

Before we talk about what happened to Asian migrants IN America, we need to understand WHERE they came from and WHY they moved. This wasn't a U.S.-specific story‚Äîit was global.

Decentering the United States

Asian migration was part of global labor circulation, not a U.S.-specific phenomenon.

  • Chinese emigration surged during the "Century of Revolution" (c. 1850‚Äì1949)
  • Migrants moved to: Southeast Asia, Hawaii, Latin America, Western United States
  • Driven by political instability, economic opportunity, and imperial expansion
×

Global Labor Circulation

In the late 1800s, capitalism and empire created massive flows of workers across continents. People moved:

  • From rural areas to mines, plantations, railroads, and cities
  • From colonized regions to imperial centers and frontier zones
  • From areas of political upheaval to places with labor demand

This was a global system. Chinese workers went to Peru, Cuba, Southeast Asia, Australia, and North America. Indian workers went to East Africa, the Caribbean, and Fiji. This movement was structured by capitalism (need for cheap labor) and empire (ability to move subjects across borders).

×

China's Century of Revolution (1850-1949)

This period saw massive upheaval in China:

  • Taiping Rebellion (1850-1864): Devastating civil war, ~20-30 million dead
  • Opium Wars: British and European imperialism forced open Chinese ports
  • Economic disruption: Traditional agriculture and trade collapsed in many regions
  • Qing Dynasty decline: Weakening central government couldn't maintain order
  • Republican Revolution (1911): Overthrow of imperial system

These crises pushed millions of Chinese to emigrate, seeking economic survival and safety.

üìö What You Need to Know

Chinese migration to the U.S. wasn't unique‚Äîit was part of a worldwide pattern. Millions of Chinese people left home during the 1800s because of war, economic collapse, and political instability.

üìñ Key Context: Why Did They Leave?

The Taiping Rebellion (1850-1864) was one of the deadliest wars in human history‚Äîpossibly 20-30 million people died. Imagine the American Civil War, but 10 times worse. Entire regions of southern China were devastated. At the same time, European powers (especially Britain) forced China to open its ports for trade, disrupting traditional economies. People left to survive.

üí≠ Important Distinction

Most Chinese migrants weren't "refugees" in the modern sense (fleeing direct persecution), and they weren't "immigrants" planning to stay permanently. They were sojourners‚Äîworkers who intended to earn money abroad and return home. Many maintained strong ties to their home villages, sent money back (remittances), and hoped to retire in China.

üåç Where Did They Go?

  • Southeast Asia: Largest destination‚Äîmillions went to Malaya, Singapore, Indonesia, Thailand
  • Americas: Peru, Cuba, California, British Columbia
  • Pacific: Hawaii, Australia, New Zealand

Sojourners, Not Settlers

üîë Key Concept: Sojourning

Chinese migrants were not refugees. Many were sojourners:

  • Intended to work temporarily and return home
  • Maintained strong homeland ties (remittances, kinship)
  • Viewed migration as a temporary economic strategy

Asian migration was transnational, circular, and deeply shaped by empire and capitalism.

üìö Why "Sojourner" Matters

The term "sojourner" is crucial for understanding how Chinese migrants saw themselves AND how white Americans saw them. A sojourner is someone who lives somewhere temporarily, intending to return home.

üí≠ What This Meant in Practice

  • Economic strategy: Work hard for a few years, save money, return wealthy
  • Family connections: Many left wives and children in China, sent money home regularly
  • Cultural maintenance: Didn't try to "become American" because they planned to go back
  • Circular migration: Some made multiple trips back and forth

ü§î The Paradox

White Americans complained that Chinese migrants "refused to assimilate"‚Äîbut U.S. law made assimilation IMPOSSIBLE by barring them from citizenship. And many Chinese didn't want to assimilate anyway because they planned to return home. This created a vicious cycle: exclusion reinforced sojourning, which reinforced stereotypes of "unassimilable aliens," which justified further exclusion.

üéì Historian's Perspective

Sucheng Chan (historian of Asian American history) emphasizes that sojourning was a rational economic strategy, not a cultural flaw. Madeline Hsu has shown how transnational networks sustained Chinese communities across the Pacific.

Part II

Labor, Race, and the Racialization of Work

How Asian labor became a racial category

Welcome‚ÄîThen Rejection

1868 Burlingame Treaty encourages Chinese labor migration

Chinese workers were recruited for:

  • Mining (California gold rush, Nevada silver)
  • Railroad construction (Central Pacific, transcontinental lines)
  • Agriculture (farms, orchards, land reclamation)
  • Urban service trades (laundries, restaurants, domestic work)
×

Burlingame Treaty (1868)

A diplomatic agreement between the United States and China that:

  • Encouraged free migration: Recognized the right of Chinese to emigrate to the U.S.
  • Protected mutual trade: Opened economic relations
  • Promised equal treatment: Chinese in the U.S. would get "most favored nation" status

Context: The U.S. wanted Chinese labor for railroad construction and Western development. China wanted to protect its emigrants and maintain trade.

Irony: Just 14 years later, the U.S. passed the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882), directly violating the spirit of this treaty.

üìö The Economic Reality

Here's the central contradiction: American businesses WANTED Chinese workers. They were reliable, skilled, and worked for lower wages than white workers demanded. Railroad companies, mine owners, and farmers actively recruited Chinese labor.

üìñ The Transcontinental Railroad

The Central Pacific Railroad (building eastward from California) employed about 12,000 Chinese workers‚Äîroughly 90% of their workforce. They did the most dangerous work: blasting tunnels through the Sierra Nevada, laying track in extreme conditions, handling explosives. Many died. Without Chinese labor, the transcontinental railroad probably wouldn't have been completed when it was (1869).

üí≠ The Contradiction

So if businesses wanted Chinese workers, why did exclusion happen? Because WHITE WORKERS saw Chinese labor as a threat. This created a split in American capitalism: employers wanted cheap labor, but white workers demanded racial exclusion to protect their jobs and wages.

üîë Key Point

The Burlingame Treaty (1868) officially welcomed Chinese migration. But by 1882‚Äîjust 14 years later‚Äîthe U.S. banned it completely. What changed? White working-class politics and the racialization of labor.

The "Coolie Labor" Framework

"Coolie labor" was framed as:

  • Unfree ‚Äî compared to slavery, contract labor
  • Degrading ‚Äî undermining white workers' dignity
  • Racially distinct ‚Äî biologically different, unassimilable
üîë Critical Insight

Hostility was not just economic‚Äîit was racial and cultural, rooted in ideas of civilization and whiteness.

×

"Coolie Labor" ‚Äî A Racialized Category

The term "coolie" originally referred to low-wage Asian laborers, but it quickly became a racist slur implying:

  • Unfreedom: Coolies were portrayed as semi-enslaved, bound by exploitative contracts
  • Degradation: Working alongside "coolies" was seen as lowering white workers to an inferior status
  • Racial otherness: Coolies weren't just poor workers‚Äîthey were racially unfit for American society

Reality vs. Stereotype: While some Chinese workers did come under exploitative labor contracts, most were free migrants seeking economic opportunity, just like European immigrants. But the "coolie" label stuck, making ALL Chinese workers seem like a threat to "free labor."

Key Scholar: Moon-Ho Jung in Coolies and Cane shows how "coolie labor" was invented as a racial category to justify exclusion.

üìö What You Need to Know

The term "coolie" became a weapon. It didn't just mean "cheap labor"‚Äîit meant racially inferior, degrading, un-American labor.

üìñ The Three Arguments Against Chinese Labor

  1. Economic: "They take our jobs and lower wages"
  2. Moral: "They live in degrading conditions and have immoral habits"
  3. Racial: "They can never become true Americans because they're racially different"

Notice how these arguments blend together. It's not just about economics‚Äîit's about RACE.

üí≠ Why This Framework Mattered

By framing Chinese labor as "coolie labor," white Americans could claim they were defending "free labor"‚Äîthe American ideal that workers should be independent, dignified, and upwardly mobile. Chinese workers, they argued, could never fit this ideal because they were racially suited only for degrading work.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Moon-Ho Jung argues in Coolies and Cane that "coolie labor" was constructed as the opposite of "free labor." It wasn't a real category‚Äîit was a racial ideology designed to exclude Asians while appearing to defend American values.

Mae Ngai points out that this racialization had lasting effects: it made Asians "unassimilable aliens" in law and culture, a status that persisted until 1965.

Part III

Gender, Family, and the Logic of Exclusion

Preventing family formation as a strategy of exclusion

Targeting Reproduction

Exclusion targeted family formation, not just labor.

  • Early Chinese migration was overwhelmingly male (95%+)
  • White Americans framed Chinese communities as:
    • Immoral (opium dens, gambling, prostitution)
    • Diseased (tuberculosis, smallpox, venereal disease)
    • Socially dangerous (bachelor societies, vice districts)

üìö Why Gender Matters in This Story

If you want to permanently exclude a group, you don't just ban workers‚Äîyou prevent them from forming families. That's what the U.S. did to Chinese migrants.

üìñ The Demographics

In 1890 California, there were roughly 27 Chinese men for every 1 Chinese woman. Why? Partly because Chinese men came as sojourners planning to return home (so they left wives behind). But also because U.S. law actively prevented Chinese women from entering.

üí≠ The Moral Panic

White Americans portrayed Chinese communities as morally degenerate "bachelor societies." Newspapers claimed Chinatowns were full of opium dens, gambling houses, and prostitution. While some of this existed (as it did in white communities too), it was massively exaggerated to justify exclusion.

üîë The Logic

No families = no permanent settlement = no claims to belonging. By preventing family formation, the U.S. ensured Chinese migrants remained "temporary" and "foreign" even if they lived here for decades.

The Page Law (1875)

üîë Key Legal Moment

The Page Law (1875) effectively barred Chinese women by labeling them presumed prostitutes.

Consequences:

  • Prevented family formation
  • Reinforced the "permanent foreigner" stereotype
  • Made assimilation structurally impossible
×

Page Law (1875)

The first federal immigration law to restrict entry based on race and gender.

Official Purpose: Ban the importation of "immoral" women (prostitutes)

Actual Effect: Created bureaucratic barriers that prevented virtually ALL Chinese women from immigrating

How It Worked:

  • Required women to undergo invasive interrogations in China and San Francisco
  • Assumed ALL Chinese women were prostitutes unless proven otherwise
  • Placed burden of proof on women to demonstrate "moral character"
  • Created long delays and denials

Impact:

Between 1875-1882, Chinese female immigration dropped by 70%. Even wives of legal residents were denied entry.

Historical Significance: This was the FIRST federal law restricting immigration‚Äî7 years BEFORE the more famous Chinese Exclusion Act. It targeted women specifically to prevent Chinese families from forming in the U.S.

üìö What You Need to Know

The Page Law (1875) is less famous than the Chinese Exclusion Act (1882), but it was actually the FIRST federal law restricting immigration. And it targeted women.

üìñ How It Worked

The law claimed to ban "immoral women" (prostitutes) from entering the U.S. But immigration officials assumed ALL Chinese women were prostitutes unless they could prove otherwise. This was nearly impossible. Women had to undergo humiliating interrogations, provide extensive documentation, and wait months or years‚Äîonly to be rejected anyway.

üí≠ The Strategy

This was brilliant (in an evil way): By targeting women, the law prevented Chinese families from forming WITHOUT openly banning all Chinese migration (that came later in 1882). It looked like a "moral" law against prostitution, but it was really a racial law against Chinese settlement.

üîë Why This Matters

Exclusion worked by disrupting REPRODUCTION, not just entry. Without families, Chinese communities couldn't grow naturally. Children couldn't be born as U.S. citizens. The community remained foreign, temporary, and vulnerable.

üéì Historian's Perspective

George Peffer and Eithne Luibh√©id have shown how immigration law has long used gender and sexuality to control racial boundaries. The Page Law wasn't about morality‚Äîit was about race.

Key Insight

üîë Analytical Takeaway

Exclusion worked by disrupting reproduction, not just entry.

This strategy made assimilation structurally impossible and reinforced the "permanent foreigner" status of Asian Americans.

ü§î Think About This

If you want to understand American racism, you have to understand how it operated at multiple levels:

  • Economic: Exclude from jobs
  • Legal: Exclude from citizenship
  • Social: Exclude from belonging
  • Biological: Exclude from reproduction

üí° Modern Relevance

This strategy of preventing family formation has been used repeatedly in U.S. history: against enslaved people (family separation), against Asian immigrants (Page Law), against Mexican farmworkers (bracero program discouraged families), and in modern immigration enforcement (family separation at the border). It's a consistent tool of racial control.

Part IV

Law as a Tool of Racial Boundary-Making

How immigration policy redefined American identity

The Legal Architecture of Exclusion

This is where race hardens into policy.

1790/1870 Naturalization limited to "white persons" and persons of African descent
1875 Page Law ‚Äî Bars Chinese women
1882 Chinese Exclusion Act ‚Äî First federal law banning immigration by race
1917 Asiatic Barred Zone ‚Äî Expands exclusion to most of Asia
1924 National Origins Act ‚Äî Comprehensive racial restrictions
×

Page Law (1875)

See earlier slide for full details. This was the first federal immigration restriction, targeting Chinese women by presuming them to be prostitutes.

×

Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)

The first federal law to ban immigration based explicitly on race/nationality.

  • Banned: Chinese laborers from entering the U.S. for 10 years
  • Allowed: Only merchants, diplomats, students, and travelers (with strict documentation)
  • Barred: Chinese from naturalization (becoming citizens)
  • Extended: Renewed multiple times, made permanent in 1902, not repealed until 1943

Significance: This fundamentally changed American immigration policy from open to restrictive, from economic to racial. It established the principle that the U.S. could exclude people based on race.

×

Asiatic Barred Zone (1917)

Part of the Immigration Act of 1917, this law expanded exclusion from just Chinese to most of Asia.

The "Zone": Banned immigration from a geographic region including:

  • India and South Asia
  • Southeast Asia (Burma, Siam, Malay Peninsula)
  • Parts of the Middle East (Arabia, Afghanistan)
  • Pacific Islands

Japan and the Philippines were excluded from the zone (Japan due to diplomatic pressure, Philippines because it was a U.S. colony).

Impact: Effectively banned almost all Asian immigration except from Japan and the Philippines.

×

National Origins Act (1924)

Also called the Johnson-Reed Act, this created a comprehensive system of racial quotas.

  • Quota system: Limited immigration from each country to 2% of that nationality present in the 1890 census
  • Why 1890? To favor Northern/Western Europeans and restrict Southern/Eastern Europeans
  • Asian exclusion: Banned ALL Asians (including Japanese) by creating category of "aliens ineligible for citizenship"
  • No quotas for Americas: Latin American and Canadian immigration remained unrestricted (but still subject to bureaucratic barriers)

Effect: This remained the foundation of U.S. immigration law until 1965. It enshrined racial preferences: Northern Europeans favored, Southern/Eastern Europeans restricted, Asians banned, Africans nearly banned.

üìö Why This Section Is Critical

This is where abstract racism becomes concrete law. These aren't just policies‚Äîthey're the legal architecture that DEFINED who could be American for nearly a century.

üìñ The Pattern

Notice the progression: 1875 (target women) ‚Üí 1882 (ban Chinese workers) ‚Üí 1917 (ban most Asians) ‚Üí 1924 (ban ALL Asians + restrict Europeans). Each law built on the previous one, expanding and tightening racial boundaries.

üîë The Critical Shift

Before 1875, U.S. immigration was basically open (except for convicts and paupers). After 1882, immigration became about RACIAL ENGINEERING‚Äîdeciding who was racially fit to be American.

üí≠ What This Meant

These laws didn't respond to Chinese migration‚Äîthey REDEFINED AMERICAN IDENTITY. They said: being American means being white (or Black, post-Civil War). Asians can never be American because they're racially ineligible.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Mae Ngai (Impossible Subjects) argues these laws created a new category: the "illegal alien." Before this, people might be deportable for specific crimes, but they weren't "illegal" for existing. After 1882, racial identity itself could make you illegal.

Erika Lee (The Chinese Exclusion Act) shows how exclusion emerged from local violence and labor conflict, then became federal policy, then became the template for all future immigration restrictions.

The Transformation of Immigration Policy

üîë Critical Shift

Immigration policy moves from economic regulation to racial engineering.

These laws do not respond to Chinese migration‚Äîthey redefine American identity.

üìö Understanding the Big Picture

Before the 1880s, the U.S. had almost no federal immigration restrictions. The Constitution left immigration to states. Anyone could come (except enslaved people, convicts, and paupers). This was partly ideological (America as a refuge) but mostly practical (the country needed workers and settlers).

üí≠ What Changed?

Race-based exclusion introduced a new principle: the federal government could decide who was fit to be American based on race and national origin. This wasn't about security or economics‚Äîit was about identity. Who gets to be "us"?

üîë Long-Term Impact

These laws shaped American society for nearly a century (1882-1965). They:

  • Kept Asian American communities small and vulnerable
  • Favored European immigration (which reinforced whiteness)
  • Created the enforcement apparatus (border patrol, deportation system) we still have today
  • Established the legal principle that immigration is about racial composition, not just labor needs

Part V

"Yellow Peril": Race, Fear, and Popular Culture

How culture prepared the ground for exclusion

Law Does Not Operate Alone

Culture prepares the ground.

üîë Key Concept

"Yellow Peril" was a system of discourse that portrayed Asians as:

  • Inscrutable
  • Unassimilable
  • Dangerous
  • Civilization-threatening
×

"Yellow Peril" Discourse

Definition: A body of ideas, images, and narratives that portrayed Asians (especially Chinese and later Japanese) as a racial threat to Western civilization.

Core Themes:

  • Inscrutability: "You can never know what they're thinking" ‚Äî portrayed as mysterious, devious, unreadable
  • Unassimilability: "They will never become American" ‚Äî culturally and racially incapable of adopting Western values
  • Danger: Economic threat (job competition), moral threat (vice and disease), military threat (invasion)
  • Mass: "The yellow horde" ‚Äî overwhelming numbers that could swamp white civilization

Origins:

The term "Yellow Peril" (German: Gelbe Gefahr) gained prominence in the 1890s, popularized by Kaiser Wilhelm II of Germany who feared rising Asian (especially Japanese) power. It reflected anxieties about:

  • Japan's military modernization (defeated China 1895, Russia 1905)
  • China's population size
  • Competition for empire and markets in Asia
  • Racial theories about "civilization" and "barbarism"

üìö What You Need to Know

"Yellow Peril" was more than racism‚Äîit was an entire worldview that framed Asians as fundamentally threatening to white Western civilization.

üìñ Key Point About Discourse

The word "discourse" might seem academic, but it's important. A discourse is a system of meaning‚Äîa way of talking, thinking, and seeing that shapes what counts as "true." Yellow Peril discourse didn't describe reality; it CREATED a reality by making people see Asians as threatening even when they weren't.

üí≠ How It Worked

Yellow Peril imagery was everywhere: newspapers, political cartoons, novels, plays, movies, postcards. It seeped into everyday conversation. People who had never met an Asian person "knew" they were dangerous because the culture told them so repeatedly.

üîë Why This Matters

Laws like the Chinese Exclusion Act didn't come out of nowhere. They were passed because millions of Americans believed Asians were a threat. And they believed that because popular culture had taught them to believe it.

Sources of Yellow Peril Discourse

Fueled By:

  • Pseudoscience (racial biology)
  • Imperial anxiety (competition for Asia)
  • Global power shifts (Japan's rise)
  • Labor conflict (white workers' fears)

Examples:

  • Political cartoons
  • Kaiser Wilhelm's Gelbe Gefahr
  • Pulp fiction (Fu Manchu)
  • Newspapers, postcards

üìö The Sources of Fear

Yellow Peril wasn't just prejudice‚Äîit was a multi-layered ideology with several sources:

1. Pseudoscience

Late 1800s racial science claimed that races were biologically distinct and unequal. "Mongolian" races were supposedly less evolved than "Caucasian" races. This gave scientific veneer to racism.

2. Imperial Anxiety

Western powers (U.S., Britain, Germany, France) competed to colonize and exploit Asia. Japan's military victories (over China 1895, Russia 1905) shocked Europeans who believed they were racially superior. If Japan could modernize and defeat white powers, what did that mean for white supremacy?

3. Labor Conflict

White workers genuinely feared economic competition. In California, Chinese workers did compete for jobs. But instead of forming multiracial unions, white workers organized on racial lines to exclude Chinese workers entirely.

4. Cultural Production

Mass media (newspapers, magazines, novels, theater, early films) constantly depicted Asians as villains. The most famous example: Fu Manchu, the evil genius criminal mastermind created by British author Sax Rohmer in 1913. Fu Manchu became the archetype of the inscrutable, dangerous Asian villain.

üîë Critical Thinking

This is discourse, not description‚Äîa system of meaning that produces fear as "truth."

Yellow Peril wasn't a response to real threats. It was a cultural construction that made people BELIEVE Asians were threatening.

ü§î Think Critically

When you see Yellow Peril images and rhetoric, ask yourself: What actual threat did Chinese laundrymen or Japanese farmers pose to American civilization? None. The "threat" was invented.

üí° Why This Matters Today

Understanding how Yellow Peril discourse worked helps you recognize similar patterns today: how fear is manufactured, how "threats" are constructed, how racism gets dressed up as legitimate concern. The mechanisms are the same.

Part VI

Survival, Adaptation, and Enclave Formation

Despite exclusion, Asian communities endured

Resistance and Resilience

Despite exclusion, Asian communities endured through:

  • Ethnic enclaves (Chinatowns, fishing villages, labor camps)
  • Self-employment (laundries, restaurants, small shops)
  • Mutual-aid societies (tongs, family associations, benevolent organizations)
  • Informal migration strategies (e.g., "paper sons")
×

Ethnic Enclaves

Concentrated communities where immigrants lived, worked, and built institutions.

Chinatowns emerged in San Francisco, New York, Los Angeles, and other cities as spaces where:

  • Chinese could find housing (often denied elsewhere)
  • Chinese businesses could operate
  • Cultural practices could be maintained
  • Mutual support networks functioned

Not by choice: Enclaves were often responses to segregation and exclusion, not voluntary isolation. Chinese wanted to live in Chinatowns because they were unwelcome elsewhere.

×

"Paper Sons"

A strategy Chinese immigrants used to circumvent exclusion laws.

How It Worked:

The 1906 San Francisco earthquake destroyed birth records. Chinese men in the U.S. (especially those with citizenship) could claim that children were born to them in China. They would sell these "slots" to young Chinese men who wanted to immigrate.

The young men would memorize detailed information about their "paper father's" village, family, and life story. When they arrived at Angel Island (the West Coast immigration station), they were interrogated extensively. If their stories matched their "father's," they were admitted as citizens' sons‚Äîeven though they weren't actually related.

Significance:

  • Showed Chinese determination to reunite families despite exclusion
  • Demonstrated sophisticated resistance to unjust laws
  • Created complex "paper families" that lasted generations

üìö What You Need to Know

This section is crucial because it shows AGENCY. Asian Americans weren't just victims‚Äîthey were active agents who found creative ways to survive and resist.

üìñ Strategies of Survival

  1. Enclaves: Built communities where they could support each other
  2. Niche economies: Found work whites didn't want (laundries, restaurants) or couldn't compete with
  3. Institutions: Created mutual-aid societies, family associations, temples, newspapers
  4. Legal resistance: Sued for rights (e.g., Yick Wo v. Hopkins 1886, Wong Kim Ark 1898)
  5. Informal strategies: "Paper sons" and other ways to evade exclusion laws

üí≠ Reframing Enclaves

White Americans criticized Chinatowns as proof that Chinese "refused to assimilate." But enclaves existed because Chinese were EXCLUDED from white neighborhoods, jobs, and institutions. They weren't choosing isolation‚Äîthey were surviving segregation.

üéì Historian's Perspective

Yong Chen (Chinese San Francisco) shows how Chinatown was both a refuge and a trap‚Äîit protected Chinese from violence but also reinforced their segregation. Erika Lee documents how Chinese immigrants used every legal and informal tool available to resist exclusion.

Important Reframing

üîë Analytical Point

Enclaves are not signs of isolation‚Äîthey are responses to enforced exclusion.

When people couldn't live, work, or belong elsewhere, they built their own institutions and communities.

üí≠ Challenge the Narrative

The dominant American narrative says: "Immigrants should assimilate into American culture." But what if American culture actively rejected you? What if you were legally barred from citizenship, residentially segregated, and violently attacked?

üîë The Reality

Enclaves were survival strategies, not cultural choices. They were necessary because exclusion was real. This is true for Asian American communities, African American communities, and other groups who faced systematic racism.

What Asian Exclusion Reveals

The Limits of American Pluralism

Asian exclusion reveals:

  • Cities became diverse‚Äîbut not equally inclusive
  • Law, labor, and culture worked together to racialize belonging
  • Urbanization intensified debates over:
    • Citizenship

üìö Pulling It All Together

This lecture has shown how American cities in the late 1800s became sites of both incredible diversity AND systematic exclusion. These weren't contradictory‚Äîthey were two sides of the same process.
