Part I

The Tenement City: Density Without Infrastructure

When people arrive faster than cities can adapt

The Basic Problem

People arrived faster than cities could adapt.

  • Explosive population growth in New York, Chicago, Boston, Philadelphia
  • Housing built for profit, not safety
  • Working-class and immigrant families crowded into subdivided apartments

ðŸ“š The Numbers

New York City's population exploded from about 60,000 in 1800 to over 3.4 million by 1900. Chicago went from virtually nothing in 1830 to 1.7 million by 1900. This growth was too fast for infrastructure to keep up.

ðŸ’­ Why Housing Was Dangerous

Landlords built housing as cheaply as possible to maximize profit. There were few regulations, and those that existed were rarely enforced. Safety, ventilation, light, and sanitation were afterthoughts.

ðŸ”‘ Key Point

This wasn't accidentalâ€”it was the result of unregulated capitalism applied to housing. Landlords made money, workers got sick and died.

Key Features of Tenement Life

  • Entire families in one room
  • Basement apartments prone to flooding
  • Poor ventilation and little natural light
  • Shared privies, often unsanitary and unconnected to sewers
  • Multiple families sharing single water sources

Living Density in Tenements

Imagine this: a family of 6-8 people living in a single room, perhaps 10 feet by 12 feet. That room served as:

  • Bedroom for all family members
  • Kitchen and dining area
  • Workspace (home manufacturing like sewing)
  • Living space

Privacy was impossible. Bathing, dressing, sleeping, eating, workingâ€”all happened in the same small space.

Disease spread easily. When one person got sick (tuberculosis, influenza, typhoid), everyone in the room was exposed constantly.

ðŸ“š What You Need to Understand

These conditions weren't just uncomfortableâ€”they were deadly. Disease thrived in overcrowded, dark, poorly ventilated spaces with contaminated water and inadequate waste disposal.

ðŸ“– Specific Conditions

  • One room: Entire families (6-10 people) in a single room
  • Basements: Below street level, prone to flooding with sewage
  • No ventilation: Windows opened to narrow air shafts, not fresh air
  • Privies: Outdoor toilets shared by dozens of families, often overflowing

ðŸ’­ The Human Cost

Infant mortality in tenement districts was staggeringâ€”sometimes 1 in 4 children died before age 5. Tuberculosis (TB) killed constantly. Diseases we barely think about today (typhoid, dysentery, diphtheria) were routine killers.

Privy Vaults and Contamination

A privy vault was essentially an outdoor toiletâ€”a hole in the ground (often lined with brick or wood) where human waste accumulated.

The Problem:

  • Overflow: When full, waste backed up and spilled into streets and basements
  • Leaching: Waste seeped through soil into groundwater
  • Wells contaminated: Drinking water wells were often just feet away from privies
  • No sewers: Most cities didn't have sewer systems until late 1800s

Disease Connection:

Cholera, typhoid, and dysentery are all waterborne diseases. When privies contaminated wells and water pumps, entire neighborhoods got sick. This was the primary disease vector in 19th century cities.

ðŸ“š Visualize This

Imagine walking down a city street in 1850s New York. You'd see: human waste in gutters, dead horses left to rot, piles of garbage, industrial waste dumped in alleys. The smell would be overwhelming. Flies everywhere.

ðŸ“– The Horse Problem

Cities relied on horses for transportation. New York had 100,000+ horses. Each horse produced 20-30 pounds of manure per day. That's over 2 million pounds of manure every single day in one city. Where did it go? Streets, alleys, vacant lots.

ðŸ’­ Why This Mattered

This wasn't just disgustingâ€”it was deadly. Rotting organic matter attracted rats, flies, and other disease vectors. Contaminated water caused epidemics. Cities were ecological disasters.

The Failure of Urban Sanitation

The Problems:

  • No coordinated waste removal
  • Street cleaning as political patronage
  • Rivers used for both drinking water AND sewage disposal
  • Private contractors unreliable

The Reality:

  • Wealthy neighborhoods cleaner
  • Poor neighborhoods ignored
  • Disease concentrated in tenement districts
  • Inequality visible in mortality rates

ðŸ“š Sanitation as Politics

Street cleaning jobs were given out as political favors (patronage). Workers were hired not for competence but for political loyalty. This meant sanitation was inconsistent and often terrible.

ðŸ’­ The Water Problem

Many cities took drinking water from the same rivers they dumped sewage into. New York drew water from the polluted East River. Chicago's sewage flowed into Lake Michiganâ€”the same lake they drew drinking water from. This was insane, but it was standard practice.

Part III

Disease Before Germ Theory: Miasma and Moral Judgment

How people understood sickness before modern medicine

The Dominant Belief: Miasma Theory

Miasma theory held that disease was caused by "bad air" from rotting filth.

  • Focus on smell rather than microbes
  • Disease spreads through poisonous vapors
  • Solution: ventilation, drainage, removing filth
×

Miasma Theory

From ancient times through the mid-1800s, most people believed disease was caused by "miasma"â€”bad air or poisonous vapors rising from rotting organic matter.

Core Beliefs:

  • Decay produces poisonous gases
  • These vapors cause illness when inhaled
  • Strong odors = dangerous miasma
  • Night air is especially dangerous (vapors settle)

What They Got Right:

Miasma theory was WRONG about the mechanism (it's not bad air, it's bacteria and viruses), but it led to some correct interventions: cleaning streets, improving drainage, removing garbage, building sewers. These things DID reduce disease, just not for the reasons people thought.

What They Got Wrong:

Miasma theory couldn't explain contagion (person-to-person transmission), couldn't identify disease-causing organisms, and led to moral judgments about the sick.

ðŸ“š Understanding Miasma

Before the 1880s-1890s (when germ theory was accepted), miasma theory dominated medical thinking. If something smelled bad, it was assumed to cause disease.

ðŸ’­ Why This Mattered

Miasma theory had some beneficial effects (cleaning cities) but also harmful ones (blaming the poor for getting sick, focusing on symptoms rather than causes).

ðŸ”‘ The Irony

Miasma theory was wrong, but actions based on it (cleaning filth, improving sanitation) actually DID reduce diseaseâ€”just not for the reasons people thought. The smell wasn't the problem; the bacteria in the filth was.

Disease as Moral Judgment

Miasma theory had social consequences:

  • Disease seen as punishment for vice, intemperance, or filthiness
  • Poor, immigrants, and African Americans blamed for illness
  • Cholera deaths viewed as suspicious or deserved
  • Calls for prayer and fasting during epidemics
ðŸ”‘ Critical Point

Disease explanations reinforced existing class and racial hierarchies.

ðŸ“š The Moral Framework

Many Americans believed disease was God's punishment for sinâ€”especially sins like drunkenness, sexual immorality, laziness, and filth. This meant that if you got sick, it was YOUR FAULT.

ðŸ“– Who Got Blamed

Poor people, immigrants (especially Irish and later Eastern Europeans), and African Americans were seen as morally inferior and therefore more prone to disease. When epidemics hit, wealthy Americans said it was because the poor were dirty, drunk, and immoral.

ðŸ’­ The Politics

This moral framework meant that instead of addressing structural causes (bad housing, contaminated water), people focused on reforming individual behaviorâ€”temperance, cleanliness, Christian piety.

ðŸ”‘ Why This Matters

Blaming victims prevented systemic solutions. If disease is a personal moral failing, you don't need to build sewers or regulate housingâ€”you just need people to be better. This protected landlords and employers from responsibility.

Cholera: The Classic Urban Disease

Using epidemic as a lens for understanding inequality

Cholera Arrives in America

Cholera was unknown in the West before the 19th century.

1832 First major pandemic strikes U.S. cities
1849 Second wave, deadlier than the first
1866 Third pandemic, concentrated in immigrant neighborhoods
×

Cholera: The Disease

Cholera is a bacterial infection of the intestines caused by Vibrio cholerae, spread through contaminated water.

Symptoms:

  • Sudden, severe diarrhea ("rice water stools")
  • Vomiting
  • Rapid dehydration
  • Death within hours if untreated

Transmission:

Spread through water contaminated with feces from infected people. In cities without sewers, cholera thrived because waste contaminated drinking water.

Why It Terrified People:

  • Sudden: Healthy in the morning, dead by evening
  • Visible: Bodies turn blue from dehydration, victims suffer visibly
  • Mysterious: Before germ theory, no one knew how it spread
  • Deadly: Killed 30-50% of those infected

Spread via global trade, empire, and steam transportation.

ðŸ“š Cholera as Global Disease

Cholera originated in India (Bengal region) and spread worldwide in the 1800s via trade routes and steam ships. It hit Europe in the 1820s and reached North America in 1832.

ðŸ“– The Three Pandemics

  • 1832: 3,000+ dead in New York City alone in a few months
  • 1849: Spread by California gold rush migrants, killed 5,000+ in New York
  • 1866: Concentrated in immigrant slums, exposed inequality

ðŸ’­ Why We're Studying Cholera

Cholera is the perfect lens for understanding 19th century urban crisis because it was: waterborne (revealed infrastructure problems), concentrated in poor areas (revealed inequality), and terrifying (forced action).

Cholera's Urban Impact

  • Struck hardest in tenement districts
  • Middle and upper classes fled cities
  • Hospitals overwhelmed; bodies left unburied
  • Immigrant ships and barges became floating death zones

Edwin Chadwick (1800-1890)

British reformer who pioneered sanitary science.

Key Work: Report on the Sanitary Condition of the Labouring Population (1842)

Chadwick surveyed working-class districts and documented the connection between filth, poverty, and disease. He argued that:

  • Bad sanitation caused disease
  • Government should build sewers and water systems
  • Public health was economically rational (healthy workers were productive)

His work influenced urban reform movements in Britain and America.

×

John Snow and the Broad Street Pump (1854)

One of the most famous stories in public health history.

The Mystery: London's Soho district suffered a severe cholera outbreak in 1854. Hundreds died in days.

Snow's Investigation: Dr. John Snow mapped every cholera death and noticed they clustered around one public water pump on Broad Street. He interviewed families and traced their water sources.

The Discovery: The Broad Street pump was contaminated by sewage from a nearby cesspool. Snow convinced authorities to remove the pump handleâ€”the outbreak ended.

Significance: This was the first time anyone proved that cholera spread through contaminated water, not "bad air." It helped establish germ theory and modern epidemiology.

Note: Steven Johnson's book The Ghost Map tells this story brilliantly.

ðŸ“š The Sanitarians

"Sanitarians" were reformers who focused on sanitationâ€”clean water, sewers, waste removalâ€”as the key to preventing disease.

ðŸ“– Key Innovations

  • Surveys: Documenting conditions systematically
  • Mapping: Showing spatial patterns of disease
  • Statistics: Using data to prove causation
  • Infrastructure: Building sewers, waterworks, drainage

Germ Theory Revolution

In the 1860s-1880s, scientists proved that diseases were caused by microscopic organisms (bacteria, viruses), not bad air.

Key Figures:

  • Louis Pasteur (France): Proved microorganisms cause fermentation and disease; developed pasteurization and vaccines
  • Robert Koch (Germany): Identified specific bacteria causing anthrax, tuberculosis, and cholera; established scientific methods for linking germs to diseases

Impact on Public Health:

  • Justified sanitation efforts (kill germs in water, food)
  • Led to antiseptic surgery
  • Made quarantine rational
  • Enabled vaccine development
  • Shifted focus from moral reform to scientific intervention
ðŸ”‘ Political Consequence

Government intervention becomes legitimate. Public health as a justification for regulation.

ðŸ“š Why Germ Theory Changed Everything

Once scientists could identify specific disease-causing organisms and prove how they spread, public health became scientific rather than moral. This made government intervention legitimateâ€”you weren't judging people's character, you were fighting germs.

ðŸ”‘ The Modern State Emerges

Public health became one of the first major functions of modern government. Cities created health departments, hired inspectors, built sewers and water systems, regulated food and milk, quarantined the sick. This was the beginning of the regulatory state.
