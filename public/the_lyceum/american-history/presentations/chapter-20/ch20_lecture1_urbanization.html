üìö What You Need to Know

This lecture reframes urbanization as a GLOBAL story of migration‚Äînot just Americans moving to cities, but people from around the world being pushed and pulled by massive economic and political forces.

üìñ Key Definitions

  • Urbanization: The process of population shift from rural to urban areas, and the transformation of society that accompanies it
  • Diaspora: The dispersion of people from their homeland, with continuing cultural and emotional ties to that homeland

üí≠ Context & Connections

This lecture covers roughly 1870-1920. It connects to: industrialization (Chapter 18), Gilded Age politics (Chapter 19), and Progressive reforms (Chapter 21). Understanding immigration patterns helps explain labor movements, political machines, and cultural conflicts.

The World in Motion

üåç Core Claim

Urbanization is not just Americans moving into cities. It is the world in motion‚Äîdriven by industrial capitalism, imperial disruption, political violence, and transportation revolutions.

Late-19th-century American cities were nodes in global systems of labor, capital, and migration.

×

Cities as Global Nodes

Think of cities like New York, Chicago, and San Francisco as connection points in a worldwide network:

  • Labor flows: Workers from Italy, Poland, China, Mexico converged on industrial centers
  • Capital flows: European investment funded American railroads and factories
  • Information flows: Telegraphs, newspapers, and letters connected communities across oceans

American cities weren't isolated‚Äîthey were embedded in a global economy.

üìö What You Need to Know

This slide establishes the lecture's core argument: we can't understand American urbanization by looking only at America. Global forces‚Äîcapitalism, imperialism, revolutions‚Äîpushed people out of their homelands. American industrial demand pulled them in.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This connects to World History: British colonialism in Ireland (Irish famine migration), Russian pogroms (Jewish migration), German/Italian unification disruptions, and Chinese exclusion from other destinations (Australia, Canada).

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Donna Gabaccia argues for seeing immigration within "global migration systems" rather than as a uniquely American story. Her work shows how the same Italian village might send migrants to Argentina, Brazil, AND the United States simultaneously.

Constrained Choices

üîë Key Insight for This Lecture

Urban growth reflects constrained choices made under pressure‚Äînot free individual preference.

Migrants were not simply seeking "opportunity." They were often fleeing violence, poverty, or persecution‚Äîand choosing among limited options.

üìö What You Need to Know

This challenges the "Land of Opportunity" narrative. Yes, immigrants came seeking better lives‚Äîbut their choices were heavily constrained. Many would have preferred to stay home if conditions allowed.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Compare to: enslaved people's "choices" under slavery (no real choice), or working-class families "choosing" factory labor over starvation. The word "choice" can obscure the pressures people face.

ü§î Discussion Prompt

If your only options were staying in a place with no jobs or moving somewhere dangerous and difficult, is that really a "free choice"?

Part I

The Lure of the City

Why cities exploded after 1870

Industrial Capitalism Reorganized Space

üè≠ Factories Required

Concentrated wage labor‚Äîthousands of workers in one place

üöÇ Transportation Revolution

Railroads, steamships, and telegraph compressed time and distance

Cities became engines of opportunity and risk.

The Compression of Time and Distance

Before 1850, crossing the Atlantic took 4-6 weeks by sailing ship. By 1900:

  • Steamships crossed in 7-10 days
  • Telegraph sent messages in minutes
  • Railroads moved goods and people faster than ever before

This made mass migration physically possible‚Äîand made cities accessible to people from all over the world.

üìö What You Need to Know

Cities didn't just "grow"‚Äîthey were reshaped by industrial capitalism. Factories needed workers close by. Railroads made it possible to concentrate raw materials AND finished goods. The result: explosive urban growth.

üìñ Key Definitions

  • Wage labor: Working for money (wages) rather than for yourself or as part of a household economy
  • Transportation Revolution: The transformation of travel and shipping through canals, railroads, and steamships (1820s-1870s)

The Numbers Tell the Story

By 1900 ~40% of Americans lived in cities
By 1920 A majority lived in urban areas for the first time
By 1900 6 cities exceeded 500,000 people

New York, Chicago, Philadelphia, St. Louis, Boston, Baltimore

üìö What You Need to Know

These statistics show the SCALE of change. In 1800, only about 6% of Americans lived in cities. By 1920, over 50% did. This was one of the most dramatic transformations in American history.

üí≠ Context & Connections

1920 is a turning point‚Äîthe census that year showed the U.S. was now majority urban. This helps explain: the rise of consumer culture, the decline of rural political power, and cultural conflicts of the 1920s (urban vs. rural values).

A Cultural Reversal

Antebellum View

Cities were seen as:

  • Corrupt and dangerous
  • Threats to republican virtue
  • Places of moral decay

"Those who labor in the earth are the chosen people of God." ‚Äî Jefferson

Post-1870 View

Cities were celebrated as:

  • Modern and dynamic
  • Centers of progress
  • Inevitable and exciting

The city as the symbol of American power

üìö What You Need to Know

Americans' attitudes toward cities changed dramatically. The Founders' generation feared cities; by 1900, cities were celebrated as symbols of American progress and modernity.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Robert Wiebe in The Search for Order argues that industrialization created a "distended society" that overwhelmed older, local community structures. Cities became the sites where Americans worked out new forms of organization.

Not "Natural Growth"

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Clarification

Urbanization is not "natural growth"‚Äîit is a structural outcome of industrial systems.

Industrial capitalism CREATED the conditions that drew people to cities. Without factories, railroads, and global trade networks, this migration would not have happened.

üìö What You Need to Know

This challenges the idea that cities just naturally attract people. Cities grew because of specific economic structures‚Äîfactories needed workers, railroads made cities accessible, capitalism concentrated wealth in urban centers.

ü§î Discussion Prompt

If cities didn't just "naturally" grow, what does this suggest about whether urbanization was good or bad? Who benefited? Who didn't?

Part II

"New Immigrants"

The scale and sources of migration after 1880

A Shift in Sources

"Old" Immigration

Before 1880

  • Northern & Western Europe
  • Britain, Ireland, Germany
  • Scandinavia

Often Protestant (except Irish)

"New" Immigration

After 1880

  • Southern & Eastern Europe
  • Italy, Poland, Russia
  • Austria-Hungary, Greece

Often Catholic, Jewish, or Orthodox

üìö What You Need to Know

Historians distinguish between "old" and "new" immigration. The shift happened around 1880-1890. "New" immigrants came from different regions, spoke different languages, and practiced different religions than earlier waves.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This distinction mattered politically‚Äînativists used it to argue that "new" immigrants were racially inferior and couldn't assimilate. These arguments would lead to the restrictive Immigration Acts of 1921 and 1924.

The Scale of Change

By 1890 15% of U.S. population was foreign-born
By 1900 Most urban residents were immigrants or their children

Migration often occurred as families, not lone adventurers.

Higher birth rates among immigrants accelerated urban growth.

×

Demographics and Urban Growth

Immigrant families tended to have more children than native-born families:

  • Traditional family patterns from home countries
  • Children provided labor and economic security
  • High infant mortality meant having more children

This meant cities grew through both immigration AND natural increase among immigrant families.

üìö What You Need to Know

These numbers help you understand the SCALE of change. Cities weren't just a little different‚Äîthey were fundamentally transformed. In many cities, you were more likely to hear Italian, Yiddish, or Polish on the street than English.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This helps explain why nativism became so intense. Native-born Americans saw their communities changing rapidly and felt threatened. It also explains the political power of immigrant communities‚Äîthey were a majority in many cities!

The popular image: hopeful immigrants seeing the Statue of Liberty, starting fresh in America.

The reality:

Part III

Conceptual Toolkit

Diaspora, not assimilation

Understanding Diaspora

üìå Definition

Diaspora: The dispersal of people from a homeland, with ongoing cultural, emotional, and institutional ties to that homeland.

Key elements:

  • Dispersal ‚Äî leaving, often under pressure
  • Memory ‚Äî longing for the homeland
  • Identity ‚Äî maintaining distinct cultural practices
  • Networks ‚Äî connections across borders

üìö What You Need to Know

"Diaspora" comes from Greek, originally referring to scattered Jewish communities. Historians now use it more broadly for any displaced population maintaining homeland connections. It's a more accurate term than "immigration" for understanding how people experienced migration.

üìñ Key Definitions

  • Diaspora: Dispersed community maintaining cultural/emotional ties to homeland
  • Transnational: Connections and identities that span national borders

Contrasting Models

‚ùå "Uprooted"

Immigrants as traumatized, losing their culture, struggling to adapt

Oscar Handlin, 1951

‚úì "Transplanted"

Immigrants as active agents who adapt culture while maintaining continuity

John Bodnar, 1985

‚úì Diaspora

Ongoing connections, hybrid identities, networks across borders

Current framework

üìö What You Need to Know

How historians think about immigration has changed over time. The "uprooted" model emphasized loss and trauma. The "transplanted" model showed cultural continuity. The "diaspora" model emphasizes ongoing connections across borders.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

John Bodnar challenged Handlin by showing that immigrants weren't passive victims but active agents who strategically adapted their cultures. His work shifted immigration history toward seeing immigrants as decision-makers rather than just "huddled masses."

Push and Pull Factors

‚¨ÖÔ∏è Push Factors

What drove people out:

  • Violence and persecution
  • Land loss and enclosure
  • Economic collapse
  • Political instability
  • Religious oppression

‚û°Ô∏è Pull Factors

What drew people in:

  • Wages and jobs
  • Safety from persecution
  • Existing networks (family, village)
  • Perception of opportunity
  • Recruiters and advertisements

üìö What You Need to Know

Push-pull is a basic framework for understanding migration. People are pushed out of their homelands by bad conditions AND pulled toward new places by opportunities. Both forces work together.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Examples: Russian Jews were PUSHED by pogroms (anti-Jewish violence) and PULLED by relatives already in America. Italian peasants were PUSHED by land consolidation and PULLED by labor recruiters promising factory jobs.

Chain Migration

üîó Definition

Chain migration: Movement that follows kinship and community ties‚Äîwhere you go depends on who you already know there.

Core insight: Cities grow through networks, not randomness.

The same Italian village might send ALL its emigrants to one specific neighborhood in Chicago. Polish families from one region might all settle in the same blocks of Pittsburgh.

üìö What You Need to Know

Chain migration explains WHY specific communities clustered in specific places. It wasn't random‚Äîit followed family and village networks. If your uncle was in Chicago, you went to Chicago. This created ethnic neighborhoods.

ü§î Discussion Prompt

How does chain migration challenge the "rugged individual" myth of immigration? What does it tell us about how people actually made decisions?

Homeland vs. Host Tension

The central dilemma of diaspora life:

Belonging without full acceptance

  • Emotional ties to the "old country"
  • Economic life in the new country
  • Children caught between two worlds
  • Never fully "at home" in either place

üìö What You Need to Know

Diaspora communities often felt caught between worlds‚Äîloyal to their homelands but building lives in America, never fully accepted in either place. This tension shaped immigrant culture, politics, and family life.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This helps explain: ethnic newspapers (connecting people to homeland news), mutual aid societies (creating community in a hostile environment), and generational conflicts (children wanted to "be American," parents wanted to preserve traditions).

Why Immigrants Clustered

Immigrants deliberately clustered to reduce risk and preserve autonomy.

Living near people who spoke your language and shared your culture meant:

  • Finding jobs through personal networks
  • Accessing familiar food, religion, customs
  • Getting help in emergencies
  • Raising children with cultural continuity

üìö What You Need to Know

Ethnic neighborhoods weren't accidents or failures‚Äîthey were STRATEGIES. Immigrants chose to cluster because it made survival easier and life more meaningful. This is the opposite of how nativists saw ethnic neighborhoods (as refusals to assimilate).

üí≠ Context & Connections

This pattern continues today. New immigrant communities still cluster‚ÄîChinatowns, Little Havanas, Korean neighborhoods. The reasons are the same: networks, support, cultural continuity.

Key Ethnic Institutions

‚õ™ Religious Institutions
Churches, synagogues, temples ‚Äî spiritual and social centers
üì∞ Ethnic Newspapers
News from home, community announcements, political mobilization
ü§ù Mutual-Aid Societies
Insurance, burial funds, emergency loans
üé≠ Fraternal Organizations
Social clubs, cultural preservation, community identity

üìö What You Need to Know

Immigrants created a dense network of institutions to serve their communities. These weren't just cultural‚Äîthey provided ESSENTIAL services that government and mainstream society didn't offer.

üìñ Key Definitions

  • Mutual-aid society: Organization where members pool resources to help each other (like early insurance)
  • Fraternal organization: Brotherhood/sisterhood based on shared identity (ethnic, religious, occupational)

What Ethnic Institutions Did

üí∞ Economic Survival

Job networks, credit, emergency funds, business connections

üé≠ Cultural Continuity

Language, music, food, holidays, traditions passed to children

üë∂ Childcare & Education

Day care, religious education, language schools

üó≥Ô∏è Political Organization

Voting blocs, advocacy, connections to city machines

üìö What You Need to Know

Ethnic institutions served multiple functions simultaneously. A church wasn't just for worship‚Äîit was also a social center, a place to find jobs, a source of emergency help, and a base for political organizing.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This helps explain the power of urban political machines. Machines connected with immigrant communities THROUGH these institutions‚Äîhelping people in exchange for votes.

Critical Reframing

üîë Key Insight

Ethnic enclaves are adaptive strategies, not failures to assimilate.

When nativists complained that immigrants "refused to become American," they misunderstood what was happening.

Ethnic communities were survival mechanisms in a hostile environment‚Äîand they eventually became pathways into American society.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is the lecture's most important reframing. Ethnic neighborhoods looked like "segregation" to outsiders, but they functioned as support systems. Most immigrants DID integrate over generations‚Äîethnic communities didn't prevent this, they facilitated it.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

John Bodnar argues that ethnic institutions allowed immigrants to adapt to American capitalism while maintaining enough cultural continuity to make life meaningful. They were neither pure resistance nor pure assimilation‚Äîthey were strategic adaptation.

Part V

Nativism

The anxiety of belonging

Understanding Nativism

üìå Definition

Nativism: Preference for native-born Americans over immigrants, often expressed through hostility, discrimination, and restrictive policies.

Nativism isn't just prejudice‚Äîit's organized political opposition to immigration.

×

Nativism as Political Movement

Nativism took many forms:

  • Political parties: Know-Nothings (1850s), American Protective Association (1890s)
  • Legislative campaigns: Chinese Exclusion Act (1882), Immigration Acts (1921, 1924)
  • Violence: Anti-Irish riots, anti-Chinese massacres, KKK terrorism
  • Cultural campaigns: "100% Americanism," English-only movements

üìö What You Need to Know

Nativism is the political response to large-scale immigration. It recurs throughout American history‚Äîdifferent targets (Irish, Chinese, Catholics, Jews, Muslims) but similar patterns of fear, discrimination, and restriction.

üìñ Key Definitions

  • Nativism: Political preference for native-born over immigrants
  • Xenophobia: Fear or hatred of foreigners (broader term)

Nativism Through Time

1840s-50s Irish Catholics targeted by Know-Nothing movement
1870s-80s Chinese targeted ‚Üí Chinese Exclusion Act (1882)
1880s-1920s Southern/Eastern Europeans, Catholics, Jews targeted
1920s National Origins Quota system restricts "undesirable" groups
×

The Know-Nothing Movement

Officially the "American Party" (1850s), members were told to say "I know nothing" about their secret activities.

  • Anti-Catholic, anti-immigrant political party
  • Won elections across the North in 1854-55
  • Collapsed over slavery issue before Civil War
  • Template for later nativist movements

üìö What You Need to Know

Nativism isn't new or unique to any era. It recurs whenever large-scale immigration transforms American society. Each wave targets different groups with similar arguments: they're "unassimilable," threaten American values, and take jobs.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

John Higham's Strangers in the Land (1955) remains the definitive study. He shows how nativism rises and falls with economic conditions and national crises, but is always present as an undercurrent in American culture.

New "Scientific" Justifications

After 1880, nativists developed new arguments:

üß¨ Social Darwinism

"Survival of the fittest" applied to races and nations

üìä Racial Hierarchies

Pseudo-scientific rankings of "superior" and "inferior" races

üß™ Eugenics

Claims about heredity, "fitness," and "breeding"

These ideas gave old prejudices a veneer of scientific legitimacy.

üìö What You Need to Know

Late 19th-century racism claimed to be "scientific." Social Darwinism and eugenics provided intellectual cover for discrimination. These ideas were WRONG‚Äîbut they were widely believed by educated people, including scientists and politicians.

üìñ Key Definitions

  • Social Darwinism: Misapplication of Darwin's evolution theory to human society, claiming inequality is natural and beneficial
  • Eugenics: Pseudoscience of "improving" human population through selective breeding

The Central Contradiction

Economic Reality

Industrial capitalism required immigrant labor.

  • Factories needed workers
  • Railroads needed builders
  • Mines needed laborers

Cultural Politics

Native-born Americans resisted immigrant presence.

  • Competition for jobs
  • Fear of cultural change
  • Racial anxieties

Capitalism wanted their labor. Culture rejected their presence.

üìö What You Need to Know

This contradiction is crucial. American industry DEPENDED on immigrant labor‚Äîbut American culture was often hostile to immigrants. This tension shaped immigration policy, labor relations, and urban politics for generations.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This contradiction continues today. Industries rely on immigrant labor (agriculture, construction, technology) while political movements oppose immigration. Same tension, different era.

Part VI

The City as Contested Space

Pluralism through tension

Not a Melting Pot

üîë Key Reframing

Cities are not melting pots‚Äîthey are contested spaces.

The "melting pot" metaphor suggests smooth blending. Reality was messier:

  • Immigrants reshaped food, religion, music, labor, and politics
  • Conflict was normal, not pathological
  • Urban life produced pluralism through tension, not harmony

üìö What You Need to Know

The "melting pot" is a comforting myth. Real urban life involved constant negotiation, conflict, and cultural transformation. Different groups didn't smoothly blend‚Äîthey competed, clashed, and gradually found ways to coexist.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Jonathan Zimmerman's Whose America? shows how fights over schools, textbooks, and curriculum reflected deeper conflicts about national identity. Education became a battleground for defining what "American" meant.
