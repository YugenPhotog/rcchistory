This lecture introduces historiography by examining how Frederick Jackson Turner transformed the West from a site of conquest into an explanation for American identity. We're asking: How do historians shape how we understand the past?

What Is Historiography?

History is not only about what happened.
History is about how we interpret what happened.

üìñ Definition

Historiography refers to:

  • The study of historical interpretation
  • How historians frame questions
  • What they emphasize, explain, or ignore
  • How those choices shape historical meaning
×

Historiography

Literally "the writing of history"‚Äîbut in scholarly usage, it means the study of how history gets written.

Historiography asks questions like:

  • Why did historians in a particular era emphasize certain topics?
  • What assumptions shaped their interpretations?
  • How have interpretations changed over time?
  • Whose perspectives were included or excluded?

When you study historiography, you're not just learning WHAT historians said‚Äîyou're analyzing WHY they said it and what that reveals about their own time and assumptions.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is a meta-level concept: we're studying how history itself gets made. Students often think history = "what happened." Historiography shows that history = "interpretations of what happened" that change over time and reflect the concerns of the historians writing it.

Turner writes at the moment the frontier becomes history rather than policy.

This is why TIMING matters in historiography. Turner isn't writing while the frontier is contested‚Äîhe's writing when it's declared "closed." That shift from policy debate to historical explanation changes what kind of questions get asked.

Part II

The Frontier Thesis

Frederick Jackson Turner's Argument

Frederick Jackson Turner

Frederick Jackson Turner (1861‚Äì1932)

  • University of Wisconsin historian
  • Later Harvard professor
  • Delivered "The Significance of the Frontier in American History" in 1893

One of the most influential essays in American historical writing

×

"The Significance of the Frontier in American History" (1893)

Turner delivered this paper at the American Historical Association meeting during the World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago.

The context: The 1890 Census had announced that a continuous frontier line no longer existed‚Äîthe West was now "settled." Turner asked: What did the frontier MEAN for American development?

The impact: This short essay became the most influential interpretation of American history for the next half-century. It shaped textbooks, public memory, and how Americans understood their national character.

üìö What You Need to Know

Turner was a young professor (32) when he delivered this paper. It didn't immediately become famous, but over the following decades it became THE dominant interpretation of American history. Understanding WHY it became so influential is as important as understanding its content.

Turner's Revolutionary Approach

Turner rejected the dominant historical frameworks of his era.

What He Rejected

History as past politics

Traditional focus on laws, leaders, institutions

"Germ theory"

American institutions inherited from European origins

What He Proposed

Environment shapes society

"The wilderness masters the colonist"

Ever-advancing frontier

Continuous westward movement creates American character

"The existence of an area of free land, its continuous recession, and the advance of American settlement westward, explain American development."
‚Äî Frederick Jackson Turner, 1893

üìö What You Need to Know

Turner was rebelling against TWO dominant approaches in 1893. First, "history as past politics"‚Äîthe idea that history was just studying kings, wars, and constitutions. Second, the "germ theory" of history‚Äîthe idea that American institutions were just transplanted European "germs" that grew in American soil. Turner said NO: the ENVIRONMENT mattered. The frontier CREATED something new.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

The "germ theory" was associated with scholars like Herbert Baxter Adams at Johns Hopkins, who argued that American democracy came from Germanic tribal institutions brought by English colonists. Turner studied under Adams but rejected his framework entirely. This was a BOLD move for a young scholar.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Notice the environmental determinism here: "The wilderness masters the colonist." Turner is arguing that geography and environment SHAPE human societies more than inherited traditions or political institutions. This was revolutionary in 1893‚Äîand deeply controversial.

What the Frontier Produced

According to Turner, repeated westward movement created:

Individualism

Self-reliance forged in frontier conditions

Egalitarianism

Social leveling in new communities

Innovation

Necessity breeding new solutions

The "Safety Valve" Thesis

The frontier provided an outlet for social and economic tension‚Äîif you were poor or dissatisfied in the East, you could go West.

üìö What You Need to Know

These are the characteristics Turner claims the frontier produced. Notice they're all POSITIVE traits that Americans like to claim for themselves. Turner is essentially saying: "The frontier made us who we are‚Äîand who we are is good."

üìñ Key Definitions

  • Safety Valve: The idea that western land prevented class conflict by giving workers an escape from industrial wage labor

The Frontier as Transformation

"The frontier is the line of most rapid and effective Americanization."
‚Äî Frederick Jackson Turner, 1893

Crucial framing:

  • Turner is not writing primarily about Native peoples, cowboys, or railroads
  • He is explaining Americans becoming Americans

The West functions as:

A process, not a place
An engine, not a subject

üìö What You Need to Know

This is the key to understanding Turner: he's not really interested in the West as a PLACE with people and events. He's interested in the West as a PROCESS that transformed Europeans into Americans. The frontier is a machine for making American identity.

Part III

American Exceptionalism

The Frontier as National Explanation

American Exceptionalism

üìñ Definition

American exceptionalism: the belief that the United States developed differently from other nations‚Äîthat its democracy and institutions are uniquely American, shaped by geography and experience rather than inheritance.

×

American Exceptionalism

The idea that America is fundamentally different from Europe and other "Old World" societies.

Common claims of exceptionalism:

  • No feudal past, no aristocracy
  • Born in revolution for liberty
  • Classless or socially mobile
  • Uniquely democratic
  • A "city on a hill" with special mission

Turner's contribution: He gave exceptionalism a GEOGRAPHIC explanation. America wasn't different because of ideas alone‚Äîit was different because of the frontier EXPERIENCE.

Critical note: Most historians today are skeptical of exceptionalism claims, seeing them as ideology rather than analysis.

üìö What You Need to Know

Before Turner, exceptionalism was often explained through Puritan origins or the Declaration of Independence. Turner gave it a geographic/environmental explanation: the frontier PROCESS made Americans different. This was enormously appealing because it seemed scientific and inevitable.

American Exceptionalism

Turner argued that 300 years of pioneering transformed European colonists into something entirely new.

How did the frontier create a unique American character?

American institutions weren't inherited‚Äîthey were forged by the frontier experience.

Democracy

Free land created political equality and self-government

Rugged Individualism

Survival on the frontier demanded self-reliance

Practical Innovation

Inventive, problem-solving turn of mind

Coarseness & Strength

Rough edges combined with resilient character

üìö What You Need to Know

This is the heart of Turner's argument about American EXCEPTIONALISM. He's saying Americans aren't just transplanted Europeans‚Äîthey're fundamentally different because of 300 years of frontier conditions. The frontier didn't just change institutions; it changed PEOPLE. Democracy, individualism, inventiveness‚Äîall products of the frontier.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Notice Turner includes both positive traits (democracy, innovation) and negative ones (coarseness). He's not entirely romantic about the frontier‚Äîhe acknowledges it created rough, sometimes violent people. But he sees this as part of American strength, not just a flaw.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This theory of American exceptionalism would become enormously influential in how Americans understood themselves throughout the 20th century. It provided a flattering origin story: Americans were exceptional not because of race or religion, but because of EXPERIENCE‚Äîthe frontier made them special.

Kinds of Frontiers and Their Modes of Advance

Turner argued the American frontier was not a single line, but a succession of advancing frontiers. Each type had its own pioneers, economy, and social effects.

1. Fur-Trading Frontier

Earliest phase: trappers and mountain men

2. Cattle Frontier

Open-range ranching and cowboy culture

3. Mining Frontier

Gold rushes and boom towns

4. Agricultural Frontier

Homesteaders and permanent settlement

Core Turner idea: The frontier process repeated itself over and over again across the continent.
Each new frontier zone went through roughly the same sequence ‚Äî from wild, extractive beginnings to settled, civilized farming communities ‚Äî continually renewing American democracy, individualism, and opportunity.

üìö What You Need to Know

Turner argued this REPEATING PROCESS‚Äînot just the frontier's existence‚Äîshaped American character. Each frontier type followed a similar pattern of transformation. When the census declared the frontier "closed" in 1890, Turner saw it as the end of a formative era in U.S. history.

×

Fur-Trading Frontier

  • Pioneers: Explorers, trappers, and mountain men
  • Mode of advance: Individual or small-group penetration into wilderness
  • Economy: Trade with Native Americans (beaver pelts for European goods)
  • Impact: Opened paths, mapped terrain, minimal permanent settlement
×

Cattle (Ranching) Frontier

  • Pioneers: Open-range cattle drives (Texas longhorns to Kansas railheads)
  • Mode of advance: Mobile herders following grass and water
  • Economy: Ranching on vast public lands
  • Impact: Created cowboy culture, lawlessness, conflict with farmers
×

Mining Frontier

  • Rushes: Gold/silver rushes (California 1849, Colorado, Black Hills, Klondike)
  • Mode of advance: Sudden, rapid in-migration of prospectors and speculators
  • Economy: Extractive, boom-and-bust cycles
  • Impact: Instant towns, violence, demand for law and supply lines
×

Agricultural (Farming) Frontier

  • Pioneers: Homesteaders, family farmers (final, most permanent phase)
  • Mode of advance: Steady westward migration, clearing land, plowing
  • Economy: Subsistence ‚Üí commercial agriculture (wheat, corn, etc.)
  • Impact: Established democracy, individualism, social institutions

The Influences on the East

Turner argued the frontier didn't only shape the West‚Äîit profoundly influenced the older, eastern parts of the United States.

1. Americanization of Immigrants

Frontier environment rapidly "Americanized" immigrants through shared experience and necessity

2. Decreased Dependence on England

Westward expansion reduced economic and cultural reliance on Britain

3. Growth of Nationalism

Frontier strengthened national identity and American political institutions:

  • Internal improvements ‚Äî canals, roads, railroads
  • Public land policy ‚Äî homestead acts

4. Frontier Individualism

Cultivated self-reliance and equality‚Äîqualities that flowed eastward and reinforced democratic ideals

Ideas born on the frontier influenced eastern society and national character‚Äînot just the West.

üìö What You Need to Know

Turner argued the frontier acted as a "safety valve" and a source of renewal for the ENTIRE country. This is why he saw the closing of the frontier in 1890 as historically significant‚Äîit wasn't just about the West, it was about losing the engine that had been driving American development for 300 years.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Notice how Turner connects frontier experience to NATIONAL policy: internal improvements, railroad legislation, land policy. The frontier wasn't just shaping individual character‚Äîit was shaping federal government priorities and the entire economy.

"Closing of a Great Historic Movement"

"... there can hardly be said to be a frontier line"
‚Äî Superintendent of the Census, 1890

Turnerian Syllogism

‚ñ∏ History of America has been the history of the frontier
‚ñ∏ Frontier is now closed (no longer exists)
‚ñ∏ America must now enter a new phase of history (something else will determine change)

üìö What You Need to Know

This is Turner's most famous argument, structured as a logical syllogism. If American history = frontier history, and the frontier is now closed, then American history must fundamentally change. This is why the 1890 census announcement was so significant to Turner‚Äîit marked the END of the formative process that made America what it was.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

The census superintendent's 1890 statement that there was no longer a clear frontier line prompted Turner's entire thesis. He delivered his paper in 1893, just three years after this announcement, arguing that this closure represented a watershed moment in American history.

üí≠ Context & Connections

Turner was writing at a moment of deep anxiety about America's future. With the frontier closed, what would happen to American democracy, individualism, and opportunity? Would America become like the "old" European nations‚Äîclass-bound, static, without outlets for expansion? This anxiety shaped Progressive Era debates about imperialism, immigration, and reform.

Why These Absences?

üîë Analytical Point

Turner explains outcomes while obscuring mechanisms.

Turner's Frontier Thesis reflects:

  • The priorities of late-19th-century professional history
  • The needs of a nation seeking coherence
  • The appeal of synthesis over conflict

Turner gives Americans a story that feels complete without confronting what completion cost.

üìö What You Need to Know

We're not saying Turner was deliberately lying or evil. We're saying his framework REQUIRED certain absences to work. You can't claim the frontier created democracy if you center the dispossession of sovereign nations. The coherence of the thesis depends on what it doesn't say.

From Turner to New Western History

Turner's thesis overlooked violence, environmental exploitation, and marginalized peoples. This opened the door for a complete reinterpretation.

Conquest Over Celebration

Not heroic march but invasions‚Äîdispossessing Indigenous peoples, creating inequalities

Multiculturalism & Inclusion

Centers voices from margins: Native Americans, Mexican Americans, Chinese laborers, women

Environment as Central Actor

Integrates ecology‚Äîdrought, overgrazing‚Äîchallenging "free land" myth

From myth of exceptional triumph to cautionary tale of imperialism, inequality, and ecological limits.

üìö What You Need to Know

Emerging in the 1980s-1990s, New Western History is a revisionist movement led by Patricia Nelson Limerick, Richard White, Donald Worster, and William Cronon. They argue the West isn't just a "frontier" that closed in 1890‚Äîit's a distinct, enduring REGION with complex, ongoing stories shaped by conquest, diversity, and human-environment interactions.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Limerick's The Legacy of Conquest calls the West "the most conquered region in the world." White's It's Your Misfortune and None of My Own weaves marginalized voices into a tapestry of overlapping histories. Cronon's Nature's Metropolis links Chicago's growth to western environmental transformation.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This isn't just academic‚Äîit informs TODAY'S debates on public lands, water rights, and Indigenous sovereignty. New Western History transforms how we understand the American story, revealing continuities rather than closure.

Part V

Turner as Cultural Work

Academic History as National Mythology

Turner's National Work

Turner is not just doing academic history.
He is doing national work.

What the frontier thesis accomplishes:

  • Converts conquest into character formation
  • Makes dispossession productive rather than troubling
  • Resolves moral tension without confronting it
  • Offers unity at a moment of political and social anxiety

üìö What You Need to Know

Remember, this is the 1890s: massive immigration, labor conflict, economic depression, anxiety about the future. Turner offers a reassuring story: America's identity was forged in the frontier, and that identity will persist even though the frontier is "closed."

Three Forms of Mythmaking

Buffalo Bill

Turned conquest into spectacle

Photography

Turned conquest into evidence

Turner

Turned conquest into explanation

All three:

  • Stabilize meaning
  • Reduce moral discomfort
  • Transform history into identity

üìö What You Need to Know

This is the synthesis across all three lectures. Buffalo Bill, Curtis, and Turner are doing the SAME cultural work through different mediums: entertainment, photography, and academic scholarship. All three make the West usable as national mythology.

Part VI

After Turner

Why He Persists

Turner's Lasting Influence

Turner's thesis:

  • Dominated much of 20th-century U.S. historiography
  • Shaped textbooks, curricula, and public memory
  • Reinforced narratives of American exceptionalism

Later historians‚Äîespecially the New Western History‚Äîchallenged:

  • Triumphalism
  • Exceptionalism
  • The omission of conquest and continuity
×

The New Western History

A movement beginning in the 1980s that challenged Turner's frontier thesis and offered alternative frameworks for understanding the American West.

Key figures:

  • Patricia Limerick ‚Äî The Legacy of Conquest (1987): Argues we should speak of "conquest" not "frontier"
  • Richard White ‚Äî It's Your Misfortune and None of My Own (1991): Comprehensive reinterpretation centering federal power and capitalism
  • Donald Worster ‚Äî Environmental history of the West

Key arguments: The West involved conquest, not just settlement. Federal government was central. The story didn't end in 1890. Race, gender, and environment matter.

üìö What You Need to Know

Turner's thesis was challenged seriously starting in the 1980s by historians who centered what Turner marginalized: Native peoples, federal power, capitalism, race, environment, and CONTINUITY (the West didn't "end" in 1890). Patricia Limerick's book title says it all: The Legacy of Conquest.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Patricia Limerick famously argued that we should replace the word "frontier" with "conquest" because it more accurately describes what happened. The frontier thesis, she argued, turned a story of winners and losers into a story of American character.

Why Turner Persists

Despite decades of critique, Turner's framework remains influential.

Why?

Elegant

Simple, powerful explanation

Optimistic

Flattering to American identity

Teachable

Easy to summarize and transmit

Good stories are hard to replace‚Äîespecially stories that make us feel good about ourselves.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is a point about how historical narratives work. Turner persists not because historians still accept his thesis, but because it's such a GOOD STORY‚Äîelegant, optimistic, easy to teach. Replacing it requires not just better evidence but a better story. Complexity is hard to teach.
