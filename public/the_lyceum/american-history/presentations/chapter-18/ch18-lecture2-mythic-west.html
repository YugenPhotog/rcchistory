This lecture explains how Americans transformed conquest and dispossession into reassuring national memory through mass entertainment and visual culture. We're asking: How do nations REMEMBER violence?

üí≠ Context & Connections

This follows directly from Lecture 1 on conquest. Having examined how conquest WORKED, we now examine how it was REMEMBERED. This connection matters because the myths created in the 1880s-1890s shaped American identity for generations.

Why Myth Matters

üîë Key Concept

Myth is not the opposite of history.
Myth is how societies organize meaning after upheaval and violence.

By the 1880s‚Äì1890s, the West was not distant history:

  • Dispossession, coercion, and violence were recent
  • Americans needed narratives that made this past feel: heroic, orderly, meaningful
×

Myth in Historical Analysis

In scholarly usage, "myth" doesn't mean "false story." It means a narrative structure that gives meaning to events and identity to communities.

Myths are:

  • Selective: They include some facts, exclude others
  • Purposeful: They serve present needs
  • Powerful: They shape how people understand themselves

The "Mythic West" isn't lies about the West‚Äîit's a carefully constructed narrative that organized violence into a usable national story.

üìö What You Need to Know

Students often think "myth = false." Clarify that historians use "myth" to mean a meaning-making narrative. The Wild West myth isn't simply lies‚Äîit's a selective story that organized real events into a particular pattern.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Richard Slotkin in his trilogy (especially Gunfighter Nation) traces how frontier mythology became central to American national identity‚Äînot as escapist fantasy but as ideological framework.

Guiding Question

How do nations remember events that are too recent, too violent, or too unsettling to leave unexplained?

The West wasn't ancient history in the 1880s‚Äîit was yesterday.

The people who made the myths lived through the events.

üìö What You Need to Know

This question should guide your thinking. We're not asking "was the myth true or false?" We're asking "what cultural WORK did the myth perform?" and "why did Americans NEED this story?"

Buffalo Bill's Wild West

Not a "circus"‚ÄîCody insisted it was an educational exhibition depicting "actual scenes" from frontier life.

Featured acts included:

  • Reenactments of "Indian attacks" on settlers
  • Sharpshooting demonstrations (Annie Oakley)
  • Stagecoach robberies
  • Pony Express riders
  • Cavalry charges

At its peak, the show employed 500+ performers and traveled by train with hundreds of horses.

üìö What You Need to Know

Buffalo Bill wasn't a marginal figure‚Äîhe was one of the most famous Americans of his era, known internationally. His show shaped how MILLIONS understood the American West. Queen Victoria attended; European heads of state visited.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Louis S. Warren, Buffalo Bill's America (2005) is the definitive biography. Warren shows how Cody's show both reflected and shaped American understanding of the West.

üìö What You Need to Know

The show's genius was making conquest feel SAFE and ENTERTAINING. Audiences could watch "Indian attacks" and cavalry charges without confronting the moral complexity of actual dispossession. Violence became spectacle; conquest became adventure.

Sitting Bull (T»üat»ü√°≈ãka √çyotake)

c. 1831‚Äì1890 ‚Äî Hunkpapa Lakota leader who led resistance to U.S. expansion.

Military leader: At Little Bighorn (1876), Sitting Bull was the spiritual leader whose vision predicted victory over Custer.

Wild West performer: In 1885, Sitting Bull toured with Buffalo Bill for one season. He reportedly earned $50/week plus bonuses. He gave much of his earnings to homeless children he encountered in cities.

Death: Killed by Indian agency police in 1890, just weeks before Wounded Knee, during the Ghost Dance crisis.

His participation in the Wild West show represents the complex, constrained choices available to Native leaders after military defeat.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is where the analysis gets complex. Native people participated in Wild West shows‚Äîsometimes for money, sometimes for mobility, sometimes for complex reasons we can't fully know. But they participated in scripts written by others, playing roles that reinforced stereotypes.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Part II

Photography and the Authority of "Truth"

The Camera as Evidence

Photography's Power

If Buffalo Bill offers spectacle, photography offers evidence.

Why Photography Matters

  • Claims objectivity
  • Appears scientific
  • Seems documentary
  • Carries cultural authority

Dominant Forms

  • Expeditionary photography
  • Ethnographic documentation
  • Studio "authenticity"
  • Landscape surveys

The camera doesn't just record‚Äîit constructs.

üìö What You Need to Know

In the late 1800s, photography was new and carried tremendous authority. People believed "the camera doesn't lie." But photographers made choices: what to photograph, how to frame it, what to include or exclude. Photography SEEMED objective but was always selective.

The "Vanishing Indian" Thesis

Curtis explicitly stated his goal was to document Native peoples before they "vanished." His introduction claimed:

"The information that is to be gathered... respecting the mode of life of one of the great races of mankind, must be collected at once or the opportunity will be lost."

The assumption: Native cultures were dying and would soon be gone entirely. Curtis saw himself as a salvage operation, preserving what would otherwise disappear.

The problem: Native peoples weren't actually vanishing‚Äîthey were adapting, surviving, and changing. Curtis's framing treated living people as already part of the past.

üìö What You Need to Know

Curtis is a complex figure. He spent decades documenting Native cultures, often with cooperation from Indigenous communities. His photographs are beautiful and historically valuable. But his FRAMEWORK‚Äîthat he was documenting a dying race‚Äîshaped everything about how he worked.

Curtis's Constructed "Authenticity"

  • Staged scenes in controlled settings
  • Removed modern clothing, tools, and environments
  • Provided "traditional" costumes and props
  • Presented Native life as timeless and already past
üîë Critical Distinction

Curtis is not simply documenting disappearance.
He is aestheticizing it.

Photography does not merely record reality. It structures how reality is understood.

üìö What You Need to Know

Curtis sometimes removed alarm clocks, modern clothing, or other "inauthentic" elements from photos. He provided traditional costumes. He wanted to photograph what he imagined as the "real" Indian‚Äîwhich meant erasing evidence that Native people were living in the present.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

Mick Gidley, Edward S. Curtis and the North American Indian provides the most thorough scholarly analysis of Curtis's methods and their implications.

üìù Check Your Understanding

Quick Question

When Edward Curtis removed modern clothing and provided "traditional" costumes for his photographs, he was:

  1. Simply documenting historical accuracy
  2. Constructing a particular vision of "authentic" Native identity
  3. Following standard scientific photography practices
  4. Respecting the wishes of his subjects

Answer: B ‚Äî Curtis was CONSTRUCTING authenticity according to his own vision of what "real Indians" should look like. By removing evidence of modern life, he placed Native peoples in a timeless past‚Äîeven as they were living, adapting people in the present.

Part III

Shared Assumptions

The Logic of Preservation and Assimilation

Late-19th-Century Reform Logic

Reformers broadly believed:

  • Native cultures were disappearing
  • That disappearance was inevitable

The perceived moral task was therefore twofold:

Preserve the Past

Photography, museums, ethnographic records

Salvage ethnography

Reshape the Future

Boarding schools, allotment, assimilation

Assimilation policy

üìö What You Need to Know

This is a conceptual slide connecting photography to policy. Curtis photographing "vanishing" cultures and Pratt running boarding schools shared the SAME ASSUMPTION: authentic "Indianness" belonged to the past. One tried to record it; the other tried to eliminate it.

The Shared Assumption

"Authentic 'Indianness' belongs to the past, not the present."

Key distinction to maintain:

  • Curtis is not advocating boarding schools
  • Boarding schools are not using Curtis directly

This connection is ideological and temporal, not causal.

They share a worldview, not a conspiracy.

üìö What You Need to Know

We're not claiming Curtis caused boarding schools or vice versa. We're identifying a SHARED ASSUMPTION across different institutions and practices: the belief that Native cultures were dying and that "real" Indian identity was already in the past.

Manifest Destiny Through the Lens

Landscape Photography and Empty Land

Landscape Photography

Emphasis on:

üìö What You Need to Know

Photographers like Carleton Watkins, Timothy O'Sullivan, and William Henry Jackson accompanied survey expeditions and created iconic images of the American West. These images emphasized vast, empty landscapes‚Äîeven though those lands were Indigenous homelands.

The Camera's Implicit Messages

"The land is unused"

"Indigenous presence is past"

"Expansion is natural"

üîë Key Insight

The camera makes conquest look peaceful.
Disappearance appears organic rather than coerced.

üìö What You Need to Know

By photographing "empty" landscapes, these images supported the ideology of Manifest Destiny. If the land appears uninhabited, then American expansion seems natural rather than violent. The absence of Native peoples in the frame erases the conquest that made that "emptiness."

Myth Is Structure

Myth is not falsehood.
Myth is structure.

The Mythic West provided:

  • A way to organize confusing, violent events
  • Heroes and villains in clear roles
  • A sense of resolution and completion
  • A usable past for national identity

üìö What You Need to Know

This is the core analytical point. Myth doesn't mean "false"‚Äîit means a STRUCTURE for organizing meaning. The Mythic West gave Americans a way to understand what had happened that avoided confronting the full moral weight of conquest.

The Mythic West created MEMORY rather than ANALYSIS. Americans could feel good about the West without really examining what had happened. This memory proved remarkably durable‚ÄîWestern mythology shaped American culture well into the 20th century and beyond.
