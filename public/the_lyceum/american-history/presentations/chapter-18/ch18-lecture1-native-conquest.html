Part I

Native Nations Before Conquest

Political, Social, and Economic Systems

Political Organization

  • Plains nations functioned as sovereign political communities
  • Authority rested in councils and recognized chiefs/headmen
  • Decision-making emphasized consensus over coercion
  • Effective for mobile societies‚Äîbut vulnerable to centralized nation-states
×

Sovereign Political Communities

Native nations governed themselves independently with their own laws, leadership structures, and diplomatic relations. They made treaties as equals with European powers and the U.S. government‚Äîevidence that even the U.S. recognized their political legitimacy.

Key point: U.S. officials often misread decentralized authority as political weakness rather than structural difference.

üìö What You Need to Know

Plains nations had sophisticated political systems‚Äîthey just looked different from European models. Instead of a single ruler with absolute power, they used councils and consensus. This wasn't "primitive"‚Äîit was a deliberate choice suited to their mobile lifestyle.

Economic Systems

Plains economies organized around:

  • Buffalo hunting as the economic foundation
  • Seasonal migration following resources
  • Regional trade networks spanning thousands of miles
üîë Key Difference

Wealth and status tied to kinship, mobility, and animals‚ÄîNOT land ownership

×

The Buffalo Economy

The buffalo provided virtually everything Plains peoples needed:

  • Food: Meat, both fresh and preserved as pemmican
  • Shelter: Hides for tipis
  • Clothing: Leather, fur, and wool
  • Tools: Bones, sinew, horns
  • Spiritual meaning: Central to religious ceremonies

This is why destroying the buffalo was such an effective weapon of conquest.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is crucial for understanding WHY the conquest took the form it did. These weren't societies that could simply be "defeated" militarily and then absorbed. Their entire way of life depended on movement, buffalo, and communal organization. To conquer them, the U.S. had to destroy these foundations.

Gender Roles and Power

Men's Roles

  • Hunting
  • Warfare
  • Diplomacy
  • Political/military authority

Women's Roles

  • Food processing
  • Shelter construction
  • Clothing production
  • Control of household resources

Women exercised substantial influence through kinship networks and marriage alliances

üìö What You Need to Know

These societies were neither egalitarian utopias nor rigid patriarchies. They operated according to internally coherent norms that don't map neatly onto European or modern categories.

üí≠ Context & Connections

When the U.S. imposed allotment and assimilation policies, they specifically targeted these gender systems‚Äîgiving land titles to men, teaching women domestic skills, and restructuring families along Euro-American lines.

The Challenge for Conquest

üîë Why This Matters

To dismantle these societies, the United States did not simply need military victory‚Äîit needed to undermine the ecological, economic, and social foundations of Native autonomy.

This explains why conquest took so many forms beyond battlefield combat.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is the analytical key to the entire lecture. Military force alone couldn't "conquer" peoples whose power didn't rest in fixed capitals or territorial boundaries. The U.S. had to attack the buffalo, attack mobility, attack communal land ownership, attack family structures, attack language and religion.

Part II

Violence and Conquest

When Battles Matter‚Äîand When They Don't

Why Battles Aren't the Center

  • Hundreds of armed conflicts occurred across the West
  • Focusing on tactics obscures:
    • Structural power imbalances
    • Demographic pressure
    • Industrial capacity
    • Long-term policy outcomes

Native resistance failed not due to lack of courage‚Äîbut due to asymmetries in logistics, population, and environmental control.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is a historiographical point: older histories focused heavily on battles and military campaigns. Modern historians emphasize that the "Indian Wars" were won less on battlefields than through systematic destruction of buffalo, railroad expansion, and reservation policies.

Two Battles That Matter Symbolically

1876 Little Bighorn ‚Äî Rare Indigenous military victory; showed limits of U.S. control
1890 Wounded Knee ‚Äî A massacre, not a battle; symbolic end of armed resistance
Key Takeaway

Battles punctuate the story, but policy finishes it.

Battle of the Little Bighorn (1876)

On June 25‚Äì26, 1876, Lakota, Northern Cheyenne, and Arapaho forces defeated the U.S. Army‚Äôs 7th Cavalry under Lt. Col. George A. Custer near the Little Bighorn River in present-day Montana.

The battle grew out of the Great Sioux War (1876‚Äì77), sparked in part by U.S. efforts to force Lakota people onto reservations and seize the Black Hills after gold was discovered there.

Why it shocked Americans: Custer and over 200 soldiers were killed‚Äîan unexpected defeat that dominated national headlines.

Historical significance: The victory demonstrated the real strength of Indigenous resistance, but it also helped justify a major U.S. escalation‚Äîmore troops, intensified campaigns, and increased pressure that pushed many Native communities onto reservations within a few years.

×

Wounded Knee Massacre (1890)

On December 29, 1890, U.S. soldiers massacred approximately 250-300 Lakota men, women, and children at Wounded Knee Creek, South Dakota.

The Lakota were being disarmed after practicing the Ghost Dance‚Äîa spiritual movement promising renewal. The military interpreted religious practice as military threat.

Historical significance: Marks the violent end of organized armed resistance on the Plains and coincides with intensified assimilation efforts.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

This slide compares two famous moments from the Plains Wars that loom large in American memory for very different reasons. Little Bighorn (1876) became a symbol of Indigenous military power, while Wounded Knee (1890) became a symbol of brutal suppression. Together, they show that dramatic clashes mattered emotionally and politically‚Äîbut they were not the main engine of conquest.

The key argument is in the takeaway line: battles are visible and dramatic, but federal policy‚Äîreservations, rations, land seizures, and forced cultural change‚Äîultimately determined the outcome of U.S.‚ÄìNative conflicts.

Breaking It Down

Little Bighorn (1876): This battle took place during the Great Sioux War, after the U.S. government tried to force Lakota and Cheyenne people onto reservations and seize the Black Hills after gold was discovered there. Lieutenant Colonel George A. Custer led the 7th Cavalry in an attack on a large Native encampment in present-day Montana. Instead of a small, scattered group, they encountered a massive coalition of Lakota, Northern Cheyenne, and Arapaho warriors. Custer split his forces and was overwhelmed; he and more than 200 of his men were killed. For background, see Battle of the Little Bighorn.

Symbolically, this was a stunning Indigenous victory that shocked the U.S. public. But the long-term result was not Native independence‚Äîit triggered a surge of military campaigns and harsher policies. Within a few years, most Plains nations involved were confined to reservations. So the battle shows both Indigenous strength and the federal government‚Äôs capacity to escalate.

Wounded Knee (1890): Fourteen years later, the situation on the Plains had changed dramatically. Most Lakota people were already confined to reservations, facing hunger, land loss, and heavy federal control. A religious movement called the Ghost Dance spread among many Native communities, promising spiritual renewal and the restoration of the world before conquest. U.S. officials misunderstood and feared the movement, treating it as a sign of rebellion. On December 29, 1890, soldiers of the 7th Cavalry surrounded a Lakota camp at Wounded Knee Creek to disarm them. A shot went off during the tense process, and troops opened fire with rifles and rapid-fire guns, killing hundreds of Lakota men, women, and children. See Wounded Knee Massacre.

Calling this a ‚Äúbattle‚Äù hides what happened. It was a massacre of largely disarmed people, and it is widely seen as marking the symbolic end of large-scale armed Native resistance on the Plains. It also shows how the U.S. military treated religious and cultural practices as security threats.

‚ÄúBattles punctuate the story, but policy finishes it.‚Äù Little Bighorn and Wounded Knee are powerful symbols, but by 1890 the decisive forces were laws, agencies, and systems‚Äîreservation confinement, land allotment, boarding schools, and bans on ceremonies‚Äîthat steadily dismantled Indigenous sovereignty.

Why This Matters

This slide helps you move beyond a ‚Äúwho won the fight‚Äù mindset. The United States could lose a spectacular battle like Little Bighorn and still tighten its grip through logistics, population pressure, railroads, and federal administration. Wounded Knee shows what happens when military power is used to enforce a system that has already boxed people in.

The broader theme in U.S. history is that westward expansion was not just a series of skirmishes‚Äîit was the spread of a modern nation-state using both violence and bureaucracy to replace Native political and cultural autonomy with federal control.

Part III

Buffalo Destruction

Economic and Ecological Warfare

Buffalo Destruction

Destruction driven by:

  • Railroad expansion
  • Commercial hide markets
  • Deliberate military strategy

The acknowledged logic:

Destroy the buffalo ‚Üí Force dependence ‚Üí Eliminate autonomy

üìö What You Need to Know

An estimated 30-60 million buffalo existed in 1800. By 1889, fewer than 1,000 remained. This wasn't accidental‚Äîit was policy. Army generals and politicians openly stated that destroying the buffalo would force Native peoples onto reservations.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

General Philip Sheridan reportedly said hunters "have done more to settle the vexed Indian question than the entire regular army." Whether this exact quote is authentic, the LOGIC was openly expressed by many officials.

Three Forms of Warfare

Economic

Destroyed the foundation of Plains economies

Ecological

Transformed the Great Plains environment

Cultural

Eliminated sacred and ceremonial center of life

üîë Analytical Point

Where military conquest proved costly or incomplete, environmental destruction succeeded.

üìö What You Need to Know

This is what scholars mean by "ecological imperialism"‚Äîconquest through environmental transformation. You can't maintain a mobile hunting society when there's nothing to hunt. This forced reservation dependency without requiring military victory in every engagement.

Regional Variation: California

The Logic of Elimination

  • Gold Rush (1849) created immediate settler pressure
  • Minimal treaty-making or reservation planning
  • Native peoples viewed as obstacles, not populations to manage

Forms of Violence

  • Vigilante campaigns
  • State-funded killing
  • Federal inaction

Interpretation

Many historians describe this as genocide

×

Genocide in California

Historian Benjamin Madley documents that California's Indigenous population fell from approximately 150,000 in 1846 to 30,000 by 1873‚Äîan 80% decline in less than 30 years.

This included:

  • State-funded militia campaigns
  • Bounties for Native scalps and heads
  • Enslavement under indenture laws
  • Deliberate starvation

Madley's book is titled An American Genocide (2016)‚Äîthe scholarly consensus increasingly supports this characterization.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

This slide shows that westward expansion did not unfold the same way everywhere. In California, the dominant pattern was not gradual treaty-making and reservation control, but rapid dispossession and extreme violence driven by the Gold Rush. Historians often describe this as a ‚Äúlogic of elimination,‚Äù meaning settlers frequently aimed to remove Native people entirely rather than incorporate or manage them.

Breaking It Down

Gold Rush (1849) created immediate settler pressure: The discovery of gold at Sutter‚Äôs Mill triggered a massive migration‚Äîover 100,000 newcomers arrived in just a few years. Mining camps, towns, and farms spread rapidly onto Native lands, especially in northern and central California. This brought disease, food disruption, and direct violence. For background, see California Gold Rush.

Minimal treaty-making or reservation planning: In 1851‚Äì1852, federal agents actually negotiated 18 treaties with California tribes, setting aside reservations. But the U.S. Senate secretly refused to ratify them and kept them hidden for decades. As a result, many Native communities were left without legally recognized land or protection. See California Indian treaties.

Native peoples viewed as obstacles, not populations to manage: Instead of seeing tribes as sovereign groups to negotiate with (as often happened on the Plains), many California settlers and officials saw Native people as barriers to mining, ranching, and settlement. This attitude justified removal, forced labor, and killing rather than containment on reservations.

Vigilante campaigns: Local settler groups organized armed raids on Native villages, often in retaliation for alleged theft or conflict, but frequently as preemptive violence to clear land. These attacks were rarely punished.

State-funded killing: The California state government reimbursed militia expeditions that carried out campaigns against Native communities. In the 1850s, the legislature approved funds to pay volunteers for these operations, effectively subsidizing violence.

Federal inaction: Even when federal officials knew about widespread violence, they often lacked troops, political will, or support to stop it. The result was a gap where settlers operated with little restraint, while Native communities had few legal or military protections.

Interpretation: genocide: Many historians use the term genocide to describe this period because violence, forced labor, displacement, and starvation combined to cause catastrophic population decline. For a general definition, see Genocide.

Why This Matters

Comparing California to the Plains helps you see that U.S. expansion followed different regional ‚Äúlogics.‚Äù On the Plains, the federal government often aimed to confine and control Native nations on reservations. In California, the explosive Gold Rush economy and weak treaty protections created conditions where elimination‚Äîthrough killing, forced labor, and displacement‚Äîwas common.

This regional variation complicates simple frontier stories. Conquest was not one uniform process; it depended on local economies, settler attitudes, and how (or whether) the federal government intervened.

üéì Historians Weigh In

Benjamin Madley ‚Äì An American Genocide

Madley documents how California‚Äôs Indigenous population collapsed from roughly 150,000 before the Gold Rush to about 30,000 by the 1870s. He traces specific militia campaigns, state reimbursements, and patterns of enslavement and starvation, arguing that this was not random chaos but a system of violence tolerated and often funded by authorities. His work is central to why many scholars now describe Gold Rush‚Äìera California as a case of genocide.

Part IV

The Reservation System

Containment and Dependency

The Reservation System

  • Reservations as tools of spatial and political control
  • Fixing mobile peoples in place enabled:
    • Surveillance
    • Ration-based coercion
    • Administrative oversight
  • Traditional authority eroded under federal agents
üîë Key Point

Dependency was not an accident but a structural feature. Reservations were designed to manage Native peoples, not preserve Native cultures.

üìö What You Need to Know

Think of reservations as combining elements of both prisons and welfare systems. Residents couldn't leave without permission, depended on government rations, and lived under federal agents who controlled daily life. This was conquest through administration.

üí≠ Context & Connections

This connects to our earlier discussions of "civil society" and institutions. The reservation system was a deliberate institutional design to transform independent peoples into dependent subjects.

Allotment: The Dawes Act (1887)

What It Did

  • Broke up communal landholding
  • Assigned individual plots (160 acres)
  • Declared "surplus" land open to whites

Underlying Goals

  • Dismantle communal sovereignty
  • Impose private property norms
  • Restructure family/gender relations

Results: Native land holdings fell from 138 million acres in 1887 to 48 million acres by 1934

üìö What You Need to Know

The Dawes Act was "reform" that accomplished conquest. Reformers genuinely believed private property would "civilize" Native peoples‚Äîbut the result was massive land theft. The "surplus" lands opened to white settlement were the real goal.

üìñ Key Definitions

  • Allotment: Breaking communal tribal lands into individual private plots
  • Assimilation: Policy of forcing Native peoples to adopt Euro-American culture, language, and lifeways

Critical framing

Allotment followed military defeat and containment; it was conquest continued by legal means.

Boarding Schools

Conquest Across Generations

  • Forced removal from families
  • Suppression of language and religion
  • Renaming, uniforms, discipline
  • Cultural erasure as explicit goal

"Kill the Indian, save the man"

‚Äî Capt. Richard Henry Pratt, founder of Carlisle

üìö What You Need to Know

This was conquest aimed at the FUTURE, not just the present. If you can't defeat a people militarily, you can try to ensure their children don't know their own language, religion, or traditions. The trauma of boarding schools continues to affect Native communities today.

üéì Senior Historians' Corner

David Wallace Adams, Education for Extinction (1995) is the standard scholarly account. Adams shows how boarding schools were explicitly designed to destroy Native identity across generations.

Resistance and Survival

Ghost Dance and Wounded Knee

  • The Ghost Dance: spiritual movement emphasizing renewal and hope
  • Not inherently violent‚Äîrooted in belief and meaning-making
  • U.S. officials interpreted religious practice as military threat
The Ghost Dance promised that faithful practice would restore the buffalo, bring back the ancestors, and renew the earth‚Äîa response to unimaginable loss.

The Ghost Dance Movement

Founded by the Paiute prophet Wovoka around 1889, the Ghost Dance spread rapidly among Plains peoples experiencing devastation from conquest.

Core beliefs:

  • Faithful dancing and moral behavior would bring renewal
  • Ancestors would return
  • The buffalo would come back
  • White settlers would disappear or leave

This was a peaceful religious movement‚Äîthe violence came entirely from the U.S. military's response.

üìö What You Need to Know

The Ghost Dance shows both the depth of despair (people hoping for supernatural intervention because earthly resistance seemed impossible) and the persistence of cultural identity (maintaining spiritual practices despite suppression). The massacre at Wounded Knee happened because officials feared what was actually a peaceful religious movement.
