Breaking It Down

Flash powder was a mixture of magnesium powder and potassium chlorate. When ignited with a match, it produced a brilliant explosive flash â€” and thick, choking smoke that filled whatever room Riis was photographing. This technology had only become available in the mid-1880s, which is part of why Riis's work was possible when it was. The camera existed before; the ability to photograph dark interiors did not.

The process was dramatic and dangerous. Riis would enter tenements late at night, often accompanied by police officers. His assistant would hold a pan of flash powder, Riis would open the camera shutter, the assistant would touch the powder with a match â€” BOOM â€” a brilliant flash, then acrid smoke filling the room. He set at least two documented fires in tenement buildings. He blew out windows. Residents, terrified by what sounded like explosions, sometimes chased Riis and his team out of buildings. In lodging houses, sleeping men scattered in panic, thinking it was a police raid.

Why this mattered: The worst tenement conditions were in interior rooms with no windows and no natural light. These were spaces that SPCC investigators might never see â€” they'd interview a family in the front room during the day and never enter the airless sleeping quarters in back where children actually lived. Riis's flash powder made it possible, for the first time, to photograph these spaces and show them to a middle-class audience that had no idea they existed.

Why This Matters

There's a powerful metaphor built into this technology: the physical danger of the flash powder mirrored the social danger of exposure. Society didn't want to see these conditions any more than tenement residents wanted photographers exploding flash powder in their rooms at midnight.
