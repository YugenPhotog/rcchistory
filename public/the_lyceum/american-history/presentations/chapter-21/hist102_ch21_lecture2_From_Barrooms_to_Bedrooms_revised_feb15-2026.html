Anthony Comstock: Pioneering Federal Control Over Sexuality

The Man and His Mission
  • U.S. Postal Inspector, 1873-1915
  • Founded NY Society for the Suppression of Vice (1873)
  • Self-appointed guardian of American morality
  • Died 1915, but laws remained
The Comstock Act (1873)
  • Banned "obscene" materials from U.S. mail
  • Defined contraception info as obscene
  • Gave federal power over sexual knowledge

A Crusade of Enforcement

3,600+ arrests personally made by Comstock

160+ tons of "obscene" literature destroyed

Thousands of items confiscated from mail

Laws remained in effect until 1970s

Federal moral regulation didn't start with progressives—but they inherited and expanded Comstock's toolkit

The Reality They Ignored

George Kibbe Turner, Muckraker of Vice

  • Journalist in the McClure's Magazine muckraking circle
  • "The City of Chicago" (1907): exposed links between machine politics, saloons, and organized prostitution
  • Blamed immigrant men—especially Jewish and Italian—for running vice networks
  • Mixed genuine investigative journalism with nativist scapegoating
  • Gave progressive reformers a villain: the foreign-born vice lord corrupting American women

Turner's Moral Panic Goes National

  • McClure's Magazine, 1909: "The Daughters of the Poor"
  • Turner exposes Chicago's vice districts in shocking detail
  • But frames prostitution as moral threat, not economic problem
  • By 1916: over 100 cities investigating prostitution

The White-Slave Traffic Act

  • Official Purpose: Prohibit transporting women across state lines "for immoral purposes"
  • Stated Target: Commercial vice and forced prostitution
  • Actual Use: Policing consensual relationships, especially interracial ones
  • Result: Morality legislation becomes tool of racial and sexual surveillance

Temperance Begins as Self-Help

The Washingtonian Movement, 1840s
  • Founded 1840, Baltimore
  • Six reformed drinkers (working-class artisans)
  • Peer support through public testimony
  • No moral condemnation—mutual aid
  • Voluntary abstinence, not legislation
Why It Worked (and Failed)
  • Explosive growth in early 1840s
  • Working-class men helping each other
  • Collapsed by late 1840s (no infrastructure)
  • Key principle: "We help each other stay sober"
This was temperance before progressives got hold of it

From Voluntary Abstinence to Federal Coercion

Margaret Sanger and Bodily Autonomy

  • Nurse in immigrant tenements, witnessed desperate women

  • Returned 1916, opened Brownsville Clinic: first U.S. birth control clinic
  • Arrested again, served 30 days in workhouse (1917)
Birth control = women's freedom from constant pregnancy, economic independence, sexual autonomy
