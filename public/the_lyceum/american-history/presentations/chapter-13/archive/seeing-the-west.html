Slide 1: Title Slide - Introduction

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

Good evening. Tonight we're going to talk about how the American West was invented—not discovered, but invented—through images. This painting we're looking at, American Progress by John Gast, perfectly captures what we're going to explore. We'll return to it in detail shortly, but notice right away: civilization moving left to right, light to darkness, order to chaos. This isn't documentation—it's argument.

Here's the central question for tonight: What does it mean to "see" something? Because images don't just show us what's there—they argue. They make claims about what matters, who belongs, what's beautiful, what's inevitable. The images we're examining tonight didn't just reflect American ideas about the West. They created those ideas. And those images are still with us.

×

Slide 2: The Power of Images

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

This massive mural hangs in the U.S. Capitol. It was commissioned by Congress in 1861—right as the Civil War began—and it tells you everything about how the federal government wanted Americans to see westward expansion.

Notice the composition: pioneers struggle heroically toward the golden west, the promised land visible in the distance. There's drama, there's divine light, there's destiny. This is propaganda, but we don't usually call it that. We call it "art."

These images turned a place where millions of people already lived into an empty wilderness. They transformed military conquest into divine destiny. They made genocide look like natural law. This matters because these images are still with us—in every Western movie, every national park poster, every political speech about American identity. We're still seeing the West through lenses ground in the 1840s.

So tonight we're going to learn to see differently. We're going to look at who made these images, how they made them, what they left out, and why it mattered.

×

Slide 3: European vs. American Landscape

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Let's start with landscape painting. This is Thomas Cole, founder of what we call the "Hudson River School" style. Notice how the composition works: vast landscape, mountains in the distance, dramatic light. Where are the people? There's maybe a tiny figure, absolutely dwarfed by nature.

This wasn't how European landscape painting worked. European landscapes showed cultivated land, human presence, civilization. But American landscape painters were making a different argument: This land is sublime. It's pristine. It's empty. It's waiting.

Here's the kicker: it wasn't empty. The lands these painters depicted—the Hudson Valley, the Catskills—these were Indigenous homelands. The Mohican, Lenape, Abenaki peoples had lived there for thousands of years. Many were still there when these paintings were made. But the paintings erased them. Not by accident. This was the whole point.

American landscape painting was creating what we might call a "doctrine of emptiness"—the visual argument that the continent was essentially uninhabited, that the land was waiting for its true destiny. And notice the light—it's always dramatic, almost divine. This isn't just pretty scenery. It's theology. The landscape is saying: This is God's design. This is manifest destiny painted in oil.

×

Slide 4: Bierstadt Goes West

Lecture Notes (4 minutes)

When artists like Albert Bierstadt headed west in the 1860s, they brought this visual vocabulary with them. Look at this massive painting—it's six feet by ten feet, designed to overwhelm viewers. Bierstadt spent weeks in the Wind River Range with a Shoshone community in 1859, and yes, he includes their encampment in the painting.

But notice where it is—in the middle ground, bathed in golden light, peaceful, permanent-looking. This looks like Eden. It looks timeless.

Here's what Bierstadt knew and didn't paint: by 1863, when this was exhibited, Shoshone communities were being devastated by smallpox, facing military pressure, losing access to traditional hunting grounds. The Fort Bridger Treaty that would confine them to reservations was signed in 1863—the same year this painting toured to enormous acclaim. Bierstadt painted a timeless paradise at the exact moment it was being destroyed.

The composition tells you what to think: the Shoshone belong in this Eden-like setting, sure, but they're part of nature, not civilization. They're elements of the landscape, like the deer or the waterfalls. And the viewer—standing where Bierstadt stood—is outside the painting, looking in, claiming it with their gaze.

These paintings sold for enormous sums—this one was purchased for $25,000, equivalent to about $600,000 today. They hung in the Capitol, in millionaires' homes, in exhibition halls. They shaped how Americans understood westward expansion—not as conquest but as fulfillment, not as violence but as destiny.

×

Slide 5: Visual Vocabulary of Manifest Destiny

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Now let's return to where we started. John Gast's American Progress is remarkably blunt about what other paintings imply. This was commissioned in 1872 by George Crofutt for a Western travel guide—this is literally advertising for westward migration.

The symbolism is explicit: Columbia, representing American civilization, floats westward stringing telegraph wire and carrying a schoolbook. She brings light—literally. The right side of the painting (the East) is bright, settled, civilized. Ships, trains, cities. The left side (the West) is dark. Storm clouds hang over distant mountains.

And what's in that darkness? Indigenous peoples and buffalo, fleeing together into the shadows. They're literally being pushed out of the light, into extinction. This isn't subtext—it's text. The painting argues that this displacement is natural, inevitable, even divinely ordained. The "Star of Empire" on Columbia's head makes the religious imagery explicit.

This image was reproduced thousands of times as a chromolithograph. It hung in homes, schools, businesses. It taught Americans how to see westward expansion—as progress, as enlightenment literally pushing back darkness.

×

Slide 6: The "Vanishing Race" as Cultural Project

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Now we shift from landscapes to people. But notice—this shift isn't as dramatic as it seems. Indigenous people in portraits are often treated the same way as Indigenous people in landscapes: as part of nature, as disappearing, as objects to be preserved rather than subjects with agency.

Starting in 1821, the federal government commissioned Charles Bird King to paint portraits of Native American leaders who came to Washington for treaty negotiations. Secretary of War James Barbour was explicit about the purpose: to preserve "the likenesses of some of the most distinguished among this extraordinary race of people" because "this race was about to become extinct."

Think about what that means. The government is negotiating treaties—often fraudulent, coercive treaties—that will dispossess these leaders' peoples of their land. And simultaneously, they're commissioning portraits for "posterity," as if extinction is natural rather than something they're actively engineering.

These aren't collaborative portraits. The subjects—leaders like Petalesharro, Monchousia, Sharitahrish—wore peace medals with President Monroe's profile, gifts that signaled their subordinate political status. They're dressed formally, posed stiffly, presented as dignified but ultimately doomed figures from a "vanishing" world.

King painted over 200 portraits. Many were destroyed in a Smithsonian fire in 1865. The loss was lamented as the disappearance of records of a disappeared people—completing the circle of erasure.

×

Slide 7: George Catlin's Grand Project

Lecture Notes (4 minutes)

George Catlin took this preservation project and turned it into a massive artistic and commercial enterprise. In the 1830s, Catlin traveled extensively through the Great Plains painting Indigenous peoples. He created over 500 paintings representing more than 40 tribes, which he exhibited in a traveling "Indian Gallery" across the United States and Europe.

His stated mission? To preserve the image of the "vanishing Indian" before they disappeared completely. He wrote: "the Indians are melting away like snow before the sun... and I have flown to their rescue—not of their lives or of their race (for they are doomed)... but to the rescue of their looks and their modes."

Notice that language: "doomed." Not being killed—doomed. As if by natural law rather than policy.

Now, Catlin genuinely respected many of his subjects. He learned languages, participated in ceremonies, built relationships over years. His paintings show remarkable attention to detail in clothing, tools, ceremonial objects. But his entire project was premised on a lie: that Indigenous peoples were naturally disappearing, fading away before the inevitable march of civilization.

They weren't vanishing—they were being systematically removed. The Indian Removal Act had passed in 1830, the year Catlin began his western travels. The Trail of Tears happened during Catlin's painting career. Indigenous peoples weren't dying of natural causes; they were being killed by disease, warfare, and forced relocation orchestrated by federal policy.

But Catlin's paintings presented this as tragedy, not genocide. As inevitable loss, not political violence. And in doing so, he created the template for how white Americans would imagine "authentic" Indianness—as something from the past, something unchanging, something doomed.

×

Slide 8: Controlling the Image of Authenticity

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Catlin's subjects had to perform authenticity in very specific ways. They needed to look "traditional" enough for white audiences to recognize them as "real" Indians. Modern clothing, Christian conversion, literacy, adaptation—anything that suggested Indigenous people living in the 1830s rather than some imagined pre-contact past—was excluded or minimized.

This created an impossible bind. Indigenous peoples were being told: assimilate or die. Learn English, convert to Christianity, adopt Euro-American agriculture and dress. But simultaneously, white artists and audiences were saying: if you assimilate, you're no longer authentically Indian. You've lost your culture. You're not worth preserving.

So Indigenous leaders in Catlin's paintings are frozen in a romanticized past, even as their actual communities are navigating impossible present circumstances. Catlin gets to decide what counts as authentically Indigenous, what's worth preserving, what will be shown to white audiences in his exhibitions.

The paintings become more real than the people. The image becomes the definition. When Indigenous peoples don't match the romantic portrait—when they wear "white" clothing, when they adopt new technologies, when they change—white Americans say "the real Indians have vanished." The paintings helped create the myth they claimed to document.

×

Slide 9: The Economics of Disappearance

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

And here's something we need to name directly: the "vanishing Indian" was profitable. Catlin's exhibitions charged admission. European audiences, especially, paid premium prices to see what they believed were images of a disappearing race. The more doomed Indigenous peoples seemed, the more valuable the paintings became.

This created a perverse incentive structure. Artists, showmen, writers—they all benefited from presenting Indigenous peoples as vanishing. Wild West shows, world's fairs, museums—entire industries were built on the spectacle of disappearance. Buffalo Bill Cody made millions touring "authentic" Indian performances.

The myth of the vanishing Indian wasn't just ideology. It was business. And the artists and entrepreneurs who promoted this myth had financial stakes in maintaining it, even as Indigenous peoples were surviving, resisting, and insisting "we're still here."

×

Slide 10: Photography Arrives

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Then photography changes everything—or seems to. The daguerreotype process arrives in America in 1839, and almost immediately, photographers begin making portraits of Native American leaders.

Why does photography matter differently than painting? Because photographs were understood as mechanical reproductions of reality. A painter interprets; a camera records. Or so people believed. Photographs carried an authority that paintings couldn't claim—they were supposedly objective, scientific, true.

Early daguerreotypes of Native American leaders often happened during the same kinds of treaty negotiations that prompted King's paintings. Leaders came to Washington, and photographers documented them. But the photographic encounter was different from the painting session. It was faster, more mechanical, required less cooperation between artist and subject.

And the results looked different. Daguerreotypes were sharp, detailed, unforgiving. They showed texture, age, individual features in ways painting didn't. They seemed to capture the person, not an idealized version. This made them more powerful as evidence—evidence of what, exactly, depends on who's looking and why.

×

Slide 11: The Politics of the Photographic Encounter

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Look closely at these early photographic portraits. The subjects are usually in studios, posed formally, often uncomfortably. They're wearing a mix of traditional and Euro-American clothing—sometimes their own choices, sometimes provided by photographers or government agents. They hold weapons or ceremonial objects, markers of identity that white audiences expected to see.

But notice what the photograph can't show you: Why are these men in Washington? Usually for treaty negotiations. Often coercive negotiations where they're being pressured to cede vast territories. The photographic session is happening in the same building where their land is being stolen.

The photographs present them as dignified, serious, powerful—but that dignity is being captured in the moment of their dispossession. The photograph becomes a record of presence at the scene of erasure. Future viewers will look at these images and say "how noble they looked" without asking "why were they there?"

And here's the deeper issue: the photograph seems neutral. It seems to just show what was there. But everything about the photographic encounter is structured by power. Who owns the camera? Who decides when to release the shutter? Who keeps the negative? Who circulates the image and controls how it's captioned, displayed, interpreted?

Photography's claim to truth makes these power dynamics invisible. The image looks objective, so we forget to ask whose objectivity, whose truth, whose gaze is being recorded.

×

Slide 12: Building an Archive of Disappearance

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

By the 1850s-1860s, systematic photographic documentation of Indigenous peoples becomes federal policy. The Smithsonian starts collecting photographs. The War Department commissions them. Ethnographers use them to create typologies, classifications, comparative studies.

This is what anthropologists call "salvage ethnography"—the urgent project of documenting disappearing cultures before they vanish. Except, again, Indigenous peoples aren't naturally vanishing. They're being forcibly removed, confined to reservations, subjected to policies designed to destroy their cultures.

So photography becomes complicit in a paradox: it documents Indigenous peoples as disappearing at the exact moment federal policy is trying to make them disappear. The camera creates evidence of the crime while treating the crime as natural history. The photographs say "look how noble they were" rather than "look what we're doing to them."

×

Slide 13: Enter the Mountain Man

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Now let's talk about a visual tradition we haven't addressed yet: the white frontiersman. While Indigenous peoples are being portrayed as vanishing, white men are being portrayed as conquering—but in a specific way. Not as soldiers or farmers, but as a particular type: the mountain man, the scout, the frontiersman who lives between worlds.

Artists like Alfred Jacob Miller romanticized trappers and mountain men—white men who adopted Indigenous technologies, married Indigenous women, lived in Indigenous territories, but remained fundamentally white and fundamentally superior in the visual narrative.

Think about what this figure does culturally. He's white but wild. He's civilized but free. He can move between the world of settlement and the world of wilderness. He knows Indigenous peoples but isn't Indigenous. He's at home in nature but will ultimately bring civilization.

This is a deeply ideological figure. He makes conquest look like adventure. He makes dispossession look like exploration. And crucially, he replaces Indigenous peoples as the protagonist in the drama of the West. Indigenous peoples become part of the backdrop—the wilderness the frontiersman navigates—rather than central actors in their own story.

×

Slide 14: The Emigrant and the Pioneer

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

As the frontier narrative develops, we get another key figure: the emigrant family, the pioneer. Look at this painting by William Powell from 1837. An Indigenous man stands with a group of white settlers, pointing west, apparently giving them directions, facilitating their journey.

This is fantasy. Powell painted this in the same decade that President Andrew Jackson's policies forcibly removed tens of thousands of Indigenous people from their ancestral lands via the Trail of Tears. The "peaceful handoff" shown here—Indigenous peoples voluntarily guiding settlers to replace them—is propaganda.

But notice what the painting does: it makes westward migration look benign, even collaborative. It shows white families—women, children, wagons full of belongings—representing civilization, domesticity, progress. They vastly outnumber the lone Indigenous figure. The land he's pointing to is already empty of his people. The settlers represent the tide of civilization inheriting the West from its original stewards.

This visual narrative—Indigenous peoples voluntarily making way for worthy white settlers—obscures the violence, the coercion, the theft. It turns ethnic cleansing into a story of natural succession, even cooperation.

×

Slide 15: Photography Captures the New Hero

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

By the 1860s-1880s, photography is creating a new kind of frontier celebrity: the scout, the buffalo hunter, the gunfighter. Men like Buffalo Bill Cody, Wild Bill Hickok, Kit Carson—they become famous through photographs, through visual mythology.

These photographs present the West as a space of masculine adventure, individual heroism, violent conquest celebrated rather than condemned. The frontiersman has evolved from mountain man to action hero. And photography makes him seem real, documented, historical rather than mythological.

But look at what's happening during this period: systematic destruction of buffalo herds to starve Plains peoples into submission; military campaigns against Apache, Lakota, Cheyenne, Nez Perce; confinement to reservations; destruction of traditional economies. The frontier heroes in these photographs are often participants in genocide.

But the photographs don't show that. They show adventure, bravery, mastery of the wilderness. They make conquest look heroic. They center white men as the protagonists of Western history, displacing Indigenous peoples entirely.

×

Slide 16: Government Surveys: Mapping for Conquest

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Now we need to talk about how photography becomes an instrument of state power. In the 1860s-1870s, the federal government launches massive surveying expeditions: the King Survey, the Wheeler Survey, the Hayden Survey. These are military-scientific projects mapping western territories for potential settlement, resource extraction, and military control.

And they all bring photographers. Why? Because Congress needs evidence. They need proof that this land is valuable, that it's worth the cost of surveying and taking, that it justifies American expansion. Photographs provide that proof with mechanical authority that paintings can't match.

William Henry Jackson's photographs from the Hayden Survey of Yellowstone in 1871 are technically remarkable—he's hauling hundreds of pounds of equipment through wilderness, coating glass plates with chemicals in a portable darkroom. But look at what they show: pristine landscapes, dramatic geological formations, sublime natural architecture. They look untouched, eternal.

Here's what Jackson didn't photograph: the Crow, Shoshone, and Bannock peoples who used that landscape seasonally, who were being forcibly removed even as Jackson set up his tripod. His Yellowstone photographs helped convince Congress to create the first national park in 1872—protecting the land by clearing out the people who'd known it for millennia.

×

Slide 17: Timothy O'Sullivan and Military Intelligence

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Timothy O'Sullivan worked for the Wheeler Survey, which was explicitly military. Lieutenant George Wheeler was mapping the Southwest for potential military operations—identifying water sources, routes, strategic positions for campaigns against Apache, Navajo, and other Indigenous groups resisting encroachment.

O'Sullivan's photographs served multiple purposes simultaneously. They're scientific records documenting geological formations, terrain. They're commercial intelligence showing mineral deposits, timber, agricultural potential. And they're instruments of conquest providing reconnaissance for military campaigns.

Some of O'Sullivan's most famous images include Indigenous people—usually posed, usually presented as ethnographic specimens rather than political actors. These weren't collaborative portraits. They were part of a larger archive of knowledge being compiled to facilitate dispossession.

The surveys treated the West as a problem to be solved, a landscape to be known and therefore controlled. Photography was the technology that made that knowing possible. Every photograph was a small act of claiming—"we have seen this, we have recorded it, therefore we can possess it."

×

Slide 18: Railroads Sell the West

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

Photography wasn't just for government. Railroad companies figured out quickly that photographs could sell the West. The Union Pacific, Northern Pacific, Santa Fe—all hired photographers for promotional campaigns.

These weren't scientific documents. They were advertisements. They showed fertile farmland, happy settlers, abundant resources, adventure waiting. They promised opportunity, land ownership, new beginnings. They were designed to fill railroad cars with paying customers heading west.

And they worked. These photographs circulated in newspapers, magazines, immigration pamphlets in Europe. They shaped how millions imagined the West—not as someone's homeland being taken, but as empty land available for the taking. Photography made that argument with mechanical authority that painting never could.

×

Slide 19: Photography's "Objective" Lie

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

So let's be clear about what photography's "objectivity" actually means. Yes, the camera records what's in front of it mechanically. But the photographer chooses where to point the camera, when to release the shutter, which negatives to print, how to crop and caption the image.

Photography's claim to truth—its mechanical, scientific authority—actually makes these choices more powerful because they become invisible. When you look at a painting, you know an artist made decisions. When you look at a photograph, it's easy to forget that a photographer made just as many decisions.

Survey photographers photographed sublime landscapes, not reservation poverty. Railroad photographers showed opportunity, not displacement. Portrait photographers posed Indigenous leaders formally, not in the contexts of coercion that brought them to the studio.

Photography gave Americans a way to see the West that felt objective, scientific, true—while actually constructing a very particular, very political vision that justified conquest and made genocide invisible.

×

Slide 20: Edward Curtis's Grand Mission

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Now we get to Edward Curtis—the culmination and most sophisticated expression of everything we've discussed. Starting around 1900, Curtis embarked on what he called a "comprehensive record" of Native American peoples before they "vanished." Over three decades, he produced roughly 40,000 images covering about 80 tribes.

This required massive resources. He got backing from J.P. Morgan, one of America's richest men. The project was published in twenty volumes—limited edition portfolios that cost the equivalent of a house. This was monumental artistic and ethnographic work.

Curtis genuinely believed he was doing something important, even noble. He learned languages, participated in ceremonies, built relationships over years. He produced technically brilliant work—he was a master of light, composition, printing. The images are undeniably beautiful.

But Curtis's entire project was based on the same lie as Catlin seventy years earlier: that Indigenous peoples were vanishing, that authentic Indianness existed only in the past, that modernity and Indigenous identity were incompatible.

Look at this image—actually titled The Vanishing Race. Navajo riders moving away from the camera into shadow and mist, literally fading into the distance. The title and composition make the argument explicit: we're watching the end of a race of people.

×

Slide 21: Constructing Authenticity Through Photography

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Curtis wasn't just documenting—he was directing, staging, constructing. He carried trunks of "traditional" clothing and props. If subjects wore "modern" clothing—and many did, because they were living in 1900, not 1800—he made them change. If there were clocks, alarm clocks, suspenders, anything suggesting Indigenous people living in the present, he removed them or edited them out.

He used specific photographic techniques to create the right mood. Soft focus to suggest timelessness and nostalgia. Sepia tones for that "old-time" feel. Orthochromatic film that rendered certain skin tones and materials in specific ways. Dramatic lighting to emphasize what he saw as nobility and tragedy.

Curtis didn't see this as dishonest. In his mind, he was capturing the "real" Indian beneath the corrupting influence of white civilization. Modern clothing was inauthentic—a sign of cultural loss. But of course, Indigenous peoples had always adapted, changed, incorporated new technologies. Cultural change isn't cultural death.

Curtis decided what counted as authentically Indigenous, what was worth preserving, what should be hidden. His subjects—living people with complex lives in the present—became actors in a historical drama about the vanishing past. He transformed living cultures into museum exhibits, living people into monuments.

×

Slide 22: The Politics of Nostalgia

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

Here's what makes Curtis's work not just problematic but actively harmful: he created these nostalgic images of the "vanishing Indian" at the exact moment the federal government was implementing its most aggressive assimilation policies.

The Dawes Allotment Act of 1887 had broken up tribal lands, forcing private property ownership that destroyed communal systems and opened millions of acres to white settlement. Boarding schools were forcibly removing Indigenous children from families, punishing them for speaking their languages, for practicing religions, for being Indigenous. The 1890 Wounded Knee Massacre had just happened. The Ghost Dance movement—a spiritual resistance movement—had been violently suppressed.

Curtis was photographing the "vanishing race" while federal policy was actively trying to make Indigenous peoples vanish—not physically, but culturally. The government wasn't watching Indigenous peoples sadly fade away. They were deliberately destroying Indigenous cultures, languages, sovereignty.

And Curtis's nostalgic images made that violence easier to accept. They said: "Look, they're already disappearing. This is tragedy, yes, but it's inevitable. We're just preserving the memory." The photographs created a sense that Indigenous peoples belonged to the past, not the present or future. That made contemporary dispossession seem less like theft and more like natural evolution.

The nostalgia wasn't innocent. It was political. It made genocide look like progress.

×

Slide 23: Curtis's Subjects Had Agency

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

We need to be careful not to treat Curtis's subjects as passive victims with no agency. Many Indigenous people who worked with Curtis did so strategically, on their own terms, for their own reasons.

Some needed the income—Curtis paid his subjects, though often minimally. Some saw opportunities to create records for their own communities. Some deliberately performed what Curtis wanted while maintaining their own dignity and interior lives that the camera couldn't capture. Some manipulated Curtis's expectations, giving him the exotic images he wanted while protecting sacred knowledge and practices from exposure.

The photographic encounter was a site of negotiation, not just domination. Indigenous people made choices within constrained circumstances. They weren't just objects of the camera's gaze—they were subjects with intentions, strategies, resistance.

That said, the power imbalance was real. Curtis controlled the camera, owned the negatives, determined circulation, wrote the captions. His vision became the authoritative record. His subjects' own interpretations of their lives, their own photographic practices, their own ways of seeing—those were marginalized or erased entirely.

So we need to hold two truths simultaneously: Curtis's subjects had agency and used it. And Curtis's project still participated in and benefited from systems of domination that were destroying Indigenous communities.

×

Slide 24: What Curtis Refused to Photograph

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

So what was Curtis refusing to photograph? What was happening in Indigenous communities while he was creating his romantic, nostalgic vision?

Reservations. By the 1890s-1910s when Curtis was working, most Indigenous peoples in the West had been forcibly confined to reservations—often on the worst land, far from traditional territories. They were subject to Indian agents who controlled food rations, travel permissions, economic activity, religious practices, even family structures.

Poverty was endemic. Promised rations often didn't arrive or were inadequate. Traditional economies—hunting, trading, seasonal migration—had been destroyed. Land allotment was breaking up communal holdings. Corruption was rampant among Indian agents.

Disease devastated communities. Tuberculosis, trachoma, other diseases spread rapidly in overcrowded, impoverished conditions. The federal government's response was often inadequate or actively harmful—including forced sterilizations of Indigenous women.

Children were being taken to boarding schools where they were punished for speaking their languages, cut off from families and cultures, subjected to abuse. The motto of these schools was "Kill the Indian, save the man."

This was the reality Curtis was contemporaneous with. But you'd never know it from his photographs. His images show dignity, tradition, timelessness—never the violence, poverty, and systematic cultural destruction actually happening.

×

Slide 25: Indigenous People Living in Modernity

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

And here's what else Curtis wouldn't photograph: Indigenous people living in modernity. Because they were. They were adapting, negotiating, finding ways to survive and maintain identity within impossible constraints.

Some became ranchers, farmers, wage laborers—not because they'd abandoned Indigenous identity but because they needed to survive in a transformed economy. Some learned English and used literacy as a tool of resistance, writing to newspapers, petitioning Congress, documenting treaty violations.

Some sent children to boarding schools strategically—not because they believed in assimilation but because they believed education might provide tools for future resistance. Some became photographers themselves, creating their own visual records on their own terms.

Indigenous peoples were living complex, modern lives in the early 20th century. They wore "white" clothing not because they'd stopped being Indigenous but because it was practical, available, sometimes required. They used new technologies—cameras, typewriters, automobiles—while maintaining languages, ceremonies, kinship systems, tribal identities.

But Curtis's photographs erased all of this. If you wore a suit, you weren't authentically Indian in his vision. If you lived in a house rather than a tipi, you'd lost your culture. If you used a camera yourself, you were too modern to photograph.

This created an impossible bind: adapt and be declared culturally dead, or perform unchanging tradition and be declared doomed.

×

Slide 26: Indigenous Counter-Narratives: Horace Poolaw

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

So let's look at an alternative archive. Horace Poolaw was a Kiowa photographer working from the 1920s through the 1970s. He documented his community's actual lived experience—people in modern dress, at powwows and rodeos, in military service, at family gatherings, living complex contemporary lives while maintaining cultural identity.

Poolaw's work is a direct challenge to Curtis. His images say: we're not vanishing. We're here. We're modern. We're Indigenous. These things aren't contradictory. We get to define what authentic Indigenous identity looks like—not you.

Poolaw photographed his daughters at powwows and at Easter egg hunts. He photographed Kiowa men in military uniform and in ceremonial dress. He photographed modern houses and traditional gatherings. He showed Indigenous people as they actually were—adapting, surviving, maintaining identity through change rather than despite it.

This is what Curtis couldn't or wouldn't see: Indigenous peoples as modern, as agents of their own lives, as people with futures rather than only pasts.

×

Slide 27: What Images Hide

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

So after everything we've looked at tonight, how do we read these images? How do we see what they're hiding while appreciating their technical and artistic achievement?

First question: what's outside the frame? For every pristine landscape, what violence happened just beyond the edge of the photograph? For every romantic portrait of the "noble savage," what contemporary suffering isn't shown? The frame isn't neutral—it's a choice about what matters, what's visible, what's worthy of attention.

Second question: who made this image and who benefited? Paintings commissioned by millionaires and hung in the Capitol aren't neutral records—they're arguments made by people with power for audiences with power. Government survey photographs served federal policy. Railroad advertisements sold land that wasn't theirs to sell. Curtis's photographs were financed by J.P. Morgan and sold to wealthy collectors.

Third question: what counter-narratives exist? For every Curtis photograph, there are Indigenous voices saying "this isn't how we see ourselves." For every empty landscape, there are tribal histories saying "we were there, we were removed, we remember." Where are those stories? How do we find them and center them?

×

Slide 28: From Doctrine of Discovery to Manifest Destiny

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

We need to connect visual culture to actual policy. The images we've examined didn't just reflect ideas—they reinforced legal structures and enabled violence.

The Doctrine of Discovery, formalized in U.S. law in 1823, declared that "discovering" Christian Europeans had superior land rights to Indigenous inhabitants. Manifest Destiny in the 1840s declared westward expansion divinely ordained. The Indian Removal Act of 1830 forced relocation. The Homestead Act of 1862 gave away Indigenous land to white settlers. The Dawes Allotment Act of 1887 broke up tribal holdings. Wounded Knee in 1890 violently suppressed resistance.

At every step, visual culture smoothed the way. Paintings and photographs made this violence look natural, inevitable, even beautiful. They helped Americans accept and even celebrate what was happening. The images did ideological work—they made genocide thinkable by making it look like progress, like destiny, like the sad but inevitable passing of a noble but doomed race.

Art wasn't separate from violence. It enabled violence. It gave Americans a way to look at dispossession and see destiny instead of theft.

×

Slide 29: Legacy in Popular Culture

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

These nineteenth-century visual traditions are still everywhere. Every Western movie—every image of the lone cowboy against vast landscape—inherits this visual vocabulary. National park photography—particularly Ansel Adams—continues the tradition of sublime, empty wilderness even though these spaces are Indigenous homelands.

The conservation movement has a complicated relationship with this history. Yes, protecting landscapes is important. But the concept of "wilderness"—empty nature that needs protection from human presence—is built on Indigenous removal. You can't create a national park without first clearing out the people who lived there. Wilderness is a colonial concept.

We still see the West through nineteenth-century eyes. We still imagine it as empty, available, sublime. We still don't see Indigenous presence unless it's historical, traditional, nostalgic. Contemporary Indigenous peoples remain largely invisible in the American imagination—or visible only when they perform the kind of "authentic" Indianness that Curtis and Catlin taught white audiences to expect.

×

Slide 30: Learning to See Differently

Lecture Notes (3 minutes)

So what do we do with this knowledge? How do we see differently?

First, we practice critical visual literacy. Every time we encounter an image—historical or contemporary—we ask: Who made this? When? For whom? What does it show and what does it hide? Who benefits from this way of seeing? What power structures does this image reinforce or challenge?

Second, we actively seek out counter-narratives. We listen to Indigenous voices, read Indigenous scholars, look at Indigenous art. We center the perspectives of people who've been erased from the frame. We recognize that there are always multiple ways of seeing any landscape, any historical moment, any cultural encounter.

Third, we understand that images shape reality, not just reflect it. The way we visualize the past affects how we understand the present and imagine the future. If we see Indigenous peoples as vanished, we don't see contemporary Indigenous communities fighting for sovereignty, land rights, water rights, cultural preservation, justice. If we see the West as empty wilderness, we don't see the ongoing histories of dispossession and resistance.

Contemporary Indigenous artists are creating powerful work that directly challenges these historical visual traditions. Artists like Kent Monkman, who paints himself into classical Western landscape paintings, disrupting the narrative. Wendy Red Star, who uses photography to satirize ethnographic imagery. Will Wilson, who creates formal portraits of Indigenous people in contemporary contexts, reclaiming the authority of the photographic portrait.

These artists are teaching us to see differently—to see Indigenous presence, Indigenous agency, Indigenous futurity rather than just Indigenous past.

×

Slide 31: The "Vanishing Race" Never Vanished

Lecture Notes (2 minutes)

I want to end with the most important point: the "vanishing race" never vanished. This was always a lie, always a myth designed to make genocide acceptable.

There are 574 federally recognized tribes in the United States today. Millions of people identify as Native American or Alaska Native. Indigenous languages are being revitalized, often by young people using modern technology. Tribal governments exercise sovereignty. Indigenous scholars, artists, activists, writers are creating vibrant contemporary cultures while maintaining connections to deep histories.

Indigenous peoples didn't survive by remaining frozen in some imagined traditional past. They survived by adapting, by being strategic, by holding onto what mattered while changing what needed to change. They survived through incredible resistance, resilience, and creativity in the face of systematic attempts at cultural and physical genocide.

The images we've examined tonight tried to erase that survival, that resistance, that presence. They tried to convince Americans that Indigenous peoples were disappearing naturally, that there was nothing to be done, that it was sad but inevitable.

But Indigenous peoples are still here. They've always been here. And their stories—their ways of seeing, their perspectives on this history, their visions of the future—deserve to be centered, not marginalized.

Slide 32: Questions for Reflection

Closing Remarks (2 minutes + Q&A)

I'll leave you with one final thought: the images we've examined tonight weren't just about the past. They were arguments about the future. They were claiming what America would become. And those claims are still being made, still being contested.

Every time we look at a photograph or painting or movie about the West, we're making a choice about what we see and what we don't see, what we remember and what we forget. That's political. That matters.

So I encourage you: look closely. Ask questions. See what's hidden. Find the counter-narratives. Remember that images are arguments, not truth. And most importantly—the "vanishing race" never vanished. Indigenous peoples are still here. They've always been here. And their stories deserve to be seen, heard, and centered.
