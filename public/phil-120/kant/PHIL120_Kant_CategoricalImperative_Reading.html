About This Reading

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) is one of the most important moral philosophers in Western history — and also one of the most difficult to read. His sentences are long, his vocabulary is technical, and his style of argument can feel abstract even when he is making a very concrete point. This edition is designed to help you work through his ideas step by step.

What you are reading is real Kant — not a simplified summary. The text comes from his 1785 work Fundamental Principles of the Metaphysic of Morals. The translation has been modernized from Thomas Kingsmill Abbott's 1895 English version, and difficult passages have been annotated to guide your reading.

Your goal is not to master every detail, but to encounter Kant's own argument and understand two core ideas: what a categorical imperative is, and what its two most important formulations mean.

Part One · Key Concepts Primer

Read this section before reading Kant's text. These are the tools you will need.

Vocabulary You Must Know Before Reading
Maxim
The personal rule or principle behind an action. When you act, there is always an underlying policy: "I will lie when it benefits me," or "I will keep my promises." That underlying policy is your maxim.
Duty
For Kant, duty is not just obligation — it is the only morally relevant reason for acting. An action has moral worth only when you do it because it is the right thing to do, not because you feel like it or because it benefits you.
Good Will
A will that acts from duty alone — not from self-interest, sympathy, or habit. For Kant, the good will is the only thing that is good without qualification. Intelligence and kindness can be misused; the good will cannot.
Rational Being
A being capable of reason — primarily human beings. For Kant, the capacity for reason is what gives persons their special moral status. It is what makes us ends in ourselves rather than mere tools.
Hypothetical Imperative
A conditional command: "If you want X, then do Y." Example: "If you want to pass the exam, then study." It only binds you if you care about the goal. Most practical instructions are hypothetical imperatives.
Categorical Imperative
An unconditional command: do this, period — regardless of what you want. For Kant, all genuine moral obligations are categorical. They bind every rational person in every situation, no exceptions.
Universalizability
The idea that a moral rule must be one that could apply to everyone without contradiction. If your personal maxim would destroy itself when adopted by all people, it fails the moral test.
Ends and Means
An end is something valuable in itself. A means is something used to achieve something else. Kant argues persons must never be treated as mere means — as tools — but always also as ends in themselves.

Part Two · The Good Will

Kant opens the Groundwork with one of the most famous sentences in the history of philosophy.

Kant's Text · Section I

Nothing in the world — or even beyond it — can possibly be called good without qualification, except a good will. Intelligence, wit, judgment, and the other talents of the mind — or courage, determination, and perseverance as qualities of temperament — are undoubtedly good and desirable in many respects. But these gifts of nature may also become extremely harmful if the will that makes use of them, and which constitutes what we call character, is not good.

Kant is saying: talent, courage, even intelligence can be turned to evil. Think of a brilliant con artist or a courageous terrorist. The only thing that cannot be twisted into something bad is a will genuinely committed to doing right — a good will.

It is the same with the gifts of fortune. Power, wealth, honor, even health, and the general well-being we call happiness, inspire pride — and often arrogance — if there is not a good will to correct their influence. A good will appears to be the indispensable condition even of being worthy of happiness.

Notice: Kant is not saying happiness is bad. He is saying happiness without a good will does not make you morally worthy of that happiness. The good will is the precondition for moral worth.

A good will is good not because of what it accomplishes or produces — not by its usefulness in attaining some goal — but simply because of the act of willing itself. It is good in itself. Even if, due to misfortune, this will lacked all power to carry out its intentions, it would still shine like a jewel by its own light, as something that has its whole value in itself.

Part Three · Acting from Duty

Kant now unpacks what a good will looks like in practice. The moral worth of an action depends entirely on whether it is done from duty — not from inclination, sympathy, or self-interest.

Kant's Text · Section I, continued

To be charitable when we can is a duty. And beyond that, there are many people so warmly sympathetic that, without any motive of vanity or self-interest, they find pleasure in spreading joy around them and take delight in the contentment of others as their own achievement. But I maintain that in such a case an action of this kind, however kind it may be, has nevertheless no genuine moral worth. It deserves praise and encouragement, but not moral esteem. The maxim lacks moral significance, because such actions are done from inclination, not from duty.

This is one of Kant's most controversial claims. A naturally warm, generous person who helps others because they enjoy it is doing something good — but Kant says it has no moral worth in the strict sense. Moral worth requires acting from duty. Ask yourself: do you agree?

Put the case that the mind of that same charitable person is clouded by grief, extinguishing all sympathy with the suffering of others. He still has the power to help, but he is not moved by their distress. Now suppose he tears himself out of this emotional numbness and performs the action with no inclination toward it — simply from duty. Then for the first time his action has genuine moral worth.

Here Kant makes his sharpest point. The person who helps when they do not feel like it — who acts from duty alone — is the one acting with full moral worth. Kant is not saying we should suppress our feelings; he is saying duty, not feeling, is the true foundation of moral action.

An action done from duty derives its moral worth not from the result it is meant to produce, but from the principle by which it is determined. The moral worth lies not in what the action achieves, but in the maxim the person acts upon.

Part Four · Two Kinds of Commands

Now Kant introduces the distinction at the heart of his theory. Read this section carefully.

Kant's Text · Section II

All imperatives [commands or rules for action] command either hypothetically or categorically. Hypothetical imperatives represent an action as necessary only as a means to something else we want. The categorical imperative represents an action as necessary in itself, without reference to any further goal — as objectively necessary.

Stop here. Make sure you have the distinction: hypothetical = "do X if you want Y"; categorical = "do X, full stop." One is conditional; the other is not.

If the action is good only as a means to something else, then the imperative is hypothetical. If the action is conceived as good in itself — as what a rational will simply must do — then the imperative is categorical.

The hypothetical imperative only says that an action is good for some purpose you may or may not care about. The categorical imperative declares an action to be necessary without any reference to purpose. It is valid for all rational beings, in all circumstances, as an absolute practical principle.

Think of it this way: "Drink water if you want to survive" is hypothetical — it only applies if you want to survive. "Do not torture innocent people" is categorical — it applies regardless of what you want or what you would gain from it.

There is therefore only one categorical imperative, and it is this:

The First Formulation · Universal Law Formula
"Act only according to that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."

Part Five · Applying the Test — The Lying Promise

Kant demonstrates the universal law formula with a concrete example. Follow the argument step by step.

Kant's Text · Section I

Let the question be, for example: May I, when I am in financial difficulty, make a promise I have no intention of keeping? I readily distinguish here between two senses of the question: whether it is prudent, or whether it is right, to make a false promise.

"Prudent" means smart, in my best interest. "Right" means morally correct. Kant is separating these two questions on purpose — they have different answers.

As for whether it is prudent: I can see clearly that it is not enough to escape my present difficulty by this trick — I must consider whether greater trouble might arise later. Since my credit once lost may be much more harmful to me than any present difficulty, it seems wiser never to promise anything I do not intend to keep. But this maxim is still only based on fear of consequences.

It is a wholly different thing to be truthful from duty and to be truthful from fear of consequences. In the first case, the very idea of the action implies a law for me. In the second, I must look first to what results might follow.

Kant's point: even if lying turns out to be unwise because you might get caught, that is not a moral reason not to lie. The moral reason has nothing to do with consequences. It has to do with what the maxim of lying becomes when universalized.

The shortest and most reliable way to answer whether a lying promise is consistent with duty is to ask myself: Could I be content if the maxim "make a deceitful promise when in difficulty" became a universal law for everyone, including myself? And I immediately recognize that while I can will the lie, I cannot will that lying should become a universal law.

For with such a law there would be no promises at all. No one would believe a promise made by anyone else, and the very institution of promising would collapse. My maxim, as soon as it became a universal law, would destroy itself.

This is the key move. Kant's argument is not "lying is bad because bad things will happen." It is: if everyone lied, the very thing that makes lying useful — that people expect honesty — would disappear. The maxim of lying is self-defeating when universalized.
Worked Example · Running the Universalizability Test

Here is how to apply the First Formulation step by step.

1
Identify your maxim. State the personal rule behind the action. Example: "I will make promises I do not intend to keep when I need money."
2
Universalize the maxim. Imagine every rational person acting on this same rule. Example: "Everyone in financial difficulty makes promises they do not intend to keep."
3
Test for contradiction. Can the universalized maxim hold together — or does it collapse into incoherence? Example: If everyone made false promises, the concept of a promise would cease to function. The maxim defeats itself.
4
Verdict. If the maxim cannot be universalized without contradiction, it is morally impermissible. You have a duty not to act on it.

Part Six · The Second Formulation — The Humanity Formula

Kant now states what many consider the most powerful version of the categorical imperative.

Kant's Text · Section II

Assuming that there is something whose existence has absolute worth — something that, as an end in itself, could be the ground of definite laws — then the supreme practical principle would be this: rational nature exists as an end in itself. Every rational being necessarily represents its own existence this way — and it is equally a ground for every other rational being.

"End in itself" means something that has value for its own sake — not because it is useful, not because we happen to value it, but because of what it is. Kant is arguing that every rational person has this kind of unconditional worth.

The practical imperative will therefore be the following:

The Second Formulation · Humanity Formula
"Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never as a means only."
Kant's Text · Section II, continued

Consider the person who is thinking of making a false promise to obtain money. They intend to use another person merely as a means to their own advantage — something the other person has not consented to and would not consent to if they knew the full truth. The person they deceive cannot possibly agree to the transaction, because they do not even know the real terms. This conflict with the principle of humanity in other persons is clear.

When you lie to someone to get their money, you are bypassing their rationality. They cannot give informed consent because you have hidden the truth from them. You are treating them as a vending machine — something to extract a result from — not as a person with dignity.

The person who intends to do nothing positive for others but is content to take nothing from them merely satisfies duty formally. But the person who deceives another to get what they want has violated something deeper: the other's standing as a rational agent — their humanity.

Notice what is new in this formulation. F1 asks: can this maxim be universalized? F2 asks: am I treating the people affected as ends, or merely as tools? These are different questions — and they can illuminate the same case in different ways.
Side by Side · F1 vs. F2 Applied to the Same Case
Scenario F1: Can the maxim be universalized? F2: Are persons treated as ends?
Lying promise
Promise to repay with no intent to do so		 No — universal lying destroys the institution of promising No — the deceived person cannot consent; they are used as a cash machine
Manipulation
Flatter someone to get a favor		 Questionable — universal flattery may undermine trust No — the person is being steered rather than respected; their reason is bypassed
