üìñ Welcome to Chapter 4!

This chapter introduces the major ethical theories you'll study throughout the course. Think of moral theories as different "lenses" for viewing ethical questions‚Äîeach highlights different aspects of morality.

You'll learn what theories ARE, the major categories of theories, and how to EVALUATE theories using three criteria.

What Is a Moral Theory?

A moral theory is an explanation of what makes an action right or what makes a person or thing good.

  • Theories explain the essence of rightness or goodness
  • They help us make judgments, derive principles, and resolve conflicts
  • They provide general standards to guide moral reasoning
üì∏ Image: "compass navigation direction moral guidance ethics"
Compass representing moral guidance and direction
×
Moral Theory

An explanation of what makes an action right or what makes a person or thing good. It identifies the fundamental property or characteristic that makes something morally right or wrong.

Example: A utilitarian theory says actions are right because they maximize happiness. The theory explains WHY an action is right‚Äînot just THAT it's right.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

A moral theory is like a scientific theory‚Äîit tries to EXPLAIN something. Just as a physics theory explains why objects fall, a moral theory explains why actions are right or wrong.

The theory identifies what makes something right‚Äîits defining characteristic. Different theories identify different characteristics!

Theory vs. Code

A moral code is just a set of rules. A moral theory explains why those rules matter.

Moral Code

"Do not kill."

"Do not lie."

"Honor your parents."

Rules without explanation

Moral Theory

Explains why killing is wrong

Resolves conflicts between rules

Handles new situations

Foundation + explanation

üí° Key insight: Codes inevitably conflict (what if telling the truth requires breaking a promise?). Theories help resolve these conflicts.
×
Moral Code

A set of rules about right and wrong behavior‚Äîlike the Ten Commandments or a professional code of ethics.

Limitation: Codes don't explain WHY rules exist or how to resolve conflicts when rules clash. They're useful but not sufficient for moral reasoning.
×
Moral Theory (vs. Code)

Unlike a code, a theory provides the underlying explanation‚Äîthe "because" behind moral rules. It helps us see beyond the rules to the fundamental principles.

üìñ Why Codes Aren't Enough

Consider: "Do not kill" and "Protect human life" could conflict in self-defense situations. A code alone can't tell you which rule takes priority. A theory can.

Also, codes can't handle genuinely new situations. Is hacking someone's computer wrong? A code written before computers can't say‚Äîbut a theory about property or privacy can.

Two Types of Moral Theories

Theories of Value

What makes a person or thing good?

Focus: goodness of character, virtue, well-being

Example: "What makes someone a good person?"

Theories of Obligation

What makes an action right or wrong?

Focus: duties, rightness of actions

Example: "Was that action morally permissible?"

This textbook focuses mainly on theories of obligation‚Äîoften called simply "moral theories."

×
Theories of Value

Theories concerned with what makes a person or thing GOOD. They address character, virtue, and well-being rather than specific actions.

Questions they answer: What is a good life? What makes someone virtuous? What things are worth pursuing?
×
Theories of Obligation

Theories concerned with what makes ACTIONS right or wrong. They focus on our duties and the moral status of particular behaviors.

Questions they answer: Is this action permissible? What should I do? What are my moral duties?

üìñ Understanding the Distinction

This distinction matters because a good person might do the wrong thing (they meant well but made a mistake), and a bad person might do the right thing (even for selfish reasons).

We'll focus on theories of obligation‚Äîwhat makes ACTIONS right‚Äîbecause that's what most ethical dilemmas are about.

The Major Categories

Moral theories divide into two major camps based on what determines rightness:

Consequentialist

Right actions are determined by their consequences

What matters: outcomes, results, effects

"The ends justify the means"

Nonconsequentialist

Rightness does not depend (entirely) on consequences

What matters: the nature of the action itself

"Some acts are inherently wrong"

×
Consequentialist Theory
(also called "teleological" ‚Äî from Greek telos, meaning "end" or "goal")

A theory asserting that what makes an action right is its consequences. The rightness depends on the amount of good the action produces.

Core idea: An action is right if it leads to good outcomes. A lie that saves a life could be morally right because of its consequences.
×
Nonconsequentialist Theory
(also called "deontological" ‚Äî from Greek deon, meaning "duty")

A theory asserting that the rightness of an action does NOT depend entirely on its consequences. The nature of the action itself matters.

Core idea: Some actions are inherently wrong regardless of consequences. Lying might be wrong even if it produces good results.

üìñ The Fundamental Split

This is THE central divide in ethical theory. Do consequences matter most, or does the action itself have inherent moral status?

Both views have intuitive appeal. Sometimes we think "the end justifies the means." Other times we think "some things are just wrong, period."

Consequentialist Theories

Different consequentialist theories differ on: who benefits and what counts as "good"

Utilitarianism

Right action = maximizes good for everyone considered

Ethical Egoism

Right action = maximizes good for oneself

Both agree that consequences determine rightness‚Äîthey just disagree about whose good matters.

×
Utilitarianism

The theory that the morally right action is the one that produces the most favorable balance of good over evil, everyone considered.

Key feature: Everyone's interests count equally. Your happiness matters no more than a stranger's. The goal is to maximize total good.
×
Ethical Egoism

The theory that the morally right action is the one that produces the most favorable balance of good over evil for oneself.

Important: This isn't just selfishness‚Äîit's a moral THEORY claiming that self-interest is the proper basis for ethics. Controversial, but philosophically interesting!

üìñ Understanding the Difference

Both theories focus on consequences, but utilitarianism is impartial (everyone counts equally) while ethical egoism is partial (only your own good matters).

Ethical egoism might seem obviously wrong, but it raises interesting questions: Why SHOULD I care about others? The egoist demands an answer.

Two Versions of Utilitarianism

Act-Utilitarianism

Judge each individual action by its consequences

"What result will THIS action produce?"

Rules are irrelevant‚Äîcalculate each case fresh

Rule-Utilitarianism

Follow rules that, if generally followed, maximize good

"What if everyone followed this rule?"

Follow the rule even when breaking it would help

üí° Example: Breaking a promise might help in THIS case (act-util approves). But if everyone broke promises freely, trust would collapse (rule-util disapproves).
×
Act-Utilitarianism

The utilitarian theory that the morally right action is the one that directly produces the most favorable balance of good over evil, everyone considered.

Approach: Calculate the consequences of each possible action, case by case. Rules are just rough guidelines‚Äîbreak them whenever doing so produces more good.
×
Rule-Utilitarianism

The utilitarian theory that the morally right action is the one covered by a rule that, if generally followed, would produce the most favorable balance of good over evil.

Approach: Ask what rule would produce the best results if everyone followed it. Then follow that rule‚Äîeven in cases where breaking it might help this one time.

üìñ Why Two Versions?

Act-utilitarianism seems to allow horrible things if they maximize good THIS time (like framing an innocent person to prevent riots). Rule-utilitarianism avoids this by focusing on rules.

But rule-utilitarianism has its own problem: isn't it irrational to follow a rule when breaking it would produce MORE good?

Nonconsequentialist Theories

These theories say rightness depends on the nature of the action itself, not (just) its consequences.

Kant's Theory

Right action = follows the categorical imperative (can be universalized)

Natural Law Theory

Right action = follows the dictates of nature/natural order

Divine Command Theory

Right action = what God commands

×
Kant's Theory

The theory that the morally right action is the one done in accordance with the categorical imperative‚Äîa supreme principle of morality derived from reason alone.

Core idea: An action is right if and only if you could rationally will that everyone act the same way in similar circumstances.
×
Natural Law Theory

The theory that the morally right action is the one that follows the dictates of nature. The natural world exhibits a rational order with purposes built in.

Core idea: Things have natural purposes. Acting in accordance with these purposes is moral; acting against them is immoral.
×
Divine Command Theory

The theory that the morally right action is the one that God commands. An action is right if and only if God says it is.

Note: This faces the famous Euthyphro dilemma: Is something right BECAUSE God commands it, or does God command it because it's right?

üìñ The Common Thread

All these theories agree that consequences alone don't determine rightness. There are moral constraints on what we can do to achieve good outcomes.

For example: lying might produce good consequences, but all three theories might still condemn it‚Äîbecause of what lying IS, not what it produces.

Kant's Categorical Imperative

Immanuel Kant

1724‚Äì1804 ¬∑ German philosopher

The categorical imperative is Kant's supreme principle of morality:

"Act only according to that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."

‚Äî Immanuel Kant

üí° In plain English: Before acting, ask: "What if EVERYONE did this?" If universalizing the action leads to contradiction, it's wrong.
×
Categorical Imperative
(kat-eh-GOR-ih-kul im-PER-uh-tiv)

An imperative (command) that applies to all rational beings regardless of their particular wants or goals. It's "categorical" because it admits no exceptions.

Example: "Break promises when convenient" cannot be universalized‚Äîif everyone broke promises freely, the institution of promising would collapse. So promise-breaking is wrong.

üìñ Understanding Kant

Kant's key insight: morality must be universal and rational. If an action is right for me, it must be right for anyone in similar circumstances.

The test: Can I consistently WILL that my principle become a universal law? If not, the action is wrong‚Äîregardless of its consequences in this particular case.

ü§î Check Your Understanding

Quick Quiz:

"Lying is wrong because it leads to bad consequences‚Äîpeople can't trust each other, society breaks down, etc."

This statement reflects which type of theory?

A) Consequentialist

B) Nonconsequentialist (Kantian)

C) Natural Law

üìñ Answer: A) Consequentialist

The statement justifies the wrongness of lying by pointing to its BAD CONSEQUENCES (breakdown of trust). This is consequentialist reasoning.

A Kantian would say lying is wrong because it CAN'T BE UNIVERSALIZED‚Äînot because of its bad effects.

A natural law theorist might say lying violates human nature or the natural purpose of speech.

Considered Moral Judgments

Considered moral judgments are our most reliable moral data‚Äîformed carefully, free from bias.

  • Formed under conditions that minimize bias and error
  • Made when we're calm, informed, and impartial
  • Like scientific observations‚Äîour moral "data"
"[Considered judgments] enter as those judgments in which our moral capacities are most likely to be displayed without distortion."

‚Äî John Rawls, A Theory of Justice

×
Considered Moral Judgments

Moral assessments that are formed carefully, under conditions that minimize bias and distorting passions. We generally trust them unless there's reason to doubt.

Examples of conditions to avoid: Being upset, frightened, or self-interested; making snap judgments; lacking relevant information.

üìñ Why This Matters

Considered moral judgments serve as the DATA that moral theories must explain. A good theory should be consistent with our most reliable moral intuitions.

If a theory implies that torturing babies for fun is okay, we reject the theory‚Äîbecause that judgment conflicts with a deeply held considered judgment.

Reflective Equilibrium

Moral reasoning involves a back-and-forth between theories and judgments until they fit together.

üì∏ Image: "balance scale equilibrium harmony adjustment"
Diagram showing theory ‚Üî judgment adjustment process

Reflective equilibrium is achieved when theory and judgments cohere as closely as possible.

üí° The Process: Sometimes we adjust our theory to fit our judgments. Sometimes we adjust our judgments to fit a compelling theory. The goal is mutual coherence.
×
Reflective Equilibrium
(re-FLEK-tiv ee-kwih-LIB-ree-um)

A state of close agreement between moral theory and considered moral judgments. Achieved through mutual adjustment of both until they fit together coherently.

Like science: Scientists adjust theories to fit data AND sometimes reinterpret data in light of strong theories. Ethics works similarly.

üìñ The Give and Take

This is how ethical reasoning actually works. We don't just accept a theory and ignore all conflicting judgments. Nor do we accept all judgments and reject all theories.

It's a dialogue: theory informs judgment, judgment tests theory, and both may be revised to achieve better coherence.

Evaluating Moral Theories

Not all moral theories are equally good. How do we judge which is best?

Just as we evaluate scientific theories by certain criteria, we can evaluate moral theories too.

First requirement: A theory must be internally coherent‚Äîits claims can't contradict each other. Incoherent theories are automatically disqualified.

Theories that pass this basic test are then evaluated using three criteria of adequacy.

×
Internal Coherence

A theory is coherent if its claims are consistent‚Äîthey don't contradict each other. A theory that says both "X is always right" and "X is sometimes wrong" is incoherent.

Why it matters: If a theory contradicts itself, at least one of its claims must be FALSE. Contradictory claims can't both be true. So incoherent theories are automatically defective.

üìñ The Parallel to Science

Science evaluates theories by criteria like simplicity, explanatory power, consistency with data, and fruitfulness. Ethics can use similar criteria!

The first requirement is non-negotiable: a theory can't contradict itself. If it does, it's immediately disqualified from serious consideration.

Criterion 1: Consistency with Considered Judgments

1 A good theory must be consistent with our considered moral judgments.

A theory that conflicts with trustworthy moral judgments is dubious and likely needs revision or rejection.

Example of failure: A theory implying that murdering innocent people to make a community happy is RIGHT would conflict dramatically with our considered judgment that such murder is WRONG.

üí° Like science: A scientific theory that predicts observations we know to be false is a bad theory. Same with moral theories and moral judgments.

üìñ Why This Criterion Matters

Considered moral judgments are our most reliable moral "data." A theory that conflicts with this data is probably wrong.

Of course, sometimes we revise judgments in light of a compelling theory. But a theory that conflicts with MANY strong judgments is in serious trouble.

Criterion 2: Consistency with Moral Experience

2 A good theory must be consistent with our moral experiences.

Our common moral experiences include:

  • We sometimes make moral judgments
  • We give reasons for moral beliefs
  • We are sometimes mistaken in our moral beliefs
  • We occasionally disagree morally
  • We sometimes act wrongly
Example of failure: A theory claiming our feelings are never wrong about morality conflicts with our experience of BEING wrong sometimes!

üìñ The Data of Moral Life

These experiences are facts about moral life that any adequate theory must accommodate. A theory that denies obvious features of moral experience is suspect.

For example, if a theory implies we never make moral mistakes, that conflicts with our clear experience of sometimes being wrong and changing our minds.

Criterion 3: Usefulness in Problem-Solving

3 A good theory must be useful in moral problem-solving.

Good moral theories help us:

  • Make reliable judgments about moral principles and actions
  • Resolve conflicts among judgments and principles
  • Handle new moral situations we haven't faced before

Usefulness is necessary but not sufficient. A theory could be useful but still wrong. But a theory that's NOT useful is of questionable value.

üìñ Practical Value

We develop moral theories for a reason‚Äîto help us navigate moral life. A theory that gives no practical guidance isn't much use.

This is like requiring scientific theories to make testable predictions. A theory that explains everything but predicts nothing is suspect.

The Moral Criteria of Adequacy

1 Consistency with considered moral judgments

2 Consistency with our moral experiences

3 Usefulness in moral problem-solving

üí° How to use them: Apply all three criteria to any moral theory. A theory that fails one is problematic. A theory that fails multiple criteria is in serious trouble. The best theory is the one that best satisfies all three.

üìñ Applying the Criteria

In the next chapters, you'll see these criteria applied to specific theories like utilitarianism and Kantian ethics.

No theory is perfect‚Äîall have strengths and weaknesses. The goal is to find the theory that, on balance, best satisfies these criteria.

Applying the Criteria: An Example

Consider a theory that says: "Right actions are those that enhance community harmony."

Problem with Criterion 1:

If killing an innocent person would enhance community harmony, this theory says it's right‚Äîbut our considered judgment says it's WRONG.

Verdict:

The theory conflicts with a strong considered moral judgment. This is reason to reject or significantly revise the theory.

üìñ Walking Through the Process

This example shows Criterion 1 in action. The "community harmony" theory has an implication (innocent killing could be right) that conflicts with a firm considered judgment (innocent killing is wrong).

This doesn't prove the theory wrong, but it's a serious strike against it. The theory would need to explain why we should abandon our judgment.

Theory Evaluation Requires Judgment

Applying the criteria is not a mechanical process‚Äîit requires thoughtful judgment.

  • There's no formula for weighing the criteria against each other
  • Different people may weigh criteria differently
  • The process is rational and objective, but not algorithmic
"Like the diagnosis of an illness, applying the criteria is based on the educated judgment of a physician using appropriate guidelines."
üí° Key point: Even though it's not mechanical, theory evaluation is still rational. We can give reasons for our judgments about theories.

üìñ Judgment, Not Calculation

This is important: you can't just plug in numbers and get an answer. Evaluating moral theories requires wisdom, experience, and careful thinking.

But this doesn't make it arbitrary! Just as a doctor uses judgment based on evidence and training, ethicists use judgment based on criteria and reasoning.

ü§î Check Your Understanding

Apply the Criteria:

Suppose someone proposes a moral theory that says: "Whatever you feel is right at the moment IS right."

Which criterion of adequacy does this theory most clearly violate, and why?

üìñ Answer: Criterion 2 (Consistency with Moral Experience)

Our moral experience clearly includes the fact that we're sometimes WRONG about morality‚Äîwe change our minds, regret past judgments, etc.

If whatever we feel is automatically right, we could never be mistaken‚Äîbut we clearly ARE sometimes mistaken. The theory conflicts with this basic moral experience.

It also arguably fails Criterion 1 (our considered judgment is that feelings can be wrong) and Criterion 3 (it's not useful because it can't resolve disagreements).

Learning from Flawed Theories

Even when we reject a theory, we can often learn something valuable from it.

Example: Many philosophers reject utilitarianism but still accept that consequences matter‚Äîjust not that they're the ONLY thing that matters.

  • A theory may capture important insights even if flawed overall
  • Examining failures helps us understand what a good theory needs
  • Rejected theories can inform improved theories
üí° The takeaway: Don't dismiss theories too quickly. Ask: What truth is this theory trying to capture? Can we preserve that insight while avoiding its problems?

üìñ Constructive Criticism

Ethical theories are attempts to capture moral truth. Even failed attempts often contain valuable insights.

The history of ethics is a dialogue where each theory builds on the insights‚Äîand avoids the mistakes‚Äîof previous ones.
