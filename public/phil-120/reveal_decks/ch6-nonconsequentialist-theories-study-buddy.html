üìñ Welcome to Chapter 6!

This chapter examines nonconsequentialist (deontological) theories‚Äîtheories that say rightness depends on the NATURE of the action, not just its consequences.

You'll study Kant's ethics and natural law theory‚Äîboth argue that some actions are wrong regardless of outcomes.

The Nonconsequentialist View

For nonconsequentialists (also called deontologists):

The rightness of an action derives not from its consequences but from its nature, its right-making characteristics.
  • An action is right because of what it is, not what it produces
  • Some actions are inherently wrong, regardless of consequences
  • Consequences may matter, but they're not the whole story
×
Nonconsequentialist Theory

A moral theory asserting that the rightness of an action does NOT depend entirely on its consequences. The nature of the action itself matters morally.

Core idea: Some actions are wrong IN THEMSELVES‚Äîlike lying or killing innocents‚Äîeven if they produce good outcomes.
×
Deontologist
(dee-on-TOL-oh-jist) ‚Äî from Greek deon, meaning "duty"

Another name for a nonconsequentialist. A deontologist focuses on duties, rules, and the intrinsic nature of actions rather than consequences.

üìñ The Key Contrast

Consequentialists: "The ends justify the means." Nonconsequentialists: "Some means are never justified, no matter the ends."

Think about lying to save a life, or torturing one person to save many. Nonconsequentialists say these might still be wrong.

Kant's Ethics

Immanuel Kant

1724‚Äì1804 ¬∑ German philosopher

Perhaps the greatest moral philosopher of the modern era

Kant's revolutionary claim:

  • Reason alone reveals the moral law
  • No appeal to utility, tradition, religion, or authority needed
  • The moral law is universal, necessary, and knowable by all rational beings

üìñ Kant's Enlightenment Project

Kant sought to ground morality in pure reason‚Äînot in feelings, consequences, or divine commands. He believed every rational person can discover the moral law through careful thinking.

This makes morality objective and universal, not dependent on culture or personal preference.

The Good Will

For Kant, the only thing good without qualification is a good will.

"Nothing can possibly be conceived in the world, or even out of it, which can be called good without qualification, except a good will."

‚Äî Immanuel Kant

  • Intelligence, courage, wealth‚Äîall can be used for evil
  • Only the will to do one's duty FOR DUTY'S SAKE is unconditionally good
  • Consequences don't determine moral worth
×
Good Will

The will to do your duty simply BECAUSE it is your duty‚Äîout of pure reverence for the moral law, not for reward, satisfaction, or good consequences.

Key point: An action has moral worth only if done from duty. Helping others because it feels good or benefits you has no moral worth‚Äîonly helping because it's RIGHT does.

üìñ Duty for Duty's Sake

This is a radical claim. Kant says if you help someone because you enjoy it, your action has no moral worth! Only acting from duty counts morally.

Many find this counterintuitive‚Äîshouldn't we praise people who ENJOY being kind?

Two Types of Imperatives

Kant distinguishes two types of commands (imperatives):

Hypothetical Imperatives

Tell us what to do IF we have certain desires

"If you want X, do Y"

"If you want money, get a job"

Categorical Imperatives

Tell us what to do regardless of our desires

"Do Y, period."

"Do not lie"

Moral duties are categorical‚Äîthey apply to everyone regardless of their desires.

×
Hypothetical Imperative

An imperative that tells us what we should do IF we have certain desires. It's conditional on what we want.

Example: "If you want to pass the exam, study hard." This only applies if you want to pass. If you don't care about passing, the imperative doesn't bind you.
×
Categorical Imperative

An imperative that tells us what we should do regardless of our particular wants and needs. It applies universally and unconditionally to all rational beings.

Example: "Do not murder." This applies whether or not you want to murder someone, whether or not murder would benefit you. No exceptions.

üìñ Why This Matters

Kant argues that morality must be categorical‚Äîit can't depend on what you happen to want. "Don't murder" doesn't become optional just because murdering would be convenient for you.

The Categorical Imperative: Version 1

Kant's first formulation of the categorical imperative:

"Act only according to that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."

‚Äî Immanuel Kant

The test:

  • Identify the "maxim" (principle) of your action
  • Ask: Could this maxim become a universal law?
  • If universalizing leads to contradiction ‚Üí action is wrong

üìñ The Universalizability Test

This captures a deep moral intuition: don't make exceptions for yourself. If an action would be wrong for everyone to do, it's wrong for you to do.

Kant thinks this is built into the very logic of morality.

Applying Version 1: Lying Promise

The Case of the Lying Promise

You need money and want to borrow it by promising to repay‚Äîknowing you won't. Is this permissible?

Kant's analysis:

  • Maxim: "When I need money, I'll make a lying promise to repay"
  • Universalized: Everyone makes lying promises when convenient
  • Result: Promises become meaningless‚Äîno one believes them
  • Contradiction: The very concept of a "promise" is destroyed

Verdict: Lying promises are self-defeating when universalized ‚Üí morally wrong.

üìñ The Self-Defeating Test

Kant's genius: some immoral actions are wrong because they're parasitic on others following the rules. Lying only "works" because most people tell the truth. If everyone lied, lying would be pointless.

The Categorical Imperative: Version 2

Kant's second formulation‚Äîthe means-ends principle:

"So act as to treat humanity, whether in thine own person or in that of any other, in every case as an end withal, never as means only."

‚Äî Immanuel Kant

  • Persons have intrinsic worth and dignity
  • Never treat people MERELY as tools for your purposes
  • Respect their autonomy, freedom, and rational agency
×
Means-Ends Principle

The rule that we must always treat people (including ourselves) as ends in themselves, never merely as means to our own ends.

Key distinction: You CAN treat someone as a means (hiring a mechanic). You can NOT treat them MERELY as a means (deceiving them, coercing them, ignoring their interests entirely).

üìñ Human Dignity

This captures the idea of human dignity. People aren't tools to be used‚Äîthey're rational agents with their own goals and worth. Respecting persons means respecting their autonomy.

This is why lying is wrong‚Äîit manipulates people, treating them as mere means to our ends.

Perfect and Imperfect Duties

Kant distinguishes two types of duties:

Perfect Duties

Duties that must be always followed

No exceptions permitted

Don't lie, don't murder, don't break promises

Imperfect Duties

Duties that allow some discretion

Must be fulfilled, but when/how is flexible

Help others, develop your talents

×
Perfect Duty

A duty that has no exceptions‚Äîit must be followed absolutely. Perfect duties are typically negative (prohibitions): don't lie, don't kill, don't break promises.

×
Imperfect Duty

A duty that must be fulfilled but allows latitude in how and when. Imperfect duties are typically positive (commands): help others in need, develop your abilities.

Example: You have a duty to help others, but you don't have to help EVERY person in need ALL the time. You have discretion about when and how to fulfill this duty.

üìñ The Duty Distinction

Perfect duties are absolute‚Äîno situation justifies lying. Imperfect duties are required but flexible‚Äîyou must be charitable sometimes, but you decide when and to whom.

ü§î Check Your Understanding

Apply Kant's Test:

You're considering cheating on an exam. Apply the categorical imperative (first version).

1. What is the maxim of your action?

2. What happens if everyone acts on this maxim?

3. Is there a contradiction?

üìñ Answer

1. Maxim: "When I want to pass without studying, I'll cheat."

2. If everyone cheated, exams would become meaningless‚Äîgrades wouldn't indicate knowledge.

3. Contradiction: The purpose of exams (to certify knowledge) would be destroyed. Cheating is parasitic on others not cheating.

Verdict: Cheating fails the universalizability test ‚Üí morally wrong.

Evaluating Kant's Theory

Strengths

‚úì Respects human dignity and rights

‚úì Emphasizes universality and impartiality

‚úì Explains why some acts are inherently wrong

‚úì Coherent and internally consistent

Weaknesses

‚úó Absolutism leads to counterintuitive results

‚úó What do we do when duties conflict?

‚úó The universalizability test may be too flexible

‚úó Seems to ignore consequences entirely

üìñ The Absolutism Problem

Kant's biggest challenge: his absolutism. He famously argued you shouldn't lie even to a murderer asking where your friend is hiding. Most people find this deeply counterintuitive.

The Problem of Absolutism

The Murderer at the Door

A murderer asks where your friend is hiding. You know the truth will lead to your friend's death. Should you lie?

Kant's answer: You must tell the truth.

  • The duty not to lie is a perfect duty‚Äîno exceptions
  • You cannot control the consequences‚Äîonly your own actions
  • The murderer is responsible for what he does with the information
‚ö†Ô∏è The problem: This seems to violate our considered moral judgment. Surely protecting an innocent life justifies a lie?

üìñ Biting the Bullet

Kant bites this bullet‚Äîhe truly believes lying is always wrong. Most philosophers find this too extreme and see it as a serious flaw in his theory.

But defenders argue: What if you lie and the friend escapes, only to be found by the murderer going in the "wrong" direction?

Natural Law Theory

Natural law theory is another major nonconsequentialist approach:

Thomas Aquinas

1225‚Äì1274 ¬∑ Medieval theologian and philosopher

Gave natural law its most influential form

Core idea: Right actions are those that accord with the natural law‚Äîthe moral principles embedded in nature itself.

×
Natural Law Theory

The theory that right actions are those that accord with natural law‚Äîthe moral principles that we can discover through reason by examining nature and human nature.

Key idea: Nature is rationally ordered with purposes. By understanding what humans are naturally inclined toward, we can discover how we SHOULD live.

üìñ From Nature to Morality

Natural law theory claims we can "read" moral values from nature. Nature reveals what is good for humans‚Äîand we should pursue those goods.

This theory remains influential today, especially in Catholic moral teaching.

How Natural Law Works

According to Aquinas:

  • Nature is rationally ordered and teleological (goal-directed)
  • Everything has a natural purpose or function
  • Humans can use reason to discover natural purposes
  • The good = fulfilling our natural purposes
Human Natural Inclinations (Aquinas):

Preservation of life, procreation, education of offspring, living in society, pursuit of truth, avoidance of harm

üìñ The Teleological View

Aquinas believed nature has purposes built into it by God. An acorn's purpose is to become an oak. Human nature has purposes too‚Äîand morality is about fulfilling those purposes.

The natural law is both objective (built into nature) and knowable (discoverable by reason).

Natural Law: Absolutist Rules

Like Kant's theory, traditional natural law is absolutist:

  • Some actions are always wrong, regardless of consequences
  • Directly killing the innocent is always wrong
  • Lying is always wrong
  • Actions that interfere with procreation are always wrong
üí° Note: This absolutism creates similar problems as Kant's theory‚Äîwhat about killing in self-defense? What about lying to the murderer?

üìñ The Absolutism Issue Again

Natural law's absolutism has the same problem as Kant's: it can require actions that seem clearly wrong (letting many die rather than directly killing one).

To handle conflicts, natural law developed the doctrine of double effect.

The Doctrine of Double Effect

The doctrine of double effect handles cases where actions have both good and bad effects:

An action with bad effects may be permissible if:

1 The action itself is morally good or neutral

2 The bad effect is not used to produce the good effect

3 The intention is to bring about the good effect

4 The good effect is at least as important as the bad effect

×
Doctrine of Double Effect

The principle that performing a good action may be permissible even if it has bad effects, but performing a bad action for the purpose of achieving good effects is never permissible.

Key distinction: INTENDING harm vs. FORESEEING harm. You may foresee that your action will cause harm, but you must not INTEND the harm as your means or end.

üìñ Intended vs. Foreseen

The key is intention. You can foresee bad effects without intending them. A doctor giving pain medication knows it might hasten death‚Äîbut intends only pain relief.

This is different from intentionally killing to end suffering.

Applying Double Effect

Chemotherapy During Pregnancy

A pregnant woman has cancer. Chemotherapy will save her life but kill the fetus. Is the treatment permissible?

Double effect analysis:

  • 1 The action (treating cancer) is morally good ‚úì
  • 2 The fetus's death is not used to save the mother ‚úì
  • 3 The intention is to cure cancer, not kill the fetus ‚úì
  • 4 A life saved ‚âà a life lost (proportionate) ‚úì

Verdict: Treatment is permissible‚Äîfetal death is foreseen but not intended.

üìñ The Intention Matters

Compare this to directly aborting to save the mother. That would fail condition 2‚Äîthe fetus's death would BE the means to saving the mother.

Double effect allows the first but not the second, even though both result in fetal death.

The Trolley Problem

The Classic Thought Experiment

A runaway trolley will kill five workers. You can divert it to another track where it will kill one worker. Should you?

Double effect analysis:

  • Action: Turning the trolley (neutral) ‚úì
  • Bad effect not used for good: One death is a side effect, not a means ‚úì
  • Intention: Save five, not kill one ‚úì
  • Proportionality: Five saved > one lost ‚úì

Verdict: Turning is permissible.

üí° But compare: Pushing a fat man onto the track to stop the trolley FAILS condition 2‚Äîhis death IS the means to saving the five. This is impermissible.

üìñ The Deep Distinction

Most people's intuitions match double effect here: turning is okay, pushing is not. Double effect captures why‚Äîeven though both result in one death to save five.

Judith Jarvis Thomson and Philippa Foot made this famous.

Evaluating Natural Law

Strengths

‚úì Provides objective moral standards

‚úì Emphasizes reason, not just faith

‚úì Respects human life and dignity

‚úì Double effect handles hard cases

Weaknesses

‚úó Is nature really teleological?

‚úó Can we derive "ought" from "is"?

‚úó Absolutism conflicts with common sense

‚úó Different views of "natural" human inclinations

üìñ The Is-Ought Problem

Critics argue you can't derive moral conclusions from facts about nature. Just because humans ARE inclined to do X doesn't mean they SHOULD. (Hume's guillotine)

Also: modern science sees nature as purposeless‚Äîno teleology. This undermines the theory's foundation.

ü§î Check Your Understanding

Apply Double Effect:

A terrorist will kill 100 hostages unless police kill 1 innocent bystander. Can police kill the bystander?

Test against all four conditions.

üìñ Answer

1. The action (killing an innocent) is NOT morally good or neutral. ‚úó FAILS

2. The bystander's death IS used to produce the good effect (saving hostages). ‚úó FAILS

Since conditions 1 and 2 fail, the action is not permissible‚Äîeven to save 100 lives.

Compare to: accidentally killing a bystander while shooting at the terrorist (might be permissible under double effect).

Learning from Kant and Natural Law

Despite criticisms, these theories embody important moral truths:

1. Universality and Impartiality

Moral rules apply equally to everyone in similar situations.

2. Respect for Persons

People have inherent dignity and must never be used as mere means.

3. Intention Matters

What we intend to do is morally significant, not just what we cause.

üìñ Core Insights

Even utilitarians accept many of these insights. The means-ends principle underlies our concept of human rights. The intention distinction explains why accidents differ from deliberate harm.

Consequentialist vs. Nonconsequentialist

Consequentialist

Rightness = best consequences

Ends can justify means

No action inherently wrong

Focus: outcomes/welfare

Nonconsequentialist

Rightness = nature of act

Some means never justified

Some acts inherently wrong

Focus: duties/rights

üí° The deep question: Can good ends justify bad means? Consequentialists say yes (sometimes). Nonconsequentialists say no (or at least: not always).

üìñ The Fundamental Divide

This is THE central debate in ethics. Most people have both consequentialist and nonconsequentialist intuitions‚Äîwe care about outcomes AND about how we achieve them.

Chapter Summary

  • Nonconsequentialist theories: rightness depends on the action's nature, not just consequences
  • Kant: only a good will (acting from duty) has moral worth
  • Categorical imperative (v1): act only on universalizable maxims
  • Categorical imperative (v2): treat persons as ends, never merely as means
  • Natural law: right actions accord with human nature's purposes
  • Doctrine of double effect: foreseen harm ‚â† intended harm
  • Both theories are absolutist, which creates problems with hard cases

üìñ The Big Picture

Nonconsequentialist theories capture important moral truths: respect for persons, the importance of rights and duties, limits on what we may do even for good ends.

But their absolutism is a serious problem‚Äîmost people think lying to save a life is permissible.
