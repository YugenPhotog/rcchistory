ðŸ“– Welcome to Feminist Ethics

This chapter introduces one of the most important developments in modern moral philosophy: the challenge to traditional ethics from feminist perspectives.

Feminist ethics isn't just ethics "for women"â€”it's a fundamental critique of how we've been doing moral philosophy and an alternative approach that captures neglected dimensions of moral life.

A Different Approach to Morality

  • Feminist ethics is not just another moral theory like utilitarianism or Kantian ethics
  • It's an alternative way of looking at the concepts and concerns of moral life
  • Focused on women's interests and experiences
  • Devoted to supporting moral equality of women and men
  • Correcting biases against women in traditional moral thinking

ðŸ“– What Makes Feminist Ethics Different

When we studied utilitarianism and Kant, we were looking at theories that compete to be THE correct moral framework. Feminist ethics is differentâ€”it's more like a lens through which to examine ALL moral theories, asking: "Does this theory work equally well for everyone, or does it have built-in biases?"

Feminist philosophers noticed that most great ethical theorists throughout history were men, writing primarily for male audiences, often assuming male perspectives. This raises the question: did they inadvertently build male-centered assumptions into their supposedly universal theories?

Responding to Hard Facts

  • Many great ethicists assumed women are somehow inferior to menâ€”less rational, less mature, less moral
  • Most women throughout the world have been (and many still are) second-class citizens
  • By law, religion, or custom: victims of violence, bigotry, coercion, dependence, professional inequality
The Claim: Ways of thinking and feeling traditionally regarded as characteristic of women have been largely neglected by moral philosophers (who have traditionally been men).

ðŸ“– Why This Matters

Feminist ethics isn't making things upâ€”it's responding to historical facts. Even philosophers we admire, like Aristotle, held views about women we would today consider deeply problematic. Aristotle thought women were naturally inferior to men in their capacity for reason.

Beyond the history of inequality, there's the question of whether "feminine" ways of moral thinking have been undervalued or ignored by mainstream philosophy.

Gendered Values in Western Ethics

"Masculine" Values

Independence, autonomy, intellect, will, hierarchy, domination, culture, transcendence, product, asceticism, war, death

"Feminine" Values

Interdependence, community, connection, sharing, emotion, body, trust, nature, immanence, process, joy, peace, life

"Western moral theory is said to embody values that are 'masculine,' insofar as they are associated, empirically, normatively, or symbolically with men."

â€” Alison M. Jaggar, feminist philosopher

ðŸ“– The Gendering of Ethics

Feminist philosophers like Alison Jaggar argue that Western ethics has been structured around values typically associated with menâ€”abstract reason, autonomy, universal principles, impartiality.

The "feminine" column represents what's been downplayedâ€”relationships, emotions, caring, context, particularity. These aren't claims about biologyâ€”they're claims about how our CULTURE has associated certain values with certain genders.

Women's Experiences Overlooked

  • Traditional philosophy focused on "public life"â€”politics, commerce, strangers interacting
  • The domestic realmâ€”intimate relationships, family, child-rearingâ€”was seen as "private" and outside ethics
  • Even philosophers who valued domestic life portrayed it as a sphere where full human excellence couldn't be realized

"Issues of special concern to women are said to have been ignored by modern moral philosophers, who have tended to portray the domestic realm as an arena outside the economy and beyond justice."

â€” Alison M. Jaggar

ðŸ“– The Public/Private Divide

Traditional moral philosophy focused on how strangers should treat each other in public contexts. But what about the domestic sphereâ€”family relationships, caring for children, tending to the sick and elderly? This was considered "private" and largely outside moral philosophy. It was also the sphere where women spent most of their time.

Feminist ethics argues that some of the most important moral experiences happen in intimate relationships.

Elements of Feminist Ethics

  • Emphasis on personal relationships: Kinship, friendship, caregiver bondsâ€”not just dealings with strangers
  • Suspicion of moral principles: Abstract rules may be too general for complex personal situations
  • Contrasting attitudes toward impartiality: Partiality toward loved ones is morally significant
  • Higher regard for emotions: Feelings can be sources of moral insight
  • Emphasis on the nonideal: Real humans are flawed, dependent, vulnerable

ðŸ“– What Feminist Ethics Emphasizes

Personal relationships: We don't treat our mother the same way we treat a stranger, and that's morally significant.

Impartiality: Utilitarians and Kantians say we shouldn't favor loved ones. But isn't there something morally good about caring specially for those close to us?

Emotions: Rather than obstacles, emotions like compassion and sympathy can guide us toward right action.

Historical Roots

Critical Thought: Feminist Ethics in History

Questions about women's morality aren't new. During the 18th and 19th centuries, thinkers including:

  • Mary Wollstonecraft
  • John Stuart Mill
  • Catherine Beecher
  • Charlotte Perkins Gilman
  • Elizabeth Cady Stanton

addressed questions: Are women's traits natural or conditioned? Are moral virtues gendered?

ðŸ“– A Long Conversation

Mary Wollstonecraft's "A Vindication of the Rights of Woman" (1792) argued that women appeared less rational than men only because they were denied education. John Stuart Mill wrote "The Subjection of Women" (1869) arguing for women's equality.

The Question of Moral Principles

  • Some feminist philosophers work within traditional frameworks
  • Others resist reducing morality to universal principles
  • They argue principles like autonomy, justice, and utility are too general for messy personal life

"Ideal theories commence and operate from the best of what humans can be, conveniently overlooking the ways in which we break down, fail, and fall apart. . . . But real life is much messier. . . . Many feminist ethicists appreciate the value of the nonideal."

â€” Sarah Clark Miller, philosopher

ðŸ“– The Limits of Abstraction

Sarah Clark Miller's quote captures the concern: traditional moral theories assume ideal rational agents. But real life involves broken, struggling, interdependent people with complicated histories. An ethics based on idealized agents may not help real people.

Challenging Impartiality

  • Impartiality is considered a defining characteristic of morality in traditional ethics
  • From the "moral point of view," all persons are equal
  • But in the domestic sphere, we are naturally partial to family and friends

Store Clerk vs. Spouse

We don't (and shouldn't) treat our spouse the same way we treat a store clerk

Special Duties

We have moral duties to those we love that we don't have to strangers

ðŸ“– Is Impartiality Always Right?

If you had to save either your own child or a stranger's child from drowning (and could only save one), would it be WRONG to save your own child? Most people's intuition is that of course you'd save your own childâ€”that's not just permissible, it might be required by the bond.

The Ethics of Care

  • A perspective emphasizing close personal relationships and virtues like compassion, kindness, love, and sympathy
  • A good example of feminist ethics in action
  • Contrasts with traditional theories preoccupied with principles and legalistic reasoning
Core Focus

The ethics of care can be thought of as an essential element in virtue ethics. Caring is a vital and inescapable part of the moral life.

ðŸ“– Understanding Ethics of Care

The ethics of care is probably the most influential product of feminist ethical thinking. It puts caring relationships at the center of moralityâ€”not just one moral value among many, but potentially central to what morality is about.

Carol Gilligan's Research

Carol Gilligan

Psychologist | Author of In a Different Voice (1982)

  • Sparked interest in ethics of care through research on moral development
  • Maintained that men and women think differently about moral issues

"Ethic of Justice" (Men)

Focus on rights, rules, and justice; deliberate about abstract principles

"Ethic of Care" (Women)

Focus on relationships and caring; aware of feelings, needs, viewpoints

ðŸ“– The Research That Started It All

Gilligan noticed that dominant theories of moral development found men scoring higher. She asked: what if the tests were biased, measuring only one KIND of moral thinkingâ€”abstract, principle-based reasoningâ€”while ignoring care-based reasoning?

Beyond Gender Differences

  • Gilligan later rejected the claim that women and men have distinct moral styles inherently linked to gender
  • She now denies these two styles are inherently linked to being male or female
  • Recent research raises doubts about whether there IS a gap between men's and women's moral thinking
Key Point: These findings do NOT dilute the relevance of caring to ethics. The ethics of care stands on its own merits, regardless of empirical questions about gender differences.

ðŸ“– The Theory Evolves

Later research has complicated the picture. Differences might not be as stark, and might be more about socialization than biology. But philosophically: even if men and women don't think differently, the ETHICS OF CARE as an approach is still valuable.

Central Features of the Ethics of Care

  • Caring matters morally: Caring is vital and inescapable to moral life
  • Focus on particular others: Attending to and meeting needs of specific people we're responsible for
  • Recognition of dependence: Humans are dependent for many yearsâ€”infancy, old age, illness, disability
  • Beyond "outside morality": Refuses to relegate care outside moral consideration

ðŸ“– What Ethics of Care Emphasizes

This approach is about caring for PARTICULAR othersâ€”not abstract benevolence toward humanity. It's about the specific people in your life whose needs you're responsible for.

It also takes human dependence seriously. Traditional theories often assume autonomous agents. But all of us are deeply dependent at various points. Morality must account for this.

"[T]he central focus of the ethics of care is on the compelling moral salience of attending to and meeting the needs of the particular others for whom we take responsibility. Caring for one's child, for instance, may well and defensibly be at the forefront of a person's moral concerns."

ðŸ“– Held's Contribution

Virginia Held emphasizes that caring isn't just nice to haveâ€”it's morally CENTRAL. Her point about dependence: we're all dependent on others for care at various points. Yet traditional theories imagine autonomous agents, ignoring the realities of dependence and care.

Human Dependence and Moral Life

"Prospects for human progress and flourishing hinge fundamentally on the care that those needing it receive, and the ethics of care stresses the moral force of the responsibility to respond to the needs of the dependent."

â€” Virginia Held

  • Moralities built on the autonomous individual largely overlook human dependence
  • The ethics of care attends to this central concern
  • It refuses to relegate care to a realm "outside morality"

ðŸ“– Why Dependence Matters

Think about Kant's ethics: all about rational autonomous agents. But what about babies? People with dementia? People in comas? They can't be autonomous rational agents, yet they clearly deserve moral consideration. The ethics of care provides this framework.

ðŸ“– Integration, Not Opposition

Baier argues we don't have to choose. We need principles of justice for public institutions, and an ethic of care for intimate relationships. The question is how to integrate them.

Care as Virtue

  • The ethics of care reminds us that caring is vital to moral life
  • If virtues matter, and if compassion/sympathy/love are virtues, then there must be a place for caring alongside principles
  • Care and principles can coexist
The Connection

You could think of caring as a central virtueâ€”a stable disposition to attend to others' needs, accompanied by compassion and concern.

ðŸ“– Care Meets Virtue Ethics

The ethics of care has much in common with virtue ethics. Both emphasize character over rules, both take emotions seriously, both focus on relationships and particular situations rather than abstract universal principles.

Considering Criticisms

  • Too narrow? Does ethics of care only apply to intimate relationships?
  • Reinforcing stereotypes? Does emphasizing women's caring role perpetuate limiting expectations?
  • What about justice? Can care-based thinking address rights and fairness?

Responses from Care Ethicists

  • Care ethics can extend to social and political issues
  • Valuing care doesn't mean only women should careâ€”everyone should
  • Care and justice can be integrated

ðŸ“– Addressing the Objections

Care ethicists argue caring can extend beyond intimatesâ€”we can care about communities, strangers in need, future generations. And they're not saying women SHOULD care moreâ€”they're saying caring is valuable and everyone should do it.
