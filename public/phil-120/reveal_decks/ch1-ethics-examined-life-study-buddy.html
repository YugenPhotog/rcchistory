üìñ Welcome to Chapter 1!

This chapter introduces the field of ethics and explains why studying morality matters. You'll learn the basic concepts, the three branches of ethics, and why critical reasoning is essential to the moral life.

Key question from Socrates: "How ought we to live?"

What Is Ethics?

Ethics, or moral philosophy, is the philosophical study of morality.

Morality refers to beliefs concerning right and wrong, good and bad.

  • Judgments about actions and character
  • Values, rules, principles, and theories
  • Beliefs that guide our actions and define who we are
×
Ethics

The philosophical study of morality. Also called moral philosophy. Ethics uses critical reasoning to systematically examine moral beliefs, principles, and theories.

Note: "Ethics" and "morality" are often used interchangeably in everyday speech. In philosophy, ethics is the STUDY of morality.
×
Moral Philosophy

Another name for ethics‚Äîthe branch of philosophy that deals with questions of right and wrong, good and bad, duty and obligation.

×
Morality

Beliefs concerning right and wrong, good and bad. These beliefs can include judgments, values, rules, principles, and theories.

Example: "Greek morality" or "Russell's morality" refers to the specific moral norms of a group or individual.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

The key distinction: MORALITY is a set of beliefs about right/wrong. ETHICS is the philosophical STUDY of those beliefs.

Think of it like: Religion is a set of beliefs; Religious Studies is the academic study of those beliefs.

Why Does Ethics Matter?

"How ought we to live?"

‚Äî Socrates (c. 470‚Äì399 BCE)

Ethics is inescapable. You cannot run away from:

  • Choices about right and wrong
  • Judgments about character (yours and others')
  • Questions about your moral outlook
  • Conflicts when your values clash with others'
üí° Key insight: Even claiming ethics doesn't matter IS a moral position‚Äîyou're still doing ethics!

üìñ The Inescapability of Ethics

The textbook emphasizes that you cannot escape ethics. Every day you make moral judgments, embrace or reject moral principles, and assess character.

Even if you try to avoid ethics by saying "morality doesn't matter," that's itself a moral claim that needs justification!

What's at Stake?

When we do ethics, we're dealing with everything we hold dear‚Äîspecifically, moral values.

Through ethics we determine:

  • What is worth living for‚Äîand dying for
  • What is the greatest good
  • What goals we should pursue
  • What virtues we should cultivate
  • What duties we should fulfill
  • What value we place on human life
×
Moral Values

The things we consider most important from a moral standpoint‚Äîwhat we believe matters most ethically. These include concepts like justice, rights, human dignity, and the common good.

üìñ The Stakes Are High

Ethics isn't just abstract theorizing‚Äîit shapes how we live. The textbook lists profound questions: What is worth dying for? What value do we place on human life?

These aren't idle questions. They affect real decisions about medicine, war, justice, and personal relationships.

The "Easy Road" in Ethics

You can avoid deep ethical thinking‚Äîbut there are serious drawbacks:

Drawback 1: Loss of Freedom

If you never question inherited beliefs, those beliefs aren't really YOURS‚Äîthey control you.

Drawback 2: Confusion & Contradiction

Unexamined moral codes often conflict. Without critical tools, you can't resolve dilemmas.

Drawback 3: Moral Stagnation

Without examination, personal moral growth becomes impossible.

üìñ Why We Can't Take the Easy Road

The textbook (via philosopher Paul Taylor) warns about "blindly embracing" inherited morality. Three problems emerge:

1) Your beliefs aren't truly yours if you never chose them. 2) You'll be lost when rules conflict. 3) You'll never grow morally.

Other branches of philosophy include:

  • Logic ‚Äî the study of correct reasoning
  • Metaphysics ‚Äî the study of reality's fundamental nature
  • Epistemology ‚Äî the study of knowledge

Philosophy

The systematic use of critical reasoning to answer the most fundamental questions in life. Philosophy examines beliefs carefully and seeks answers supported by good reasons.

The branch of philosophy that studies correct reasoning‚Äîhow to evaluate arguments, identify fallacies, and reason validly.

üìñ Ethics in Context

Ethics is one branch of philosophy alongside logic, metaphysics, and epistemology. All branches use critical reasoning to examine fundamental questions.

Ethics specifically focuses on questions about right/wrong and good/bad.

Three Divisions of Ethics

Normative Ethics

The study of principles that guide our actions and judgments

"What should I do?"

Metaethics

The study of the meaning and logic of moral beliefs

"What does 'good' mean?"

Applied Ethics

The application of moral norms to specific issues or cases

"Is this action right?"

×
Normative Ethics

The study of the principles, rules, or theories that guide our actions and judgments. "Normative" refers to norms or standards.

Questions it asks: Is this moral principle justified? Is utilitarianism a good moral theory? What makes an action right?
×
Metaethics
(MET-uh-eth-iks)

The study of the meaning and logical structure of moral beliefs. It "steps back" from asking what's right to ask what "right" even MEANS.

Questions it asks: What does it mean for something to be "good"? Is there such a thing as moral truth? Can moral claims be justified?
×
Applied Ethics

The application of moral norms to specific moral issues or cases, particularly in professions like medicine or law.

Examples: Medical ethics, business ethics, journalistic ethics, environmental ethics. These fields apply moral principles to real-world situations.

üìñ The Three Branches

This course focuses mainly on NORMATIVE ethics‚Äîstudying moral theories and principles. But it also touches on metaethics (Chapter 2) and applied ethics (Part 4).

Think of it as: Normative = what's right; Metaethics = what "right" means; Applied = how to apply it.

Science vs. Philosophy on Morality

Descriptive Ethics

The scientific study of moral beliefs and practices

Describes what people actually believe and do

Sociology, anthropology, psychology

Normative Ethics

The philosophical study of moral principles

Determines what people should believe and do

Philosophy, moral reasoning

üí° Key distinction: Science describes; philosophy prescribes. Science says what IS; ethics says what OUGHT to be.
×
Descriptive Ethics

The scientific study of moral beliefs and practices. Its aim is to describe and explain how people actually behave and think morally‚Äînot to judge whether they're right.

Example: A sociologist studying what percentage of Americans believe lying is wrong. This describes beliefs without evaluating them.

üìñ Is vs. Ought

This is a crucial distinction. Descriptive ethics (science) tells us what people believe. Normative ethics (philosophy) tells us what they SHOULD believe.

You can't get "ought" from "is"‚Äîknowing that many people believe X doesn't tell you whether X is actually right.

Values and Obligations

In ethics, we must distinguish between two types of concepts:

Value

About what is morally good, bad, praiseworthy, blameworthy

Applies to: persons, character traits, motives, intentions

"She is a good person"

Obligation

About what is obligatory, a duty, right or wrong

Applies to: actions, what we should or shouldn't do

"She has a duty to tell the truth"

×
Value (Moral)

Concepts about what is morally good, bad, praiseworthy, or blameworthy. Value judgments typically apply to persons, character, motives, and intentions.

Examples: "He is a good person." "Her motives were blameworthy." "Courage is a virtue."
×
Obligation

Concepts about what is obligatory, a duty, or what we should or ought to do. Obligation judgments typically apply to actions.

Examples: "You ought to tell the truth." "Stealing is wrong." "We have a duty to help others."

üìñ Two Types of Moral Concepts

This distinction matters because you can have a good PERSON do a wrong ACTION (good intentions, bad outcome) or a bad PERSON do the right ACTION (selfish motives, helpful result).

Value = character/persons. Obligation = actions/duties.

Two Types of Value

Instrumentally Valuable

Valuable as a means to something else

Also called "extrinsically valuable"

Money, medicine, tools

Intrinsically Valuable

Valuable in itself, for its own sake

Valuable without being a means to anything

Happiness, pleasure, virtue, beauty

üí° Test: Ask "Why is X valuable?" If the answer is "because it leads to Y," X is instrumentally valuable. If "it just IS valuable," it's intrinsically valuable.
×
Instrumentally Valuable
(also: extrinsically valuable)

Valuable as a means to something else. The value comes from what it can get you, not from what it is in itself.

Example: Money is instrumentally valuable‚Äîit's not valuable in itself, but because of what it can buy. A pen is valuable because it can write letters.
×
Intrinsically Valuable

Valuable in itself, for its own sake, without being a means to something else. The value is inherent in the thing itself.

Example: Happiness is often considered intrinsically valuable‚Äîwe don't want happiness to get something else; we just want happiness for its own sake.

üìñ Means vs. Ends

This distinction is fundamental in ethics. Instrumental value = means to an end. Intrinsic value = an end in itself.

Different ethical theories disagree about what has intrinsic value: happiness? virtue? knowledge? human life?

Elements of Ethics

The Preeminence of Reason

Ethics requires critical reasoning‚Äînot just feelings.

  • Feelings matter but are unreliable guides alone
  • They may reflect prejudice, culture, or self-interest
  • Moral judgments must be supported by good reasons
  • Careful reasoning helps us evaluate our feelings
‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: "It feels right" is not a sufficient moral justification. We need to ask WHY it feels right and whether those reasons hold up.
×
Critical Reasoning

The careful, systematic evaluation of statements, claims, or arguments. In ethics, it involves evaluating logical arguments and carefully analyzing moral concepts.

Key components: Evaluating logical arguments, analyzing concepts, identifying assumptions, testing claims against evidence and counterexamples.

üìñ Reason Over Feeling

The textbook emphasizes that while feelings matter (they give us empathy and warn us of injustice), they're not enough alone.

Feelings can be wrong‚Äîshaped by prejudice or ignorance. We need reasons to support moral judgments.

Elements of Ethics

The Universal Perspective

Moral norms and judgments follow the principle of universalizability:

If an action is right (or wrong) for one person, it is right (or wrong) for everyone in relevantly similar situations.
  • Moral judgments can't apply only to YOU
  • If lying is wrong for me, it's wrong for you in similar circumstances
  • Reasons must apply universally, not just to individuals
×
Principle of Universalizability

The idea that a moral statement (principle, rule, or judgment) that applies in one situation must apply in all other situations that are relevantly similar.

Example: If you say lying is wrong when YOUR friend lies to you, you must also say it's wrong when you lie to your friend‚Äîthe situations are relevantly similar.

üìñ Consistency in Moral Judgments

This is a fundamental principle of moral reasoning. You can't say "lying is wrong for others but okay for me" without a RELEVANT difference between the situations.

This connects to Kant's categorical imperative, which you'll study later.

Elements of Ethics

The Principle of Impartiality

The principle of impartiality: All persons are considered equal and should be treated accordingly.

  • Everyone's interests deserve equal weight
  • No special treatment without morally relevant reasons
  • Treat equals equally
Example of Violation:

"Everyone must refrain from stealing‚Äîexcept Mr. X, who may steal whenever he wants." This violates impartiality because there's no morally relevant reason for the exception.

×
Principle of Impartiality

The idea that from the moral point of view, all persons are considered equal and should be treated accordingly. Each person's interests deserve equal consideration.

Key point: "Treat equals equally." If there's no morally relevant difference between people, they should be treated the same way.

üìñ Moral Equality

This doesn't mean everyone is identical‚Äîit means everyone's interests count equally unless there's a relevant reason for difference.

A rule that exempts someone arbitrarily (like by celebrity status) violates impartiality and isn't a genuine moral rule.

Elements of Ethics

The Dominance of Moral Norms

Not all norms are moral norms. There are also:

  • Legal norms (laws, statutes)
  • Aesthetic norms (standards for art, beauty)
  • Prudential norms (practical self-interest)

But moral norms have a special status: they dominate other norms.

üí° Example: A prudential norm ("Never help strangers") must yield to a moral norm ("Help a stranger in an emergency if you can do so safely").
×
Moral Norms (Dominance of)

Moral norms are considered weightier or more important than other types of norms. When moral principles conflict with other considerations (legal, aesthetic, prudential), moral considerations usually override.

Example: If a law conflicts with a moral principle, we judge the LAW to be bad‚Äîmoral norms take precedence. This is the justification for civil disobedience.

üìñ Morality Trumps Other Norms

When moral principles conflict with other norms, morality usually wins. A bad law is judged bad ON MORAL GROUNDS‚Äîshowing that moral norms dominate legal ones.

This is why we can say a law is "unjust"‚Äîwe're applying moral standards to judge the law.

üìñ Ethics for Everyone

The textbook argues that ethics (the philosophical study) is useful for believers and nonbelievers alike. Critical reasoning provides a common language for discussing moral issues across religious boundaries.

The Divine Command Theory

The divine command theory says God is the source of morality: actions are right if and only if God commands them.

The Euthyphro Dilemma

Posed by Socrates in Plato's dialogue

Is an action right because God commands it, or does God command it because it is right?

Horn 1: Right because God commands it

‚Üí Morality becomes arbitrary (God could command anything)

Horn 2: God commands it because it's right

‚Üí Rightness is independent of God's will

×
Divine Command Theory

The theory that right actions are right because God commands them, and wrong actions are wrong because God forbids them. God's will defines morality.

The Euthyphro Problem: If morality depends entirely on God's will, then morality seems arbitrary‚ÄîGod could make murder right by commanding it. But if God commands things because they're already right, then rightness is independent of God.

üìñ A Classic Dilemma

The Euthyphro dilemma has challenged divine command theory for 2,400 years. Both horns seem problematic for the theory.

This doesn't prove God doesn't exist‚Äîjust that morality may not depend entirely on God's commands.

Why Believers Need Ethics

Even religious believers benefit from moral philosophy because:

  • Religious rules are general and need interpretation for specific cases
  • Commandments can conflict‚Äîwhich takes priority?
  • Believers disagree with each other on moral issues
  • Public discourse requires common ground across traditions
üí° Key insight: Great religious thinkers‚ÄîAquinas, Leibniz, Maimonides, Kant‚Äîhave used reason to examine morality. Faith and reason can work together.

üìñ Faith and Reason Together

The textbook emphasizes that religious moral codes often need philosophical interpretation. "Love your neighbor"‚Äîbut who counts as a neighbor? Religious texts don't always say.

Philosophy provides tools for resolving these questions that religious traditions alone may not supply.
