üìñ Welcome to Chapter 5!

This chapter examines consequentialist moral theories‚Äîtheories that judge actions by their outcomes. You'll learn about ethical egoism, utilitarianism (both act and rule versions), and social contract theory.

Key question: Should we judge actions by their consequences, and if so, whose good should we maximize?

The Consequentialist Appeal

Consequentialist theories share an attractive, commonsensical core:

Right actions must produce the best balance of good over evil.
  • What matters most is the results of our actions
  • We naturally judge actions by what they produce
  • Moral choice becomes (seemingly) straightforward calculation
×
Consequentialist Theory

A moral theory asserting that what makes an action right is its consequences. The rightness of an action depends on the amount of good it produces.

Core idea: Don't focus on what kind of action it is‚Äîfocus on what results it brings about. Good consequences = right action.

üìñ Why Consequentialism Appeals

Consequentialism matches our everyday moral intuitions in many cases. We often justify actions by pointing to their good results: "I lied to protect her feelings."

It also seems to make moral decisions straightforward‚Äîjust calculate which action produces the most good!

Ethical Egoism

Ethical egoism says the right action is the one that advances your own best interests.

  • Your only moral duty: maximize good for yourself
  • Others' interests matter only if helping them benefits you
  • Not the same as selfishness or recklessness
üí° Important distinction: Ethical egoism ‚â† doing whatever you want. A smart egoist considers long-term interests, cooperates with others, and avoids antagonizing society.
×
Ethical Egoism

The theory that the right action is the one that advances one's own best interests. Each person's moral duty is to promote their own good.

Note: This is a NORMATIVE theory‚Äîit says what you SHOULD do, not just what you naturally DO. It claims self-interest is the proper basis for morality.

üìñ Understanding Ethical Egoism

Ethical egoism is often misunderstood. It's not about being cruel or short-sighted. A wise egoist recognizes that cooperation, honesty, and relationships often serve their long-term interests.

The theory forces us to ask: Why SHOULD I care about others' interests?

Two Versions of Ethical Egoism

Act-Egoism

Apply the egoistic principle to individual acts

Ask: "Does THIS action promote my self-interest?"

Calculate each case separately

Rule-Egoism

Follow rules that maximize self-interest

Ask: "Does following this rule promote my self-interest?"

Follow the rule consistently

×
Act-Egoism

The theory that to determine right action, you must apply the egoistic principle to individual acts. Act A is preferable to Act B because it promotes your self-interest better.

×
Rule-Egoism

The theory that to determine right action, you must see if an act falls under a rule that, if consistently followed, would maximize your self-interest.

Example: "Be honest in business dealings" might be a rule-egoist's rule‚Äîbecause honesty builds trust that serves long-term self-interest.

üìñ The Same Split as Utilitarianism

Notice this mirrors the act/rule distinction in utilitarianism. Act-egoism calculates each case; rule-egoism follows rules that generally maximize self-interest.

Rule-egoism might prohibit deception because, in the long run, being known as dishonest hurts your interests.

The Argument from Psychological Egoism

The main argument FOR ethical egoism relies on psychological egoism:

1. We can only do what our self-interest motivates us to do (psychological egoism)

2. We are not morally obligated to do what we cannot do

3. Therefore, we are only obligated to do what our self-interest motivates us to do

‚ö†Ô∏è Problem: Even if psychological egoism is true, this only shows actions can't be right unless they serve self-interest. It doesn't prove self-interest is the ONLY criterion.
×
Psychological Egoism

The view that the motive for all our actions is self-interest. Whatever we do, we do because we want to promote our own welfare.

Key point: This is a DESCRIPTIVE claim about human psychology, not a normative claim about what we SHOULD do. It says we ARE selfish, not that we SHOULD be.

üìñ Is Psychological Egoism True?

Critics point out that people sometimes do things against their self-interest: donate kidneys to strangers, risk their lives for others, develop self-destructive habits.

The egoist might say these are REALLY self-interested (we get satisfaction), but this risks making the theory unfalsifiable.

Is Psychological Egoism True?

Critics argue psychological egoism rests on a conceptual confusion:

The Egoist's Claim:

We perform "unselfish" acts because doing so gives us satisfaction.

Therefore, all actions are really about getting satisfaction for ourselves.

The Confusion:

We desire something other than satisfaction, and THEN experience satisfaction as a result of getting what we desired.

Satisfaction is a BY-PRODUCT, not the goal.

üí° Example: A parent saves their child from fire. They feel satisfaction‚Äîbut only BECAUSE they desired to save the child. The desire was for the child's safety, not for their own satisfaction.

üìñ The Satisfaction Confusion

Philosopher Joel Feinberg makes this point brilliantly. If Jones only desires his own happiness, he'll never find it‚Äîbecause happiness comes from pursuing OTHER things.

The person who truly pursues only their own happiness is "hopelessly unrealistic" and will never achieve it.

Evaluating Ethical Egoism

Applying the moral criteria of adequacy:

1 Consistency with considered judgments?

‚ùå Fails. Ethical egoism sanctions actions we regard as abominable (e.g., murdering your father for inheritance if you won't get caught).

2 Consistency with moral experience?

‚ùå Fails. Violates impartiality‚Äîarbitrarily treats one person (oneself) as more important than others without morally relevant difference.

üìñ The Impartiality Problem

This is probably the most serious objection. Ethical egoism says your interests matter more than everyone else's‚Äîbut WHY? What's morally special about YOU?

Treating equals unequally without good reason is the definition of discrimination.

Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism is one of the most influential moral theories in history.

Jeremy Bentham

1748‚Äì1832 ¬∑ English philosopher

First developed the theory systematically

John Stuart Mill

1806‚Äì1873 ¬∑ English philosopher and economist

Refined and defended the theory

×
Utilitarianism

The theory that the morally right action is the one that produces the most favorable balance of good over evil, everyone considered.

Key difference from egoism: Everyone's happiness counts equally. Your happiness matters no more than a stranger's.

üìñ Historical Impact

Utilitarianism became a powerful instrument of social reform. Bentham and Mill advocated for women's rights, prison reform, animal welfare, and aid to the poor‚Äîall justified by maximizing overall happiness.

The Principle of Utility

Bentham called the utilitarian principle the principle of utility:

"By the principle of utility is meant that principle which approves or disapproves of every action whatsoever, according to the tendency which it appears to have to augment or diminish the happiness of the party whose interest is in question."

‚Äî Jeremy Bentham

Mill called it the greatest happiness principle.

×
Principle of Utility

Bentham's name for the foundational utilitarian principle: actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce unhappiness.

×
Greatest Happiness Principle

Mill's name for the utilitarian principle: actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness.

Mill's definition: "By happiness is intended pleasure, and the absence of pain; by unhappiness, pain, and the privation of pleasure."

üìñ Happiness as the Standard

For both Bentham and Mill, happiness (pleasure) is the ONLY intrinsic good. Everything else is valuable only as a means to happiness.

This makes utilitarianism a HEDONISTIC theory‚Äîpleasure is the ultimate good.

ü§î Check Your Understanding

Quick Quiz:

What is the key difference between ethical egoism and utilitarianism?

A) Egoism focuses on consequences; utilitarianism does not

B) Egoism maximizes good for oneself; utilitarianism maximizes good for everyone

C) Egoism is rule-based; utilitarianism is act-based

üìñ Answer: B

Both theories are consequentialist (focus on consequences), but they differ on WHOSE good matters.

Egoism: maximize YOUR good. Utilitarianism: maximize EVERYONE'S good equally.

Classic (Act) Utilitarianism

Act-utilitarianism is the simplest form of the theory:

The Core Principle:

The right action is the one that directly produces the greatest balance of happiness over unhappiness, everyone considered.

  • Each situation requires a fresh calculation
  • No rules to follow‚Äîjust maximize happiness
  • Everyone's happiness counts equally
×
Act-Utilitarianism

The utilitarian theory asserting that the right action is the one that directly produces the most favorable balance of good over evil, everyone considered.

Method: For each possible action, calculate the total happiness it would produce. Choose the action with the highest score.

üìñ The Calculation Method

Bentham developed the "hedonic calculus" to measure happiness: intensity, duration, certainty, nearness, fecundity (likelihood of more pleasure), purity, and extent (how many affected).

The action with the best total score is the morally right one.

Bentham vs. Mill on Happiness

Bentham: Quantity

Happiness varies only in quantity‚Äîintensity, duration, etc.

All pleasures are equal in kind.

"Pushpin is as good as poetry" (if equal pleasure)

Mill: Quality

Happiness varies in quality as well as quantity.

Higher pleasures (intellectual) > lower pleasures (bodily).

"Better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied"

üí° Mill's test: Those who have experienced both higher and lower pleasures prefer the higher ones. The majority preference of the experienced determines quality.

üìñ The Quality Problem

Mill's distinction is intuitively appealing but philosophically problematic. How do we rank pleasures objectively? People disagree about which pleasures are "higher."

Critics say Mill smuggles in non-utilitarian values (like intellectual virtue) that can't be justified purely by pleasure.

Testing Act-Utilitarianism

The Case of Dr. X

Dr. X is one step from curing all heart disease‚Äîbut needs fresh heart tissue from a living person. She finds a homeless man with no family or friends. She kills him secretly, perfects the cure, and saves millions of lives. She feels no guilt.

Act-utilitarian verdict: Dr. X did the right thing‚Äîmillions saved outweighs one death.

‚ö†Ô∏è The problem: This conflicts with our considered moral judgment that murdering an innocent person is wrong, regardless of the good consequences.

üìñ The Justice Problem

This is act-utilitarianism's most serious problem: it seems to sanction obviously unjust actions if they maximize overall happiness.

Other examples: framing an innocent person to prevent riots, torturing one child to make millions happy.

Critiques of Act-Utilitarianism

1 Conflicts with justice

May require harming innocents for the greater good

2 Conflicts with rights

Rights can be violated if doing so maximizes happiness

3 Conflicts with obligations

Promise-keeping, truth-telling can be overridden by tiny gains in utility

4 The "no-rest" problem

We should always be maximizing utility‚Äîwatching TV is wrong if we could be helping others

üìñ Demanding Too Much?

The "no-rest" problem: act-utilitarianism seems impossibly demanding. Right now you could donate your TV money to charity, volunteer at a shelter, etc. Are you doing wrong by relaxing?

Some utilitarians bite this bullet; others modify the theory.

Rule-Utilitarianism

Rule-utilitarianism was developed partly to address act-utilitarianism's problems:

The Core Principle:

The right action is the one covered by a rule that, if generally followed, would produce the most favorable balance of good over evil, everyone considered.

  • Focus on rules, not individual acts
  • Ask: "What if everyone followed this rule?"
  • Follow the rule even when breaking it might help this time
×
Rule-Utilitarianism

The utilitarian theory asserting that the right action is the one covered by a rule that, if generally followed, would produce the most favorable balance of good over evil, everyone considered.

Key difference: We evaluate RULES by their consequences, then follow the best rules‚Äîeven in cases where breaking them would produce more happiness this one time.

üìñ Why Rules Help

Rule-utilitarianism can explain why we shouldn't murder innocents even for good consequences: a rule allowing such murders would produce terrible long-term effects (fear, distrust, etc.).

Rules provide stability and predictability that act-by-act calculation cannot.

Applying Rule-Utilitarianism

The Terrorist Scenario

A terrorist is killing hundreds. The only way to stop him is to kill his innocent wife and children. An act-utilitarian might say: do it‚Äîsave more lives. What does a rule-utilitarian say?

Rule-utilitarian analysis:

  • Rule 1: "Don't kill innocent people" ‚Üí if followed, prevents chaos and fear
  • Rule 2: "Kill innocents to stop terrorists" ‚Üí if followed, leads to abuse, fear, breakdown of trust

Verdict: Following Rule 1 produces more long-term happiness. Don't kill the innocents.

üìñ Rules Protect Against Short-Sightedness

Rule-utilitarianism captures the insight that consistent rule-following produces better outcomes than case-by-case calculation, which is prone to bias and error.

Critiques of Rule-Utilitarianism

The Collapse Problem: Critics argue rule-utilitarianism collapses into act-utilitarianism.

The argument:

  • Rules with exceptions maximize utility better than absolute rules
  • "Don't steal except when..." is better than "Don't steal"
  • As we add exceptions, rules become specific to situations
  • Eventually, rules just say "Do what maximizes utility in this case"
  • That's act-utilitarianism!
üí° The dilemma: Either rules have exceptions (collapse into act-util) or they don't (sometimes require non-utility-maximizing actions).

üìñ The Rule Specificity Problem

Rule-utilitarians respond by insisting on rules of appropriate generality‚Äîspecific enough to be useful, general enough to maintain the benefits of predictability.

But critics say any stopping point is arbitrary.

Learning from Utilitarianism

Despite criticisms, utilitarianism embodies important moral truths:

1. Consequences Matter

The effects of our actions on people's welfare are morally relevant.

2. Impartiality

Everyone's interests count equally‚Äîno one is inherently more important.

3. Beneficence

We should promote human welfare‚Äîmorality aims at making things better.

üìñ Keeping the Baby

Even if we reject utilitarianism as a complete moral theory, we should preserve its core insights. Consequences DO matter. Impartiality IS important. Beneficence IS a virtue.

Most plausible moral theories incorporate these utilitarian elements.

Social Contract Theory

Social contract theory offers another consequentialist approach:

Core Idea:

Morality arises from a social contract that self-interested, rational people would agree to in order to secure peace, prosperity, and safety.

Without such an agreement, life would be (in Hobbes's words) "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

×
Social Contract Theory

The doctrine that morality arises from a social contract that self-interested and rational people would abide by in order to secure a degree of peace, prosperity, and safety.

Key insight: Moral rules are those that benefit everyone when everyone follows them. We agree to restrain ourselves in exchange for others restraining themselves.

üìñ Morality from Self-Interest

Social contract theory tries to derive morality from rational self-interest. Even egoists should accept moral constraints because everyone benefits from cooperation.

The rules we all follow are the rules that make social life possible and beneficial.

ü§î Check Your Understanding

Apply the Theory:

A surgeon has five patients dying from organ failure. A healthy visitor comes in for a check-up. The surgeon could kill the visitor and use their organs to save all five patients.

What would an act-utilitarian say? What's the problem with this answer?

üìñ Answer

Act-utilitarian answer: Kill the visitor‚Äî5 lives saved > 1 life lost = more happiness.

The problem: This grossly violates our considered moral judgment that murdering an innocent person is wrong. It also violates the victim's rights and the principle of justice.

This is why many reject act-utilitarianism‚Äîit can sanction obviously immoral actions.

Hobbes's Social Contract

Thomas Hobbes

1588‚Äì1679 ¬∑ English philosopher

Author of Leviathan

Hobbes's pessimistic view of human nature:

  • Without society, life is a "war of every man against every man"
  • No morality exists in the state of nature
  • Morality requires a powerful sovereign (the "Leviathan") to enforce rules
  • We obey moral rules to avoid punishment and chaos
×
State of Nature

Hobbes's term for the condition of humanity without civil government. In this state, there is no law, no morality, no property‚Äîonly a constant struggle for survival.

Hobbes's famous line: Life in the state of nature is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."

üìñ Hobbes's Argument

Hobbes argues that rational self-interest leads us to accept a social contract. Even though we lose some freedom, we gain security and the possibility of peaceful cooperation.

Morality is essentially the set of rules that make social life beneficial for everyone.

Evaluating Social Contract Theory

Strengths

‚úì Explains why morality is objective (based on rational agreement)

‚úì High on usefulness (clear guidance on right/wrong)

‚úì Doesn't require God or mysterious moral facts

Weaknesses

‚úó Did anyone actually consent to this contract?

‚úó What about those who can't participate (infants, disabled)?

‚úó Why be moral when you can get away with cheating?

üìñ The Consent Problem

The biggest challenge: no one actually signed a social contract. Some say we give "implicit consent" by enjoying society's benefits‚Äîbut is that enough to be bound?

John Rawls later developed a more sophisticated version using a "veil of ignorance."

Chapter Summary

  • Consequentialist theories judge actions by their results
  • Ethical egoism: maximize YOUR good (fails impartiality)
  • Utilitarianism: maximize EVERYONE'S good equally
  • Act-utilitarianism: calculate each case (can sanction injustice)
  • Rule-utilitarianism: follow best rules (may collapse into act-util)
  • Social contract theory: morality from rational self-interested agreement
  • All consequentialist theories face the problem of conflicts with justice and rights
