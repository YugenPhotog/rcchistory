The Central Question

When we say something is morally right or wrong, what exactly are we claiming?

  • Are we stating a fact that's true for everyone?
  • Are we expressing a personal opinion?
  • Are we just venting an emotion?
üì∏ Image: "philosopher thinking question marks contemplation abstract"
Person deep in thought with question marks
×
Fact

A statement describing how things actually are, independent of beliefs or feelings.

Example: "Water freezes at 32¬∞F" is true whether you believe it or not.
×
Opinion

A personal judgment that may vary from person to person, often thought to be neither objectively true nor false.

Example: "Chocolate ice cream is the best" is based on personal preference.
×
Emotion

A feeling (like anger, disgust, approval) that influences reactions but isn't itself a claim about the world.

Example: Saying "Yuck!" expresses disgust‚Äîit doesn't claim anything is true or false.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

This frames the entire chapter. Every theory we examine is a different answer to: What are we doing when we make moral judgments?

Think about when you say "Stealing is wrong." Are you stating a fact, sharing a preference, or venting a feeling?

Four Views on Morality

Objectivism

Moral truths exist independently of opinions

Subjective Relativism

Morality is relative to individuals

Cultural Relativism

Morality is relative to cultures

Emotivism

Moral claims express emotions, not facts

üìñ The Big Picture

These four theories give different answers to what makes things right or wrong, and whether we can be mistaken about morality.

Moral Objectivism

Moral objectivism says moral truths exist independently of what anyone thinks or feels.

  • Moral facts exist‚Äîlike mathematical or scientific facts
  • These facts are true for everyone, regardless of culture
  • People can be mistaken about morality
üí° Study Buddy: Objectivism does NOT mean rules have no exceptions! It just means there are genuine moral truths to discover.
×
Moral Objectivism
(ob-JEK-ti-viz-um)

The theory that moral truths exist independently of what individuals or societies think.

Example: "Torturing innocents for fun is wrong" is TRUE as a fact‚Äîeven if everyone believed otherwise.

üìñ Understanding Objectivism

This is the "common sense" view. When you say "murder is wrong," you probably think you're stating something TRUE.

Common misconception: Objectivism ‚â† Absolutism. You can believe moral facts exist while also thinking rules have exceptions.

Subjective Relativism

Subjective relativism says an action is morally right if the individual approves of it.

  • Morality is relative to individuals
  • Your approval makes an action right (for you)
  • No one can be objectively wrong about morality
"Morality becomes like taste in ice cream‚Äîyou prefer chocolate, I prefer vanilla, and neither of us is wrong."
×
Subjective Relativism
(sub-JEK-tiv REL-uh-ti-viz-um)

The view that an action is morally right if one approves of it. Your approval makes it right for you.

Example: If Ann approves of X and Carlos disapproves, X is right for Ann but wrong for Carlos. Neither is mistaken.

üìñ Key Distinction

Objectivist: "I approve of honesty because it's right."

Subjective relativist: "Honesty is right (for me) because I approve of it."

Notice how the direction of explanation flips!

Cultural Relativism

Cultural relativism says an action is morally right if one's culture approves of it.

  • Morality is relative to cultures, not individuals
  • What's right in one culture may be wrong in another
  • No culture's morality is objectively superior
üì∏ Image: "world cultures diversity globe mosaic"
Diverse cultural symbols around a globe
×
Cultural Relativism
(KUL-chur-ul REL-uh-ti-viz-um)

The view that an action is morally right if one's culture approves of it.

Example: If Culture A approves of practice X and Culture B disapproves, X is right in A and wrong in B.

üìñ Key Difference from Subjective Relativism

It's not YOUR approval that matters‚Äîit's your CULTURE'S approval. If you disagree with your culture, you're the one who's wrong!

Emotivism

Emotivism says moral statements are neither true nor false‚Äîthey're expressions of emotion.

  • Moral utterances are like exclamations: "Boo!" or "Hooray!"
  • Saying "Stealing is wrong" = "Stealing‚Äîboo!"
  • There are no moral facts to disagree about
üí° Study Buddy: Emotivism is a type of noncognitivism‚Äîmoral statements don't express beliefs that can be true or false.
×
Emotivism
(ee-MOH-ti-viz-um)

The view that moral utterances are neither true nor false but are expressions of emotions or attitudes.

Key philosopher: A.J. Ayer (1910-1989) argued moral statements "express no proposition which can be either true or false."
×
Moral Facts

Objective truths about what is morally right and wrong‚Äîfacts that exist independently of beliefs or feelings.

Emotivists deny these exist. When we make moral claims, we're expressing attitudes, not describing facts.
×
Noncognitivism
(non-KOG-ni-ti-viz-um)

The view that moral judgments do NOT express beliefs that can be true or false. Instead, they express attitudes, emotions, or commands.

Contrast with cognitivism: Cognitivists think "Murder is wrong" expresses a belief that CAN be true or false.

üìñ The Radical View

Emotivism is the most radical position. It says when we argue about ethics, we're not disagreeing about facts‚Äîjust expressing different feelings.

Problems with Subjective Relativism

If subjective relativism is true, troubling things follow:

  • Moral infallibility: You can never be mistaken
  • No genuine disagreement: When you and I disagree, we're both "right"
  • Extreme cases: If Hitler approved of genocide, it was right for him
‚ö†Ô∏è Core problem: Our moral experience tells us we can be mistaken. We sometimes realize past beliefs were wrong.
×
Moral Infallibility
(in-FAL-ih-BIL-ih-tee)

The condition of being incapable of making a moral mistake. Whatever you believe is right IS right.

The problem: Haven't you ever realized you were wrong about a moral issue? Subjective relativism says that's impossible.

üìñ Why This Matters

These aren't "gotcha" arguments. They show subjective relativism conflicts with deep features of our moral experience.

The Argument for Cultural Relativism

Cultural relativism is often based on this argument:

1. People's judgments about right and wrong differ from culture to culture.

2. If judgments differ, then right and wrong are relative to culture.

‚à¥ Therefore, right and wrong are relative to culture.

The argument is valid‚Äîbut is it sound?

×
Valid Argument

An argument where IF the premises are true, the conclusion MUST be true. Validity is about structure, not whether premises are actually true.

Valid but unsound: "All cats are purple. Fluffy is a cat. ‚à¥ Fluffy is purple." (Valid structure, false premise!)
×
Sound Argument

An argument that is (1) valid AND (2) has all true premises. A sound argument guarantees a true conclusion.

Key question: Is Premise 2 true? Does diversity of opinion PROVE morality is relative?

üìñ The Logical Flaw

People disagree about whether Earth is flat. Does this prove "the shape of Earth is relative"? Of course not! Disagreement doesn't prove there's no right answer.

ü§î Check Your Understanding

Reflection Question:

"People disagree about morality. Therefore, morality is relative."

What's the logical problem with this argument? Can you think of a parallel example showing why disagreement doesn't prove relativism?

üìñ Think About It

Parallel examples: People disagree about climate change‚Äîdoes that mean climate facts are relative? Ancient people thought Earth was flat‚Äîwas it flat FOR THEM?

Problems with Cultural Relativism

Problem 1: Cultural Infallibility

If cultural relativism is true, cultures cannot be morally mistaken.

  • If a culture approves of genocide, genocide is right in that culture
  • Nazi Germany's approval made the Holocaust permissible (for them)
  • We cannot legitimately criticize other cultures' practices
"Because they were approved by their respective societies, they were just as morally justified as the activities of Albert Schweitzer, Jonas Salk, or Florence Nightingale."
‚Äî Textbook, p. 27

üìñ The Holocaust Problem

Most people find this conclusion repugnant. But if you accept cultural relativism, you're committed to it.

Problems with Cultural Relativism

Problem 2: Social Reformers Are Always Wrong

Cultural relativism implies social reformers who disagree with their culture are automatically immoral.

  • Martin Luther King Jr. challenged segregation laws
  • By cultural relativist logic, he was morally wrong
  • The culture's approval is the ultimate authority
üì∏ Image: "civil rights movement march MLK historical"
Civil rights protesters marching for equality
×
Social Reformer

A person who works to change social practices they believe are unjust‚Äîoften against prevailing cultural views.

The paradox: We admire reformers as moral heroes. But cultural relativism says they were WRONG to challenge their cultures.

üìñ Moral Progress Impossible

If cultural relativism is true, cultures can CHANGE their views but never IMPROVE them‚Äîthere's no standard higher than the culture itself.

Problems with Cultural Relativism

Problem 3: The Tolerance Contradiction

Many accept cultural relativism because they value tolerance. But:

If tolerance is objectively good...

Then cultural relativism is FALSE (there's at least one objective value).

If tolerance is NOT objectively good...

Then intolerant cultures are just as "right" as tolerant ones.

Cultural relativists cannot consistently advocate tolerance.

×
Tolerance

Willingness to accept practices or beliefs that differ from one's own.

The contradiction: To say "we SHOULD be tolerant" is an objective moral claim‚Äîbut cultural relativism denies objective moral claims!

üìñ A Self-Undermining Theory

The very value (tolerance) that makes the theory appealing is incompatible with the theory itself!

Do Cultures Really Disagree?

Diversity of moral judgments may not reflect diversity of moral principles.

Example: Killing fathers at age 60

Some cultures practiced this. Different moral principles? Or different factual beliefs?

  • They believed you enter heaven at death in your current condition
  • They believed men peak physically/mentally at 60
  • Given these beliefs, killing at 60 expresses care for father's well-being

The underlying principle‚Äî"care for your parents"‚Äîis the same.

üìñ Key Insight

Many apparent moral disagreements are really FACTUAL disagreements. If cultures share basic principles, the argument for relativism loses force.

Cross-Cultural Moral Agreement

Research suggests cultures share many core moral principles:

  • Prohibitions against murder, lying, and incest
  • Obligations of fairness and reciprocity
  • Duties to care for children and parents
  • Values of loyalty, courage, and generosity
üí° Study Buddy: Philosopher Walter T. Stace notes anthropologists found "common moral elements" across extremely diverse cultures.
×
Moral Universals

Moral principles appearing across all or nearly all human cultures, suggesting they may reflect objective moral truths.

Examples: Nearly all cultures prohibit unprovoked murder, value care for children, and recognize fairness.

üìñ Evidence Against Relativism

If there ARE shared moral principles across cultures, morality isn't simply relative. There may be common human morality expressed differently.

The Society-Identification Problem

Which society determines what's right for you?

  • You belong to many groups: nation, religion, profession, neighborhood
  • These groups may have conflicting moral views
  • Which one is "your culture"?
"What society do you belong to if you are an Italian American Buddhist living in Atlanta, who is a member of the National Organization for Women and a breast cancer support group?"
‚Äî Textbook, p. 28

üìñ A Practical Problem

Cultural relativism assumes simple, distinct cultures. But modern people have multiple overlapping memberships with conflicting values.

A.J. Ayer on Emotivism

üì∏ Image: "A.J. Ayer philosopher portrait"
A.J. Ayer (1910-1989)

A.J. Ayer argued moral statements express no proposition that can be true or false.

"It is as if I had written 'Stealing money!!'‚Äîwhere the exclamation marks show that a special sort of moral disapproval is being expressed."
‚Äî A.J. Ayer
×
A.J. Ayer (1910-1989)

British philosopher and proponent of logical positivism‚Äîmeaningful statements must be logically true or empirically verifiable.

Ayer argued ethical statements fail both tests, so they must be expressions of emotion, not factual claims.

üìñ The Boo-Hooray Theory

Sometimes called the "Boo-Hooray theory" because it reduces moral claims to "Boo!" (disapproval) and "Hooray!" (approval).

Functions of Moral Language

According to emotivism, moral utterances serve two functions:

1. Express Emotions

Vent feelings of approval or disapproval

"Violence is wrong" = expressing disgust

2. Influence Others

Try to make others feel the same way

"Violence is wrong" = persuading YOU to disapprove

What moral statements do NOT do: state facts about the world.

üìñ Radical Implications

If emotivism is true, moral arguments are really just sophisticated forms of manipulation‚Äînot genuine rational persuasion.

Problems with Emotivism

Problem 1: Moral Disagreement

Emotivism implies genuine moral disagreements are impossible.

  • Jane says "Abortion is right" (expressing approval)
  • Ellen says "Abortion is wrong" (expressing disapproval)
  • Are they disagreeing? Or just having different feelings?
‚ö†Ô∏è The problem: We think moral disagreements are REAL‚Äîlike disagreeing about facts, not just having different tastes.

üìñ Not Just Different Flavors

It's like "Yum, chocolate!" vs. "Yuck, chocolate!"‚Äîdifferent preferences, but not really DISAGREEING about anything. But moral debates feel different.

ü§î Quick Quiz

According to emotivism, when you say "Helping others is good," you are:

A) Stating a fact about helping

B) Expressing your culture's approval

C) Expressing approval (like saying "Hooray!")

D) Making a claim true relative to your values

üìñ Answer: C

Emotivism says moral statements express emotions‚Äînot facts (A), not specifically cultural (B), and not "true" in any sense (D).

Confusion alert: Subjective relativism says the claim IS true (for you). Emotivism says it's neither true nor false.

Problems with Emotivism

Problem 2: The Role of Reasons

We normally think moral claims can be supported by reasons.

"Euthanasia is wrong."

"Why?"

"Because it violates the sanctity of life and could lead to abuse."

Emotivism says these "reasons" don't logically support the claim‚Äîthey just psychologically influence attitudes.

üìñ Reasons vs. Manipulation

Emotivism blurs the line between rational persuasion (giving reasons) and manipulation (using rhetoric to change feelings). Most people think moral reasoning is more than manipulation.

Problems with Emotivism

Problem 3: Nothing Is Actually Good or Bad

Emotivism implies there are no properties of goodness or badness in the world.

"Suppose a six-year-old girl is living in Syria during the civil war. Assad's henchmen firebomb the village... the girl is burned from head to toe and endures excruciating pain for three days before she dies."
‚Äî Textbook, p. 30

When we say "How horrible!"‚Äîare we just expressing feelings? Or recognizing something truly bad?

üìñ The Deepest Problem

We don't just have feelings ABOUT suffering‚Äîwe think suffering IS bad. Emotivism seems to deny this basic truth about the world.

Evaluating the Theories

The textbook concludes that some form of moral objectivism best explains our moral experience.

  • Subjective Relativism: Implies we're infallible‚Äîcontradicts experience
  • Cultural Relativism: Implies cultures are infallible, reformers wrong
  • Emotivism: Denies moral facts, can't explain reasoning
  • Objectivism: Allows moral mistakes, progress, and reasoning
üí° Key insight: The alternative theories can't adequately explain moral disagreement, progress, or the role of reasons in ethics.

üìñ The Bottom Line

Objectivism isn't proven‚Äîbut it better fits how we actually experience morality than the alternatives.
