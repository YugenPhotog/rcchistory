Moral Reasoning

The process of carefully thinking through ethical issues using logic, evidence, and arguments‚Äînot just gut feelings or inherited beliefs.

Key point: Moral reasoning is just critical thinking applied to ethics. It's the same skill you use in any context where you evaluate claims.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

The chapter opens by emphasizing that ethics requires REASONING. We can't just rely on feelings, tradition, or authority‚Äîwe need to think carefully about moral issues.

The good news: moral reasoning uses the same critical thinking skills you already use elsewhere!

Claims and Arguments

Critical reasoning starts with identifying statements.

Statements ‚úì

"Murder is wrong."

"The ship sailed."

"5 + 5 = 10"

Can be true or false

Not Statements ‚úó

"Why is Anna laughing?"

"Hand me the screwdriver."

"For heaven's sake!"

Questions, commands, exclamations

×
Statement

An assertion that something is or is not the case. Statements are either true or false‚Äîeven if we don't know which.

Key test: Can you say "That's true" or "That's false" in response? If yes, it's a statement. Questions, commands, and exclamations fail this test.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

This distinction seems basic but is crucial. Arguments are made of statements‚Äîclaims that can be true or false. Questions and commands aren't statements.

Note: "Murder is wrong" IS a statement according to most views in ethics (even though emotivists would disagree‚Äîremember Chapter 2!).

What Is an Argument?

An argument is a group of statements where some (premises) support another (the conclusion).

Premise 1: Capital punishment helps deter crime.

Conclusion: Capital punishment is morally permissible.

üí° Study Buddy: An "argument" in philosophy ‚â† a heated disagreement! It means giving reasons to support a claim.
×
Argument

A group of statements, one of which is supposed to be supported by the rest. NOT a shouting match or quarrel!

Pattern: "I believe X because Y and Z." The reasons (Y and Z) are premises; the main claim (X) is the conclusion.
×
Premise
(PREM-iss)

A supporting statement in an argument‚Äîa reason given for believing the conclusion.

Think of it as: The evidence or reasons you're offering. An argument can have one premise or many.
×
Conclusion

The statement in an argument that the premises are intended to support‚Äîthe main claim being argued for.

Tip: Look for the conclusion FIRST when analyzing arguments. Ask: "What is this person trying to convince me of?"

üìñ Understanding This Slide

This is the foundation of the chapter. Every argument has this basic structure: reasons (premises) supporting a claim (conclusion).

Key insight: Only when reasons are given for a claim do we have an argument. A bundle of unsupported claims is NOT an argument.

Indicator Words

Indicator words signal premises and conclusions in arguments.

Conclusion Indicators

therefore, hence, so, thus, consequently, it follows that, as a result, it must be that

Premise Indicators

because, since, for, given that, due to the fact that, the reason being, assuming that

‚ö†Ô∏è Caution: Indicator words aren't foolproof! They sometimes appear when no argument is present. Always look for the conclusion first.
×
Indicator Words

Terms that often signal the presence of a premise or conclusion in an argument, or indicate whether an argument is deductive or inductive.

Pro tip: The most reliable way to find arguments is to look for the CONCLUSION first. Ask: "What claim is being defended?" Then find the reasons.

üìñ Understanding This Slide

These words are helpful clues, but don't rely on them blindly. The word "since" can introduce a premise ("since you asked...") or just indicate time ("since yesterday").

Best strategy: Find the conclusion first by asking "What is the person trying to prove?" Then the premises are whatever reasons they give.

Arguments vs. Non-Arguments

Not every passage with claims is an argument!

NOT an argument:

"The number of abortions in this state is increasing. More people favor greater access to abortion. This is an outrage."

Just assertions + expression of indignation. No reasons given.

IS an argument:

"The number of abortions is increasing, and more people favor access to abortion. Therefore, the trend is toward greater acceptance of abortion."

Reasons given to support a conclusion.

üìñ Key Distinction

Many people think they've made an argument when they've just stated their opinion strongly. But stating your opinion‚Äîeven passionately‚Äîisn't arguing for it.

An argument requires giving REASONS. If someone just piles up claims without connecting them logically, there's no argument.

Deductive Arguments

Deductive arguments aim to provide logically conclusive support for their conclusions.

Classic example:

All men are mortal.

Socrates is a man.

Therefore, Socrates is mortal.

If the premises are true, the conclusion must be true. This is called a valid argument.

×
Deductive Argument

An argument intended to give logically conclusive support to its conclusion. If successful, the truth of the premises guarantees the truth of the conclusion.

Key words: "necessarily," "it must be that," "it logically follows," "conclusively"
×
Valid Argument

A deductive argument that actually achieves what it's supposed to‚Äîthe conclusion really DOES follow logically from the premises.

Important: Validity is about FORM, not content. A valid argument can have false premises!

Valid but unsound: "All fish are purple. My cat is a fish. ‚à¥ My cat is purple." (Valid form, false premises!)

üìñ Understanding Deduction

Deductive arguments are "all or nothing." Either the conclusion follows necessarily (valid) or it doesn't (invalid). There's no middle ground.

The Socrates example is the classic form called a "syllogism." If both premises are true, there's NO WAY the conclusion could be false.

Inductive Arguments

Inductive arguments aim to provide probable support for their conclusions.

Example:

Almost all men at this college have high SAT scores.

Therefore, Julio (a male student here) probably has high SAT scores.

Even if the premise is true, the conclusion could be false. The argument is strong or weak.

×
Inductive Argument

An argument intended to give probable support to its conclusion. Even with true premises, the conclusion might be false‚Äîbut it's likely true.

Key words: "probably," "likely," "in all probability," "odds are," "chances are"
×
Strong Argument

An inductive argument where, if the premises are true, the conclusion is probably true (more likely than not).

Example: "90% of students passed. Maria is a student. Therefore, Maria probably passed." Strong!
×
Weak Argument

An inductive argument where, even if the premises are true, the conclusion is not probable.

Example: "20% of students passed. Maria is a student. Therefore, Maria probably passed." Weak!

üìñ Understanding Induction

Most real-world reasoning is inductive. We can't PROVE Julio has high SAT scores, but given the evidence, it's a reasonable bet.

Inductive arguments come in degrees‚Äîstrong, moderate, weak. Unlike deductive arguments, which are simply valid or invalid.

Valid vs. Sound

Valid

The conclusion logically follows from the premises

About FORM/structure

Sound

Valid AND all premises are actually true

About form AND content

Valid but NOT sound:

All cats are reptiles. (FALSE!)

Fluffy is a cat.

‚à¥ Fluffy is a reptile.

The logic works, but the first premise is false.

×
Valid (Deductive)

A deductive argument where the conclusion logically follows from the premises. IF the premises were true, the conclusion would HAVE to be true.

Remember: Validity doesn't guarantee truth‚Äîit guarantees logical connection.

×
Sound Argument

A deductive argument that is (1) valid AND (2) has all TRUE premises. A sound argument guarantees a true conclusion.

This is what we want! A sound argument proves its conclusion. An unsound argument proves nothing.

üìñ Critical Distinction

Students often confuse valid and sound. Remember: VALID = good logic. SOUND = good logic + true premises.

You can have perfect logic with false premises (valid but unsound). You need BOTH for a sound argument.

ü§î Check Your Understanding

Quick Quiz:

"All birds can fly. Penguins are birds. Therefore, penguins can fly."

Is this argument:

A) Valid and sound

B) Valid but not sound

C) Invalid

üìñ Answer: B) Valid but not sound

The LOGIC is valid‚ÄîIF all birds could fly, and penguins are birds, then penguins would fly. The conclusion follows from the premises.

But it's NOT sound because the first premise is FALSE. Not all birds can fly (penguins, ostriches, etc.).

Common Valid Forms

Modus Ponens (Affirming the Antecedent)

Form:

If p, then q.
p.
‚à¥ q.

Example:

If Maria walks to work, she'll be late.

She is walking to work.

‚à¥ She will be late.

This form is always valid‚Äîno matter what you plug in for p and q.

×
Modus Ponens
(MOH-dus POH-nenz) ‚Äî Latin: "mode of affirming"

A valid argument form where you affirm the "if" part (antecedent) of a conditional statement, allowing you to conclude the "then" part (consequent).

Memory aid: "If P then Q, and P is true, so Q must be true." You're "ponens-ing" (affirming) the antecedent.

üìñ The Workhorse of Logic

Modus ponens is probably the most common valid argument form. You use it constantly without realizing it.

"If it's raining, the ground is wet. It's raining. Therefore, the ground is wet." That's modus ponens!

Common Valid Forms

Modus Tollens (Denying the Consequent)

Form:

If p, then q.
Not q.
‚à¥ Not p.

Example:

If Maria walks to work, she'll be late.

She is not late.

‚à¥ She did not walk to work.

Also always valid. Denying the result lets you deny the cause.

×
Modus Tollens
(MOH-dus TAHL-enz) ‚Äî Latin: "mode of denying"

A valid argument form where you deny the "then" part (consequent), allowing you to conclude the "if" part (antecedent) is also false.

Memory aid: "If P then Q, but Q is false, so P must be false too." You're "tollens-ing" (denying) the consequent.

üìñ Reasoning Backwards

Modus tollens lets you reason "backwards" from effects to causes. If the effect didn't happen, the cause didn't happen (assuming the conditional is true).

Detective work often uses modus tollens: "If he was the killer, he'd have gunpowder residue. He doesn't. So he's not the killer."

Common INVALID Forms

Affirming the Consequent ‚ùå

Form:

If p, then q.
q.
‚à¥ p. ‚Üê INVALID!

Example:

If Maria walks, she'll be late.

She is late.

‚à¥ She walked. ‚ùå

Maybe she was late for another reason!

×
Affirming the Consequent

An INVALID argument form. Just because the "then" part is true doesn't mean the "if" part caused it. There could be other causes!

Why it fails: "If it rained, the ground is wet. The ground is wet. Therefore it rained." But maybe someone used a hose!

üìñ A Common Mistake

This is a VERY common logical error. Just because Q happened doesn't mean P caused it‚Äîother things could cause Q too.

Maria could be late because of traffic, oversleeping, or many other reasons besides walking.

Common INVALID Forms

Denying the Antecedent ‚ùå

Form:

If p, then q.
Not p.
‚à¥ Not q. ‚Üê INVALID!

Example:

If Maria walks, she'll be late.

She didn't walk.

‚à¥ She won't be late. ‚ùå

She could still be late for other reasons!

×
Denying the Antecedent

An INVALID argument form. Just because the "if" part didn't happen doesn't mean the "then" part won't happen. Other causes might produce the same result.

Why it fails: "If it rains, the ground is wet. It didn't rain. Therefore the ground isn't wet." But someone could have watered it!

üìñ Another Common Mistake

Just because one cause is absent doesn't mean the effect won't occur. There can be multiple sufficient causes for the same effect.

Maria not walking doesn't guarantee she'll be on time‚Äîthe bus could break down!

The Counterexample Method

How do you test if an argument form is valid? Use the counterexample method.

Test this argument:

If capital punishment deters crime, death row inmates will decrease.

But capital punishment doesn't deter crime.

‚à¥ Death row inmates will not decrease.

Counterexample (same form, obviously false conclusion):

If lizards are mammals, they have legs.

Lizards are NOT mammals.

‚à¥ Lizards don't have legs. ‚Üê FALSE!

True premises, false conclusion = invalid form.

×
Counterexample Method

A technique for testing validity: construct a "twin" argument with the SAME FORM but with obviously TRUE premises and an obviously FALSE conclusion.

If you can do this, the original form is INVALID (because valid forms can't have true premises and false conclusions).

üìñ A Powerful Tool

The counterexample method is based on a fundamental fact: a valid argument CANNOT have true premises and a false conclusion. If the form allows this, it's invalid.

The lizard example proves "denying the antecedent" is invalid‚Äîwe found true premises leading to a false conclusion.

Moral Statements in Arguments

A moral statement affirms that an action is right/wrong or a person/character is good/bad.

Moral Statements

"Capital punishment is wrong."

"Jena should not have lied."

"Cruelty to animals is immoral."

Nonmoral Statements

"Many people oppose capital punishment."

"Jena did not lie."

"Animals are treated cruelly."

üí° Key words: wrong, right, should, ought, good, bad, immoral, permissible, obligatory
×
Moral Statement

A statement affirming that an action is right or wrong, or that a person (or motive/character) is good or bad. It makes a moral evaluation.

Test: Does it say something SHOULD or SHOULDN'T be done? Or that something IS good/bad? Then it's moral.
×
Nonmoral Statement

A statement that describes facts without making moral evaluations. It says what IS the case, not what OUGHT to be.

Examples: "Most people oppose X" describes a fact about opinions. "X is wrong" makes a moral judgment. Big difference!

üìñ Crucial Distinction

This distinction matters because moral arguments have a special structure. You can't derive a moral conclusion from purely nonmoral premises!

Nonmoral statements describe how things ARE. Moral statements say how things OUGHT to be.

Structure of Moral Arguments

A typical moral argument needs at least one moral premise AND at least one nonmoral premise.

1. Committing a violent act to defend yourself is morally permissible. (Moral premise)

2. Assaulting someone attacking you is a violent act of self-defense. (Nonmoral premise)

3. Therefore, assaulting someone attacking you is morally permissible. (Moral conclusion)

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Rule: You cannot derive a moral conclusion from only nonmoral premises! You need at least one moral premise to "bridge the gap."

üìñ The Is-Ought Gap

This is sometimes called the "is-ought gap" or "Hume's guillotine." You can't get from "is" statements alone to "ought" statements.

The moral premise provides the general principle. The nonmoral premise shows this case falls under that principle. Together they yield the moral conclusion.

Implied Premises

Many arguments have implied (unstated) premises‚Äîand these are often the most controversial!

Stated argument:

1. Capital punishment does not deter crime.

2. Therefore, capital punishment is immoral.

Wait‚Äîthere's a logical gap!

With implied premise made explicit:

1. Administering a punishment that doesn't deter crime is immoral. (implied)

2. Capital punishment does not deter crime.

3. Therefore, capital punishment is immoral.

×
Implied Premise

A premise that is assumed but not explicitly stated. Often the missing premise is a moral principle that connects the stated facts to the moral conclusion.

Why this matters: The unstated premises are often the most questionable! Making them explicit lets you evaluate them.

üìñ Finding Hidden Assumptions

When you see a logical gap‚Äîthe conclusion doesn't follow from the stated premises‚Äîlook for the implied premise that would fill the gap.

Often the implied premise is the WEAKEST part of the argument. The person may not have stated it because it's controversial!

Testing Moral Premises

How do you evaluate moral premises? Use counterexamples!

Moral premise to test:

"Causing the death of an incapacitated person is wrong."

Counterexamples that challenge it:

  • What about self-defense? (killing to save your own life)
  • What about war? (soldiers killing enemy combatants)
  • What about to save others? (killing one to save many)

Counterexamples force us to refine the moral premise to handle exceptions.

×
Counterexample (for Moral Premises)

A case that seems to violate the moral principle but doesn't seem wrong‚Äîforcing us to revise the principle.

Process: 1) State the principle. 2) Find a case it would condemn. 3) Ask if that case really IS wrong. 4) If not, revise the principle.

üìñ Refining Moral Principles

Counterexamples don't always destroy a moral premise‚Äîsometimes they help us state it more precisely.

"Causing death is wrong" becomes "Causing death is wrong, except in self-defense, to save lives, etc." The revised principle is stronger because it handles objections.

ü§î Check Your Understanding

Identify the Implied Premise:

"Abortion kills a human being. Therefore, abortion is wrong."

What unstated premise would make this argument valid? Is that premise true?

üìñ Answer

Implied premise: "Killing a human being is wrong."

This makes the argument valid. But is the premise TRUE without exceptions? What about self-defense? War? The death penalty? The debate shifts to whether the general principle holds‚Äîand that's where the real disagreement lies!

Avoiding Bad Arguments

Fallacies are common patterns of bad reasoning. Learning them helps you avoid being fooled!

  • Fallacies are often persuasive even though flawed
  • They appear frequently in moral debates
  • Knowing them helps you spot weaknesses in arguments
üì∏ Image: "warning sign critical thinking logical fallacy trap"
Warning sign representing logical traps to avoid
×
Fallacy

A common form of bad argument. Fallacies are often psychologically persuasive but logically flawed‚Äîthey don't actually support their conclusions.

Why study them? They're EVERYWHERE in political debates, advertising, social media, and everyday conversations. Knowing them is intellectual self-defense!

üìñ Why Fallacies Matter

Fallacies are named because they're so common. People fall for them constantly‚Äîand use them constantly.

The best defense against being manipulated by bad arguments is knowing what bad arguments look like.

Fallacy: Begging the Question

Begging the question means assuming what you're trying to prove.

Example:

"Women in Muslim countries have the right to vote because all women in Muslim countries have the right to vote."

The conclusion just restates the premise!

Circular arguments prove nothing‚Äîthey assume the very thing in question.

×
Begging the Question
(also called "circular reasoning")

Using a statement as both a premise AND the conclusion of an argument. The argument says, in effect, "P is true because P is true."

Note: In everyday speech, "begging the question" often means "raising the question." But in logic, it means circular reasoning!

üìñ Sneaky Circularity

Circular arguments are sometimes disguised by using different words for the same claim. "God exists because the Bible says so, and the Bible is true because it's God's word."

Fallacy: Equivocation

Equivocation uses the same word with different meanings.

Example:

1. A fetus is an individual that is indisputably human. (= has human DNA)

2. A human is endowed with rights that cannot be invalidated. (= a person)

3. Therefore, a fetus has rights.

"Human" means different things in premises 1 and 2!

×
Equivocation

Using the same term with two different meanings in an argument, making it appear that the conclusion follows when it doesn't.

Defense: When a key term appears in multiple premises, ask: "Is it being used the same way each time?"

üìñ Slippery Words

In the example, "human" in premise 1 means "biologically human" (having human DNA). In premise 2, it means "a person with moral status." These aren't the same!

The argument LOOKS valid but equivocates on "human."

Fallacy: Appeal to Authority

Appeal to authority relies on someone who isn't actually an expert on the topic.

Example:

"This famous actor says vaccines are dangerous, so we shouldn't vaccinate our children."

Being famous doesn't make someone a medical expert!

üí° When is authority legitimate? When the person IS an expert in the relevant field AND experts in that field generally agree.
×
Appeal to Authority

Relying on someone's opinion as evidence when they're not actually an expert on the topic, or when experts disagree.

Two rules: 1) Cite experts who are experts IN THE RELEVANT FIELD. 2) If experts disagree, that's reason for doubt.

üìñ Expertise Is Domain-Specific

A Nobel Prize winner in physics isn't automatically an authority on economics. Celebrity status doesn't confer expertise on anything.

Legitimate appeals to authority cite people who have actual expertise in the relevant area AND whose views represent expert consensus.

Fallacy: Straw Man

The straw man fallacy misrepresents someone's argument to make it easier to attack.

Example:

Person A: "I think our professional ethics code should be secular so all members can use it."

Person B: "A obviously wants to strip religion from our profession and banish faith from ethics entirely. We can't let this happen!"

B attacks a distorted version of A's actual position.

×
Straw Man

Misrepresenting someone's claim or argument‚Äîmaking it seem more extreme or ridiculous‚Äîthen attacking the distorted version instead of what they actually said.

Why "straw man"? A straw man is easier to knock down than a real person. Similarly, a distorted argument is easier to refute than the real one.

üìñ Fight Fair

The straw man is extremely common in political debates. People often attack extreme versions of their opponents' views rather than what they actually believe.

Good reasoning requires engaging with the STRONGEST version of opposing arguments, not the weakest.

Fallacy: Appeal to Emotion

Appeal to emotion substitutes feelings for reasons.

Example:

"The defendant is obviously guilty. Look at him in the courtroom‚Äîhe's terrifying and menacing. And think of the poor victim's parents..."

Feelings of fear and pity aren't evidence of guilt!

Note: Emotions aren't always irrelevant‚Äîsometimes they ARE pertinent to moral questions. The fallacy is using emotions instead of reasons.

×
Appeal to Emotion

Trying to convince someone to accept a conclusion by appealing only to emotions (fear, guilt, anger, pity, etc.) rather than providing relevant reasons.

Key question: Are the emotions being evoked actually relevant to whether the claim is TRUE?

üìñ Emotions Have Their Place

The textbook makes an important distinction: emotions CAN be relevant to moral questions. If we're discussing whether an action caused suffering, empathy for victims is relevant.

The fallacy occurs when emotions are used as a SUBSTITUTE for reasoning, not as a COMPONENT of it.

Fallacy: Slippery Slope

The slippery slope fallacy claims one action will inevitably lead to disaster‚Äîwithout evidence.

Example:

"If we legalize marijuana, soon everyone will be using hard drugs, society will collapse, and we'll have anarchy in the streets."

Where's the evidence that this chain of events would actually happen?

üí° Not always fallacious! If there's good evidence the chain of events would occur, it's a legitimate argument.
×
Slippery Slope

Arguing that one action will inevitably lead to a chain of negative consequences, without providing evidence that this chain would actually occur.

When it's NOT a fallacy: If you can provide evidence that each step in the chain is likely, the argument may be sound. The fallacy is assuming the slide without evidence.

üìñ Show Your Work

The problem with slippery slope arguments isn't that chain reactions never happen‚Äîsometimes they do! The problem is ASSUMING they'll happen without evidence.

If you claim A leads to B leads to C leads to disaster, you need to show each link is probable.

Fallacy: Appeal to the Person

The ad hominem fallacy attacks the person making an argument rather than the argument itself.

Examples:

"We should reject Alice's argument about taxes‚Äîshe's a libertarian."

"Jerome says we should give more to charity, but he's rich. Ignore him."

"Maria argues for animal rights, but she owns a fur coat. Hypocrite!"

A claim must stand or fall on its own merits‚Äînot the characteristics of who makes it.

×
Ad Hominem
(ad HOM-ih-nem) ‚Äî Latin: "to the person"

Rejecting a claim based on characteristics of the person who makes it (their politics, wealth, character, motives, consistency) rather than addressing the argument itself.

Remember: Bad people can make good arguments. Good people can make bad arguments. Evaluate the ARGUMENT, not the person.

üìñ Play the Ball, Not the Player

Someone's political affiliation, wealth, or even hypocrisy doesn't make their argument wrong. A hypocritical doctor who smokes can still be right that smoking is unhealthy.

The argument must be evaluated on its own merits‚Äîthe quality of reasoning and evidence, not who's offering it.

Fallacy: Hasty Generalization

Hasty generalization draws broad conclusions from too few examples.

Example:

"I met two people from France who were rude. Therefore, French people are rude."

Two people can't represent an entire nation!

Another example:

"Three pro-life demonstrators were arrested for assault. So pro-lifers are lawbreakers."

Three people can't represent millions!

×
Hasty Generalization

Drawing a conclusion about an entire group based on too small or unrepresentative a sample.

Key question: Is the sample large enough AND representative enough to support the general claim?

üìñ Sample Size Matters

This is essentially a bad inductive argument. The sample is too small or biased to support the generalization.

Stereotypes often come from hasty generalizations‚Äîencountering a few members of a group and assuming they represent everyone.

Cognitive Biases

Cognitive biases are mental shortcuts that lead us astray.

Confirmation Bias

Seeking evidence that supports our beliefs while ignoring evidence against them.

Availability Error

Overweighting vivid, memorable examples over reliable statistics.

Motivated Reasoning

Reasoning to support what we want to believe, not to find truth.

Dunning-Kruger Effect

The less we know, the more confident we are. Ignorance of our own ignorance.

×
Cognitive Bias

A systematic pattern of deviation from rationality. Our brains take shortcuts that often work but sometimes lead us badly astray.

×
Confirmation Bias

The tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information that confirms our existing beliefs, while ignoring contradictory evidence.

Antidote: Actively seek out evidence AGAINST your beliefs. If you can't find any, look harder!
×
Availability Error

Judging probability based on how easily examples come to mind, rather than actual statistics. Vivid events (plane crashes) seem more common than they are.

Example: After seeing news about a shark attack, people overestimate shark danger (very rare) while underestimating car accidents (very common).
×
Motivated Reasoning

Starting with a conclusion you want to reach, then finding "reasons" to support it‚Äîrather than following evidence wherever it leads.

Warning sign: If you find yourself only accepting evidence for views you already hold, you may be engaged in motivated reasoning.
×
Dunning-Kruger Effect

People with low ability at a task overestimate their ability, while experts often underestimate theirs. We don't know what we don't know.

Implication: The most confident people in the room may be the least informed. True expertise often comes with humility.

üìñ Know Your Brain's Weaknesses

These biases affect EVERYONE‚Äîeven (especially?) smart people. Awareness is the first step to counteracting them.

The textbook emphasizes: to think critically, we must fight against our own psychological tendencies toward sloppy reasoning.

ü§î Identify the Fallacy

What fallacy is being committed?

"Professor Smith argues that we should increase the minimum wage. But Professor Smith is a socialist, so we shouldn't listen to her."

A) Straw man

B) Ad hominem (appeal to the person)

C) Hasty generalization

D) Slippery slope

üìñ Answer: B) Ad hominem

The argument attacks Professor Smith's political views rather than addressing her actual argument about minimum wage.

Even if she IS a socialist, that doesn't make her argument wrong. Her argument should be evaluated on its own merits.
