ðŸ“– Welcome to Chapter 9

This chapter is different from the previous ones. Instead of introducing a new moral theory, it synthesizes what we've learned and provides a practical framework for making moral decisions.

Think of this as your "ethics toolkit"â€”a systematic approach you can apply to real moral dilemmas you'll face in life.

Why We Need a Framework

  • The theories we've studiedâ€”utilitarianism, Kant, natural law, virtue ethicsâ€”each offer invaluable moral insights
  • But each theory also overlooks at least one important feature of morality
  • No single theory has proven entirely adequate for providing moral guidance
The Problem: Our best theories have been "found wanting" because they conflict with our considered moral judgments in some way.

ðŸ“– The Limits of Single Theories

After studying several major moral theories, you might be wondering: "Which one is right?" The honest answer is that none of them is perfect.

Utilitarianism sometimes leads to conclusions that violate our sense of justice. Kant's theory can produce rigid results in hard cases. Virtue ethics doesn't always tell us what to do. Each theory captures something important but misses something else.

This chapter offers a different approach: instead of committing to one theory, we'll develop a framework that draws on the strengths of multiple approaches.

The Role of Considered Moral Judgments

  • Assessments about moral dilemmas made after careful reflection
  • As clear and unbiased as possibleâ€”what some call "moral common sense"
  • Fallible and revisable, but they constitute credible evidence for moral principles
Examples of Considered Judgments

Wantonly killing people is wrong. Slavery is a moral abomination. Equals must be treated equally. Respecting innocent people's rights is morally required. Inflicting undeserved suffering is evil.

ðŸ“– Starting with What We Know

Considered moral judgments are our starting point. These are strong moral convictions we hold after careful reflectionâ€”not knee-jerk reactions or prejudices, but thoughtful conclusions about clear cases.

When a moral theory conflicts with these judgments, we have good reason to question the theory. If a theory told us slavery was morally acceptable, we'd reject the theory rather than accept slavery.

This doesn't mean our judgments are infallibleâ€”they can be revised. But they serve as important data that any adequate moral theory must explain.

Theory Shapes Judgment, Judgment Tests Theory

  • Moral common sense is the starting point of our theorizing and the corroboration of what we learn
  • When a theory conflicts with common sense, we may revise either the theory OR our judgment
  • The goal: achieve the closest possible agreement between theory and considered judgment

Theory â†’ Judgment

A good theory can show us our judgment should be revised

Judgment â†’ Theory

Strong judgments can show a theory is flawed

ðŸ“– A Two-Way Street

Moral reasoning is a dialogue between principles and judgments. Sometimes a good argument shows us we should change our minds about a particular case. Other times, our strong intuitions about a case show us something is wrong with a principle.

For example: utilitarianism might convince us that our judgment about a particular case was wrong because we weren't considering everyone's welfare equally. But our firm judgment that torturing innocents is wrong might show us that pure utilitarianism needs modification.

The philosopher John Rawls called this process "reflective equilibrium"â€”moving back and forth until our principles and judgments cohere.

Absolute vs. Prima Facie Principles

Absolute Principles

Rules that must always be followed with no exceptions

Problem: Can lead to terrible outcomes in unusual cases

Prima Facie Principles

Principles that apply unless overridden by a weightier duty

Advantage: Allows flexibility while maintaining moral structure

Key Insight: Our moral common sense tells us that duties can be overridden by weightier ones, and that prima facie duties remain important even when overridden.

ðŸ“– Why Prima Facie Works Better

Remember Kant's example from Chapter 6: if you must tell the truth even when a murderer asks where your friend is hiding, that seems too rigid. Our considered judgment says saving a life can override the duty not to lie.

Prima facie (Latin for "at first appearance") principles give us the best of both worlds. They're strong guidelines that apply in most situations, but they can be overridden when a more important duty conflicts with them.

Importantly, when a prima facie duty is overridden, it doesn't disappear. You might still feel regret or owe an explanation, because the duty was real even though it was outweighed.

Principlism

  • A moral theory consisting of multiple prima facie principles
  • Principles must be weighed and balanced against each other
  • Based on our considered moral judgments
  • Avoids the rigidity of absolute rules while maintaining moral structure
The Three Core Principles

This chapter proposes three fundamental prima facie principles that can cover most moral duties: Respect, Justice, and Beneficence.

ðŸ“– A Workable Framework

Principlism offers a practical middle ground. It acknowledges that morality is complex and can't be reduced to a single rule, but it still provides structure through a small set of fundamental principles.

The philosopher W.D. Ross (1877â€“1967) first developed the idea of prima facie duties, arguing there were at least seven. More recent philosophers have argued we can work with fewer, more general principles.

This chapter focuses on three: respect for persons, justice, and beneficence. These three can accommodate most of the moral concerns we've discussed throughout the course.

Principle 1: Respect

  • The guiding value of Kant's theory and other nonconsequentialist approaches
  • Respect is owed to all persons because they have intrinsic worth and dignity
  • Persons must be treated as ends in themselves, never merely as means

Negative Rights

Rights that obligate others not to interfere

Positive Rights

Rights that obligate others to help us obtain something

ðŸ“– The Foundation of Rights

The principle of respect captures what Kant was getting at: people aren't just instruments to be used for others' purposes. Every person has inherent dignity and deserves to be treated accordingly.

This principle generates rightsâ€”both negative (don't interfere with my autonomy, don't lie to me, don't coerce me) and positive (help me when I'm in dire need). In this chapter, positive rights fall under beneficence.

The principle of respect prohibits lying, cheating, coercing, harassing, falsely imprisoning, and manipulating peopleâ€”all violations of their dignity as persons.

Principle 2: Justice

  • Requires that persons be treated fairly and given what they are due
  • Reflects a central fact about moral life: morality requires impartiality
  • Equals must be treated equally in relevantly similar situations

Retributive Justice

Fair use of punishment for wrongdoing

Distributive Justice

Fair distribution of benefits and burdens in society

Why Justice Matters: Racial discrimination is wrong precisely because it treats one group differently without morally relevant reasons.

ðŸ“– Fairness and Equal Treatment

The principle of justice captures our strong intuitions about fairness. It's wrong to treat people differently based on morally irrelevant factors like race, gender, or social class.

Justice is connected to respect but distinct from it. Respect focuses on treating people as ends; justice focuses on treating similar cases similarly. Both can override concerns about consequences.

This is one of the strongest criticisms of pure utilitarianism: it might permit gross injustice if the overall utility is positive. The principle of justice reminds us that how benefits and burdens are distributed matters, not just their total amount.

Principle 3: Beneficence

The proper way to account for consequences in moral decision-making.

Three Dimensions of Beneficence

1. Nonmaleficence

Do not deliberately harm others (kill, disable, rob, terrorize)

2. Prevent/Remove Harm

Act to prevent harm, remove evil, and promote good

3. Maximize Good

Strive for the most favorable balance of good over evil

ðŸ“– Taking Consequences Seriously

Beneficence is where consequentialist considerations enter the framework. We do have duties related to outcomesâ€”duties not to harm, duties to help, and (in some cases) duties to maximize good.

But notice these are THREE separate elements, not one unified principle. The duty not to harm (nonmaleficence) is usually stronger than the duty to help. And we're not always required to maximize goodâ€”that would be too demanding.

This nuanced approach captures what's right about utilitarianism (consequences matter) while avoiding its excesses (we're not required to sacrifice everything for maximum utility).

There's also an element of respectâ€”using customers as mere means to profit while knowingly exposing them to risk. And justiceâ€”if only some customers are harmed, they bear an unfair burden.

But the core conflict is beneficence vs. self-interest. The principlist framework would generally say that the duty to prevent serious harm outweighs profit considerations, especially when the company created the risk.

When Principles Conflict

  • In the toughest moral cases, principles will conflict
  • There is no stable formula for assigning weights to principles
  • We have only generalizations to guide us
Key Generalization

We usually have a stronger duty not to violate rights (harm people) than we do to bring them benefit. Your duty not to steal from your brother is stronger than your duty to help him with homework.

Remember: Merely "having a feeling" that one principle overrides another is not a reliable basis for moral decisions. Weighting should be grounded in reason and revealed by considered moral judgments.

ðŸ“– The Hard Work of Ethics

This is where ethics gets difficult. When principles conflict, there's no algorithm that automatically produces the right answer. We have to think carefully, consider the specific circumstances, and make a judgment.

But we're not completely without guidance. Some generalizations hold across most cases: duties not to harm are usually stronger than duties to benefit. Duties to those we have special relationships with (family, promises) are usually stronger than duties to strangers. And the more serious the harm or benefit, the more weight that consideration carries.

The key is to be able to give REASONS for thinking one principle outweighs another in a particular case.

Duties and Supererogation

  • Our duty to benefit others does not demand that we help all persons
  • We have stronger duties to family, friends, and those close to us
  • We are not obligated to make extreme sacrifices for strangers

The Duty of Rescue

We may have a duty to save a drowning stranger if we can do so without extreme risk or cost to ourselves. But beneficence doesn't require you to risk your own life for a stranger.

Supererogatory Acts

Actions that go above and beyond the call of duty. Heroic self-sacrifice is admirable, but not morally required.

ðŸ“– The Limits of Duty

This is an important corrective to some versions of utilitarianism. We're not morally required to devote every waking moment to maximizing good in the world. We have lives of our own, relationships that matter, personal projects we're entitled to pursue.

A "supererogatory" act is one that's morally praiseworthy but not required. Running into a burning building to save a stranger is heroic, but we wouldn't say someone was immoral for not doing it.

This connects to the ethics of care: we DO have stronger duties to those we have relationships with. You're not wrong to prioritize your own children over strangers, even if utilitarian calculus might suggest otherwise.

A Framework for Moral Decision-Making

1Determine What Principles Apply

Is this about justice, respect, avoiding harm, providing benefit? Which norms are relevant, and are they in conflict?

2Weigh and Balance the Principles

Assess relative importance by assigning weights with good reasons. Consider considered moral judgments as a guide.

3Choose the Right Action

Determine which duty holds the most weight, then decide how to implement that action morally and practically.

ðŸ“– Your Ethical Toolkit

This three-step process gives you a systematic approach to moral dilemmas. Let's walk through it:

Step 1: First, analyze the situation. What's at stake? Whose rights or interests are involved? Which of our three principles (respect, justice, beneficence) are relevant? Often multiple principles apply, and they may point in different directions.

Step 2: This is the hardest step. When principles conflict, you need to weigh them against each other. Use generalizations (harm avoidance usually trumps benefit provision) and your considered moral judgments about similar cases.

Step 3: Once you've identified the weightiest duty, you still need to figure out how to fulfill it. Sometimes there are further questions about the best way to act.

Step 1: Determine What Principles Apply

  • Ask: Is this decision about doing justice, respecting persons, avoiding harm, or providing benefit?
  • Often multiple principles are relevant
  • Identify if the principles are in conflict

Example: Loyalty vs. Justice

Your friend is on trial and you must testify under oath about what you knowâ€”which will incriminate them.

Principles at play: Duty of fidelity to friend vs. duty of justice (honesty, respecting legal process)

ðŸ“– Identifying the Moral Landscape

The first step is diagnosis: what kind of moral situation is this? Sometimes it's straightforwardâ€”a question purely about fairness, or purely about preventing harm. But often multiple principles intersect.

In the testimony example, you face a conflict between your loyalty to your friend (a duty of special relationship) and your duty to be honest and support the justice system. Neither principle simply disappears because the other exists.

Being explicit about what principles are in play prevents you from overlooking important moral considerations.

Step 2: Weigh and Balance the Principles

  • Assess the relative importance of conflicting principles
  • Assign weights and justify those assignments with good reasons
  • Use considered moral judgments as your guide

Example: Alicia and the Grandfather

Alicia promised to take her wheelchair-bound grandfather to a movie, but her boss asks her to work late on a critical project.

Weighing: How serious is grandfather's disappointment vs. job/company consequences? Is this a pattern of broken promises? What are the stakes on each side?

ðŸ“– The Art of Moral Weighing

This step requires careful thinking about the specific circumstances. Moral principles are general, but real situations have particulars that matter.

In Alicia's case, relevant factors include: How important is this project? How often has she broken promises to her grandfather? How much will he be hurt? Does she have alternatives (reschedule the movie, delegate the work)?

There's no formula, but there are relevant considerations. The goal is to give reasons that others could recognize as legitimate, not just assert that one duty "feels" more important.

Step 3: Choose the Right Action

  • Determine which moral duty holds the most weight
  • Even after choosing, questions remain about how to implement the action
  • Implementation may involve further moral considerations

Example: The Lottery Winner

You win $500,000 and decide to donate it to a homeless shelter. But:

  • Which shelter will use the funds most effectively?
  • Lump sum or installments?
  • Is it fair to give nothing to your own needy family?

ðŸ“– From Decision to Action

Even after you've identified the right general course of action, there may be further questions about how to do it well. These implementation questions can themselves involve moral considerations.

In the lottery example, once you've decided to donate, you still face questions about HOW to donate effectively and fairly. These aren't trivialâ€”a poorly implemented good intention can produce less good (or even harm).

This reminds us that moral decision-making is often an ongoing process, not a single choice.

Self-Evident Moral Truths

  • Some argue our basic moral principles are self-evidentâ€”justified merely by understanding them
  • Like "Whatever has a shape has a size" or "No bachelors are married"
  • We know them through reason and reflection, not empirical investigation

Examples of self-evident moral beliefs:

  • Equals should be treated equally
  • It is wrong to punish the innocent
  • It is wrong to inflict unnecessary suffering
  • It is wrong to torture people for fun

ðŸ“– Are Some Moral Truths Obvious?

This addresses a deep question: where do moral principles come from? Some philosophers argue that at least some moral truths are self-evidentâ€”once you understand what they mean, you can see they're true.

"It is wrong to torture innocent people for fun" seems to be like this. You don't need to run experiments or gather evidence. If someone claims not to see that it's wrong, we suspect they don't fully understand what torture, innocence, or wrongness mean.

This gives our basic moral principles a firm foundation. We're not just making them up or following arbitrary conventionsâ€”we're recognizing truths about how persons ought to be treated.

Sample Case 1: Confidentiality vs. Duty to Warn

Dr. Samsara's Dilemma

Dr. Samsara is a psychologist bound by professional confidentiality. Her patient Eric confides that he intends to kill a student named Alicia who ended their relationship. Dr. Samsara believes the threat is serious.

What should she do?

Duty of Respect

Patients have a right to confidentiality

Duty of Beneficence

Prevent serious harm to others when possible

ðŸ“– Applying the Framework

Step 1: Two principles conflictâ€”respect (confidentiality) vs. beneficence (preventing serious harm).

Step 2: The duty to prevent serious harm (murder) is weightier than the duty of confidentiality. While confidentiality is important, it's not absoluteâ€”especially when breaking it could save a life.

Step 3: Dr. Samsara should warn Alicia and possibly authorities. The case is based on the real Tarasoff case, which established that therapists have a "duty to warn" potential victims.

Dr. Samsara in the example chose confidentiality, Alicia died, and we judge her choice as wrongâ€”showing how considered moral judgments inform our framework.

Sample Case 2: Making Money While Hurting People

OncoSystem's Price Hike

OncoSystem develops ProChem, the only effective treatment for a rare leukemia. The drug costs $20/tablet. New CEO Harold Morgan raises the price to $500â€”a 2,500% increase. Most patients can no longer afford it.

What principles apply?

  • Fidelity to investors: Morgan has a duty to maximize shareholder returns
  • Beneficence: Duty not to harm those who need the drug
  • Respect: Not to use patients merely as means to profit

ðŸ“– Analysis

This case involves multiple principles. Morgan has legitimate duties to investors. But he also has duties not to harm vulnerable people and not to exploit their desperate situation.

The price gouging violates beneficence: he's causing suffering and potentially death by making a life-saving drug unaffordable. It also violates respect: he's treating sick people merely as means to profit.

Whatever loyalty Morgan owes investors is outweighed by the stronger duties not to harm people and not to exploit them. A reasonable profit is one thing; extreme exploitation of the vulnerable is another.

Step 2: Initially, trying a conversation might satisfy both duties. But if Marcela doesn't change and accidents continue, the duty to prevent harm grows weightier.

Step 3: The action might be stagedâ€”first conversation, then escalation if needed. This shows that moral decision-making isn't always all-or-nothing.

The Inherent Messiness of Moral Life

  • There is no avoiding the inherent messiness of moral decision-making
  • The principlist framework is not perfectâ€”no theory is
  • But it offers a reliable method for minimizing arbitrariness and taking essential moral principles into account

"The hard truth about moral theorizing is that it never seems to result in a widely accepted, complete, or unblemished theory. The proposed framework presented here is no exception to the rule, but it does have the advantage of incorporating what seem to be the most manifest and least questionable elements of the moral life."

ðŸ“– Embracing Complexity

This is an honest assessment. Ethics doesn't give us an algorithm that automatically produces right answers. Real moral dilemmas are hard precisely because they involve genuine conflicts between legitimate values.

But having a framework is still better than having nothing. It helps us think systematically, ensures we don't overlook important considerations, and gives us a way to explain and justify our decisions.

The goal isn't moral certaintyâ€”it's thoughtful, well-reasoned moral judgment.
