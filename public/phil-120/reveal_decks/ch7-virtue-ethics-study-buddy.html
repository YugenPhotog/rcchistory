ðŸ“– Welcome to Virtue Ethics

This chapter introduces you to a fundamentally different way of thinking about ethics. Instead of asking "What should I do?" (like utilitarians) or "What is my duty?" (like Kantians), virtue ethics asks "What kind of person should I be?"

Think of it this way: consequentialists give you a calculator for ethics, Kantians give you a rulebookâ€”but virtue ethicists hand you a mirror and ask you to examine your own character.

The speaker notes throughout this deck will help you understand not just what these concepts mean, but why they matter and how to apply them.

A Different Moral Question

Other Theories Ask:

"What should I do?"

Focus on actions, rules, consequences

Virtue Ethics Asks:

"What should I be?"

Focus on character, habits, excellence

Key Insight: Moral conduct flows naturally from moral characterâ€”not from following rules or calculating outcomes.

ðŸ“– The Fundamental Shift

Here's the core insight that makes virtue ethics distinctive: while other moral theories focus on what you should do in specific situations, virtue ethics focuses on who you should become as a person.

Think about someone you deeply admire morallyâ€”maybe a grandparent, a mentor, or a historical figure. Do you admire them because they followed rules really well? Or because they calculated consequences accurately? Probably not. You admire them because of who they AREâ€”their character traits like honesty, compassion, courage, or wisdom.

This is the virtue ethicist's insight: if you become a genuinely good person, good actions will flow naturally from your character. You won't need to consult a rulebook or do mental math about consequences.

What is Virtue Ethics?

  • A moral theory that makes virtue the central concern
  • Moral conduct is supposed to radiate naturally from moral virtues
  • Actions are derived from virtues, not from rules or consequences
  • Character is more fundamental than individual actions
The Core Claim

To act morally is to act from virtueâ€”from deeply embedded character traits that reflect excellence.

ðŸ“– Understanding Virtue Ethics

Virtue ethics represents one of the oldest approaches to moral philosophy, dating back to ancient Greece. But it's not just historically interestingâ€”it captures something many people find intuitively appealing about morality.

The key idea is that moral actions aren't just about following the right rules or producing the right outcomesâ€”they're about expressing the right kind of character. A truly honest person doesn't avoid lying because they calculated it would produce bad consequences, or because a rule tells them not to. They're honest because honesty is part of who they are.

Think about the difference between someone who helps you reluctantly because they think it's their duty, versus someone who helps you warmly because they genuinely care. Both might do the same action, but virtue ethics says there's a moral difference in how they do it.

ðŸ“– Meet Aristotle

Aristotle is arguably the most influential philosopher in Western history, and his ethical writings remain remarkably relevant today. While most modern virtue ethicists adapt or critique his views, virtually all of them engage with his framework.

What made Aristotle's approach distinctive was how he connected ethics to his broader understanding of the world. For Aristotle, understanding how to live well required understanding human nature, the nature of happiness, the role of habits, and how communities shape individuals.

His key ethical work, the Nicomachean Ethics (named for his son Nicomachus), remains one of the most studied texts in moral philosophy. If you want to understand virtue ethics deeply, Aristotle is the place to start.

The Goal: Eudaimonia

  • Every living being has an end toward which it naturally aims
  • Life is teleological: meant to be something and aspire toward something
  • For humans, the greatest good is eudaimoniaâ€”happiness, flourishing, the good life
  • Not mere pleasure, but the full realization of the good life

"Happiness is an activity of the soul in accordance with complete or perfect virtue."

â€” Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics

ðŸ“– What Is Eudaimonia?

Eudaimonia (pronounced you-dye-MOAN-ee-uh) is often translated as "happiness," but this is misleading. The English word "happiness" often suggests a feelingâ€”like pleasure or contentment. Eudaimonia is something deeper.

Think of it as "flourishing" or "living well and doing well." It's not about feeling good in the moment; it's about living a complete, excellent human life. Someone might be sick, or facing hardship, and still be living a eudaimonic life if they're exhibiting virtue and excellence in how they handle those challenges.

Aristotle's view is teleologicalâ€”everything has a purpose or function. An acorn's purpose is to become an oak tree. A knife's purpose is to cut well. What's a human being's purpose? To live fully in accordance with reasonâ€”and that means cultivating virtue.

How Do We Achieve Eudaimonia?

  • Fulfill the function distinctive to human beings
  • What's distinctive about humans? Reason
  • The life of reason entails a life of virtue
  • Virtues are rational modes of behaving
The Connection

The virtuous life helps human beings achieve true happiness AND is the realization of true happiness. Virtues make you good AND help you live a good life.

ðŸ“– The Path to Flourishing

Here's Aristotle's elegant argument: To flourish as anything, you need to fulfill your function well. What's the distinctive function of human beings? It's not just living (plants do that) or sensing (animals do that)â€”it's living according to reason.

So human flourishing means exercising our capacity for reason excellently. And what does that look like? It means developing virtuesâ€”stable character traits that guide us to act and feel appropriately in various situations.

Notice the beautiful symmetry: virtues both LEAD TO happiness (they help you achieve the good life) and CONSTITUTE happiness (being virtuous IS part of what it means to live well). It's not that you develop virtues as a means to some separate goal called happinessâ€”the virtuous life IS the happy life.

What is a Virtue?

  • A stable disposition to act and feel according to an ideal or model of excellence
  • A deeply embedded character trait that affects countless situations
  • Not just about actionsâ€”includes motivations and feelings

ðŸ“– Understanding Virtues

A virtue is not just a one-time action or a passing feelingâ€”it's a stable part of who you are. Think of honesty as a virtue: an honest person doesn't just happen to tell the truth occasionally. They're reliably honest across many situations because truthfulness has become part of their character.

Aristotle makes an important distinction between two types of virtues. Intellectual virtues (like wisdom and prudence) can be taught through instructionâ€”you can learn them in a classroom. But moral virtues (like courage or fairness) can only be developed through practice and habit.

This is huge: you can't become courageous by reading about courage. You become courageous by doing courageous things repeatedly until courage becomes second nature. As Aristotle famously put it, we become builders by building, and we become virtuous by doing virtuous acts.

Virtues Are Learned Through Practice

"[M]oral virtue comes about as a result of habit.... [N]one of the moral virtues arises in us by nature.... [W]e get [the virtues] by first exercising them... we become builders by building and lyreplayers by playing the lyre; so too we become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts."

â€” Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics

  • Intellectual virtues: taught like math or logic
  • Moral virtues: learned only through repeated practice
  • Character is formed through habit over time

ðŸ“– Practice Makes Virtuous

This is one of Aristotle's most practical insights. Think about learning to play an instrument or a sport. You don't become good at basketball by reading about itâ€”you become good by practicing, by actually shooting baskets thousands of times until the motion becomes natural.

Virtue works the same way. You can't become honest just by deciding to be honest one day. You become honest by practicing honesty repeatedly, in small ways and big ways, until telling the truth becomes your default response.

This has important implications for moral education. You can't just teach children rules and expect them to be virtuous. You have to give them opportunities to practice virtuous behavior, to develop good habits, until virtue becomes part of who they are.

The Golden Mean

  • A moral virtue is the midpoint between two behavioral extremes
  • One extreme is excess, the other is deficit
  • The virtuous life is a life of moderation

Example: Courage

Excess: Foolhardiness/Recklessness (too much)

Virtue: Courage (the mean)

Deficit: Cowardice (too little)

Remember: The "mean" isn't mathematicalâ€”it's relative to the situation and the person. The courageous action for a trained soldier differs from the courageous action for a civilian.

ðŸ“– Finding the Middle Path

The Golden Mean is one of Aristotle's most famous ideas, and it's often misunderstood. He's NOT saying you should be mediocre or wishy-washy. He's saying that virtues hit a sweet spot between having too much of something and too little.

Take courage: the coward has too little (they flee from every danger), the reckless person has too much (they charge into danger without thinking). The courageous person hits the right balanceâ€”they face genuine dangers when it's appropriate, but they don't throw their life away foolishly.

Importantly, the mean isn't a mathematical midpoint. It varies by situation and person. What's courageous for a firefighter might be reckless for an untrained person. Practical wisdom (phronesis) helps us find the right balance in each situation.

ðŸ“– Answer: B

B) Excess: Wastefulness | Deficit: Stinginess

Generosity is the virtue of giving appropriately. If you give too much (more than you should, without regard for your own needs or the worthiness of recipients), that's wastefulness or prodigalityâ€”an excess. If you give too little and hoard your resources, that's stinginessâ€”a deficit.

The generous person gives the right amount, to the right people, at the right time, in the right way. They're neither a pushover who gives away everything nor a miser who keeps everything for themselves.

Options C and D confuse different virtues entirelyâ€”kindness/cruelty and honesty/dishonesty aren't on the same spectrum as generosity.

Virtue in Action

Case: Helen and Honesty

Helen, a practitioner of Aristotelian virtue ethics, hears her friend William lie to avoid paying a debt. She doesn't need to consult a rule like "Do not lie."

She sees that William's action is dishonest because she has cultivated the virtue of honesty. Her trained character recognizes dishonesty immediately.

  • Virtue ethicists judge character, not just isolated actions
  • The question isn't "Was this action honest?" but "Is this person honest?"
  • Virtuous people develop moral perceptionâ€”they see what situations require

ðŸ“– How Virtue Works in Practice

This case illustrates how virtue ethics operates differently from rule-based approaches. A Kantian might ask "Does lying violate the categorical imperative?" and work through the logic. A utilitarian might calculate the consequences of the lie.

But Helen doesn't need to do any of that. Through years of practicing honesty, she's developed what we might call "moral perception"â€”an ability to recognize dishonesty when she sees it, just as a trained musician immediately hears when a note is wrong.

Moreover, Helen cares about William's CHARACTER, not just this one action. She's concerned that he lacks the virtue of honestyâ€”that this lie reveals something about who he is as a person.

Character Includes Motivation

  • For virtue ethicists, acting from a virtuous character includes having the right motivations and feelings
  • Virtues are stable dispositions that naturally include appropriate emotions

Dutiful Caregiver

Cold, calculating

Loving Caregiver

Tends mother from love, sympathy, loyaltyâ€”in addition to duty

Warm, complete

Most people see the second as a better model of the moral life.

ðŸ“– Why Motivation Matters

This is a powerful critique that virtue ethics offers against Kantian ethics. For Kant, what matters is doing your duty BECAUSE it's your duty. Your feelings are irrelevantâ€”in fact, acting from inclination rather than duty might even diminish the moral worth of your action.

But think about the caregiving example. Wouldn't you feel strange if your child took care of you in your old age while saying "I don't actually want to do this, but it's my duty"? Most of us want to be cared for by people who actually CARE about us, not just people who are doing their duty.

Virtue ethics captures this intuition. A truly virtuous person doesn't just do the right thingâ€”they do it with the right feelings and motivations. Their emotions are aligned with their actions.

Moral Exemplars

  • How do we know what a virtuous person would do?
  • Virtue ethics looks to moral exemplarsâ€”people who embody the virtues
  • We learn virtue partly by imitating those we admire

"The primary focus is not on abstract ideal types of persons but on actual ideal persons. Discovering the proper moral example and imitating that person... replace casuistic reason as the most significant aspects of the moral life."

â€” Louis Pojman, philosopher

Examples of moral exemplars: Socrates, Gandhi, Jesus, the Buddha, Thomas Aquinas, and many others throughout history

ðŸ“– Learning from Moral Heroes

One distinctive feature of virtue ethics is its emphasis on moral exemplarsâ€”real people who exemplify virtue. Instead of giving you abstract rules to follow, virtue ethics says "Look at people like Gandhi, or Socrates, or your grandmother, and ask: What would they do? How can I become more like them?"

This reflects how we actually learn morality in real life. Children don't become good by memorizing rulesâ€”they become good by imitating the adults they admire. We all have people we look up to morally, and we try to emulate their virtues.

The virtue ethicist's advice for moral decision-making isn't "Calculate the consequences" or "Check the rule"â€”it's "Think about the most virtuous person you know. What would they do in this situation?"

Strengths of Virtue Ethics

  • Better account of motivation: Explains why acting from duty alone seems cold and incomplete
  • Focus on character: Recognizes that being a good person matters, not just doing good actions
  • Room for moral achievement: Allows for growth and moral aspiration, not just rule-following
  • Fits moral experience: Matches how we actually make moral judgments about people
The Intuitive Appeal

Virtue-based ethics seems to meet the minimum requirement of coherence and fits with our commonsense moral judgments and experience.

ðŸ“– What Virtue Ethics Gets Right

Virtue ethics has experienced a major revival in the last few decades because it captures aspects of morality that other theories seem to miss.

First, it gives a richer account of moral motivation. We don't just want people to do the right thingâ€”we want them to WANT to do the right thing, to do it naturally and warmly rather than grudgingly.

Second, it acknowledges that being a good person is different from just performing good actions. Someone who does good deeds but is inwardly vicious hasn't achieved what morality really demands.

Third, it makes room for moral heroes and moral growth. We can aspire to become more virtuous, to develop our character over time. Morality becomes a lifelong project of self-improvement, not just a set of rules to follow.

Criticism: The Action-Guidance Problem

  • Critics argue that virtue ethics gives us little guidance about what to do
  • Saying "Do what a virtuous person would do" seems circular

The Dilemma

A desperately poor stranger steals money from you. Should you have him arrested? Give him more money? Ignore the whole affair?

Virtue ethics says: "Do what a virtuous person would do."

But what exactly would a virtuous person do? The theory seems to offer no clear answer.

ðŸ“– The Main Objection

This is probably the most serious criticism of virtue ethics. When you face a tough moral decision, you want guidance. Utilitarianism says "Maximize happiness." Kant says "Follow the categorical imperative." But virtue ethics seems to just say "Be virtuous"â€”which doesn't tell you what to actually DO.

The problem is especially acute because the theory defines right action as "what a virtuous person would do." But how do we know what they'd do without already knowing what's right? It seems circular.

Defenders of virtue ethics have responses (which we'll see), but this remains a genuine challenge for the theory.

The Virtue Ethicist's Response

"[A] great deal of specific action guidance could be found in rules employing the virtue and vice terms ('v-rules') such as 'Do what is honest/charitable; do not do what is dishonest/uncharitable.'"

â€” Rosalind Hursthouse, philosopher

  • Guidance IS available in our vocabulary of virtues and vices
  • Our vice terms are extensive: irresponsible, feckless, lazy, inconsiderate, uncooperative, harsh, intolerant, selfish, mercenary, indiscreet, tactless, arrogant...
  • These terms implicitly tell us what NOT to do

ðŸ“– V-Rules: An Elegant Response

Rosalind Hursthouse offers a clever response: virtue ethics DOES give action guidance, just in a different form. Instead of abstract principles, it gives us "v-rules"â€”rules based on virtues and vices.

Think about how rich our vocabulary of vices is. When we call someone "selfish," "dishonest," "cowardly," or "cruel," we're implicitly saying what kinds of actions to avoid. The rule "Don't be dishonest" is just as action-guiding as "Don't lie."

In fact, Hursthouse points out that our list of vice terms is much longer than any list of duties a Kantian could produceâ€”which suggests virtue ethics might actually give MORE guidance, not less.

Criticism: Conflicting Virtues

The Murder Trial

Your best friend is on trial for murder. You're under oath and must testify about what you know of the caseâ€”and what you know will incriminate her.

If you lie: You're loyal but dishonest

If you tell the truth: You're honest but disloyal

The virtues of loyalty and honesty conflict. Virtue ethics gives no guidance on which virtue should win.

ðŸ“– When Virtues Clash

This is another serious challenge. Sometimes different virtues pull us in opposite directions. In the murder trial case, you can't be BOTH fully loyal AND fully honest. You have to chooseâ€”and virtue ethics doesn't seem to tell you how.

Notice that this is similar to the problem of conflicting duties in Kantian ethics (we discussed this in Chapter 6). If you have a duty not to lie AND a duty to protect your friend, what do you do?

However, defenders of virtue ethics argue that this isn't a fatal flawâ€”it's just an acknowledgment that morality is sometimes genuinely difficult. No theory can make hard cases easy.

Criticism: Circularity

  • Virtue ethics says: "The right action is what a virtuous person would do"
  • But how do we identify a virtuous person?
  • "A virtuous person is one who does right actions"
  • This seems circularâ€”we need an independent standard
The Challenge: Critics argue we need independent moral standards to judge both actions AND character. We can't just point to "virtuous people" without already knowing what virtue is.

ðŸ“– The Circularity Objection

This is a subtle but important objection. The argument goes like this:

1. Virtue ethics says right actions are what a virtuous person would do.
2. But how do we know who's virtuous? By looking at whether they do right actions.
3. So we need to know what right actions are to identify virtuous people, but we need to identify virtuous people to know what right actions are.

This seems like a vicious circle. Critics argue we need some independent standardâ€”like utility or the categorical imperativeâ€”to break the circle.

Virtue ethicists might respond that we DO have independent ways to identify virtue (through eudaimonia, or through moral exemplars we recognize), but this debate continues.

Can Virtuous People Do Wrong?

The Benevolent Doctor

Dr. Green possesses all the proper virtues: she is benevolent, compassionate, wise, and courageous. But she doesn't know whether stem cell research is right or whether helping a terminal patient commit suicide is moral.

A person may have all the virtues and still act wrongly out of ignorance or because the moral issue is genuinely difficult.

  • Critics say: The rightness of actions can't depend entirely on character
  • We need independent moral standards to evaluate complex ethical issues

ðŸ“– Character Isn't Everything

This objection highlights an important limitation. Being a good person doesn't automatically tell you what to do in genuinely difficult moral situations. Dr. Green might be as virtuous as anyone, but that doesn't answer the question of whether euthanasia is permissible.

This suggests that virtue ethics might need to be supplemented with principles or rules for cases where character alone doesn't provide guidance. Many contemporary virtue ethicists acknowledge this and try to incorporate elements from other theories.

The philosopher William Frankena argued that "principles without traits are impotent and traits without principles are blind"â€”suggesting we might need BOTH.

Scenario Analysis:

Maria volunteers at a homeless shelter every weekend. She tells her friends she does it because "it's the right thing to do" but admits she finds the work unpleasant and the people there off-putting. She goes purely from a sense of duty.

How would a virtue ethicist evaluate Maria's actions?

ðŸ“– Answer

A virtue ethicist would have a mixed evaluation of Maria:

Positive: Maria IS doing good actionsâ€”helping people in need. And she demonstrates some virtue in following through on her commitments despite finding them unpleasant.

Concern: However, a virtue ethicist would note that Maria lacks the full virtue of compassion or benevolence. A truly compassionate person wouldn't just DO acts of charityâ€”they would CARE about the people they help. Their motivations and feelings would be aligned with their actions.

The virtue ethicist might say Maria is on the PATH to virtue (she's doing virtuous acts, which can develop into virtuous character), but she hasn't arrived yet. True virtue includes having the appropriate emotions and motivations, not just doing the right things reluctantly.

Compare this to how Kant would evaluate Maria: she might actually score HIGHER for acting from duty rather than inclination!

Learning from Virtue Ethics

  • Virtue and character are unavoidable parts of our moral experience
  • We regularly judge the moral permissibility of actions AND the goodness of character
  • The significance of virtue has forced all moral theories to accommodate it

"[P]rinciples without traits are impotent and traits without principles are blind."

â€” William Frankena, philosopher

The Question Is Not Whetherâ€”But How Much

The question is not whether we should care about virtues, but how much we should care and how we can incorporate them into our moral lives.

ðŸ“– The Enduring Contribution

Even if virtue ethics doesn't provide a complete moral theory, it has made lasting contributions to moral philosophy. It has forced all other theories to take character more seriously.

Frankena's quote captures the modern consensus: we probably need BOTH principles and virtues. Rules without good character are cold and mechanical. But good character without principles leaves us without guidance in hard cases.

Many philosophers now advocate for some combinationâ€”using principles to guide action while also cultivating virtuous character. The debate is about how to balance these elements, not whether both matter.

Why Virtue Ethics Persists

  • Virtue ethics speaks to an important ethical truth: character matters
  • As moral creatures, we judge both actions AND the goodness of character
  • Kant grants no bonus points for acting from virtue; utilitarianism doesn't require virtuous motives
  • But virtue seems essential to moral experience
The Big Picture: Even if virtue ethics alone doesn't provide all the answers, it reminds us that morality is about more than following rules or calculating outcomes. It's about becoming good people who naturally do good things.

ðŸ“– The Lasting Appeal

Virtue ethics has enjoyed a major revival since the 1950s, and it continues to attract philosophers and ordinary people alike. Why?

Because it captures something deeply true about morality: we don't just want good actions, we want good people. We want people who are honest, not just people who don't lie when they calculate it's wrong. We want people who are genuinely kind, not just people who help others because a rule tells them to.

As you develop your own moral views, virtue ethics reminds you that morality is a lifelong project of character development, not just a matter of learning rules or calculating consequences.
