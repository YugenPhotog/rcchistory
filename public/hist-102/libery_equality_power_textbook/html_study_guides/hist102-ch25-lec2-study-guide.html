Part I: Topic Overview

The Great Depression produced two complementary cultural strategies that together helped sustain American democracy through the 1930s. The first — witnessing — used state-sponsored documentary photography, primarily through the Farm Security Administration (FSA), to make poverty visible as a public emergency rather than a private failure. The second — escaping — channeled swing music, Hollywood film, and radio broadcasting into morale-sustaining pleasure that gave Americans the psychological resources to keep functioning as citizens. Both strategies were right, and both were compromised: witnessing was state-directed, racially selective, and conducted without subjects' full consent; escaping reproduced racial hierarchies even as it created democratic spaces. Together they reveal how cultural production functions not merely as entertainment but as a form of governance — and raise an unsettling question about whether culture that makes democratic failure bearable may reduce the urgency of actually fixing it.

Part III: Study Checklist

  • I can explain the difference between "witnessing" and "escaping" as cultural strategies and why the lecture calls them complementary rather than opposites.  (Culture as a Mode of Governance)
  • I can describe what made the FSA photography program historically unprecedented compared to the earlier work of Jacob Riis and Lewis Hine.  (The FSA Photography Program)
  • I can explain how Roy Stryker's shooting scripts complicate the idea that FSA photographs were objective documentary records.  (The FSA Photography Program)
  • I can analyze Dorothea Lange's photographic methodology — including her use of shallow depth of field and the direct gaze — and explain what each formal choice argues about the subject.  (Dorothea Lange and the Ethics of Moral Witness)
  • I can explain the concept of documentary extraction using Florence Thompson's story and connect it to the argument of Let Us Now Praise Famous Men.  (Dorothea Lange and the Ethics of Moral Witness)
  • I can describe Benny Goodman's racial integration and the Cotton Club's segregation policy as two simultaneously true — and contradictory — features of the swing era.  (Swing Music and the Democracy of the Dance Floor)
  • I can explain why radio's simultaneity made FDR's Fireside Chats politically powerful and connect that same property to the War of the Worlds panic.  (Radio: The Invisible Thread of National Community)
  • I can connect the FSA archive's racial selectivity, Amos 'n' Andy, and the swing industry's exploitation of Black musicians as three simultaneous mechanisms of visibility and erasure.  (The Critical Turn: Whose Suffering, Whose Story?)
  • I can state and explain the lecture's closing question: if cultural production makes democratic failure bearable, does it reduce the urgency of fixing it?  (Closing Synthesis)
