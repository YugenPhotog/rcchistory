Part I: Topic Overview

The New Deal did not limit its interventions to the economy. Facing a Depression that persisted despite years of federal programs, the Roosevelt administration turned to culture as a political instrument — funding photography, theater, visual art, and oral history projects whose explicit purpose was to build public and congressional support for its programs. The Farm Security Administration's photography division, directed by Roy Stryker, produced between 160,000 and 270,000 images between 1935 and 1943, deploying detailed "shooting scripts" to capture the suffering that justified continued federal spending. The most famous product of that operation — Dorothea Lange's Migrant Mother (1936) — was selected in Washington, distributed without its subject's named consent, and has defined the visual memory of the Depression ever since.

Meanwhile, the culture that actually sustained ordinary Americans during the Depression was produced not by federal agencies but by private enterprise responding to popular demand. Swing music, Hollywood films, and commercial radio provided joy, community, and escape at no cost to taxpayers — and, in the case of venues like the Savoy Ballroom, achieved a degree of racial integration that no New Deal program matched. The lecture argues that the New Deal's most durable product was neither its economic programs nor its cultural output, but the story it constructed about its own success: a carefully managed archive of selected images and managed narratives that still shapes how the Depression is remembered.

  • I can describe the two-column structure of what the FSA archive included versus what it excluded, and explain why the pattern of exclusion is not random.  (The FSA Photography Program)
  • I can analyze Dorothea Lange's account of the Migrant Mother session and identify the power asymmetry it reveals between photographer and subject.  (Dorothea Lange and State-Directed Witnessing)
  • I can compare Migrant Mother and American Gothic as FSA photographs and explain why their different levels of circulation reflect the political calculus of the New Deal coalition.  (Dorothea Lange and State-Directed Witnessing)
  • I can explain what made swing music a "bottom-up democratic" culture, and contrast this with the logic that governed the New Deal's state-sponsored cultural programs.  (Swing Music and the Democracy of the Dance Floor)
  • I can describe the racial asymmetry at the heart of the swing era — Black artistic production, white commercial distribution — and explain how it compares to the New Deal's own racial record.  (Swing Music and the Democracy of the Dance Floor)
  • I can explain the lecture's argument that the federal government's most consequential intervention in Hollywood was censorship (Hays Code) rather than support, and what this tells us about state involvement in cultural production.  (Hollywood's Golden Age)
  • I can identify the "implicit bargain" between FDR and the broadcast networks and explain why the fireside chats are described as "reputation management for policy that was not working."  (Radio: The Invisible Thread of National Community)
  • I can state the unemployment figures that support the lecture's claim that the New Deal did not end the Depression, and explain why full employment is credited to World War II rather than to New Deal programs.  (Whose Suffering, Whose Story)
  • I can articulate the lecture's central thesis in two or three sentences: what the New Deal's cultural programs produced, who they excluded, and what their most durable legacy is.  (Two Cultural Formations)
