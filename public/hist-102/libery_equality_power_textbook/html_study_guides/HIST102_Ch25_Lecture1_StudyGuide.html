Part I: Topic Overview

This lecture challenges the familiar story of the Great Depression in which Herbert Hoover was a heartless villain who did nothing and FDR was a heroic savior whose New Deal ended the crisis. The evidence is more complicated: Hoover was actually more active than the myth allows — running the largest peacetime deficit in U.S. history, rejecting his Treasury Secretary's advice to liquidate the economy, and launching more public works than the previous thirty years combined. Meanwhile, FDR's New Deal produced genuine structural reforms (Social Security, the FDIC, the SEC) alongside serious policy failures — including programs that contradicted each other, a policy-induced recession in 1937, and systematic exclusions of Black Americans from New Deal benefits. Most critically, the Depression was not ended by the New Deal: unemployment stood at 14.6% in 1940 after a decade of programs. It was wartime mobilization — federal spending reaching 40% of GDP — that finally ended the crisis, achieving a scale of fiscal action that peacetime democratic politics had refused to permit.

Part III: Study Checklist

Check each item when you feel confident. Use the lecture deck, popups, and speaker notes to review.

  • I can explain the difference between Hoover's voluntarism and direct federal relief — and why that difference mattered in 1929. (Herbert Hoover)
  • I can describe at least two things Hoover actually did during the Depression that contradict the "do-nothing" myth. (What Hoover Actually Did)
  • I can explain why the Smoot-Hawley Tariff and the Revenue Act of 1932 made the Depression worse, not better. (Why Hoover Still Failed)
  • I can explain what the Bank Holiday was, why it worked, and connect it to FDR's theory that confidence was itself a cause of the Depression. (Franklin D. Roosevelt)
  • I can describe the core contradiction of the New Deal: how the NRA and AAA both tried to raise prices in ways that actually reduced purchasing power. (The New Deal's Core Contradiction)
  • I can explain who paid the political price for the New Deal coalition — specifically how the AAA harmed Black sharecroppers and tenant farmers. (Scarcity as Policy)
  • I can identify the three causes of the Roosevelt Recession of 1937–38 and explain why historians call it policy-induced rather than externally caused. (The Roosevelt Recession, 1937–1938)
  • I can compare New Deal peak spending (~10% of GDP) to wartime mobilization (~40% of GDP) and explain what the difference tells us about why the Depression finally ended. (What Actually Ended the Depression)
  • I can distinguish between New Deal programs that survived (FDIC, Social Security, SEC) and those that failed or were struck down — and explain what the difference reveals about what kinds of reform last. (What the New Deal Left Behind)
  • I can state the lecture's governing thesis — "History rewards fit between ideas and moments, not good intentions" — and apply it specifically to both Hoover and FDR. (The Governing Premise / The Verdict)
