Part I: Topic Overview

The New Deal did not merely intervene in the economy — it seized the camera. Beginning in 1935, the Roosevelt administration built a cultural apparatus including the Federal Art Project, Federal Theatre Project, Federal Writers' Project, and the FSA Historical Section, all funded by taxpayer dollars and directed by politically appointed administrators with a clear mandate: build public and congressional support for programs that were not working. This lecture argues that this state-directed culture was propaganda — not a slur, but a structural description — because its conclusions were determined before its evidence was gathered. The FSA's shooting scripts, its destroyed negatives, and its systematic exclusion of Black poverty and private recovery all point to the same fact: the archive was a political argument, not a documentary record.

At the same time, ordinary Americans were spending 15 cents to escape into Hollywood films, tuning their radio consoles to Jack Benny and The Shadow, and packing integrated dance floors at the Savoy Ballroom to hear Duke Ellington and Benny Goodman. This private culture — driven by popular desire and private profit, with no congressional appropriation and no shooting script — delivered emotional sustenance, genuine racial integration, and social honesty that the state's managed cultural programs could not match. The lecture's central argument is that the New Deal's cultural apparatus manufactured a myth — the story of how federal intervention saved America — and that this lecture reads that myth against the grain, exposing who commissioned the images, who set the terms, and whose stories were never told.
