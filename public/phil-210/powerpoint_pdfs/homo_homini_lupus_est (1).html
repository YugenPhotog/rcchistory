Section I

Human Nature

What kind of creature are we, really?

Human nature is fundamentally the same and universal regardless of time and place — this is why we can understand each other, whether our neighbors today or the ancient Chinese or Japanese. Because we have the same nature, when we read about the ancients' desire for power, wealth, or sex, we understand that. When we read about their desire to care for their families and children, we understand that too. Human beings are born good and innocent, but "encumbered." We are born into a corrupted (secular) or "fallen" (religious) world with agency — the ability to choose. We are encumbered because we have a natural tendency to choose badly and/or make mistakes. Still, we do have moral agency and can choose good or evil. Our ability to think — that inner dialogue — to choose, and our subsequent actions determine our nature. Monsters or Saints are made, not born.

Human Nature

  • Human nature is fundamentally the same and universal
  • Human beings are born good and innocent, but "encumbered"
  • We have moral agency — the capacity to choose good or evil
  • Our inner dialogue, choices, and actions determine our nature
Monsters or Saints are made, not born.

The wrench in the works is human evil. How can we understand evil? For example, how do we understand when two men break into the home of Dr. William Petit, his wife and two daughters — beat him nearly to death, rape and strangle his wife, sexually assault the 11-year-old daughter, then tie both daughters to beds, douse them with gasoline, and light them on fire while still alive? Many people want to understand evil by pathologizing it — they assume evildoers are mentally ill. And while this may be true for some, for most, evil is the only explanation. This kind of evil is unfathomable. It is so far distant from normal human nature that surely we cannot share the same nature. This is what Immanuel Kant calls "radical evil."

The Wrench in the Works

The Problem: Human Evil
  • We are "encumbered" — we have a natural tendency to choose badly
  • According to Kant: an innate propensity to evil
  • This propensity = subordinating the moral law to self-conceit
So how do we account for the evil that humans do to one another?

Section II

Radical Evil

Immanuel Kant and the depths of human corruption

Some want to understand evil by pathologizing it — might be true for some, but for others evil is the only explanation. This kind of evil is unfathomable — what Kant calls "radical evil." The failure of human moral agents to observe the moral law is symptomatic of a character or disposition (Gesinnung) that has been corrupted by an innate propensity to evil, which is to subordinate the moral law to self-conceit (def. undue pride in oneself). Because this propensity corrupts an agent's character as a whole, and is the innate "source" of every other evil deed, it may be considered "radical."

Radical Evil (Kant)

  • Some want to explain evil by pathologizing it — assume evil-doers are mentally ill
  • For some cases this may be true; for others, evil is the only explanation
  • This kind of evil is unfathomable — beyond normal human comprehension
  • Kant names it: "Radical Evil"
📸 Immanuel Kant portrait · c. 1790 · Public Domain · Wikimedia Commons

Immanuel Kant · c. 1790 · Public Domain · Wikimedia Commons

Consistent evil choices have a transformative effect — it "corrupts…character as a whole." They become an enemy to God (goodness) and their nature is changed. Evil choices create an evil nature. Because their nature is now so far different from ours, we cannot understand it — thus the discussion is over. We can only describe the actions; we cannot understand the person. This is the crucial distinction: for ordinary wrongdoing, we can explain the motive, the weakness, the temptation. For radical evil, we are left only with description. Understanding is foreclosed.

Evil Choices, Evil Nature

"The failure of human moral agents to observe the moral law is symptomatic of a character corrupted by an innate propensity to evil — to subordinate the moral law to self-conceit. Because this propensity corrupts an agent's character as a whole… it may be considered 'radical.'"
— Immanuel Kant, Religion within the Bounds of Bare Reason
  • Consistent evil choices have a transformative effect on character
  • Evil choices create an evil nature — the whole character is corrupted
  • Such evil becomes incomprehensible — we can only describe the acts

Section III

Extreme Evil & the Banality of Evil

Hannah Arendt and the horror of ordinary perpetrators

What about evils committed by totalitarian regimes and ideologies — the Holocaust, the Rape of Nanjing, the 100 million deaths under communist regimes? Hannah Arendt called this "extreme evil" — or crimes against humanity. These are atrocities committed on such a large scale that they necessarily involve the actions and support of otherwise normal, good people. People who otherwise would have been normal good people had they not been thrown into those extreme situations. It is these people who give us pause. Are we just like them? How would we act in a similar situation? This can be understood — and in fact must be understood. Hannah Arendt calls this the banality of evil.

Extreme Evil

Crimes Against Humanity
  • Atrocities committed by totalitarian regimes on a massive scale
  • Necessarily involve the actions of otherwise normal, good people
  • Raises the unsettling question: Are we just like them?
  • Arendt insists: this can and must be understood
📸 Hannah Arendt · 1975 · Public Domain

Hannah Arendt · 1975 · Public Domain · Wikimedia Commons

What causes good people to do evil is often ideology. Specifically the ideology of fascism or communism. Anytime you have an ideology that subordinates the intrinsic value of the individual for some alleged greater good — no matter what the claimed greater good is, God, the State, or class, etc. — these types of atrocities happen. It happened in Communist Russia and Nazi Germany. And it happened in Fascist Japan. Like communism, fascism subordinates the value of the individual — and beginning in the 1920s–30s Japan took a fascist turn. When the value of the individual is subordinated to some greater good, the individual becomes expendable and sacrificed to that greater good. If you feel you are creating paradise on earth, then anyone standing in your way must be got rid of. Further, both fascism and communism attempted to change human nature itself — to create the "new man."

The Banality of Evil

  • What causes good people to do evil? Most often: ideology
  • Any ideology that subordinates the individual to some "greater good" produces atrocities
  • Totalitarian regimes create superfluous people — those who have lost the ability to think
  • Examples: Communist Russia, Nazi Germany, Fascist Japan
The individual becomes expendable — sacrificed to the "greater good."
In totalitarianism, such as fascism and communism, the human as a human being becomes superfluous. This designation applies to both the victim and the perpetrator. It eliminates all spontaneity, all creativity, all individuality. It dehumanizes everybody. Totalitarianism strives not toward despotic rule over men, but toward a system in which men are superfluous. Total power can be achieved and safeguarded only in a world of conditioned reflexes, of marionettes without the slightest trace of spontaneity. Man can be fully dominated only when he becomes a specimen of the animal-species man. Thus, totalitarian ideologies create living corpses — people unable to think.

The Superfluous Human

  • Under totalitarianism, the human as a human being becomes superfluous
  • This applies to both victim and perpetrator
  • Eliminates all spontaneity, creativity, and individuality
  • It dehumanizes everybody
"…men are taught they are superfluous through a way of life in which punishment is meted out without connection with crime, in which exploitation is practiced without profit, and where work is performed without product…"
— Hannah Arendt, Origins of Totalitarianism
Pause here on this quote. Arendt is describing what totalitarianism actually does — it doesn't just rule people, it renders them unnecessary as persons. The bureaucrat following orders is superfluous. The prisoner in the camp is superfluous. The party official is superfluous. All have been reduced to functions, not persons. This is the logical end of any ideology that subordinates the individual to the collective: eventually the individual simply ceases to matter at all. The regime needs bodies to serve functions — not persons to exercise judgment.

Men Made Superfluous

"Men insofar as they are more than animal reaction and fulfilment of functions are entirely superfluous to totalitarian regimes. Totalitarianism strives not toward despotic rule over men, but toward a system in which men are superfluous… [man] can be fully dominated only when he becomes a specimen of the animal-species man."
— Hannah Arendt, Origins of Totalitarianism
Result: totalitarian ideologies produce living corpses — people unable to think.
To Arendt, "to think" means something very specific — it is the inner dialogue with the self; the place where we are constantly asking ourselves: "Is this action right? Does this action put my self-interest above the needs and rights of others?" Totalitarianism desires to change human nature into one who no longer has the ability to think, to judge between right and wrong — but simply to serve the state. "Evil comes from a failure to think." This is the core of the banality of evil. It is not that the perpetrators were demonic. It is that they stopped asking the question.

The Inability to Think

To Think (Arendt's definition): the inner dialogue with the self — constantly asking, "Is this action right? Am I placing my self-interest above the rights of others?"
  • Totalitarianism desires to destroy this inner dialogue
  • It creates citizens who no longer judge between right and wrong — who simply serve the state
"Good can be radical; evil can never be radical… it can spread like a fungus over the surface of the earth… Evil comes from a failure to think."
— Hannah Arendt, Eichmann in Jerusalem
Arendt arrived at the banality of evil by observing the trial of Adolf Eichmann in Jerusalem in 1961. She went expecting a monster — a man who had organized the deportation of millions of Jews to the death camps. Instead she found a mindless bureaucrat. He was proud of his loyalty to Hitler and his efficiency in "just following orders." Here was a man capable of defiantly moving millions of Jews to their deaths, without even holding a grudge against them. He didn't hate them. He was just doing his job. Most of the people responsible for atrocities in Nazi Germany, Communist Russia, or Fascist Japan were simply un-thinking bureaucrats or soldiers. This is not to say there were not radically evil individuals who took advantage of the situation to inflict their evil will — but atrocities happen when most people do not or cannot think and choose the good.

Adolf Eichmann & the Banality

  • Arendt's concept arrived from observing the Eichmann Trial, 1961
  • Not the evil monster she expected — a mindless bureaucrat
  • Proud of his loyalty and efficiency in "just following orders"
  • Capable of moving millions to their death without even holding a grudge
Most perpetrators are un-thinking bureaucrats — not monsters.

Section IV

Prevention

Memory, education, and the courage to think

Education is key — but not just education for education's sake. Dr. Joseph Mengele held a doctorate in anthropology and a medical degree. Dr. Shiro Ishii was a trained microbiologist and general. Both were highly educated. Both were monsters. Education must teach people how to think — critical thinking and the moral courage to act on those judgments. We need a society where people have both the ability and the liberty to think; and citizens with the courage to act on moral choices. American education may be going down a bad path. When safe spaces, enforced trigger warnings, and violent protests against speech become the norm — when identity politics trumps the value of individual rights — we are slouching toward the very conditions that make the banality of evil possible.

Education Is Key

  • Education is essential — but not education for its own sake
  • Dr. Joseph Mengele & Dr. Shiro Ishii were both highly educated
  • Education must teach people how to think — critical reasoning and moral courage
  • We need citizens with the ability, liberty, and courage to act on moral choices
Credentials are not conscience. Diplomas do not guarantee decency.
Carol Gluck of Columbia University: "How did it happen? And how can we together try to prevent this from happening again. If memory and history are not used for a better future, but only to re-enact the hostilities of a bad past then none of us is doing our job." Do I want you to hate the Japanese? Absolutely not. The Japanese are a wonderful people with a fascinating history and rich culture. What I do want you to understand, hate, and be on the constant lookout for is fascism — and any other twin ideology that devalues the individual. After an assignment like this, it is important to remind ourselves that there IS good in the world — and for you to go take part in it and contribute to that good.

Memory & History

"If memory and history are not used for a better future, but only to re-enact the hostilities of a bad past — then none of us is doing our job."
— Carol Gluck, Columbia University
  • The lesson of atrocity is not hatred of a people — but hatred of ideologies that devalue the individual
  • Fascism, communism, and any creed that makes the individual expendable
The warning signs are not exotic. They are ordinary. Any ideology — religious, political, or social — that makes the individual expendable for some larger cause has crossed the line. The specific "greater good" being invoked doesn't matter. God, the State, the Race, the Class, Social Justice — the mechanism is the same. Protect individual rights. Cultivate the inner dialogue. And act. Edmund Burke's point is simple: evil does not require evil people. It only requires good people who go silent and passive.

The Standing Warning

The Danger Signs
  • Any ideology that subordinates the individual to a "greater good"
  • Suppression of free thought and dissent
  • Creation of superfluous classes of people
The Response
  • Protect and defend individual rights
  • Cultivate the inner dialogue — the habit of moral thinking
  • Act — good people who do nothing enable evil

Hannah Arendt covered the trial for The New Yorker. She expected a monster. Instead she found a mediocre bureaucrat who claimed he was simply following orders and doing his job efficiently. This observation led to her concept of the "banality of evil."

Mengele & Ishii — Educated Perpetrators

Dr. Josef Mengele was a trained physician and SS officer who conducted barbaric medical experiments on prisoners at Auschwitz. He held a doctorate in anthropology as well as a medical degree.

Dr. Shiro Ishii was a microbiologist and general who commanded Japan's Unit 731 in Manchuria — a covert biological and chemical warfare research unit that conducted lethal human experiments on thousands of prisoners.

Both were highly credentialed. Education without the cultivation of moral thinking produced monsters, not healers.
