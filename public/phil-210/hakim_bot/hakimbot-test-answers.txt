THOUGHT QUESTION 1
Al-Fatihah, the Throne Verse, and Surah 112 all connect God's oneness to worship and guidance. Al-Fatihah moves from praise of God as compassionate judge to the worshipper's request, "Guide us on the straight path" (Qur'an 1:1-7). The Throne Verse presents God as living, self-sustaining, sovereign, and all-knowing, which explains why worship belongs to God alone (Qur'an 2:255). Surah 112 rejects divine offspring and comparison, making tawhid uncompromising (Qur'an 112:1-4). Creation passages such as Qur'an 30:20-25 and 51:56-58 add that human beings are created as signs and for worship, so responsibility follows from dependence on God.

THOUGHT QUESTION 2
The Qur'an presents Abraham, Moses, Mary, Jesus, and Muhammad as part of one prophetic history. Abraham and Ishmael model submission and connect revelation to the sanctuary (Qur'an 2:124-132). Moses receives revelation, worship, and mission language at the fire (Qur'an 20:9-24). Mary and Jesus are honored deeply, and Jesus is born miraculously and speaks as a prophet and servant of God (Qur'an 19:16-36). At the same time, the Qur'an disputes Christian claims of divine sonship, especially where it says God does not beget and Jesus is a servant (Qur'an 19:35-36; 112:3). Muhammad is then presented as messenger, example, and seal of the prophets, which makes Islam claim both continuity and final correction (Qur'an 33:21, 33:40).

THOUGHT QUESTION 3
Islamic worship in the Qur'an is not only inward belief or isolated ritual. Fasting is prescribed so believers may become God-conscious (Qur'an 2:183), and required alms are directed toward concrete social groups such as the poor, needy, debtors, and travelers (Qur'an 9:60). Pilgrimage joins reverence, sacrifice, and feeding others, while saying that neither blood nor flesh reaches God but piety does (Qur'an 22:26-37). Qur'an 2:177 and 4:135 combine belief and worship with justice, generosity, promise-keeping, and truthful witness. This means ritual and ethics are supposed to form one way of life.

THOUGHT QUESTION 4
Two difficult clusters are gender and family authority, and conflict verses. In Qur'an 4:34-35, the Arabic term qawwamun and the sequence of marital conflict make interpretation highly disputed, so context and later Muslim readings matter. Qur'an 33:35 also has to be read beside it because it stresses the spiritual equality of men and women. For conflict, Qur'an 9:1-6 must be read with its treaty exemptions and asylum command, not only with 9:5. Qur'an 2:190 and 8:61 also matter because they limit fighting and require a turn toward peace when peace is offered. Historical setting and disputed terms do not erase the troubling language, but they do change how responsibly it should be read.
