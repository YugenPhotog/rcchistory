## Early Chinese Civilization

- Around 3,000 BCE cultures begin converging into what becomes China  
- By 1500 BCE powerful kings and aristocrats govern agrarian society  
- **Shang Dynasty** – first historically documented dynasty  
- Kings = priest‑kings who led rituals  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Explain *sacerdotal* = priestly.

---

# Writing: Chinese Characters

- Pictographic  
- Ideographic  
- Logographic  

### Significance

- Unified communication  
- Cultural continuity  
- Spread to Korea, Japan, Vietnam  

### Chinese Classics

- Book of Documents  
- Book of Changes (I Qing / Yijing)  
- Book of Songs  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Stress the importance of written continuity for Chinese tradition.

---

# Shang Dynasty Religion

- Oracle bones used for divination  
- Kings mediated with **Shangdi (Lord on High)**  
- Ancestor worship central  
- Human sacrifice in royal tombs  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Explain religion‑politics fusion.

---

# Zhou Dynasty

- Shang defeated in 1045 BCE  
- Zhou justify conquest through **Mandate of Heaven (Tian)**  
- King becomes “Son of Heaven”  
- Later instability leads to **Spring and Autumn** and **Warring States**

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Compare Mandate of Heaven with later political legitimacy theories.

---

# Zhou Religious Culture

- Human sacrifice declines  
- Ancestor veneration grows  
- Confucius & Laozi emerge in this period  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Anchor timeline around 500 BCE.

---

# Confucius (551–479 BCE)

- Born in State of Lu  
- Political adviser and teacher  
- Teachings recorded in **Analects**  
- Saw himself as transmitter of tradition  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Clarify that Confucius *did not found a religion*.

---

# Core Confucian Virtues

- Ritual propriety (Li)  
- **Ren — Benevolence / Humaneness**  
- Trustworthiness  
- Public‑mindedness  
- **Xiao — Filial Piety**  
- Rectification of Names  
- Moral education  
- Harmony  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Emphasize social‑ethical focus.

---

# Five Cardinal Relationships

Superior → Inferior

- Emperor → Subject  
- Father → Son  
- Husband → Wife  
- Older Brother → Younger Brother  
- Friend ↔ Friend *(reciprocal)*  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Explain hierarchy + mutual obligation.

---

# Zhong — Loyalty

Duty extended to all relationships.

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Loyalty is moral, not blind obedience.

---

# Confucian Statecraft

- Order through hierarchy  
- Morality as political tool  
- Ruler models virtue  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Contrast with Legalism later.

---

# Mencius (371–289 BCE)

- Major interpreter of Confucius  
- Human nature is **basically good**  
- All people can become sages  
- Moral “sprouts” (innate compassion)  
- Example: child about to fall into a well  
- Rule through virtue  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Ask students if they agree humans are naturally good.

---

# Confucianization of China

- Dong Zhongshu promotes teachings in 2nd c. BCE  
- Confucianism becomes **state ideology** under Han dynasty  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Important turning point.

---

# Neo‑Confucianism (Song Dynasty)

- Revival movement (960–1279 CE)  
- Engaged Buddhist & Daoist metaphysics  
- Still focused on ethics and social order  
- Four canonical books established  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Stress cross‑pollination among traditions.

---

# Zhu Xi (1130–1200)

- Synthesizes tradition  
- Seeks moral order & cosmic structure  
- Thought spreads to Korea & Japan  

---

# Daoism — A Brief Introduction

- Dao = “the Way”  
- Underlying natural order of the cosmos  
- Daoists seek harmony with nature  
- Begins as philosophy → later religion  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Avoid stereotype “hippie religion.”

---

# Qi — Vital Force

- Energy flowing through all life  
- Central to medicine and martial arts  
- Harmony = balanced Qi  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Compare to “spirit” metaphorically — but emphasize cultural difference.

---

# Yin / Yang

- Yin = dark, receptive  
- Yang = light, active  
- Complementary & dynamic  
- Not good vs. evil  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Key difference from Western dualisms.

---

# Laozi and the Dao De Jing

- Legendary figure  
- Associated with short poetic text  
- Themes: humility, simplicity, non‑striving  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Explain paradox language.

---

# Zhuangzi

- Playful, poetic thinker  
- Uses parables  
- Radical relativism of perspective  
- Emphasis on spontaneity and freedom  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Students often enjoy his stories.

---

# Dao as Natural Order

- Ineffable  
- Ever‑changing  
- Unified whole  

---

# Wu‑Wei — Non‑Action

- Acting effortlessly in harmony  
- Not laziness — alignment with reality  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Use water metaphor.

---

# Knowledge and Perspective

- Ordinary knowledge limited  
- True wisdom = broader perspective  
- Blind men & elephant story  

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Tie to epistemology.

---

# Final Takeaways

- Confucianism = ethical‑social order  
- Daoism = harmony with nature  
- Both shape Chinese culture profoundly

> **Instructor Notes:**  
> Ask: Where do these philosophies overlap?
