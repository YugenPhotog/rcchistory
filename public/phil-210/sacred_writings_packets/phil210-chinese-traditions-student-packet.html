Before You Read

This packet brings together selected passages from four foundational Chinese texts: the Analects, the Mencius, the Daode Jing, and the Zhuangzi. The first two are commonly associated with Confucian traditions; the latter two are foundational for Daoist thought. In Chinese history, these traditions have often overlapped, competed, and been practiced alongside one another.

The selections should not be read as two simple opposites. Confucian texts ask how people can cultivate character and create humane social relationships. Daoist texts question forceful striving, fixed viewpoints, and attempts to control the natural course of things. Both are concerned with the Dao, or Way, but they imagine the human task differently.

This packet uses Pinyin spellings: Dao rather than Tao, Daode Jing rather than Tao Te Ching, and Zhuangzi rather than Chuang Tzu. The English renderings are new course translations from Classical Chinese, with translation choices documented in the instructor audit.

How to cite the reading

Use a text and passage reference: (Analects 12.2), (Mencius 2A.6), (Daode Jing 48), or (Zhuangzi 3).

Primary Sources

Selected Readings

1The Analects: Learning, Family, and Cultivation

The Analects preserves short conversations and sayings associated with Confucius and his followers. These passages connect learning to practice, reflection, family relationships, and the gradual formation of character.

Analects 1.1

The Master said: "Is it not a pleasure to learn and, in due season, practice what one has learned? Is it not a joy to have friends come from afar? If others do not recognize you and you are not resentful, are you not an exemplary person?"

Analects 1.2

Master You said: "It is rare for someone who is filial toward parents and respectful toward elder brothers to be fond of defying those above. And no one who is not fond of defying those above is likely to delight in creating disorder. The exemplary person attends to the root; when the root is established, the Way grows. Filiality and respect for elder brothers are surely the root of humaneness."

Analects 2.4

The Master said: "At fifteen, I set my heart on learning. At thirty, I stood firm. At forty, I was free from confusion. At fifty, I understood Heaven's mandate. At sixty, my ear was attuned. At seventy, I could follow my heart's desires without crossing the boundary."

Analects 2.15

The Master said: "Learning without reflection leads to confusion; reflection without learning leads to peril."

2Humaneness, Reciprocity, and Social Order

Confucian ethics is not simply a private list of virtues. It asks how inner cultivation, ritual propriety, reciprocity, language, law, and public responsibility should fit together.

Analects 4.15

The Master said: "Zeng, my Way is threaded together by one principle." Zengzi replied, "Yes." After the Master left, the other students asked what he meant. Zengzi said: "The Master's Way is simply conscientiousness and reciprocity."

Analects 12.1

Yan Yuan asked about humaneness. The Master said: "To overcome the self and return to ritual propriety is humaneness. If for one day you overcome the self and return to ritual propriety, all under Heaven will return to humaneness. Practicing humaneness begins with yourself - does it begin with others?" Yan Yuan asked for the particulars. The Master said: "Do not look, listen, speak, or act contrary to ritual propriety." Yan Yuan said: "Though I am not quick, I ask to put these words into practice."

Analects 12.2

Zhonggong asked about humaneness. The Master said: "When you go out, act as though receiving an important guest. When you direct the people, act as though assisting at a great sacrifice. Do not impose on others what you yourself do not desire. Then in public life there will be no resentment, and in private life there will be no resentment." Zhonggong said: "Though I am not quick, I ask to put these words into practice."

Analects 13.3

Zilu said: "If the ruler of Wei were waiting for you to govern, what would you put first?" The Master said: "Surely the rectification of names." Zilu said: "Really? You are being roundabout. Why rectify them?" The Master said: "How crude you are, Zilu! The exemplary person leaves blank what he does not know. If names are not correct, speech does not accord. If speech does not accord, affairs are not accomplished. If affairs are not accomplished, rites and music do not flourish. If rites and music do not flourish, punishments do not fit. If punishments do not fit, the people do not know where to place hand or foot. Therefore, what the exemplary person names must be speakable, and what is spoken must be practicable. In speech, the exemplary person permits no carelessness."

Analects 15.23

Zigong asked: "Is there one saying that can be practiced for an entire lifetime?" The Master said: "Perhaps reciprocity: do not impose on others what you yourself do not desire."

3Mencius: The Four Moral Sprouts

Mencius develops a powerful Confucian account of moral possibility. His famous thought experiment about a child at a well argues that ethical capacities begin in ordinary human responsiveness, but require development.

Mencius 2A.6

Mencius said: "All people have a heart that cannot bear the suffering of others. The ancient kings had such a heart, and therefore they had government that could not bear the suffering of others. With a heart that cannot bear the suffering of others, to practice government that cannot bear the suffering of others would be as easy as turning the world in one's palm. Why say that all people have such a heart? Suppose someone suddenly sees a small child about to fall into a well. Anyone would feel alarm and compassion - not in order to gain favor with the child's parents, not in order to win praise from neighbors and friends, and not because the sound of the child's cry would be unpleasant. From this we see: one without compassion is not human; one without a sense of shame and aversion is not human; one without deference and yielding is not human; one without a sense of right and wrong is not human. Compassion is the sprout of humaneness; shame and aversion are the sprout of righteousness; deference and yielding are the sprout of ritual propriety; the sense of right and wrong is the sprout of wisdom. Having these four sprouts is like having four limbs. To say that one cannot develop them is to injure oneself; to say that one's ruler cannot develop them is to injure one's ruler. Everyone who has these four sprouts should know to extend and fulfill them, as a fire begins to burn or a spring begins to break through. If they are fulfilled, they will be sufficient to care for all within the Four Seas; if they are not fulfilled, they will not be sufficient even to serve one's parents."

4The Daode Jing: Dao, Water, Return, and Paradox

The Daode Jing speaks in compact, paradoxical sayings. Rather than offering a systematic doctrine, it repeatedly directs attention to the generative Dao, non-contention, stillness, return, and the limits of self-display.

Daode Jing 1

A Dao that can be spoken is not the enduring Dao. A name that can be named is not the enduring name. The nameless is the beginning of Heaven and Earth; the named is the mother of the ten thousand things. Therefore, free from desire, one perceives its mystery; caught in desire, one perceives only its manifestations. These two arise from the same source but are named differently. Together they are called profound. Profound upon profound: the gateway of all mystery.

Daode Jing 8

The highest goodness is like water. Water benefits the ten thousand things and does not contend; it dwells in places people disdain, and so comes close to the Dao. In dwelling, it chooses good ground; in heart, good depth; in relations, goodness; in speech, trustworthiness; in governing, good order; in work, ability; in movement, good timing. Because it does not contend, it is without blame.

Daode Jing 16

Reach the utmost emptiness; hold firmly to stillness. The ten thousand things arise together, and I watch them return. Things flourish in profusion, yet each returns to its root. Returning to the root is called stillness; this is called returning to one's allotted course. Returning to the allotted course is called constancy; knowing constancy is clarity. Not knowing constancy, one acts blindly and brings disaster. Knowing constancy, one is accommodating; being accommodating, one is impartial; being impartial, one is kingly; being kingly, one accords with Heaven; according with Heaven, one accords with Dao; according with Dao, one endures, and throughout one's life is not in danger.

Daode Jing 22

Bent, then whole; crooked, then straight; hollow, then full; worn, then renewed; little, then gained; much, then confused. Therefore the sage embraces the one and becomes a model for all under Heaven. Not displaying himself, he is seen; not asserting himself, he is recognized; not boasting, he has merit; not exalting himself, he endures. Because he does not contend, no one under Heaven can contend with him. The old saying, "Bent, then whole" - could it be empty words? Truly, one returns to wholeness through it.

5The Daode Jing: Wu Wei and Governance

Wu wei is often mistranslated as "doing nothing." These passages are better read as a critique of forced, self-asserting, and meddlesome action, especially in political life.

Daode Jing 37

The Dao constantly does nothing contrived, yet nothing is left undone. If rulers can hold to it, the ten thousand things will transform themselves. When, transformed, they begin to desire and act, I would steady them with the uncarved simplicity that has no name. Uncarved simplicity brings freedom from desire; free from desire and at rest, all under Heaven will settle itself.

Daode Jing 48

In pursuing learning, one increases day by day. In pursuing the Dao, one decreases day by day. Decrease and decrease again, until one reaches non-contrived action. Through non-contrived action, nothing is left undone. One takes the world by remaining free from meddlesome affairs; once one is full of affairs, one is not fit to take the world.

Daode Jing 57

Govern a state with rectitude; employ surprise in war; take all under Heaven by leaving things unmeddled with. How do I know this is so? From this: the more prohibitions there are under Heaven, the poorer the people become; the more sharp tools people possess, the more disordered the state becomes; the more techniques and cleverness people have, the more strange contrivances arise; the more laws and orders are displayed, the more thieves and robbers appear. Therefore the sage says: "I do nothing contrived, and the people transform themselves. I love stillness, and the people correct themselves. I do not meddle, and the people enrich themselves. I have no desires, and the people return to simplicity."

6Zhuangzi: Skill, Transformation, and Death

The Zhuangzi tests ordinary habits of certainty. Its stories use dream, craft, humor, and grief to ask what it would mean to move with transformation rather than try to control every outcome.

Zhuangzi 2, "The Butterfly Dream"

Once, Zhuang Zhou dreamed that he was a butterfly - a fluttering butterfly, pleased with itself and doing as it wished. It did not know that it was Zhou. Suddenly he awoke, and there he was: unmistakably Zhou. But did Zhou dream that he was a butterfly, or is the butterfly dreaming that it is Zhou? Between Zhou and the butterfly there must be a difference. This is called the transformation of things.

Zhuangzi 3, "Cook Ding"

Cook Ding was carving an ox for Lord Wenhui. Wherever his hand touched, shoulder leaned, foot stepped, or knee pressed, there was a sound; whenever the knife moved, it made a rhythm. It matched the dance of the Mulberry Grove and the cadence of an ancient musical piece. Lord Wenhui said: "Wonderful! Can skill reach such a height?" Cook Ding set down his knife and replied: "What I care about is the Dao; it has gone beyond skill. When I first began cutting up oxen, all I saw was a whole ox. After three years, I no longer saw a whole ox. Now I meet it with spirit rather than looking with my eyes. Sensory awareness stops, and spirit moves where it wants. I follow the natural patterns, strike through the great gaps, guide the knife through the great openings, and follow what is already so. I have never touched a ligament or tendon, much less a major joint. A good cook changes knives every year because he cuts; an ordinary cook changes them every month because he hacks. My knife is nineteen years old. It has cut up thousands of oxen, yet its edge is as fresh as if just sharpened. There are spaces between the joints, and the blade has no thickness. When what has no thickness enters what has space, there is ample room for the blade to move. That is why, after nineteen years, its edge is still fresh. Yet whenever I come to a difficult place, I see the difficulty, become alert and cautious, fix my gaze, slow my movement, and move the knife with the slightest touch. Then - apart it comes, like a clod of earth falling to the ground. I stand with the knife raised, look around, hesitate in satisfaction, wipe the knife clean, and put it away." Lord Wenhui said: "Wonderful! Hearing Cook Ding's words, I have learned how to nourish life."

Zhuangzi 18, "Zhuangzi's Wife Dies"

When Zhuangzi's wife died, Huizi came to offer condolences. Zhuangzi was sitting with his legs spread, drumming on a basin and singing. Huizi said: "To have lived with someone, raised children, and grown old together - not to weep when she dies is already enough. But to drum on a basin and sing: is that not too much?" Zhuangzi said: "No. When she first died, how could I have avoided being deeply affected? But I traced back to the beginning: originally there was no life; not merely no life, but no form; not merely no form, but no vital breath. Mixed in the dim and indistinct, a transformation produced vital breath; vital breath transformed into form; form transformed into life; and now there is another transformation into death. It is like the movement of the four seasons: spring, summer, autumn, and winter. She lies peacefully in a vast chamber, and for me to wail after her would show that I do not understand the course of things. So I stopped."
