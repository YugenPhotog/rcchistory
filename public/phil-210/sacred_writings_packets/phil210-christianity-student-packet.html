Before You Read

This packet guides you through a focused set of New Testament readings on the identity and teachings of Jesus, his death and resurrection, the inclusion of Gentiles, representative teachings from Paul, and the relationship between faith and works in James. Because the New Testament is widely available, the packet does not reproduce the full passages. You are responsible for locating and reading every assigned citation in a complete Bible.

Bible translation policy

You may use any complete, recognized Bible translation, including the NRSVue, NIV, CEB, NABRE, ESV, KJV, or another standard edition. Record the translation you use when quoting. Wording will differ slightly across translations, but the chapter-and-verse references remain the same. Paraphrases such as The Message may be useful supplements but should not be your only text for close reading.

How to use each reading guide

Read the entire assigned passage before relying on the commentary. The commentary tells you what to watch for, not what you must conclude. Pay attention to literary setting, speaker, audience, and the distinct voice of each New Testament author. Christianity contains many traditions, and Christians do not interpret every passage in exactly the same way.

Key Terms

TermWorking definition
Gospel“Good news”; also the name given to the four New Testament narratives of Jesus’s life, teaching, death, and resurrection.
Kingdom of GodGod’s active rule or reign, central to Jesus’s preaching. It is described as already drawing near while also awaiting fulfillment.
Messiah / Christ“Anointed one.” Messiah comes through Hebrew; Christ comes through Greek. The New Testament applies both titles to Jesus.
Son of GodA title expressing Jesus’s unique relationship to God. Different New Testament writings develop its meaning in distinctive ways.
Son of ManJesus’s frequent self-designation in the Gospels; it can suggest humanity, suffering, authority, and an end-time figure associated with Daniel 7.
IncarnationThe Christian teaching that the divine Word became genuinely human in Jesus.
DiscipleA learner or follower whose life is shaped by a teacher and way of life.
ParableA brief narrative or comparison that invites interpretation, often used by Jesus to disclose and challenge assumptions about God’s kingdom.
ResurrectionGod’s raising of Jesus from the dead and, in early Christian hope, the future raising of the dead.
GentileA person who is not Jewish. Whether Gentile converts needed circumcision and Torah observance became a major early Christian dispute.
JustificationPauline language for being placed in right relationship with God; Christian traditions differ in how they explain its relation to faith, grace, and works.
CreedA formal communal statement of belief. The Nicene Creed summarizes later Christian teaching about God, Christ, the Spirit, church, baptism, and resurrection.

1 · Jesus: Beloved Son, Messiah, and Incarnate Word

These readings present three related but distinct New Testament approaches to Jesus’s identity. Read each passage in its own literary voice before combining them.

Read Mark 1:9–15
Baptism, beloved Son, Spirit, and the kingdom

Mark begins Jesus’s public ministry with baptism, divine affirmation, testing in the wilderness, and proclamation. Watch how quickly the scene moves from identity to mission.

  • What is said to Jesus at his baptism?
  • What role does the Spirit play?
  • What response does Jesus demand when he announces that God’s kingdom has drawn near?
Anchor phrases: “beloved Son” · “kingdom of God” · “repent and believe”
Read Mark 8:27–35
Peter’s confession and the suffering Messiah

Peter names Jesus as Messiah, but Jesus immediately redefines expectations through suffering, rejection, death, and discipleship. The passage asks not only who Jesus is, but what following him requires.

  • Why does Peter resist Jesus’s prediction?
  • How does Jesus connect his own path with the path of disciples?
  • What kind of Messiah does the passage present?
Anchor phrases: “You are the Messiah” · “take up their cross”
Read John 1:1–14
The Word becomes flesh

John opens not with Jesus’s birth or baptism but with the divine Word, creation, life, light, rejection, and incarnation. Notice how John’s language places Jesus within a cosmic theological frame.

  • What relationship does the Word have to God and creation?
  • What happens when the Word enters the world?
  • What does it mean for the Word to become “flesh” rather than merely appear human?
Anchor phrases: “the Word” · “light” · “became flesh”

2 · The Teachings of Jesus

The Sermon on the Mount links life under God’s rule with character, reconciliation, enemy-love, prayer, obedience, and concrete action.

Read Matthew 5:1–12
The Beatitudes

Jesus calls unexpected people blessed and promises reversal, comfort, mercy, and participation in God’s kingdom. Ask how these blessings challenge ordinary measures of success and power.

  • Who is called blessed?
  • Which qualities describe character, and which describe suffering or social condition?
  • What future does each blessing imagine?
Read Matthew 5:17–20 and 5:38–48
Jesus, Torah, retaliation, and love of enemies

These selections should be read together. Jesus denies that his mission simply abolishes Torah, then presses beyond retaliation toward nonvengeance and enemy-love.

  • How does Jesus describe his relation to the law and prophets?
  • What alternatives to retaliation appear?
  • Why is love of enemies connected to imitation of God?
Anchor phrases: “fulfill” · “turn the other cheek” · “love your enemies”
Read Matthew 6:9–15
The Lord’s Prayer and forgiveness

The prayer moves from God’s name, kingdom, and will to daily bread, forgiveness, testing, and deliverance. Pay special attention to the reciprocal language of forgiveness.

Read Matthew 7:12–14 and 7:21–27
The Golden Rule, the narrow way, and doing the teaching

Matthew’s sermon closes by distinguishing hearing from doing. Religious speech alone is not enough; the wise person builds through practice.

  • How does the Golden Rule summarize “the law and the prophets”?
  • What is the difference between saying and doing?
  • What makes one foundation stable?
Read Matthew 22:34–40
Love of God and neighbor

Jesus answers with two Torah passages: love of God and love of neighbor. Compare this reading with the Shema and Holiness Code from the Judaism packet.

Anchor phrases: “love the Lord your God” · “love your neighbor”

3 · Parables of Neighbor-Love and Mercy

Read Luke 10:25–37
The Good Samaritan

The story answers a legal expert’s attempt to define the limits of neighbor obligation. The Samaritan is socially and religiously unexpected, while respected figures pass by.

  • How does Jesus shift the question from “Who is my neighbor?” to becoming a neighbor?
  • What actions demonstrate compassion?
  • Why is the identity of the helper significant?
Read Luke 15:11–32
The Lost Son and the Elder Brother

Both sons are alienated: one through departure and waste, the other through resentment. The father’s extravagant welcome raises questions about repentance, restoration, fairness, and resistance to mercy.

  • What brings the younger son home?
  • What does the father do before the son can complete his planned speech?
  • Why does the elder brother refuse the celebration?

4 · Death and Resurrection

Read Mark 15:33–39
Jesus’s death

Mark presents darkness, Jesus’s cry of abandonment, the torn Temple curtain, and a Roman centurion’s confession. The scene combines desolation with revelation.

  • What does Jesus quote in his final cry?
  • What might the torn curtain signify within Mark’s story?
  • Who recognizes Jesus after his death?
Read Mark 16:1–8
The empty tomb and Mark’s earliest ending

The women find the stone moved and hear that Jesus has been raised. Most modern Bibles note that the earliest surviving manuscripts end at verse 8, with fear and silence rather than an appearance of Jesus. Read your Bible’s textual note.

Read 1 Corinthians 15:1–8
Paul’s received resurrection tradition

Paul reminds the Corinthians of a tradition he had received and passed on: Christ died, was buried, was raised, and appeared to witnesses. This is among the earliest surviving Christian summaries of the resurrection proclamation.

5 · Circumcision and Gentile Inclusion

The early Jesus movement began within Judaism. As Gentiles joined, Christians disputed whether male Gentile converts needed circumcision and fuller Torah observance.

Read Acts 15:1–11, 19–21, 28–29
The Jerusalem Council

Acts presents a communal debate involving testimony, scriptural reasoning, and a compromise letter. Note what burden is rejected and what expectations remain.

  • What do some believers demand of Gentile converts?
  • How does Peter argue from experience?
  • What decision is communicated to Gentile communities?
Read Galatians 2:11–16
Paul confronts Peter at Antioch

Paul’s own letter preserves a sharper conflict over shared meals between Jewish and Gentile believers. Compare this first-person account with the more orderly council narrative in Acts.

  • Why does Peter withdraw from table fellowship?
  • Why does Paul call the behavior hypocrisy?
  • How does the social practice of eating together become a theological issue?
Historical caution

These debates were internal disputes among early followers of Jesus, many of whom were Jewish. Do not use them to portray Judaism as a religion of empty legalism. The central question was how Gentiles could join a Jewish messianic movement and what covenantal obligations would apply to them.

6 · Representative Pauline Teachings

Read Philippians 2:5–11
Christ’s self-emptying, service, and exaltation

Paul places this poetic passage within an ethical appeal for humility and concern for others. Christ’s status is expressed through self-giving rather than grasping.

  • What movement occurs from divine status to servanthood and death?
  • How does God respond?
  • Why does Paul present Christ’s story as a model for communal life?
Read Galatians 3:23–29
Baptismal identity and belonging to Abraham

Paul describes a new communal identity “in Christ” that crosses major divisions. Ask what this claim means, and also what social differences continued in actual Christian communities.

Read 1 Corinthians 13:1–13
Love within a divided community

This chapter is often read at weddings, but Paul writes to a congregation divided by status, rivalry, and spiritual pride. Love is presented as the indispensable measure of gifts and knowledge.

Anchor phrases: “patient” · “kind” · “the greatest of these is love”

7 · James: Faith Enacted

Read James 1:22–27
Doers, not hearers only

James connects authentic religion with action, disciplined speech, and care for vulnerable people. Mere exposure to teaching can become self-deception if it does not shape conduct.

Read James 2:14–26
Faith without works is dead

James attacks a claim of faith that fails to feed or clothe people in need. His examples emphasize embodied loyalty and action rather than verbal assent alone.

Anchor phrase: “faith without works is dead”
Paul and James

Do not force the two writers into an easy agreement or a simple contradiction. Paul often opposes making “works of law,” especially circumcision and other boundary requirements, a condition of Gentile inclusion. James opposes empty claims of faith without merciful action. Their arguments have different immediate targets, while their emphases remain genuinely different.

Thought Questions

Answer each question in 5–8 complete sentences. Ground every response in at least two assigned passages and identify your Bible translation when quoting. These responses prepare you for the Christianity chatbot conversation.

Thought Question 1. Compare Mark 1:9–15, Mark 8:27–35, and John 1:1–14. What does each passage contribute to Christian claims about Jesus as Son, Messiah, suffering figure, and incarnate Word?

Thought Question 2. Using the Sermon on the Mount and at least one parable, explain how Jesus connects life under God’s kingdom with mercy, enemy-love, prayer, obedience, or care for outsiders.

Thought Question 3. Compare Acts 15 with Galatians 2:11–16. What was at stake in the debate over circumcision and shared meals, and what do the differing presentations reveal about conflict in the early Christian movement?

Thought Question 4. Using selections from Paul and James, explain how Christian faith is related to humility, love, communal inclusion, and action. Where do Paul and James emphasize different problems?

Supplemental Reading · The Nicene Creed

Why include a later creed?

The New Testament contains multiple voices and forms of language about Jesus. During later doctrinal disputes, church leaders formulated creeds to define an orthodox interpretation of God, Christ, and the Holy Spirit. The creed below is a modern classroom rendering of the form associated with the councils of Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381). Christian liturgical translations vary. Eastern churches omit the later Western phrase “and the Son” in the statement about the Spirit’s procession.

We believe in one God, the Father almighty, maker of heaven and earth, of all things visible and invisible.

We believe in one Lord, Jesus Christ, the only-begotten Son of God, begotten from the Father before all ages: God from God, Light from Light, true God from true God; begotten, not made; of one being with the Father. Through him all things were made.

For us and for our salvation he came down from heaven; by the Holy Spirit and the virgin Mary he became incarnate and became truly human. He was crucified for us under Pontius Pilate, suffered, and was buried. On the third day he rose again according to the Scriptures. He ascended into heaven and is seated at the right hand of the Father. He will come again in glory to judge the living and the dead, and his kingdom will have no end.

We believe in the Holy Spirit, the Lord and giver of life, who proceeds from the Father, who with the Father and the Son is worshiped and glorified, and who has spoken through the prophets.

We believe in one holy, universal, and apostolic church. We acknowledge one baptism for the forgiveness of sins. We look for the resurrection of the dead and the life of the age to come. Amen.
