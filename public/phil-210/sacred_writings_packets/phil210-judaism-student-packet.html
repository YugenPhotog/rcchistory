Primary-Source Student Reading Packet

Before You Read

This packet introduces several of the texts through which Judaism has understood creation, covenant, liberation, commandment, holiness, justice, exile, suffering, and the continuing work of interpretation. Most of the readings come from the Tanakh, the Jewish scriptural collection commonly divided into Torah, Prophets, and Writings. The final selections come from the rabbinic tradition, which helped shape Judaism after the destruction of the Second Temple.

Do not read these passages as a single systematic creed. Biblical books arose in different periods and literary forms: narrative, law, prophecy, poetry, and wisdom dialogue. They sometimes place different questions beside one another rather than resolving every tension. Rabbinic texts continue that conversation by interpreting inherited Torah and applying it to communal and ethical life.

The packet begins with two creation narratives, then moves through Abraham's call, the Exodus, Sinai, the Shema, and the Holiness Code. The prophets challenge worship separated from justice. Jeremiah and Psalm 137 explore life in exile from very different emotional positions. Job refuses easy explanations for undeserved suffering. Pirkei Avot and the Babylonian Talmud show Torah becoming an ongoing practice of study, debate, community, and action.

Translation and divine-name conventions

Biblical selections are a new PHIL 210 classroom adaptation of the Hebrew Masoretic Text, using the public-domain 1917 Jewish Publication Society translation as a textual base. The four Hebrew letters of the divine name (YHWH) appear as "the LORD," following a long Jewish reading tradition. In worship and study, many Jews say Adonai ("Lord") or HaShem ("the Name") rather than pronounce the written name. Rabbinic selections are freshly rendered from Hebrew or Aramaic source texts.

How to cite the reading

Use the book and verse or tractate reference supplied in the heading, for example: (Genesis 1:27), (Leviticus 19:34), (Job 38:4), (Pirkei Avot 1:14), or (Shabbat 31a).

Reading difficult sacred texts

Sacred writings sometimes preserve grief, anger, social hierarchy, violence, and ideas that modern readers find troubling. Read historically and closely. Ask what a passage is doing in its literary setting, how later Jewish interpreters have read it, and whether the text describes an emotion, issues a command, tells a story, or argues a position. Description is not always endorsement.

Key Terms

Term Working definition
Tanakh The Jewish scriptural collection: Torah (Teaching), Nevi'im (Prophets), and Ketuvim (Writings).
Torah Teaching or instruction; especially the Five Books of Moses, and more broadly the revealed and interpreted guidance of Judaism.
Covenant (berit) A binding relationship that creates promises and obligations between God and a people.
Image of God (tzelem Elohim) The Genesis 1 description of human beings as bearing a distinctive God-related dignity and vocation.
Holy (qadosh) Set apart for God and aligned with divine purposes; in Leviticus, holiness includes ethical conduct in ordinary life.
Resident stranger (ger) A non-Israelite living within Israelite society and therefore vulnerable to exploitation but protected by Torah.
Justice / righteousness (tzedek) Right ordering of relationships and public life; the Hebrew term can carry both legal and moral meanings.
Steadfast kindness (hesed) Loyal, enduring kindness rooted in relationship and covenant.
Shema The declaration beginning "Hear, Israel" in Deuteronomy 6:4, recited in Jewish prayer.
Rabbinic Judaism The form of Judaism centered on Torah study, interpretation, prayer, and communal practice that developed after the Second Temple period.
Mishnah / Talmud The Mishnah is an early rabbinic collection of oral teaching; the Talmud combines Mishnah with extensive rabbinic discussion and interpretation.

Selected Readings

Read the passages in sequence. Pay attention to how narrative, law, prophecy, poetry, wisdom, and rabbinic interpretation develop different but related dimensions of Jewish religious life.

1 Creation, Human Dignity, and Human Limits

Genesis opens with two creation narratives. The first presents an ordered cosmos in which humanity, male and female, bears the image of God and Sabbath completes creation. The second narrows its focus to the human, the garden, relationship, moral limits, and exile.

2 Covenant and Liberation

The call of Abram joins divine blessing to a responsibility that reaches beyond one family. The burning-bush narrative presents God as hearing enslaved people and commissioning Moses. At Sinai, Israel's special status is expressed as a demanding vocation.

3 Commandment, Memory, and Love of God

At Sinai, commandment begins with a reminder of liberation: "I brought you out of the house of slavery." Deuteronomy then places covenantal teaching in the heart, home, body, and daily schedule. Love of God is joined to justice for the vulnerable stranger.

4 Holiness in Everyday Life

The Holiness Code refuses to separate devotion to God from economic, legal, interpersonal, and communal conduct. Holiness includes harvest practices, wages, disability, truthful speech, impartial judgment, love of neighbor, care for immigrants, and honest commerce.

5 Prophetic Justice, Exile, and Memory

The prophets do not reject worship itself. They reject worship used as a substitute for justice. The exile readings then show two responses to displacement: constructive responsibility for a foreign city and anguished refusal to forget a destroyed home.

6 Wisdom and the Problem of Suffering

The book of Job stages a dispute over whether suffering can be explained as deserved punishment. Job's friends eventually defend conventional answers; Job protests. The divine speeches expand the frame to a creation whose complexity exceeds human control, yet the conclusion also says Job spoke more rightly than his friends.

7 Rabbinic Wisdom and the Ongoing Life of Torah

Rabbinic Judaism treats Torah as a living inheritance requiring study, argument, practice, and transmission. These brief teachings emphasize community, moral urgency, humility, self-discipline, and the duty to keep learning.

Pirkei Avot | Sayings of the Fathers

Pirkei Avot 1:2

Shimon the Righteous was among the last survivors of the Great Assembly. He used to say: The world stands on three things - on Torah, on worship, and on acts of loving-kindness.

Pirkei Avot 1:14

Hillel used to say: If I am not for myself, who will be for me? But if I am only for myself, what am I? And if not now, when?

Pirkei Avot 2:4

Hillel said: Do not separate yourself from the community. Do not trust yourself until the day of your death. Do not judge another person until you have reached that person's place. Do not say something cannot be understood if in the end it can be understood. And do not say, 'When I have free time I will study,' for perhaps you will never have free time.

Pirkei Avot 2:16

Rabbi Tarfon used to say: You are not required to finish the work, but neither are you free to abandon it. If you have studied much Torah, you will receive great reward. Your employer is trustworthy to pay the wages of your labor, but know that the full reward of the righteous belongs to the world to come.

Pirkei Avot 4:1

Ben Zoma said: Who is wise? One who learns from every person. Who is strong? One who masters destructive impulses. Who is rich? One who rejoices in one's portion. Who is honored? One who honors other people.

Translation note: Pirkei Avot is a tractate of the Mishnah devoted largely to ethical and educational teachings attributed to early rabbis.

Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 31a | Hillel and the Torah on One Foot

Shabbat 31a

A non-Jew came before Shammai and said, 'Convert me on condition that you teach me the whole Torah while I stand on one foot.' Shammai drove him away with the builder's measuring stick in his hand. The man then came before Hillel, who converted him. Hillel said: 'What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow. That is the whole Torah; the rest is interpretation. Go and study.'

Translation note: The story contrasts two teachers without claiming that Torah can be reduced to a slogan. Hillel ends with an imperative: "Go and study."

Thought Questions

Answer each question in 5-8 complete sentences. Ground every response in at least two specific passage references. These questions prepare you for the Judaism chatbot conversation.

Thought Question 1
Genesis 1 and Genesis 2-3 tell creation differently. What does each narrative suggest about human dignity, responsibility, relationship, and limits? Explain one important tension or complement between them.

Thought Question 2
The Ten Commandments begin with liberation from slavery, while the Shema and Holiness Code extend covenant into daily life. Explain how freedom, commandment, love of God, and care for neighbors or strangers fit together in these readings.

Thought Question 3
Compare the voices of Amos, Micah, Jeremiah 29, and Psalm 137. How do these texts connect faith to justice, civic responsibility, memory, grief, or resistance under conditions of political power?

Thought Question 4
Job rejects a simple explanation of suffering, while Pirkei Avot and Hillel emphasize study, community, self-mastery, and unfinished moral work. Using both biblical and rabbinic readings, explain what a wise response to suffering or moral uncertainty might require.

Translation and Source Note

Biblical readings: PHIL 210 classroom adaptations from the Hebrew Masoretic Text. The public-domain 1917 Jewish Publication Society translation supplied the principal English base. Wording has been modernized and compared across standard Jewish and scholarly renderings. Verse numbering follows common English editions.

Rabbinic readings: fresh classroom renderings from the Hebrew text of Pirkei Avot and the Hebrew-Aramaic text of Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 31a. Short explanatory notes identify places where translation or historical context affects interpretation.

The purpose of this packet is close reading in an introductory world-religions course. It does not replace a full Jewish study Bible, traditional commentary, or study with qualified teachers. Students should distinguish the historical meaning of a passage, later Jewish interpretation, and their own evaluation.

Editorial principle

The packet does not smooth away disagreement among biblical books or between biblical and rabbinic voices. Its selections are arranged to let students see Judaism as a tradition of covenantal memory, ethical practice, interpretation, questioning, and continuing study.
