The Bhagavad Gita

Duty, Action, Knowledge, and Devotion

Selected verses in a course translation

Before You Read

The Bhagavad Gita, “Song of the Lord,” is a dialogue embedded in the Mahābhārata, the great Sanskrit epic of ancient India. The conversation takes place immediately before a catastrophic war between two branches of a royal family. Arjuna, one of the Pāṇḍava warriors, asks Krishna—his friend and charioteer—to position their chariot between the armies. When Arjuna recognizes relatives, teachers, and friends on both sides, he loses the will to fight.

Krishna’s response moves through several forms of religious reasoning. He speaks about the enduring self, social and moral duty, disciplined action, meditation, divine manifestation, and devotion. The text does not simply choose action over reflection. It asks how a person can act decisively without treating the results as personal possessions.

This packet presents selected verses rather than the Gita’s complete 700-verse argument. Short introductions supply the immediate context of omitted passages. Read the selections as parts of a developing conversation, not as isolated quotations. Notice especially how the meanings of dharma and yoga shift with context.

A note on perspective  The Bhagavad Gita is interpreted differently across Hindu traditions and by modern scholars. This packet introduces major themes without treating any one interpretation as the only Hindu reading.

People and Essential Terms

Arjuna: Pāṇḍava warrior whose crisis prompts the teaching.

Krishna: Arjuna’s charioteer, teacher, and ultimately revealed divine lord.

dharma: Right order, duty, law, or way of life; its meaning depends on context.

karma: Action and its morally consequential results.

yoga: A disciplined way of joining, focusing, or integrating oneself; not limited to posture practice.

ātman: The self; translations and Hindu schools differ over how it should be understood.

bhakti: Devotion, loving participation, or allegiance directed toward a deity.

avatāra: A divine “descent”; a later technical term often used to interpret Krishna’s manifestations.

Names and Epithets in the Reading

Names for Krishna: Keśava, Govinda, and Hari.

Names for Arjuna: Pārtha (“son of Pṛthā”), son of Kuntī, descendant of Bharata, Dhanañjaya (“winner of wealth”), Savyasācin (“the ambidextrous archer”), and Pāṇḍu’s son.

Gāṇḍīva: Arjuna’s celebrated bow; its slipping from his hand signals the collapse of a legendary warrior’s resolve.

Janaka: An exemplary philosopher-king invoked to show that spiritually accomplished people can attain perfection through action.

O King: Sañjaya is narrating the battlefield events to the blind king Dhṛtarāṣṭra.

1. The Crisis on the Battlefield

The armies of two branches of the same royal family face one another at Kurukṣetra. Arjuna, a celebrated warrior, asks Krishna—his charioteer—to place their chariot between the armies. When Arjuna recognizes teachers, relatives, and friends on both sides, the abstract duty of war becomes a personal moral catastrophe.

1.28

Overcome by profound compassion and sinking into despair, Arjuna spoke: “Seeing these relatives of mine, Krishna, assembled and eager to fight—”

1.29

My limbs give way; my mouth is dry. My body trembles, and my hair stands on end.

1.30

The bow Gāṇḍīva slips from my hand, and my skin burns. I cannot remain standing; my mind seems to whirl.

1.31

I see omens of misfortune, Keśava. I can see no good in killing my own people in battle.

1.32

I desire neither victory, nor kingship, nor pleasures. What use is a kingdom to us, Govinda? What use are enjoyments, or even life?

2. The Enduring Self

Arjuna refuses to fight and asks Krishna to instruct him. Krishna begins by distinguishing the perishable body from the enduring embodied self. These verses address death from a particular Hindu philosophical perspective; they do not yet resolve every ethical concern Arjuna raised.

2.11

You grieve for those who should not be grieved for, yet you speak as though you were wise. Those who are truly discerning grieve neither for the dead nor for the living.

2.12

There was never a time when I did not exist, nor you, nor these kings; nor will any of us cease to exist hereafter.

2.13

Just as the embodied self passes in this body through childhood, youth, and old age, so it passes into another body. A discerning person is not confused by this.

2.18

These bodies are said to have an end; the embodied self is eternal, indestructible, and immeasurable. Therefore, fight, descendant of Bharata.

2.19

One who thinks the self kills, and one who thinks it is killed, both fail to understand. The self neither kills nor is killed.

2.20

It is not born, nor does it die. It has not come into being, nor will it cease to be. Unborn, eternal, everlasting, and ancient, it is not killed when the body is killed.

2.21

How can a person who knows this self to be indestructible, eternal, unborn, and unchanging cause anyone to be killed, or kill anyone, Pārtha?

2.22

As a person casts off worn-out clothes and puts on others that are new, so the embodied self casts off worn-out bodies and enters others that are new.

3. Dharma and the Warrior’s Obligation

Krishna next turns from the nature of the self to Arjuna’s social and moral position. Arjuna is a kṣatriya, a member of the class traditionally responsible for protection and rule. Krishna argues that refusing this particular conflict would mean abandoning Arjuna’s own dharma.

2.31

Considering your own dharma as well, you should not waver. For a warrior, nothing is higher than a battle required by dharma.

2.32

Fortunate are the warriors who encounter such a battle unsought, Pārtha—an open gateway to heaven.

2.33

But if you refuse this battle required by dharma, you will abandon your own dharma and honor, and you will incur guilt.

4. Action Without Attachment

The Gita does not solve Arjuna’s crisis by recommending withdrawal from action. It recasts action as a discipline: perform what is yours to do without making personal possession of the outcome your motive. Action can then serve sacrifice, social order, and the welfare of the world.

2.47

Your proper sphere is action alone, never its results. Never let the results of action be your motive, but do not become attached to inaction either.

2.48

Established in yoga, perform your actions while giving up attachment, Dhanañjaya. Remain even-minded in success and failure; this equanimity is called yoga.

2.49

Action is far inferior to the discipline of understanding, Dhanañjaya. Seek refuge in this understanding. Pitiful are those whose motive is the result.

2.50

One joined to disciplined understanding relinquishes both good and evil deeds here. Therefore devote yourself to yoga: yoga is skillfulness in action.

3.7

But the person who restrains the senses with the mind and undertakes the discipline of action with the organs of action, without attachment, excels.

3.8

Perform the action that is yours to do. Action is better than inaction; without action, you could not even maintain your body.

3.9

Except for action performed as sacrifice, action binds this world. Therefore, son of Kuntī, perform action for that purpose, free from attachment.

3.19

Therefore, always perform the action that must be done, without attachment. By performing action without attachment, a person reaches the highest.

3.20

It was through action that Janaka and others attained perfection. You should also act with the welfare and stability of the world in view.

3.21

Whatever a respected person does, others follow. Whatever standard that person establishes, the world adopts.

5. The Divine Restoration of Dharma

Krishna now reveals that his teaching and presence are not merely human. When the moral order declines, he says, he manifests himself within history. Later Hindu traditions frequently interpret these verses through the doctrine of avatāra, divine descent.

4.7

Whenever dharma declines and adharma rises, descendant of Bharata, I bring myself forth.

4.8

For the protection of the good, the destruction of evildoers, and the reestablishment of dharma, I come into being age after age.

6. Training the Mind

The sixth chapter treats meditation as repeated discipline rather than a single moment of insight. Krishna acknowledges that the mind wanders; the practitioner’s task is to notice and return it.

6.26

Wherever the restless, unsteady mind wanders, one should draw it back from there and bring it under the direction of the self.

7. The Universal Form

Arjuna asks to see Krishna’s divine form. Krishna grants him a transformed mode of sight. Arjuna sees the cosmos gathered within Krishna, including creation and destruction. The vision reframes the coming battle within divine power and cosmic time, but it also terrifies him.

11.8

But you cannot see me with your ordinary eye. I give you divine sight; behold my sovereign power.

11.9

Sañjaya said: Having spoken thus, O King, Hari, the great Lord of Yoga, revealed to Pārtha his supreme, sovereign form.

11.12

If the light of a thousand suns were to rise at once in the sky, that might resemble the radiance of that great being.

11.13

There, within the body of the God of gods, Pāṇḍu’s son saw the whole universe in its many divisions gathered together as one.

11.32

The Blessed Lord said: I am Time, the mighty destroyer of worlds, now engaged in destroying these peoples. Even without you, none of the warriors arrayed in the opposing armies will remain.

11.33

Therefore rise and win glory. Conquer your enemies and enjoy a flourishing kingdom. They have already been struck down by me; you, Savyasācin, become merely the instrument.

8. The Character of Devotion

After the overwhelming vision, the Gita turns toward bhakti, devotion to Krishna. Devotion is described not only as an inward feeling but also through stable habits of character and relationships with other beings.

12.13

One who hates no creature, who is friendly and compassionate, free from possessiveness and ego, even-minded in pain and pleasure, and patient—

12.14

the disciplined person who is always content, self-controlled, and firm in resolve, whose mind and understanding are dedicated to me—such a devotee is dear to me.

12.15

One from whom the world does not shrink, and who does not shrink from the world, who is free from elation, resentment, fear, and agitation, is dear to me.

12.16

One who is independent of expectation, pure, capable, impartial, untroubled, and who relinquishes self-serving undertakings—such a devotee is dear to me.

12.17

One who neither rejoices nor hates, neither grieves nor desires, and who gives up concern with favorable and unfavorable results—such a devoted person is dear to me.

12.18

One who is the same toward enemy and friend, in honor and dishonor, in cold and heat, in pleasure and pain, and who is free from attachment—

12.19

who regards blame and praise alike, is restrained in speech, content with whatever comes, without a fixed sense of possession, steady-minded, and devoted—that person is dear to me.

12.20

Those who honor this immortal teaching of dharma as it has been declared, who trust it and make me their highest aim—those devotees are exceedingly dear to me.

9. Deliberation and Surrender

Near the end, Krishna first tells Arjuna to reflect and choose. He then offers a final, sweeping command to seek refuge in him alone. Reading these verses together reveals one of the Gita’s central tensions: disciplined choice culminates in surrender.

18.63

Thus I have taught you wisdom more secret than secrecy itself. Reflect on it completely, and then act as you choose.

18.66

Abandoning all dharmas, come to me alone for refuge. I will release you from every evil consequence; do not grieve.

  • C. A form of meditation.
  • D. A royal title.

  • A. Dharma.
  • B. The cosmos.
  • C. The embodied self in a body.
