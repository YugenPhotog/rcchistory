Before You Read

The Dhammapada is a collection of short verses in the Pali Canon of Theravada Buddhism. Its 423 verses address the mind, ethical conduct, wisdom, craving, suffering, and liberation. Individual verses may have originated in different teaching situations; later commentaries supply stories for many of them.

This packet groups selected verses by theme rather than reproducing the entire anthology. Read each section as a compact argument or practice prompt, but also notice shifts in vocabulary and emphasis. A repeated refrain may look simple while making a demanding claim about how suffering is produced and ended.

The course translation is based on the Pali text of the Chaṭṭha Saṅgāyana edition. It favors clear contemporary English while retaining key terms where one English equivalent would narrow the meaning too much.

How to cite the reading

Use the verse number, not the packet page: (Dhammapada 5) or, after naming the text, simply (5). For a range, use (129–130).

Selected Readings

1. Mind, Action, and Hostility

The Dhammapada opens with a strong claim: experience is shaped by the quality of mind from which speech and action arise. It then turns from individual intention to a cycle of grievance and retaliation.

1

Mind precedes phenomena; mind is their chief; they are made by mind. If one speaks or acts with a corrupted mind, suffering follows, as the wheel follows the ox’s foot.

2

Mind precedes phenomena; mind is their chief; they are made by mind. If one speaks or acts with a clear mind, happiness follows like a shadow that never leaves.

3

“He insulted me, struck me, defeated me, robbed me” – in those who hold on to such thoughts, hostility is not stilled.

4

“He insulted me, struck me, defeated me, robbed me” – in those who do not hold on to such thoughts, hostility is stilled.

5

Hostilities never end through hostility in this world. They end through non-hostility. This is an enduring principle.

2. Heedfulness

Heedfulness means sustained moral and mental attentiveness. These verses contrast it with negligence and connect disciplined effort to nirvana.

21

Heedfulness is the path to the deathless; negligence is the path to death. The heedful do not die; the negligent are as if already dead.

22

Clearly understanding this distinction, the wise delight in heedfulness and rejoice in the domain of the noble ones.

23

Meditating, persevering, and always energetic, the wise reach nirvana, the unsurpassed freedom from bonds.

3. Training the Mind

The mind is pictured as unstable, quick, and difficult to guard. The verses do not treat this instability as permanent; repeated training can redirect it.

33

As a fletcher straightens an arrow, the wise person straightens the quivering, unsteady mind, difficult to guard and restrain.

34

Like a fish pulled from its watery home and thrown onto dry ground, this mind thrashes about to escape Māra’s domain.

35

The mind is hard to restrain, quick, and alights wherever it desires. Good is the taming of the mind; a tamed mind brings happiness.

42

Whatever an enemy may do to an enemy, or one hostile person to another, a wrongly directed mind can do still greater harm.

43

Neither mother nor father nor any other relative can do as much good as a rightly directed mind.

4. Self-Examination

The practice now turns inward. Moral attention begins with one’s own actions and omissions rather than another person’s faults.

50

Do not attend to the faults of others, or to what others have done or left undone. Attend instead to what you yourself have done and left undone.

5. Foolishness and Learning

In the Dhammapada, a fool is not simply someone who lacks information. The deeper failure is an inability to recognize, absorb, and practice the teaching.

60

Long is the night for one who is awake; long is a league for one who is tired. Long is the round of rebirth for fools who do not know the true teaching.

62

“I have sons; I have wealth” – the fool torments himself. He does not even possess himself; how then sons, how then wealth?

64

Even if a fool associates with a wise person for a lifetime, he understands the teaching no more than a spoon tastes soup.

65

Even if a discerning person associates with a wise person for only a moment, he quickly understands the teaching, as the tongue tastes soup.

6. Self-Mastery

A conventional victory may display force, but this verse ranks mastery of oneself above conquest of others.

103

One may defeat a thousand men a thousand times in battle, but the greatest victor is the one who conquers oneself.

7. Shared Vulnerability and Nonviolence

These paired verses ground nonviolence in a simple act of moral imagination: compare others with yourself. Fear of violence and love of life become reasons not to kill or cause killing.

129

All tremble at violence; all fear death. Comparing others with oneself, one should neither kill nor cause another to kill.

130

All tremble at violence; life is dear to all. Comparing others with oneself, one should neither kill nor cause another to kill.

8. Craving Exposed

Tradition places these verses at the Buddha’s awakening. The speaker recognizes the “house-builder” responsible for repeated existence and declares that its work has ended.

153

Through many births in saṃsāra I wandered, seeking but not finding the builder of this house. Repeated birth is suffering.

154

House-builder, you are seen! You will not build a house again. All your rafters are broken; the ridgepole is destroyed. The mind has reached the unconditioned; it has attained the end of craving.

9. Moral Agency and Refuge

Here attā functions in the practical sense of oneself. The verses emphasize training, responsibility, and the limits of relying on another person for purification.

160

Oneself is one’s own protector; who else could the protector be? With oneself well trained, one gains a refuge difficult to gain.

165

By oneself is evil done; by oneself is one defiled. By oneself is evil left undone; by oneself is one purified. Purity and impurity depend on oneself; no one purifies another.

10. A Summary of the Teaching

These famous verses compress Buddhist practice into moral restraint, cultivation of the good, purification of mind, patience, moderation, and disciplined contemplation.

183

Avoid all evil, cultivate the good, and purify your own mind: this is the teaching of the Buddhas.

184

Patient endurance is the highest austerity; nirvana is supreme, say the Buddhas. One who harms another is not a renunciant; one who injures another is not an ascetic.

185

Not insulting, not harming, restraint according to the discipline, moderation in food, a secluded place to sit and sleep, and commitment to the higher mind: this is the teaching of the Buddhas.

11. Happiness Without Hatred or Possession

Happiness is presented as a way of living that does not depend on matching the hostility, distress, or grasping of the surrounding world.

197

Happily indeed we live, free from hostility among those who are hostile. Among hostile people, we dwell without hostility.

198

Happily indeed we live, free from affliction among those who are afflicted. Among afflicted people, we dwell without affliction.

199

Happily indeed we live, free from grasping among those who grasp. Among grasping people, we dwell without grasping.

200

Happily indeed we live, possessing nothing. We will feed on joy like the radiant gods.

12. Attachment, Grief, and Fear

A sequence of related terms – what is dear, affection, delight, sensual desire, and craving – is linked to grief and fear. The repetition invites comparison among these forms of attachment.

212

From what is dear arises grief; from what is dear arises fear. For one freed from what is dear, there is no grief – how then fear?

213

From affection arises grief; from affection arises fear. For one freed from affection, there is no grief – how then fear?

214

From delight arises grief; from delight arises fear. For one freed from delight, there is no grief – how then fear?

215

From sensual desire arises grief; from sensual desire arises fear. For one freed from sensual desire, there is no grief – how then fear?

216

From craving arises grief; from craving arises fear. For one freed from craving, there is no grief – how then fear?

13. Overcoming Anger

The response to anger is not another form of anger. The text pairs non-anger with goodness, generosity, and truthfulness as active practices.

223

Conquer anger with non-anger; conquer what is bad with what is good; conquer the miser with giving; conquer the liar with truth.

224

Speak the truth; do not become angry; when asked, give even a little. By these three practices one may approach the gods.

14. Three Characteristics

The final selection presents three marks central to Buddhist analysis: impermanence, suffering or unsatisfactoriness, and not-self. Notice that the first two verses speak of conditioned things, while the third speaks more broadly of all phenomena.

277

“All conditioned things are impermanent.” When one sees this with wisdom, one becomes disenchanted with suffering. This is the path to purification.

278

“All conditioned things are suffering.” When one sees this with wisdom, one becomes disenchanted with suffering. This is the path to purification.

279

“All phenomena are not-self.” When one sees this with wisdom, one becomes disenchanted with suffering. This is the path to purification.
