New Testament Sacred Text Workshop

Guided Reading Check — In-Class Activity

Instructions

For each passage below, read the text carefully before answering. The first two questions for each passage check your reading comprehension — there is a correct answer grounded in the text. The third question is reflective — there is no single right answer, but your response should show genuine engagement with what you just read.

Write in complete sentences. When you are finished, click Save to PDF at the bottom of the page and upload the PDF file to Canvas.

Passage 1

Luke 4:16–21 — The Nazareth Manifesto

Jesus has returned to his hometown of Nazareth after his baptism and time in the wilderness. On the Sabbath he enters the synagogue, as was his custom, and is handed the scroll of the prophet Isaiah. What he reads — and what he says afterward — functions as his public mission statement. This is the moment Jesus defines, in his own words, what he believes he has been sent to do. Pay close attention to who he says he has come for and what he claims is happening in that room.
GRC 1Reading

Jesus reads from the scroll of Isaiah in the synagogue. According to the passage, what specific groups does Jesus say the Spirit has anointed him to serve? List at least three from the text.

GRC 2Reading

After reading from Isaiah, Jesus hands the scroll back to the attendant and sits down. What does he then say to the people watching him, and how do they initially react?

GRC 3Reflection

Jesus stands in his hometown synagogue and declares that an ancient prophecy has been fulfilled right now, in front of them. If you had been sitting in that synagogue, how do you think you would have responded — and what would have made that moment either compelling or difficult to believe?

Passage 2

Luke 10:25–37 — The Good Samaritan

A legal expert approaches Jesus with a question about eternal life — but the exchange quickly becomes a debate about the boundaries of moral obligation. Jesus responds not with a definition but with a story. The parable’s hero is a Samaritan, a member of a group that first-century Jewish audiences would have regarded with deep suspicion and hostility. By the time Jesus asks his final question, the lawyer’s original question has been turned completely inside out. Read the exchange carefully, paying attention to who asks what, and how Jesus redirects each question.
GRC 4Reading

A lawyer asks Jesus two questions in this passage. What are they, and how does Jesus respond to each one? Be specific about the order of the exchange.

GRC 5Reading

Three people pass the injured man on the road. Who are they, and what does each one do? Why would the identity of the third person — the one who helps — have been surprising to the original audience?

GRC 6Reflection

The parable ends with Jesus turning the lawyer’s question inside out: instead of “who is my neighbor?” Jesus asks “who acted as a neighbor?” Why do you think that reframe matters? Does it change what the parable is asking of the reader?

✦   ✦   ✦

Passage 3

1 Corinthians 13:1–13 — The Love Chapter

Paul’s first letter to the Corinthians is not a meditation on romance — it is a corrective letter to a fractured, competitive church community that was ranking members by their spiritual gifts and using those gifts as status markers. Speaking in tongues, prophecy, and miraculous knowledge had become sources of pride and division. Paul’s famous hymn to love is his response to that conflict: a deliberate argument that how you act matters more than what you can do. Read it as a pointed intervention, not a poem, and notice how the opening verses set up everything that follows.
GRC 7Reading

Paul opens the chapter by listing several impressive spiritual gifts and abilities. What does he say each one amounts to if it is not accompanied by love? Be specific about the examples he gives.

GRC 8Reading

Verses 4–7 define love through a series of concrete statements about what love does and does not do. List at least five characteristics Paul attributes to love in this section — some positive, some negative.

GRC 9Reflection

Paul is writing to a specific church community that was apparently competitive, fractured, and preoccupied with spiritual status. Knowing that context, do you read this passage differently than you would as a standalone poem or wedding reading? What changes when you treat it as a corrective letter rather than a celebration?

✦   ✦   ✦

Passage 4

Ephesians 2:8–9 & Romans 3:28 — Paul on Faith and Salvation

These two short passages represent the core of Pauline theology on one of Christianity’s most contested questions: how is a person made right before God? Paul’s answer is unambiguous — salvation is not something you earn or achieve through moral effort or religious observance. It is received, not accomplished. This position, sometimes called the doctrine of grace alone or faith alone, would later become the central flashpoint of the Protestant Reformation. As you read, notice the precision of Paul’s language — he is not just making a positive claim, he is explicitly ruling something out and explaining why.
GRC 10Reading

In Ephesians 2:8–9, Paul identifies two things: where salvation comes from and where it does not come from. What are both, and what reason does Paul give for ruling out the second?

GRC 11Reading

Romans 3:28 makes Paul’s position in a single compressed statement. What specific claim does he make, and what phrase does he use to describe the relationship between faith and the law?

GRC 12Reflection

Paul is writing to communities hungry for belonging and desperate to know how to be right before God. Why do you think the message “you cannot earn this” would have been either liberating or deeply unsettling to a first-century audience — and which do you think it would be for most people today?

Passage 5

James 2:14–26 — Faith and Works

The letter of James may be the most uncomfortable text in the New Testament if you have just finished reading Paul. James does not soften his position or qualify it carefully — he states it bluntly, illustrates it with a concrete example, and then backs it up with two figures from the Hebrew Bible. Whether James was directly responding to Paul’s teaching, to a misreading of Paul, or simply articulating an independent tradition is a question scholars still debate. What is not debated is that the tension is real. Read James on his own terms first, without trying to resolve the contradiction. The group discussion will be your opportunity to work through it together.
GRC 13Reading

James opens with a blunt rhetorical question about faith without works. What concrete scenario does he use to illustrate his point, and what does he conclude from it?

GRC 14Reading

James invokes two Old Testament figures as evidence for his argument. Who are they, what did each one do, and what does James say their actions demonstrated?

GRC 15Reflection

You have now read both Paul and James. Without resolving the debate yet — save that for discussion — which position do you find more intuitive, and why? What assumptions about religion, morality, or human nature are you bringing to that instinct?

Group Discussion

The Faith & Works Debate — Paul vs. James

You have now read two writers who appear to directly contradict each other on one of Christianity’s most consequential questions. Paul insists that salvation comes through faith alone, not works — so that no one can boast. James insists that faith without works is dead — and backs it up with Abraham and Rahab. This tension was so sharp that Martin Luther wanted to remove James from the New Testament entirely, calling it an “epistle of straw.” Your group’s task is not to resolve this debate definitively — scholars and theologians have been arguing about it for two thousand years — but to map the disagreement clearly and think carefully about what is actually at stake.
D–1Discussion

Establish the positions. Before your group can argue, you need to agree on what each writer is actually claiming. In your own words, state Paul’s position in one sentence and James’s position in one sentence. Then identify the single sharpest point of conflict between them — the place where the two sentences most directly collide.

D–2Discussion

Test the standard resolution. Many theologians argue that Paul and James are not actually contradicting each other — that Paul is addressing how you are saved (by faith, not moral achievement), while James is addressing what salvation looks like afterward (genuine faith produces action). Does your group find this resolution convincing? What would Paul have to concede for it to work? What would James have to concede?

D–3Discussion

Consider the real-world stakes. Imagine two churches: one built entirely on Paul’s framework (salvation by grace through faith alone) and one built entirely on James’s framework (faith that does not produce action is dead). What would each community look like in practice? What problems might each one develop over time? Which framework do you think is more vulnerable to abuse — and why?

D–4Discussion

Connect back to the prophetic tradition. Earlier in this course you read Amos 5 and Isaiah 58 — texts in which the prophets argued that ritual observance without justice is worthless before God. Where does that prophetic tradition fit in the Paul/James debate? Is James continuing the Hebrew prophets’ argument in a new key? Or is something genuinely different happening in the New Testament context?
