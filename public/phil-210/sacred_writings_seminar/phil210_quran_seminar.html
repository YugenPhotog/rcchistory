Passage 1

Surah Al-Fatiha (1) & Surah Al-Ikhlas (112) — The Opening and the Declaration

Muslims recite Surah Al-Fatiha — the seven-verse opening of the Quran — in every unit of the five daily prayers. It functions as both an act of worship and a summary of the believer’s entire relationship with God: praise, acknowledgment of dependence, and a petition for guidance. Surah Al-Ikhlas, just four verses long, is sometimes called one-third of the Quran in Islamic tradition because it distills the entire doctrine of God’s nature into a single concentrated statement. Together, these two Surahs introduce the theological core of Islam. Pay close attention to what the believer asks for and how God is described.
GRC 1Reading

In Surah Al-Fatiha, what does the believer ask God for, and what two paths does the Surah contrast? Be specific about how each path is described in the text.

GRC 2Reading

Surah Al-Ikhlas (112) makes four distinct statements about the nature of God. List all four. What concept does this Surah most directly and emphatically reject?

GRC 3Reflection

Al-Fatiha describes a God who is “Lord of the Worlds” and “Master of the Day of Judgment” — and yet the believer can petition this God directly for guidance. Does that combination of transcendence and accessibility surprise you? What does it suggest about how Islam understands the relationship between God and the individual?

Passage 2

Surah Al-Ahzab 33:21 & 33:40 — The Seal of the Prophets

These two verses from Surah Al-Ahzab establish the Quran’s definitive teaching on Muhammad’s nature and authority. Verse 33:21 introduces the concept of Muhammad as uswa hasana — a “beautiful model” or “excellent example” — making his life a template for how believers should live. Verse 33:40 assigns him the title “Seal of the Prophets,” placing him at the end of a long prophetic line that includes Abraham, Moses, and Jesus. Read both verses carefully. The Quran is making a precise theological claim: Muhammad is fully human, decisively final, and practically exemplary — all three at once.
GRC 4Reading

According to verse 33:21, for whom is Muhammad described as an “excellent example,” and what conditions does the verse attach to that claim? What does this tell you about the practical function Muhammad is meant to serve in a believer’s life?

GRC 5Reading

Verse 33:40 states that Muhammad “is not the father of any of your men, but he is the Messenger of God and the Seal of the Prophets.” What is the theological significance of the title “Seal of the Prophets”? What does it rule out about the possibility of future prophets?

GRC 6Reflection

The Quran insists simultaneously that Muhammad is fully human (not divine) and that his life is the definitive model for all believers. Do you find that combination coherent or in tension? How does it compare to how other religions you have studied in this course treat their founders or central figures?

Passage 3

Surah Al-Baqarah 2:177 & 2:183–185 — Righteousness and the Practice of Faith

Surah Al-Baqarah is the longest chapter of the Quran and its most comprehensive legal and ethical text. Verse 2:177 is one of the most important single verses in the entire Quran: it redefines righteousness away from mere ritual observance toward a comprehensive vision of belief, charity, and moral character. Verses 2:183–185 then introduce Sawm — the obligation to fast during Ramadan — and are notable for the accommodations they make for the sick and the traveler. Read carefully and notice how the Quran moves between describing who a righteous person is and specifying what a righteous person does.
GRC 7Reading

Verse 2:177 lists multiple elements of righteousness. Identify at least five distinct components the verse includes — covering belief, charity, and conduct. How does the verse characterize the people who fulfill all of these?

GRC 8Reading

Verses 2:183–185 introduce the obligation of Ramadan fasting. What reason does the text give for why fasting is required? What two categories of people are permitted to skip the fast, and what are they required to do instead?

GRC 9Reflection

Verse 2:177 opens by saying that “righteousness is not that you turn your faces toward the east or the west” — a direct challenge to the idea that correct ritual posture is the heart of religion. What do you think the Quran is pushing back against here? Does this critique remind you of anything you have read in the Hebrew prophets earlier in this course?

Passage 4

Surah Al-Baqarah 2:190–193 & 2:256 — The Ethics of Struggle

These verses represent the Quran’s first and foundational treatment of armed conflict. Verses 2:190–193 were revealed in the context of the early Muslim community in Medina, which faced military attack from Meccan forces that had expelled them from their homes. The Quran permits fighting here — but hedges the permission with explicit conditions and limits. Verse 2:256, one of the most quoted verses in Islamic interfaith dialogue, states the Quran’s position on religious coercion. Read these passages together and pay close attention to what the text permits, what it prohibits, and what conditions it attaches to each.
GRC 10Reading

Verses 2:190–193 both permit and constrain fighting. What specific limits does the text place on warfare — who may be targeted, under what conditions, and when must fighting stop? Identify at least three specific constraints from the text.

GRC 11Reading

Verse 2:256 states: “There is no compulsion in religion.” What does the verse say follows from this? What contrast does it draw, and what does it suggest is the basis of genuine faith?

GRC 12Reflection

The same chapter that permits armed defense also declares “no compulsion in religion.” Do you find these two positions coherent together, or do they pull in different directions? What does the combination suggest about how the Quran understands the relationship between force and faith?

Passage 5

Surah Al-Ma’idah 5:32 & Surah At-Tawbah 9:4–6 — The Sanctity of Life and the Sword Verse

Verse 9:5 — the so-called “Sword Verse” — is one of the most cited and most misunderstood passages in the Quran. Critics often quote it in isolation to argue that Islam commands the killing of non-believers. Muslim scholars argue that removing it from context fundamentally distorts its meaning. For this passage you are reading 9:4 and 9:6 alongside 9:5 — the verses immediately before and after it — to reconstruct the actual situation being addressed. Pair them with verse 5:32, which states the Quran’s broadest claim about the value of human life, and consider how both belong to the same religious tradition. The group discussion will be your opportunity to work through the full implications together.
GRC 13Reading

Read verses 9:4, 9:5, and 9:6 together as a unit. What specific situation do verses 9:4 and 9:6 describe — who is being discussed, and what conditions are mentioned? How does that surrounding context change your reading of the command in 9:5?

GRC 14Reading

Verse 5:32 states that killing one innocent person is “as if he had killed all of mankind,” and saving one life is “as if he had saved all of mankind.” Where does the Quran say this principle originally comes from, and to whom is it addressed in the text?

GRC 15Reflection

You have now read both the “Sword Verse” in context and the Quran’s most sweeping statement about the sanctity of human life — and both come from the same book. How do you think a Muslim scholar would hold these two passages together? And more broadly: does reading a text in context change your ability to interpret it fairly? Group Discussion

Jihad, Violence, and the Problem of Context

You have now read the Quran’s most important passages on armed struggle alongside its most powerful statements about the sanctity of life and the prohibition on religious coercion. These texts coexist in the same book, in the same tradition, and have been interpreted in radically different ways — by scholars, jurists, reformers, and extremists — across fourteen centuries. Your group’s task is not to resolve this debate definitively but to map the interpretive problem clearly and think carefully about what is actually at stake when we read a religious text about violence.
D–1Discussion

Establish the tension. Before your group can argue, agree on what the text actually says. In your own words, state in one sentence what the Quran permits in 9:5 and in one sentence what it limits in 2:190–193 and 2:256. Then identify the single sharpest tension between those positions — the place where the two readings most directly collide.

D–2Discussion

Test the contextual defense. Muslim scholars argue that 9:5 addresses a specific historical situation — a broken treaty during a military conflict in 7th-century Arabia — and cannot be extracted and applied as a general command. Does your group find this argument convincing? What does it require us to believe about how scripture works? If context can limit a command this sharply, what does that mean for how we should read all religious texts?

D–3Discussion

Consider the double standard. Throughout this course we have read violent or troubling passages in the Hebrew Bible — conquest narratives in Deuteronomy, the destruction of Amalek, divine commands in Joshua. Those texts are rarely cited as evidence that Judaism or Christianity is inherently violent. Why do you think the Quran’s passages on warfare are treated differently in popular Western discourse? Is that difference justified, or does it reflect a double standard? What would a consistent standard of textual interpretation require?

D–4Discussion

Connect back across traditions. Earlier in this course you read Amos 5 and Isaiah 58 — texts in which the Hebrew prophets argued that ritual observance without justice is worthless before God. Today in verse 2:177 you read the Quran making a strikingly similar move: righteousness is not about turning toward Mecca, it is about caring for orphans and keeping your promises. Are these traditions making the same argument in different idioms — or does the difference in the details matter more than the surface similarity?
