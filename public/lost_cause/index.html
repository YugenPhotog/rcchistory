HIST 101 • US History to 1877

The Myth of the Lost Cause

How the South Won the War of Memory

This two-part lecture series examines how white Southerners constructed a mythological narrative about the Civil War—one that transformed military defeat into moral victory, erased slavery's centrality, and shaped American memory for over a century.

Part One

Origins of the Myth

1861–1877

How did the Lost Cause emerge during and immediately after the Civil War? We trace the myth from its origins as a wartime coping mechanism through its crystallization into five core claims by 1877.

  • The psychology of defeat and the need for meaning
  • The five core claims: states' rights, loyal slaves, saintly leaders, harmonious society, and fanatical abolitionists
  • Key architects: Edward Pollard and Jubal Early
  • Transformation into civil religion by 1877

Part Two

Institutionalization & Legacy

1877–Present

How did a regional coping mechanism become a national memory regime? We examine how the Lost Cause was institutionalized through education, monuments, popular culture, and political technology.

  • United Daughters of the Confederacy and monument campaigns
  • The Dunning School and academic legitimation
  • Birth of a Nation and Gone with the Wind
  • Confederate symbols during Civil Rights and today
